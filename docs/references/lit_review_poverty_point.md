## 1. Poverty Point Archaeology: Chronology, Construction, and Site Organization

### Grooms, S.B., Ward, G.K., & Kidder, T.R. 2023. Convergence at Poverty Point: a revised chronology of the Late Archaic Lower Mississippi Valley. *Antiquity* 97(396).
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.15184/aqy.2023.155
- Revises chronology of Poverty Point and related sites using new radiocarbon dates. Demonstrates that material traits usually assumed to define Poverty Point culture (artifact forms, raw material exchange, earthwork building) developed before the type-site was occupied or its earthworks constructed. Proposes that the Poverty Point site represents an endpoint, a convergence of many groups with distinct histories and practices, rather than a center of innovation that radiated outward. Directly relevant to the aggregation model in the signaling framework.

### Kidder, T.R. & Grooms, S.B. 2024. Chronological hygiene and Bayesian modeling of Poverty Point sites in the Lower Mississippi Valley, circa 4200 to 3200 cal BP. *American Antiquity* 89(1).
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1017/aaq.2023.64
- Applies chronological hygiene screening to radiocarbon dates from Poverty Point-era sites and uses Bayesian modeling to refine site chronologies. Challenges models of long-term continuity reaching back to Middle Archaic mound builders and suggestions that Poverty Point was a sedentary "Great Town." Important for calibrating the temporal parameters in the simulation model.

### Ward, G.K., Grooms, S.B., Schroll, A., & Kidder, T.R. 2022. The view from Jaketown: Considering variation in the Poverty Point culture of the Lower Mississippi Valley. *American Antiquity* 87(4):758-775.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1017/aaq.2022.44
- Demonstrates that Jaketown, previously interpreted as peripheral to Poverty Point, was occupied earlier and maintained distinct practices. People used PPOs and imported lithics at Jaketown by 4525-4100 cal BP, at least 400 years earlier than at the type-site. Proposes a framework focused on variation in material culture, architecture, and foodways rather than a center-periphery hierarchy. Challenges diffusionist models central to some signaling interpretations.

### Grooms, S.B. 2023. Anarchy, institutional flexibility, and containment of authority at Poverty Point (USA). *World Archaeology* 54(4).
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1080/00438243.2023.2169339
- Argues that Poverty Point builders used "institutional flexibility," creating and then dismantling hierarchical power systems during limited periods of gathering, then reverting to more autonomous power relations when dispersed. Directly relevant to the fission-fusion aggregation dynamics in the signaling model and the question of how cooperation was maintained without permanent hierarchy.

### Hargrave, M.L., Clay, R.B., Dalan, R.A., & Greenlee, D.M. 2021. The complex construction history of Poverty Point's timber circles and concentric ridges. *Southeastern Archaeology* 40(3):192-211.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1080/0734578X.2021.1961350
- Combines magnetic gradient surveys (~25 ha), magnetic susceptibility studies, coring, and excavation. Identifies 36 timber circles (diameters up to 62 m) in and near the plaza. Closely spaced posts and cluster patterns suggest extensive rebuilding. Innermost ridges (1-2) show longer, more complex construction histories than outer ridges, implying construction progressed outward over time. Provides granular construction sequence data for calibrating the model's monument investment parameters.

### Hargrave, M.L., Clay, R.B., Greenlee, D.M., & Dalan, R.A. 2023. Two types of ritual space at the Poverty Point site 16WC5. *American Antiquity*.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1017/aaq.2023.17
- Identifies two distinct types of ritual space: 38 circles (8-66 m diameter, mean 35 m) in the plaza interpreted as ritual timber-post facilities, and the concentric ridges distinguished by magnetic "perimeter" anomalies and habitation materials. Targeted excavation found large postholes in three circles; a fourth consists of pits. Directly relevant to understanding the functional differentiation of constructed space and whether monument investment served signaling vs. domestic purposes.

### Ortmann, A.L. 2010. Placing the Poverty Point mounds in their temporal context. *American Antiquity* 75(3):657-678.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.7183/0002-7316.75.3.657
- Comprehensive chronological analysis of Poverty Point mound construction. Provides the temporal framework for when mounds were built and used (ca. 1750-970 cal BC), with evidence supporting rapid construction late in site history. Important baseline for the model's construction chronology parameters.

### Kidder, T.R. 2011. Transforming hunter-gatherer history at Poverty Point. In K.E. Sassaman & D.H. Holly Jr. (eds.), *Hunter-Gatherer Archaeology as Historical Process*, pp. 95-119. University of Arizona Press, Tucson.
- [UNREAD]
- Book chapter arguing that Poverty Point challenges conventional understandings of hunter-gatherer complexity and cultural development. Situates Poverty Point within a historical process framework rather than treating it as a static cultural type. Relevant theoretical framing for the signaling argument.

### Kidder, T.R., Ortmann, A.L., & Arco, L.J. 2008. Poverty Point and the archaeology of singularity. *SAA Archaeological Record* 8(5):9-12.
- [PARTIAL] (already listed in CLAUDE.md Key References but not yet in the reference pipeline)
- Argues Poverty Point is a sui generis site, comparable to Stonehenge or Chaco Canyon in its uniqueness. Emphasizes that it was produced by a non-agricultural population. Relevant for framing the "paradox" of hunter-gatherer monumentality.

### Ortmann, A.L. & Kidder, T.R. 2017. Look to the earth: the search for ritual in the context of mound construction. *Archaeological and Anthropological Sciences* 10:1401-1421.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1007/s12520-016-0369-1
- Examines how mound building involved purposeful selection of soils and sediments for specific color, texture, or engineering properties, and how the organization of deposits communicates information. Argues that the building process itself was a ritual act, not merely a construction project. Relevant to interpreting monument construction as a signaling behavior embedded in ritual practice.

### Kidder, T.R., Grooms, S.B., & Baumgartel, O. 2024/2025. The archaeology of awe: monumental architecture, communal ritual, and community formation at Poverty Point, USA. *Journal of Archaeological Method and Theory* 31.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1007/s10816-024-09645-w
- Proposes that Poverty Point was a place of "awe," a prosocial emotion that leads to stronger social bonds. Argues the site was a collaborative gathering place for egalitarian hunter-gatherer groups united by shared ritual obligations, not a product of hierarchical command. Draws on psychological research demonstrating awe's role in community formation. Directly relevant to the cooperation mechanisms in the signaling model.

### Dalan, R.A. & Greenlee, D.M. 2007. Magnetic evidence of ridge construction and use at Poverty Point. *American Antiquity* 72(4).
- [UNREAD]
- Uses magnetic data to identify ridge construction components, providing evidence for single-stage and multi-stage construction, repairs, and possible deconstruction. Relevant to understanding the construction investment timeline.

### Kidder, T.R., Henry, E.R., & Arco, L.J. 2018. Rapid climate change-induced collapse of hunter-gatherer societies in the lower Mississippi River valley between ca. 3300 and 2780 cal yr BP. *Science China Earth Sciences* 61:1-12.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1007/s11430-017-9128-8
- Documents the collapse of the Poverty Point culture triggered by climate change causing exceptional flooding and Mississippi River course reorganization. A large flood buried Poverty Point-era deposits at Jaketown around 3310 cal yr BP. The lower Mississippi Valley was abandoned for ~500 years before Early Woodland recolonization ca. 2780 cal yr BP. Critical for understanding the terminal conditions of the signaling system and model collapse dynamics.

### Sherman, S. et al. 2023. Assessing raw material diversity at Poverty Point (16WC5) using non-destructive reflectance spectroscopy. *Geoarchaeology* 38.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1002/gea.21940
- First systematic spectroscopic analysis of 845 lithic bifaces at Poverty Point. Non-destructive reflectance spectroscopy proves more accurate than macroscopic identification for provenance. Relevant to quantifying the scale and diversity of the exotic material exchange network that the signaling model treats as individual-level costly signals.

### Sherman, S. et al. 2025. A dual-method chert sourcing approach: analytical provenance of bifaces at the Poverty Point site (16WC5), Louisiana. *Southeastern Archaeology* 44(1-2).
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1080/0734578X.2025.2601940
- Combines expert visual assessment with quantitative spectral data (VNIR and FTIR spectroscopy) for lithic sourcing, using LASSO regression and random forest analysis. Produces more robust source determinations than either method alone. Provides empirical sourcing data for parameterizing exchange-distance costs in the model.

### Hays, C.T., Weinstein, R.A., & Stoltman, J.B. 2016. Poverty Point Objects reconsidered. *Southeastern Archaeology* 35(3):213.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1080/0734578X.2016.1165050
- Examines PPOs across nine southeastern regions. Thin-section analyses of 66 samples demonstrate that PPOs circulated among different sites, not just produced locally. The Poverty Point culture was unique in the care, variety, and standardized forms of its baked-clay objects. Relevant to understanding whether PPOs could serve as signals of group identity or regional affiliation.
- NOTE: Distinguish from Hays 2018 (feasting with PPOs), which is already processed.

### Sassaman, K.E. & Brookes, S.O. 2017. Situating the Claiborne soapstone vessel cache in the history of Poverty Point. *American Antiquity* 82(2).
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1017/aaq.2017.3
- New AMS dates on carbon deposits from seven soapstone vessels from the Claiborne site show the cache was deposited ~200 years later than the heyday of Poverty Point exchange (3600-3400 cal BP), during or shortly before site abandonment. Relevant to understanding the temporal arc of exchange networks and their relationship to the signaling system's decline.

### Gibson, J.L. & Melancon, C. 2004. In the beginning: Social contexts of first pottery in the lower Mississippi valley. In R. Saunders & C.T. Hays (eds.), *Early Pottery: Technology, Function, Style, and Interaction in the Lower Southeast*, pp. 169-195. University of Alabama Press.
- [UNREAD]
- Discusses the social contexts surrounding the adoption of pottery in the lower Mississippi Valley, including at Poverty Point. Relevant to understanding material culture innovation and its relationship to aggregation dynamics.

## 2. Late Archaic Monumentality: Precursors and Parallels

### Saunders, J.W., Mandel, R.D., et al. 2005. Watson Brake, a Middle Archaic mound complex in northeast Louisiana. *American Antiquity* 70(4):631-668.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.2307/40035868
- Oldest earthwork mound complex in North America (~3500 BCE), predating Poverty Point by ~1800 years. Eleven mounds connected by ridges, built over ~500 years by hunter-gatherers. Extensive coring, stratigraphic studies, 25 radiocarbon dates, and 6 luminescence dates. Demonstrates that monumental construction by non-agricultural societies has deep roots in the lower Mississippi Valley. Essential context for arguing that Poverty Point's monumentality was not anomalous but part of a regional tradition.

### Saunders, J.W. 2004. Are we fixing to make the same mistake again? In J.L. Gibson & P.J. Carr (eds.), *Signs of Power: The Rise of Cultural Complexity in the Southeast*, pp. 146-161. University of Alabama Press.
- [UNREAD]
- Warns against applying hierarchical chiefdom models to Middle Archaic mound sites. Reviews Monte Sano, Hedgepeth, Frenchman's Bend, and Watson Brake mound complexes. At least 13 Middle Archaic mound sites exist in Louisiana, challenging the notion that monumental construction requires agricultural surplus or centralized authority. Directly relevant to the egalitarian signaling argument.

### Saunders, J.W. et al. 1997. A mound complex in Louisiana at 5400-5000 years before the present. *Science* 277(5333):1796-1799.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1126/science.277.5333.1796
- The initial publication that pushed back the date of mound construction in North America by ~2000 years. Forced revision of assumptions about the relationship between agricultural surplus and monumentality. A foundational citation for any argument about hunter-gatherer monumental construction.

### Randall, A.R. 2015. *Constructing Histories: Archaic Freshwater Shell Mounds and Social Landscapes of the St. Johns River, Florida*. University Press of Florida.
- [UNREAD]
- Reinterprets Archaic freshwater shell mounds as burial mounds, ceremonial gathering places, and habitation spaces central to the social geography of hunter-gatherer societies. Shell mounds built over 7000+ years in the St. Johns region, with some ridges exceeding 75,000 m3 of material. Provides a model for understanding how monument construction anchors mobile hunter-gatherer social landscapes, directly parallel to the Poverty Point argument.

### Sassaman, K.E. & Randall, A.R. 2012. Shell mounds of the middle St. Johns basin, northeast Florida. In R.L. Burger & R.M. Rosenswig (eds.), *Early New World Monumentality*, pp. 53-77. University Press of Florida.
- [UNREAD]
- Documents U-shaped shell monuments as products of commingling between pottery-making immigrants and established populations, producing contested social orders. Provides a comparative case for multi-community aggregation and monument construction by hunter-gatherers, with relevance to the convergence model proposed for Poverty Point.

### Thompson, V.D. & Pluckhahn, T.J. 2012. Monumentality and ritual materialization in the southeast. *American Antiquity* 77(1):132-163.
- [UNREAD]
- [CITE-CHECK: verify exact author list and page range]
- Examines shell rings on the Atlantic and Gulf coasts (ca. 4800-3200 cal BP). Over 50 documented shell rings from South Carolina to Mississippi. Some served as residential villages, others as seasonal feast gathering points, others as ceremonial centers. Directly comparable to Poverty Point as Late Archaic monumental aggregation sites built by hunter-gatherers.

### Thompson, V.D. 2023. Considering ideas of collective action, institutions, and "hunter-gatherers" in the American Southeast. *Journal of Archaeological Research* 31:503-560.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1007/s10814-022-09179-3
- Argues that archaeologists have underutilized collective action and institutional frameworks for studying hunter-gatherers in the Southeast. A focus on institutional change provides a way to connect histories across temporal units and understand how traditions developed, were maintained, and were reinvented. Highly relevant theoretical companion to the costly signaling model, offering complementary institutional mechanisms for cooperation.

### Sassaman, K.E. 2010. *The Eastern Archaic, Historicized*. AltaMira Press, Lanham.
- [UNREAD]
- Major synthesis of the Archaic period in eastern North America from a historical process perspective. Challenges neoevolutionary frameworks that treat Archaic societies as static "stages." Relevant theoretical framing for understanding Poverty Point within the longer trajectory of southeastern hunter-gatherer histories.

### Anderson, D.G. & Sassaman, K.E. 2012. *Recent Developments in Southeastern Archaeology: From Colonization to Complexity*. SAA Press.
- [UNREAD]
- Period-by-period synthesis of southeastern prehistory. Includes discussion of Archaic mound building, exchange networks, and emergent complexity. Useful general reference for situating Poverty Point within the broader regional trajectory.

## 3. Hunter-Gatherer Aggregation, Cooperation, and Social Organization

### Grove, M. 2009. Hunter-gatherer movement patterns: Causes and constraints. *Journal of Anthropological Archaeology* 28(2):222-233.
- [UNREAD]
- [CITE-CHECK: verify exact citation]
- Examines the relationship between resource distribution and hunter-gatherer mobility patterns, including the role of aggregation in social organization. Relevant to modeling band movement and aggregation decisions.

### Hamilton, M.J., Milne, B.T., Walker, R.S., Burger, O., & Brown, J.H. 2007. The complex structure of hunter-gatherer social networks. *Proceedings of the Royal Society B* 274:2195-2202.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1098/rspb.2007.0564
- Demonstrates that population structure in hunter-gatherer societies exhibits self-similar (fractal-like) patterns, with the number of individuals at each successively higher organizational level showing a constant ratio close to 4. This self-similarity holds both within and across cultures and continents. Relevant to modeling the nested social structure (band, community, regional network) in the Poverty Point system.

### Grove, M. 2010. Logistical mobility reduces subsistence risk in hunting economies. *Journal of Archaeological Science* 37(8):1829-1840.
- [UNREAD]
- [CITE-CHECK: verify exact citation]
- Models how logistical mobility strategies reduce subsistence risk in hunting economies. Relevant to understanding why aggregation at a central place like Poverty Point might buffer risk compared to fully mobile strategies.

### Hill, K.R., Walker, R.S., Bozicevic, M., et al. 2011. Co-residence patterns in hunter-gatherer societies show unique human social structure. *Science* 331(6022):1286-1289.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1126/science.1199071
- Shows that hunter-gatherer bands consist of many unrelated individuals, with co-residence patterns driven by bilateral kinship and individual choice rather than rigid descent rules. Relevant to modeling the fluid band composition assumed for Poverty Point aggregation.

### Hill, K.R. et al. 2014. Hunter-gatherer inter-band interaction rates: Implications for cumulative culture. *PLoS ONE* 9(7):e102806.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0102806
- Quantifies inter-band visitation and interaction rates among modern hunter-gatherers. Provides empirical parameters for modeling the frequency and duration of aggregation events.

### Aureli, F. et al. 2008. Fission-fusion dynamics: New research frameworks. *Current Anthropology* 49(4):627-654.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1086/586708
- Comprehensive framework for understanding fission-fusion social dynamics across primates and humans. Identifies key variables (spatial cohesion, subgroup size, subgroup composition) that characterize fission-fusion variation. Provides theoretical foundation for modeling Poverty Point's seasonal aggregation-dispersal pattern.

### Grove, M. 2010. Variability in the organization and size of hunter-gatherer groups. *Journal of Human Evolution* 60(1):1-13.
- [UNREAD]
- [CITE-CHECK: verify exact citation and year]
- Analyzes variation in hunter-gatherer group size and organization, arguing that foragers do not uniformly live in small-scale societies. Relevant to modeling population size during Poverty Point aggregation events.

### Shott, M.J. 2023. Hunter-gatherer fission-fusion in ethnography and archaeology: The case of eastern North America's Fisher Paleoindian site. *Journal of Anthropological Archaeology* 70:101510.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1016/j.jaa.2023.101510
- Applies fission-fusion models to archaeological data from eastern North America, including discussion of Archaic period analogues. Provides an archaeological test case for the kind of aggregation-dispersal dynamics modeled for Poverty Point.

## 4. Costly Signaling Theory Applied to Archaeology

### Kantner, J. & Vaughn, K.J. 2012. Pilgrimage as costly signal: Religiously motivated cooperation in Chaco and Nasca. *Journal of Anthropological Archaeology* 31(1):66-82.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1016/j.jaa.2011.10.001
- Applies costly signaling theory to pilgrimage at Chaco Canyon and Cahuachi (Nasca). Argues that pilgrimage serves as a costly signal of religious commitment that facilitates cooperation among strangers. Directly comparable to the Poverty Point model: both involve monumental centers drawing dispersed populations for periodic gatherings. The parallel framework (pilgrimage = aggregation; great house = mound) strengthens the theoretical foundation.

### Neiman, F.D. 1997. Conspicuous consumption as wasteful advertising: A Darwinian perspective on spatial patterns in Classic Maya terminal monument dates. In C.M. Barton & G.A. Clark (eds.), *Rediscovering Darwin: Evolutionary Theory and Archaeological Explanation*, pp. 267-290. American Anthropological Association.
- [UNREAD]
- Early application of costly signaling/conspicuous consumption to archaeological monumental construction. Uses Classic Maya stela erection frequencies as competitive signaling between polities. Provides a precedent for treating monument construction as a costly signal, though in a more hierarchical context than Poverty Point.

### Boone, J.L. 1998. The evolution of magnanimity: When is it better to give than to receive? *Human Nature* 9(3):235-262.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1007/s12110-998-1009-y
- Explains conspicuous consumption and status reinforcement behavior in terms of costly signaling theory. Proposes that the evolutionary function of status reinforcement lies not only in lifetime reproductive success but in survival probability through rare demographic bottlenecks. Relevant to explaining why Poverty Point participants invested in signaling during periods of environmental uncertainty.

### Gintis, H., Smith, E.A., & Bowles, S. 2001. Costly signaling and cooperation. *Journal of Theoretical Biology* 213:103-119.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1006/jtbi.2001.2406
- Formal game-theoretic model showing how costly signals can promote cooperation when individuals vary in quality and when costs and benefits satisfy specific conditions. Provides the mathematical foundation for the signaling-cooperation link assumed in the Poverty Point model.

### Smith, E.A. & Bliege Bird, R. 2000. Turtle hunting and tombstone opening: Public generosity as costly signaling. *Evolution and Human Behavior* 21(4):245-261.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1016/S1090-5138(00)00031-3
- Ethnographic test of costly signaling theory among the Meriam of Torres Strait, Australia. Documents how turtle hunting and feast sponsorship function as honest signals of quality. Provides ethnographic analogue for understanding how communal labor contributions at Poverty Point might function as signals.

### Bliege Bird, R., Smith, E.A., & Bird, D.W. 2001. The hunting handicap: Costly signaling in human foraging strategies. *Behavioral Ecology and Sociobiology* 50:9-19.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1007/s002650100338
- Shows that Meriam men's turtle hunting is a costly signal of phenotypic quality, with successful hunters gaining social and reproductive benefits. Demonstrates that foraging strategies can simultaneously serve subsistence and signaling functions. Relevant to understanding dual-function behaviors at Poverty Point (e.g., collective fishing as both subsistence and signal).

### Codding, B.F. & Jones, T.L. 2013. Environmental productivity predicts migration, demographic, and linguistic patterns in prehistoric California. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 110(36):14569-14573.
- [UNREAD]
- [CITE-CHECK: verify relevance to signaling; may be tangential]
- Documents how environmental productivity shaped population movements and social organization among California hunter-gatherers. Potentially relevant to understanding how ecotone productivity at Poverty Point's location shaped aggregation dynamics.

### Cronk, L. 2005. The application of animal signaling theory to human phenomena: Some thoughts and clarifications. *Social Science Information* 44(4):603-620.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1177/0539018405058203
- Reviews and clarifies the application of animal signaling theory to human cultural phenomena. Addresses common misunderstandings of costly signaling theory in anthropological contexts. Useful for refining the theoretical framework.

## 5. Lower Mississippi Valley Paleoclimate and Paleoecology

### Delcourt, P.A. & Delcourt, H.R. 1981. Vegetation maps for eastern North America: 40,000 yr B.P. to the present. In R.C. Romans (ed.), *Geobotany II*, pp. 123-165. Plenum Press.
- [UNREAD]
- Comprehensive vegetation maps at 4000-year intervals documenting temperate forest ecosystem responses to climate change. Includes the lower Mississippi Valley. Relevant for reconstructing the ecological setting during the Poverty Point occupation and parameterizing resource availability in the model.

### Delcourt, H.R. & Delcourt, P.A. 1985. Quaternary paleoecology and vegetation science of the southeastern United States. In W.C. Bryant Jr. & R.G. Holloway (eds.), *Pollen Records of Late-Quaternary North American Sediments*, pp. 1-37. AASP Foundation.
- [UNREAD]
- Provides palynological data from southeastern sites relevant to reconstructing the vegetation and climate context during the Poverty Point period. Essential for environmental parameter estimation.

### Kidder, T.R. 1996. New directions in Lower Mississippi Valley archaeology. In K.E. Sassaman & D.G. Anderson (eds.), *Archaeology of the Mid-Holocene Southeast*, pp. 198-211. University Press of Florida.
- [UNREAD]
- Synthesizes geoarchaeological and environmental data for the lower Mississippi Valley during the mid-Holocene, including floodplain dynamics and channel migration relevant to understanding the landscape context of Poverty Point.

### Asmerom, Y. et al. 2013. Variable winter moisture in the southwestern United States linked to rapid glacial climate shifts. *Nature Geoscience* 3:114-117.
- [UNREAD]
- [CITE-CHECK: verify year and whether geographic scope reaches lower Mississippi Valley]

## 6. Long-Distance Exchange Networks in Eastern North America

### Hill, M.E. et al. 2016. Assessing the provenance of Poverty Point copper through LA-ICP-MS compositional analysis. *Journal of Archaeological Science: Reports* 6:388-397.
- NOTE: This appears to be the same as Hill et al. 2010 already in the pipeline, possibly a later formal publication of the same data. Verify whether both are needed.

### Truncer, J. 2004. Steatite vessel age and occurrence in temperate eastern North America. *American Antiquity* 69(3):487-513.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.2307/4128403
- Comprehensive review of steatite vessel chronology and distribution in eastern North America. Relevant to understanding the temporal and spatial dimensions of the soapstone exchange network that connected Poverty Point to Appalachian quarries (~900 km). Important for parameterizing exchange costs and distances.

### Sassaman, K.E. 2006. Dating and explaining soapstone vessels: A comment on Truncer. *American Antiquity* 71(2):336-345.
- [UNREAD]
- Critique and expansion of Truncer's steatite vessel chronology, with implications for understanding the temporality and social meaning of long-distance exchange in the Southeast. Relevant to the exotic goods component of the signaling model.

### Goad, S.I. 1979. Middle Archaic exchange in the Southeast. *Southeastern Archaeological Conference Bulletin* 22:33-36.
- [UNREAD]
- Early documentation of Middle Archaic exchange networks predating Poverty Point. Establishes that long-distance exchange in the Southeast has deep antecedents, not originating with the Poverty Point culture. Relevant for understanding the pre-existing social infrastructure that Poverty Point aggregations drew upon.

### Walthall, J.A. 1981. *Galena and Aboriginal Trade in Eastern North America*. Illinois State Museum, Scientific Papers 17. Springfield.
- [UNREAD]
- Comprehensive study of galena exchange across eastern North America. Galena from Missouri/Illinois sources (~800 km) is one of the key exotic materials at Poverty Point. Provides source identification and distribution data relevant to modeling exchange network costs.

## 7. Hunter-Gatherer Complexity and Theoretical Frameworks

### Sassaman, K.E. (ed.) 2004. *Signs of Power: The Rise of Cultural Complexity in the Southeast*. University of Alabama Press.
- [UNREAD]
- [CITE-CHECK: verify Sassaman as editor; Gibson & Carr may be editors instead]
- Edited volume addressing emergent complexity in the Southeast, including chapters on Middle and Late Archaic mound complexes. Contains Saunders 2004 chapter on Middle Archaic mounds. Provides the broader archaeological context for interpreting Poverty Point.

### Prentiss, A.M. & Kuijt, I. 2004. Complex hunter-gatherers in evolution and history: A North American perspective. *Journal of Archaeological Research* 12:75-119.
- [UNREAD] DOI: 10.1023/B:JARE.0000040231.67149.a8
- Major review of complex hunter-gatherer studies in North America. Documents tensions between evolutionary and historical epistemologies. The southeastern United States gives evidence of mound construction apart from significant subsistence change, turning focus to symbolic and structural dimensions. Provides theoretical context for the Poverty Point case.

### Hayden, B. 2014. *The Power of Feasts: From Prehistory to Present*. Cambridge University Press.
- [UNREAD]
- Develops the "aggrandizer" model for transegalitarian societies where feasting drives social differentiation. Argues that surplus conditions enable competitive feasting, which creates nascent inequality. Relevant as an alternative or complementary framework to costly signaling for explaining Poverty Point feasting evidence (cf. Hays 2018).

### Hayden, B. 1995. Pathways to power: Principles for creating socioeconomic inequalities. In T.D. Price & G.M. Feinman (eds.), *Foundations of Social Inequality*, pp. 15-86. Plenum Press.
- [UNREAD]
- Introduces the aggrandizer concept and competitive feasting model. Distinguishes egalitarian from transegalitarian hunter-gatherers based on resource surplus. Important alternative theoretical framework that should be addressed in the manuscript.

### Kelly, R.L. 2013. *The Lifeways of Hunter-Gatherers: The Foraging Spectrum*. Cambridge University Press.
- NOTE: Already listed in CLAUDE.md Key References but not yet in the reference pipeline. Comprehensive synthesis of hunter-gatherer variability. Essential for parameterizing the model's assumptions about band size, mobility, and subsistence.

### Conkey, M.W. 1980. The identification of prehistoric hunter-gatherer aggregation sites: The case of Altamira. *Current Anthropology* 21(5):609-630.
- NOTE: Already listed in CLAUDE.md Key References but not yet in the reference pipeline. Foundational paper on archaeological identification of aggregation sites. Provides criteria (artifact diversity, stylistic complexity) applicable to Poverty Point.

## 8. Comparative Cases and Regional Context

### Kidder, T.R. 2004. Plazas as architecture: An example from the Raffman site, northeast Louisiana. *American Antiquity* 69(3):514-532.
- [UNREAD]
- [CITE-CHECK: verify exact title and year]
- Examines plaza construction as an architectural form in northeast Louisiana. Relevant to understanding the role of the central plaza at Poverty Point as a constructed gathering space.

### Jackson, H.E. 1990. Poverty Point adaptive systems in the lower Mississippi Valley: Subsistence remains from the J. W. Copes site. *North American Archaeologist* 11(1):1-29.

# Gap Analysis

## Well-Covered Topics
1. **Poverty Point site description and construction**: Strong coverage through Webb (1956, 1968, 1982), Ford (1954), Kidder (2002), Ortmann (2007), Ortmann & Kidder (2013), Britt et al. (2002). The recently identified papers (Hargrave et al. 2021, 2023; Dalan & Greenlee 2007; Ortmann 2010) would add valuable detail on construction sequences.
2. **Exotic materials and exchange**: Good baseline from Webb, Lehmann (1990), Blackburn (2003), Hill et al. (2010/2016), Lipo (2012). Sherman et al. (2023, 2025) would add modern analytical sourcing data.
3. **PPO studies**: Pierce (1997) and Hays (2018) provide opposing interpretations. Hays, Weinstein & Stoltman (2016) would add important circulation data.
4. **Paleoclimate**: Shuman & Marsicek (2016), Shuman (2024), Kidder (2006), and Kaufman et al. (2020) provide strong environmental context.

## Moderately Covered Topics
5. **Chronology**: Kidder (2006) and Kidder & Grooms (2025) are processed. Adding Grooms et al. (2023), Kidder & Grooms (2024), Ward et al. (2022), and Ortmann (2010) would substantially strengthen the chronological framework, especially the revised "convergence" model.
6. **Costly signaling theory**: Dunnell & Greenlee (1999), Hamilton & Buchanan (2009) provide foundations. Bliege Bird & Smith (2005) and Hawkes & Bliege Bird (2002) are in the CLAUDE.md references but not yet processed. The theoretical gaps (Gintis et al. 2001; Kantner & Vaughn 2012; Boone 1998; Smith & Bliege Bird 2000) would strengthen the formal signaling argument.

## Significant Gaps
7. **Late Archaic comparative cases**: The project has almost nothing on Watson Brake, shell rings, or other contemporary monumental sites. Saunders et al. (1997, 2005), Sassaman & Randall (2012), Thompson (2023), and Randall (2015) are essential for the "why here, why then" questions.
8. **Fission-fusion social dynamics and aggregation theory**: The project lacks formal treatment of fission-fusion dynamics beyond Conkey (1980). Aureli et al. (2008), Hamilton et al. (2007), Hill et al. (2011, 2014), and Shott (2023) would provide the ethnographic and theoretical grounding for the aggregation model.
9. **Social organization and power at Poverty Point**: Grooms (2023) on institutional flexibility and anarchy is critical for the egalitarian signaling argument. The "archaeology of awe" paper (Kidder, Grooms, & Baumgartel 2024/2025) provides a complementary prosocial emotions framework.
10. **Collapse and post-Poverty Point dynamics**: Kidder, Henry, & Arco (2018) on climate-induced collapse is a major gap; the project has Kidder (2006) but needs the Jaketown flood data to explain termination.
11. **Feasting and competitive generosity**: Hayden (2014, 1995) on aggrandizers and competitive feasting needs to be addressed as the principal alternative to the signaling explanation, especially given Hays's (2018) feasting interpretation.
12. **Steatite exchange specifics**: Truncer (2004) and Sassaman (2006) would add temporal depth and social meaning to the soapstone exchange that Sassaman (2005) touches on.
