## Theme 1: Paleoclimate and Environmental Variability

Sources providing climate proxy data relevant to calibrating environmental uncertainty (sigma) in the signaling model. The model requires quantitative estimates of resource variability during the Poverty Point interval (ca. 3700-3100 cal BP).

### Kidder, T.R., Liu, H., & Li, Y. (2018). Rapid climate change-induced collapse of hunter-gatherer societies in the lower Mississippi River valley between ca. 3300 and 2780 cal yr BP.

**Citation:** Kidder, T.R., Liu, H., & Li, Y. 2018. Rapid climate change-induced collapse of hunter-gatherer societies in the lower Mississippi River valley between ca. 3300 and 2780 cal yr BP. *Science China Earth Sciences* 61:172-181. DOI: 10.1007/s11430-017-9128-8
**Access level:** Abstract only
**Relevance:** High -- directly documents climate forcing during Poverty Point collapse
**Theme:** Paleoclimate and environmental variability
**Evidence type:** Empirical
**[PARTIAL: annotation based on abstract/metadata only]**

**Annotation:** Uses geoarchaeological data from Jaketown (Mississippi) to document a major flood event at ca. 3310 cal BP that buried occupation deposits. Links Mississippi River course reorganization and increased flood frequency to the collapse of Poverty Point-period societies between ca. 3300 and 2780 cal BP.

**What's known:** Flood chronology from Jaketown; correlation of climate-driven flooding with cultural collapse; extends Kidder 2006 analysis with additional proxy data.
**What's unknown:** Specific flood magnitude estimates; proxy resolution for the 3700-3300 BP interval (pre-collapse); whether the flooding data can be translated to sigma values for the model.

**Citation:** Medina-Elizalde, M. et al. 2022. *Paleoceanography and Paleoclimatology* 37. DOI: 10.1029/2021PA004346
**Access level:** Abstract only
**Relevance:** High -- closest high-resolution speleothem proxy to Poverty Point latitude
**Theme:** Paleoclimate and environmental variability
**Evidence type:** Empirical
**[PARTIAL: annotation based on abstract/metadata only]**

**Annotation:** Presents stalagmite oxygen isotope records from Alabama covering the full Holocene, with data spanning abrupt climate events including the 4.2 ka event. Alabama caves at ~33 degrees N are the closest high-resolution speleothem sites to Poverty Point (32.6 degrees N).

**What's known:** Stalagmite delta-18O record spanning Holocene; documents SE US hydroclimate response to abrupt events.
**What's unknown:** Temporal resolution during the 3700-3100 BP interval specifically; whether drought or wet episodes dominate during Poverty Point occupation; quantitative precipitation estimates vs. qualitative wet/dry assessments.

## Theme 3: Poverty Point Chronology and Construction

Sources providing chronological frameworks and construction data for Poverty Point and related sites. Critical for the temporal dynamics of the signaling model and calibrating construction costs.

### Grooms, S.B., Ward, G.M.V., & Kidder, T.R. (2023). Convergence at Poverty Point: a revised chronology of the Late Archaic Lower Mississippi Valley.

**Citation:** Grooms, S.B. et al. 2023. *Antiquity* 97(396):1453-1469. DOI: 10.15184/aqy.2023.100
**Access level:** Abstract only
**Relevance:** High -- most recent comprehensive Bayesian chronology
**Theme:** Poverty Point chronology and construction
**Evidence type:** Empirical/Methodological
**[PARTIAL: annotation based on abstract/metadata only]**

**Annotation:** Applies Bayesian chronological modeling to revise the Poverty Point cultural sequence. Shows Jaketown predates the type-site for key cultural traits, recasting Poverty Point as a convergence point for groups with distinct regional histories rather than a center of innovation that diffused outward. People used PPOs and imported lithics at Jaketown by 4525-4100 cal BP.

**What's known:** Bayesian radiocarbon framework; Jaketown's earlier adoption of PP cultural traits; convergence rather than diffusion model.
**What's unknown:** Full date compilation; details of the Bayesian model structure; implications for the tempo of earthwork construction.

**Relevance:** High -- rigorous chronological framework with 157 radiocarbon dates
**Theme:** Poverty Point chronology and construction

# Gap Analysis (Updated after full processing of 16 sources)

## Well-Covered Topics

1. **Poverty Point construction chronology and labor investment.** Now comprehensively covered. Kidder & Grooms 2024 (157 dates, Bayesian modeling), Grooms et al. 2023 (Jaketown predates PP), Kidder et al. 2021 (RW3 in days-weeks), Ortmann & Kidder 2013 (Mound A in <90 days, 1,000-3,000 laborers), Hargrave et al. 2021 (25 ha geophysics, 64 ridge components), Clay 2023 (36 timber circles). Key result: earthwork construction compressed to ca. 3300-3225 cal BP, late in the occupation. Fall seasonality inferred.

2. **Costly signaling theory in archaeology.** Well-covered by Quinn 2019 (comprehensive review, analytical framework), Peacock & Rafferty 2013 (bet-hedging alternative), and Dunnell & Greenlee 1999 (waste hypothesis). Key theoretical tension identified: Peacock & Rafferty claim CST requires stable environments while bet-hedging requires variable ones. Our model bridges this gap.

3. **Exotic material sourcing.** Copper (Hill et al. 2010, 2016), steatite (Smith in Byrd 1991), and general raw material diversity (Sherman 2023) documented. Gibson 2000 (not yet acquired) reportedly provides the 71-metric-ton total exotic material estimate.

4. **Interpretive frameworks.** Sanger 2023 (institutional flexibility) and Sanger 2024 (awe) provide complementary frameworks. Neither competes with CST; both can be integrated. Sanger 2023 explains authority containment during aggregation; Sanger 2024 identifies a proximate psychological mechanism.

5. **Collapse mechanism.** Kidder, Henry & Arco 2018 provides precise flood chronology (3310 cal BP at Jaketown), 530-year abandonment gap, and complete loss of the signaling system (no monuments, no exchange, no lapidary art post-flood).

6. **Comparative cases.** Saunders et al. 2005 provides Watson Brake as pre-PP comparison (mound building without exchange, episodic construction, ecotone location, 1,300-year gap to PP).

## Under-Covered Topics (gaps that remain)

7. **Quantified subsistence data.** Jackson (1986, 1989) remains the primary source. Saunders et al. 2005 adds Watson Brake fish data (56+ taxa). Still need caloric return rates by resource type and season for modeling aggregation benefits.

8. **Paleoclimate proxies for the LMV specifically.** CONFIRMED CRITICAL GAP. Medina-Elizalde et al. 2022 has a growth hiatus during 4.3-2.6 ka BP (no data during PP occupation). Bird et al. 2017 covers only the last 2100 years. Liefert & Shuman 2022 covers 4.2 ka event but from western US. No high-resolution proxy directly covers the PP interval at PP latitude. Must rely on: Shuman & Marsicek 2016 (compilation), Shuman 2024 (multi-scale analysis), and the AMOC-wetting mechanism from Medina-Elizalde applied by analogy.

9. **Ecotone ecology and resource patch structure.** Webb (1982) identifies ecotone positioning as the "ecological constant," and both PP and Watson Brake show ecotone locations. But no published study quantifies resource diversity or patch covariance at Macon Ridge. May need ecological literature rather than archaeological sources.

10. **Demographic estimates.** Ortmann & Kidder 2013 provide 1,000-3,000 laborers for Mound A (91,700 person-days). Kidder & Grooms 2024 note an "extraordinarily large labor force." But residential population estimates remain poorly constrained. Kidder & Grooms 2025 estimate ~1,820 km2 catchment.

11. **Iterative vs. single-episode construction.** Tension between Hargrave et al. 2021 (64 ridge components, modular iterative building) and Kidder et al. 2021/Ortmann & Kidder 2013 (individual episodes completed in days-weeks). Resolution: each aggregation event may have produced rapid construction, but the full site accumulated over multiple such events. Needs explicit treatment in the model.

# Key Theoretical Tensions to Address in Manuscript

1. **CST vs. bet-hedging.** Peacock & Rafferty 2013 argue these are distinct mechanisms (CST for stable, bet-hedging for variable environments). Our model must explicitly argue that signaling can be adaptive under uncertainty when it facilitates cooperation, dissolving this distinction.

2. **Rapid vs. iterative construction.** Hargrave et al. 2021's 64 ridge components suggest sustained modular building, while Kidder et al. 2021 and Ortmann & Kidder 2013 demonstrate individual episodes were rapid. Resolution: each aggregation-driven construction episode was fast, but the full site is the product of many such episodes.

3. **Convergence vs. diffusion.** Grooms et al. 2023 and Kidder & Grooms 2024 demonstrate PP was a convergence endpoint, not an innovation center. This strengthens the aggregation hypothesis but complicates models assuming a single origin point for the signaling system.

4. **Saunders et al.'s rejection of bet-hedging.** Watson Brake mounds built during stable intervals, not crises. May support our threshold model: signaling emerges at intermediate sigma, not extreme conditions.

5. **Just-so-story critique.** Quinn 2019 identifies this as central challenge. Our formal model directly addresses this by generating testable predictions.
