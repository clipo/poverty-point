# Counter-Arguments to Costly Signaling Theory Applied to Monumental Architecture

**Date compiled**: 2026-03-22
**Purpose**: Identify published critiques, alternative frameworks, and empirical challenges to costly signaling theory (CST) as applied to monumental architecture, with emphasis on Poverty Point, Hopewell/Middle Woodland, and hunter-gatherer contexts. This review supports the Discussion section of the Poverty Point Costly Signaling manuscript.

**Status convention**: [UNREAD] = source not available in the repo; [PARTIAL] = abstract/summary only, full text not verified; [IN-REPO] = PDF or claims extraction available in the repo.

---

## 1. Theoretical Critiques (Signaling Theory Itself Is Flawed or Unfalsifiable)

### 1.1 The Handicap Principle Has Been Refuted in Biology

**Source**: Penn, D.J. and Szamado, S. 2020. The Handicap Principle: how an erroneous hypothesis became a scientific principle. *Biological Reviews* 95(1):267-290. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: The Zahavian handicap principle, which underpins much of costly signaling theory, is "illogical, non-Darwinian and erroneous." Theoretical models have shown that signaling costs paid at equilibrium are neither sufficient nor necessary to maintain signal honesty. Honesty can evolve through differential benefits, differential costs, or differential trade-offs rather than through wasteful expenditure. Penn and Szamado argue the handicap principle should be abandoned entirely.

**Relevance to our model**: This is the most fundamental critique. If the handicap principle is wrong, then the entire theoretical apparatus linking "costliness" to "honesty" requires revision. However, our model does not rely strictly on the Zahavian handicap principle. We model signaling through differential costs and benefits (agents that can afford to signal do so; those that cannot, cannot), which is consistent with the revised signaling theory Penn and Szamado advocate (trade-off-based honesty rather than pure waste). Our model's key mechanism is that monument construction and exotic acquisition are differentially costly depending on group capacity, which maintains honesty without requiring pure waste.

**What our model addresses**: Our framework is compatible with the post-handicap revision. Signals in our model are honest because of differential costs (capacity-dependent), not because of pure waste.

**What our model does not address**: We have not explicitly engaged with Penn and Szamado's critique or stated how our model differs from the strict handicap formulation. The manuscript should clarify this.

### 1.2 CST in Hunter-Gatherer Research Relies on Overemphasis of Realized Costs

**Source**: Stibbard-Hawkes, D.N.E. 2019. Costly signaling and the handicap principle in hunter-gatherer research: A critical review. *Evolutionary Anthropology* 28(3):144-157. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: Three problems with CST as applied to hunter-gatherers: (a) overemphasis on demonstrating realized costs, which are neither necessary nor sufficient to diagnose costly signaling; (b) lack of clear predictions about what specific qualities the signal advertises; (c) insufficient attention to the broadcast effectiveness of the signal and its value as a heuristic for receivers.

**Relevance to our model**: All three critiques apply. (a) Our model must specify what makes monument construction an honest signal beyond its cost. (b) We need to articulate clearly what quality is being signaled: group commitment, cooperative capacity, resource access, or something else. (c) We need to explain how the signal is received and by whom: what information do visiting bands extract from the monument?

**What our model addresses**: The model specifies that construction signals group cooperative capacity and commitment, and that the signal is received by other aggregating bands who use it to calibrate cooperation decisions. The physical permanence and unfakeability of earthworks addresses broadcast effectiveness.

**What our model does not address**: We could strengthen the argument by specifying more precisely the receiver's decision rule and how the signal content maps onto cooperation payoffs.

### 1.3 CST Creates "Just-So Stories" in Archaeology

**Source**: Codding, B.F. and Jones, T.L. 2007. Man the Showoff? Or the Ascendance of a Just-So Story: A Comment on Recent Applications of Costly Signaling Theory in American Archaeology. *American Antiquity* 72(2):349-357. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: Three challenges to CST models in archaeology: (1) the relationships among foraging, provisioning, prestige, and fitness remain actively contested; (2) signaling activities contribute very little to archaeological residues that accumulate in the record; (3) CST explicitly precludes the type of runaway positive feedback loops that some scholars implicate as driving cultural collapse.

**Relevance to our model**: Critique (1) is partially relevant: our model operates at the group level (bands signaling to other bands), not individual foraging prestige. Critique (2) is less relevant because monumental construction leaves massive archaeological residues, unlike the hunting behaviors Codding and Jones discuss. Critique (3) is relevant to our collapse mechanism.

**What our model addresses**: Monument construction produces abundant, permanent archaeological residues, directly countering critique (2). Our collapse mechanism is driven by environmental change reducing sigma below the signaling threshold, not by runaway feedback.

**What our model does not address**: We should explicitly address why group-level monumental signaling differs from individual-level hunting signaling that Codding and Jones critique.

---

### 1.4 Reply Defending CST

**Source**: Hildebrandt, W.R. and McGuire, K.R. 2012. Costly Signaling and the Ascendance of No-Can-Do Archaeology: A Reply to Codding and Jones. *American Antiquity* 77(3):560-564. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: Defends CST applications against Codding and Jones, arguing that critique represents "No-Can-Do Archaeology" that dismisses useful theoretical frameworks prematurely. The debate remained unresolved.

**Relevance to our model**: Documents that the CST debate in American archaeology is ongoing and unresolved. Our model should contribute to this debate with a formal, testable framework rather than ad hoc application.

---

### 1.5 CST Lacks Methodological Development for Archaeological Application

**Source**: Quinn, C.P. 2019. Costly Signaling Theory in Archaeology. In: *Handbook of Evolutionary Research in Archaeology*, edited by A.M. Prentiss, pp. 275-294. Springer. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: CST has been criticized for lack of methodological development in archaeology, resulting in "just so" stories. Quinn notes persistent doubts about CST's explanatory utility and calls for more rigorous analytical frameworks. Key issues: archaeologists cannot perform the cost-benefit analyses economists use to test signaling models, and connecting archaeological evidence to evolutionary theory remains difficult.

**Relevance to our model**: Our agent-based model provides exactly the kind of rigorous, formal framework Quinn calls for. The model generates quantitative predictions about construction rates, aggregation size, exotic goods distributions, and collapse timing that can be compared to archaeological data.

**What our model addresses**: Formal ABM with testable predictions addresses Quinn's call for methodological rigor.

**What our model does not address**: We should cite Quinn's review and position our model as responding to the methodological gap.

## 2. Empirical Critiques (Evidence Does Not Support Signaling at Specific Sites)

### 2.1 Kidder and Grooms Reject Prestige-Competition Frameworks for Poverty Point

**Source**: Kidder, T.R. and Grooms, S.B. 2025. Performance, ritual, and revitalization at Poverty Point. *Southeastern Archaeology* 44(3-4). [IN-REPO: kidder_grooms_2025_claims.md]

**Argument**: Performances at Poverty Point were "not attempts to distract from reproduction, symbols of power, displays of magic, or ritualized economic practices, in which 'individuals acquire prestige, ritual status, and greater access to material goods by sponsoring important events for the community'" (rejecting Stanish 2017:10). The authors reject hidden-power models and individual prestige accumulation as drivers. Instead, they argue for collective moral obligation and revitalization in response to environmental crisis.

**Relevance to our model**: This is a direct critique of prestige-based signaling frameworks applied to Poverty Point. However, our model is not a prestige-accumulation model. We model cooperative signaling at the group level (bands signaling commitment to cooperation), not individual prestige accumulation. The behavioral pattern Kidder and Grooms describe (voluntary aggregation, cooperative labor investment during environmental crisis, costly material deposition) aligns with our model's predictions even though their interpretive language differs.

**What our model addresses**: Group-level cooperative signaling does not require individual prestige accumulation. Voluntary participation, no hierarchy, and environmentally triggered aggregation are all features our model generates.

**What our model does not address**: The Indigenous moral economy framework (cosmological revitalization) provides a proximate motivation for behavior that our model treats mechanistically. We should acknowledge this as a complementary rather than competing framework.

---

### 2.2 No Evidence of Hierarchy or Wealth Concentration at Poverty Point

**Source**: Kidder, T.R. and Grooms, S.B. 2025 [IN-REPO]; Ortmann, A.L. 2007 [IN-REPO: ortmann_2007_claims.md]; Webb, C.H. 1982 [IN-REPO]

**Argument**: "There is no evidence of institutionalized leaders, no economic specialization beyond the presence of skilled artisans, no houses expected of a population center, no evidence of prolonged, generational construction or occupation, and no burials" (Kidder and Grooms 2025, citing Gibson 2010:35). Mound summits lack domestic or elite-associated debris (Ortmann 2007). Exotic goods are distributed across the site, not concentrated in elite areas (Webb 1982:67; Jackson 1986:58).

**Relevance to our model**: This pattern is consistent with our model. We do not predict hierarchy. Our model predicts that signaling is a collective activity where groups voluntarily participate because it enhances cooperation and risk-pooling. The even distribution of exotic goods across ridges is consistent with individual-level signaling without elite monopolization.

---

### 2.3 Material Flows Into Poverty Point But Nothing Goes Out

**Source**: Kidder, T.R. and Grooms, S.B. 2025. [IN-REPO]

**Argument**: "While lithic materials measured by the metric ton come into Poverty Point, nothing visible goes out" (Kidder and Grooms 2025:7). This is inconsistent with redistribution/trade-center models.

**Relevance to our model**: One-directional material flow is consistent with our signaling interpretation: materials are "consumed" through display and ritual deposition as part of signaling, not redistributed. The irreversibility of deposition makes the signal honest. However, our model should explicitly account for one-way material flows rather than assuming bidirectional exchange.

---

### 2.4 Exotic Goods Are Not Elite-Restricted

**Source**: Jackson, H.E. 1986. [IN-REPO: jackson_1986_claims.md]; Johnson 1980 (cited in Jackson).

**Argument**: Johnson (1980) demonstrated that in the Yazoo Basin, "access to exotic materials depended on distance from the source, rather than proximity to the proposed regional redistributive center (Jaketown)." Exotic goods are not restricted to larger sites. Jackson concludes "there is little evidence that a chiefdom level of society was attained in the Yazoo Basin."

**Relevance to our model**: Distance-dependent access is consistent with individual acquisition costs in our model. Exotic goods function as signals where cost is proportional to distance from source, not social rank. This actually supports our framework.

## 3. Alternative Frameworks (Better Explanations Exist)

### 3.1 Revitalization Movement Framework

**Source**: Kidder, T.R. and Grooms, S.B. 2025. [IN-REPO: kidder_grooms_2025_claims.md]

**Argument**: Poverty Point was a site of cosmological revitalization (sensu Wallace 1956), where people from across eastern North America aggregated voluntarily during severe environmental disruption to perform rituals of world renewal. Construction and exotic deposition were components of these performances, motivated by moral obligation rather than prestige competition.

**Relevance to our model**: The revitalization framework is not incompatible with costly signaling. In our model, environmental disruption (high sigma) triggers aggregation and cooperative investment. Whether participants understand their motivation as "world renewal" or "cooperation building" does not change the evolutionary dynamics. The revitalization framework provides a proximate mechanism (subjective motivation) for the ultimate mechanism (fitness-enhancing cooperation under uncertainty) our model describes. We should frame these as complementary rather than competing.

**What our model does not address**: We do not model the ideological content of the signaling system. The revitalization framework predicts specific symbolic features (rejection of old order, novel material expressions, prophetic leadership) that our model does not generate.

---

### 3.2 Collective Action Theory

**Source**: Carballo, D.M. (ed.) 2013. *Cooperation and Collective Action: Archaeological Perspectives*. University Press of Colorado. [UNREAD]

**Source**: Blanton, R. and Fargher, L. 2008. *Collective Action in the Formation of Pre-Modern States*. Springer. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: Monuments can be explained through collective action theory (CAT) rather than costly signaling. CAT emphasizes bottom-up group problem-solving and institutional mechanisms (social norms, mutual monitoring, sanctioning) that sustain cooperation without requiring costly waste. Ceremonial architecture fosters social integration through public ritual settings where cooperation is monitored and reinforced through social retribution (scorn, ostracism) and reward (feasts, status recognition).

**Relevance to our model**: Collective action theory and costly signaling are not mutually exclusive. CST provides a mechanism for how cooperation is signaled and established; CAT provides a mechanism for how cooperation is maintained through institutions. Our model implicitly incorporates elements of CAT (cooperation benefits, aggregation norms), but we should make the relationship between CST and CAT explicit.

**What our model addresses**: Our model includes cooperation benefits and aggregation decisions that parallel CAT's institutional mechanisms.

**What our model does not address**: We do not model sanctioning or cheater detection explicitly. Adding these would strengthen the model's engagement with CAT.

---

### 3.3 Stanish's Ritual Cooperation Model

**Source**: Stanish, C. 2017. *The Evolution of Human Co-operation: Ritual and Social Complexity in Stateless Societies*. Cambridge University Press. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: Ritual practices encode rules of behavior that organize cooperation in stateless societies without coercive authority. Stanish argues that "irrational" prosocial behaviors control cheating within small groups and are bound into ritual practice. Monuments reinforce social cohesion by highlighting collaboration benefits and justifying sanctions against cheaters. Stanish explicitly discusses Poverty Point as one of his archaeological examples.

**Relevance to our model**: Stanish's model is compatible with ours at the behavioral level, but differs in emphasis. Stanish focuses on ritual as the mechanism for sustaining cooperation; we focus on signaling as the mechanism for establishing cooperation between unfamiliar groups. Both predict cooperative monument construction without coercive hierarchy. Kidder and Grooms (2025) explicitly reject Stanish's prestige-through-sponsorship mechanism ("individuals acquire prestige, ritual status, and greater access to material goods by sponsoring important events for the community," Stanish 2017:10).

**What our model addresses**: We model the conditions under which cooperative signaling becomes adaptive, providing the "why" that Stanish's ritual framework does not fully explain.

**What our model does not address**: We should engage more explicitly with Stanish's treatment of Poverty Point.

---

### 3.4 Pilgrimage Framework

**Source**: Spivey, S.M., Saunders, J.W., and Allen, T.S. 2015. Pilgrimage to Poverty Point? Cited in Hays 2018. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: Poverty Point was a pilgrimage destination where diverse groups traveled to participate in ceremonies and exchange. The pilgrimage model emphasizes the sacred/cosmological significance of the site rather than its role as a signaling venue.

**Relevance to our model**: Pilgrimage and signaling are not mutually exclusive. A pilgrimage site is precisely the kind of aggregation context where costly signaling would occur, as it brings together dispersed groups who need to establish and maintain cooperative relationships. The pilgrimage model provides the geographic/social mechanism for aggregation; our model provides the evolutionary logic for what happens during aggregation.

---

### 3.5 Structure, Event, Process (Sassaman)

**Source**: Sassaman, K.E. 2005. Poverty Point as Structure, Event, Process. *Journal of Archaeological Method and Theory* 12(4):335-364. [IN-REPO: sassaman_2005_claims.md]

**Argument**: Sassaman applies agency and practice theory to interpret Poverty Point as a multicultural event of ethnogenesis. The architects incorporated both ancient mounds and symbolic representations of far-flung exchange connections into a design that was "both corporate and pluralistic." The nested ridges may encode ranked spatial relationships. Poverty Point was "the ultimate ethnogenetic event of ancient Native America." This framework emphasizes historical contingency and social structure rather than adaptive signaling.

**Relevance to our model**: Sassaman's interpretation is complementary. He describes the social conditions (multicultural aggregation, diverse exchange connections, ethnogenesis) that our model treats as the context for signaling. His emphasis on historical contingency challenges purely adaptationist explanations and reminds us that Poverty Point's specific form is historically determined even if the broad pattern (cooperation under uncertainty) is adaptive.

**What our model does not address**: Historical contingency and the specific cultural content of Poverty Point's architecture.

---

### 3.6 Sensory Exploitation and Monumentality

**Source**: Joye, Y. and Verpooten, J. 2013. An Exploration of the Functions of Religious Monumental Architecture from a Darwinian Perspective. *Review of General Psychology* 17(1):53-68. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: CST cannot fully account for the functions of religious monumental architecture. A complementary mechanism, "sensory exploitation," is needed. Monumental architecture taps into an adaptive "sensitivity for bigness," generating awe that promotes prosocial behavior and openness to supernatural beliefs. This mechanism operates alongside, not instead of, costly signaling.

**Relevance to our model**: This critique does not reject CST but argues it is insufficient alone. Our model focuses on the signaling function of construction labor investment, not the sensory/psychological impact of the finished monument. Incorporating a "bigness" effect could enhance the model's realism, but it is not a refutation of signaling.

---

### 3.7 Hopewell Earthworks as Astronomical Observatories and Village Surrogates

**Source**: Lepper, B.T. et al. Various publications on Newark Earthworks lunar alignments. [UNREAD]

**Source**: Bernardini, W. 2004. Hopewell geometric earthworks: a case study in the referential and experiential meaning of monuments. *Journal of Anthropological Archaeology* 23(3):331-356. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: Hopewell earthworks served as astronomical observatories (oriented to the 18.6-year lunar cycle) and "village surrogates" where dispersed communities gathered to express social ties. A widely dispersed population, not exclusively affiliated with individual monuments, gathered repeatedly to build a related set of ceremonial centers. These explanations emphasize functions other than competitive signaling.

**Relevance to our model**: The "village surrogate" model for Hopewell closely parallels our aggregation model for Poverty Point. Both involve dispersed populations gathering at monumental sites. The astronomical function does not contradict signaling; the precision of alignments could itself serve as a costly signal of communal knowledge and coordination capacity. These alternatives are primarily about additional functions, not replacements for signaling.

---

### 3.8 Gobekli Tepe: Hunter-Gatherer Monumentality Without Clear Signaling

**Source**: Dietrich, O. et al. Various publications on Gobekli Tepe, DAI project. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: Gobekli Tepe (Pre-Pottery Neolithic, ca. 9,600-8,200 BCE) is a monumental megalithic complex built by hunter-gatherers, probably used in connection with funerary rituals and repetitive feasting. Researchers emphasize that feasting "must also have stressed the economic potential of these hunter-gatherers to repeatedly feed such large crowds." The site demonstrates hunter-gatherer monumental capacity without explicit invocation of signaling theory.

**Relevance to our model**: Gobekli Tepe is the most prominent comparative case for hunter-gatherer monumentality. The feasting and communal gathering interpretation parallels Poverty Point. Whether or not signaling language is used, the behavioral pattern (large-scale cooperative investment by non-agricultural populations) is consistent with signaling dynamics. Gobekli Tepe strengthens the case that our model addresses a general phenomenon, not a unique anomaly.

## 4. Methodological Critiques (Models Are Untestable or Circular)

### 4.1 Archaeological CST Models Lack Testable Predictions

**Source**: Codding and Jones 2007 [UNREAD]; Quinn 2019 [UNREAD]; Stibbard-Hawkes 2019 [UNREAD]

**Argument**: CST applications in archaeology have been criticized for producing post hoc narratives rather than testable predictions. Any costly behavior can be labeled a "signal" after the fact, making the theory unfalsifiable in practice. Archaeologists lack the ability to perform cost-benefit analyses comparable to economists' tests of signaling models, and the connection to evolutionary theory remains vague.

**Relevance to our model**: This is perhaps the most important methodological critique. Our ABM directly addresses it by generating quantitative, falsifiable predictions: (1) monument construction should correlate with periods of high environmental uncertainty (sigma); (2) construction should occur during aggregation events, not continuously; (3) exotic goods should be distance-cost-dependent, not rank-dependent; (4) the system should collapse when sigma drops below a threshold. These predictions are testable against archaeological and paleoclimate data.

**What our model addresses**: Formal ABM with parameter sweeps and sensitivity analysis generates testable predictions, directly responding to the unfalsifiability critique.

**What our model does not address**: We should explicitly frame our predictions as falsifiable tests and identify what evidence would refute the model.

---

### 4.2 Signaling Activities Leave Little Archaeological Residue

**Source**: Codding and Jones 2007. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: In individual-level signaling contexts (e.g., hunting), the signaling activities contribute very little to archaeological residues. This makes CST difficult to test archaeologically.

**Relevance to our model**: This critique applies to individual-level hunting signals but not to monumental construction. Monument building is one of the most archaeologically visible activities imaginable: 750,000-1,000,000 cubic meters of moved earth at Poverty Point. Exotic goods (2,790 plummets, 10,000+ projectile points, 1,214 stone beads) are also highly visible. Our model concerns a signaling domain that produces massive archaeological residues, making this critique inapplicable.

---

### 4.3 CST Cannot Explain Collapse Through Positive Feedback

**Source**: Codding and Jones 2007. [UNREAD]

**Argument**: CST explicitly precludes the type of runaway positive feedback loops that some scholars implicate as driving cultural collapse. If signals must be honest, escalation should be self-limiting.

**Relevance to our model**: Our model explains collapse through environmental change (sigma dropping below threshold), not through runaway escalation. This is consistent with CST: when conditions change such that signaling is no longer adaptive, the system declines. The 500-year hiatus after Poverty Point's abandonment (Kidder 2006) and the correlation with climate change support this mechanism.

---

### 4.4 Poverty Point Is a Sample of One

**Source**: Ortmann, A.L. 2007. [IN-REPO: ortmann_2007_claims.md]

**Argument**: "Poverty Point is the only known Late Archaic mound site of its size, requiring all comparisons to be made solely on the basis of our understanding of one site" (Ortmann 2007:313). With n=1, it is difficult to test any general theoretical framework.

**Relevance to our model**: Valid concern. However, our model generates predictions that can be tested across a range of sites (Poverty Point, Claiborne, Jaketown, smaller camps) and against the environmental record. The model also makes cross-cultural predictions applicable to Hopewell, Gobekli Tepe, and other cases of cooperative monumentality. We should explicitly discuss the n=1 limitation and how the model's cross-site and cross-cultural predictions mitigate it.

## 5. Counter-Arguments Already Documented in Repo Sources

### 5.1 Jackson's Rejection of the Chiefdom Model

**Source**: Jackson, H.E. 1986. [IN-REPO: jackson_1986_claims.md]

**Key claims**: (1) Evidence for social ranking relies on only 25 provenienced beads out of 1,000+ (Claim 12). (2) Exotic goods are widely distributed, not elite-restricted (Claim 13). (3) "The allocation of labor in public works projects does not necessarily imply a redistributive function of a ruling elite" (Claim 20). (4) Distance from source, not proximity to redistribution center, determines access to exotics (Claim 21).

**Assessment**: Jackson's critique of the chiefdom model supports our signaling framework by eliminating the hierarchical alternative. His own framework (social network extension for risk-buffering through exchange) is compatible with costly signaling.

---

### 5.2 Sassaman's Agency/Practice Theory Alternative

**Source**: Sassaman, K.E. 2005. [IN-REPO: sassaman_2005_claims.md]

**Key claims**: (1) The concentric ridges are a Poverty Point innovation without Middle Archaic precedent (Claim 7). (2) "The greatest inherent contradiction to Poverty Point is that it is at once a highly localized monumental act, duplicated in form nowhere else, and the locus for delivery of craft items and raw materials from places hundreds of kilometers away" (Claim 9). (3) Claiborne is a one-sixth scalar model of Poverty Point (Claim 12).

**Assessment**: Sassaman's framework emphasizes multicultural aggregation and ethnogenesis, which is the social context our model operates within. The scalar replication at Claiborne is interesting for signaling: the architectural form itself carried symbolic value that was reproduced, potentially as a signal of affiliation.

---

### 5.3 Kidder and Grooms' Explicit Rejection of Prestige Models

**Source**: Kidder, T.R. and Grooms, S.B. 2025. [IN-REPO: kidder_grooms_2025_claims.md]

**Key claims**: (1) Performances at Poverty Point were not symbols of power, displays of magic, or prestige-competition events (Claim 42). (2) "Ritual performance here did not hide an agenda of people aspiring to rule or manage their brethren" (Claim 43). (3) Poverty Point was performed into existence by a cosmopolitan community discharging a moral obligation to mend creation (Claim 44).

**Assessment**: Kidder and Grooms reject prestige-based signaling but not cooperative signaling. Their description of voluntary, non-coercive, environmentally triggered aggregation and collective construction is precisely what our model predicts. The disagreement is about proximate motivation (moral obligation vs. strategic signaling), not about the observable behavioral pattern.
