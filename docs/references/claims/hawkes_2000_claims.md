## Claims

### Claim 1: The hunting hypothesis disputed

- **Statement**: "Hunting has long been highlighted as the keystone innovation. According to the classic argument, males hunt to provide food to share with their mates and offspring." Hawkes contends this is not what the data show: "if hunters don't support their mates and offspring, then hunting does not usher in the nuclear family as a fundamental economic unit. The basis for rejecting that 'hunting hypothesis' has given rise to another. The 'politics' of big-game hunting and meat sharing suggest that when divisible resources are large enough to be of interest to many, their recurrent acquisition favors emotional control and the use of conventions in strategies for conflict that avoid costly fights."
- **Page**: pp. 60, 73
- **Type**: Interpretive (paper's central argument)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Hadza ethnographic data (Hawkes et al. 1991, 1997, 1998); chimpanzee comparative data (Boesch and Boesch 1989; Wrangham and Bergmann-Riss 1990)
- **Depends on**: Hadza dataset; chimpanzee meat-sharing observations
- **Notes**: Central thesis of the chapter. Hawkes argues hunting is not paternal provisioning but male competitive display.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (within the Hadza case)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the key conceptual move from provisioning to signaling. Foundational for costly-signaling applications to hunter-gatherer behavior.

### Claim 2: Hadza big-game success rate

- **Statement**: "Hadza hunters kill or scavenge large animals at an overall mean rate of 4.89 kg (live weight) of prey per hunter-day"; "the large prey taken by hunters gave an average of about 1 kg (live weight) per consumer per day"; this is "more than twice Lee's (1979) estimate for !Kung foragers living in the Dobe area of Botswana in the 1960s."
- **Page**: p. 62
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Hawkes et al. 1991 (data collection in 1985-1986)
- **Depends on**: Hadza field observations
- **Notes**: Field period of about a year (1985-1986).
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (direct measurement)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the high *average* return rate that initially appears to justify a provisioning interpretation, before the variance argument cuts against it.

### Claim 3: Hadza daily success rate is very low

- **Statement**: "The average daily *success* rate, on the other hand, is .034 prey per hunter. An average hunter can expect a full month of failures for every day he scores. The *geometric* mean of arithmetic means of weeklong runs would be the more appropriate evaluation of real nutritional payoffs for consumers depending on this food source. Based on products rather than sums, geometric means fall to zero whenever zeros are encountered. With this measure, the expected provisioning payoff for big-game hunting is nothing at all."
- **Page**: p. 65
- **Type**: Empirical (the rate) + interpretive (the implication)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Hadza field data
- **Notes**: This is the variance/risk argument that destroys the provisioning interpretation. A hunter has roughly 3.4% daily success.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Critical for the costly-signaling argument: hunting is unreliable, so it cannot be a provisioning strategy. The argument applies directly to monumental investment if labor-bonanza events are similarly unpredictable.

### Claim 4: Big-game hunting is inferior to gathering on average rates

- **Statement**: "If large animals are estimated at 1500 Cal/kg live weight (following Lee 1979), a rate of .12 kg/hr (live weight) gives 180 Cal/hr. This rate is lower than all but the very youngest children earn gathering (Blurton Jones et al. 1989; Hawkes, O'Connell, and Blurton Jones 1995). In the long run, big-game hunting is inferior to available alternative strategies for provisioning families."
- **Page**: p. 65
- **Type**: Empirical (calculation from field data)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Lee 1979; Blurton Jones et al. 1989; Hawkes, O'Connell, and Blurton Jones 1995
- **Notes**: Hawkes is making the contrarian case that big-game hunting cannot be defended as a provisioning strategy, since gathering yields more Cal/hr.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (within the Hadza case)
- **Assessment**: A strong empirical argument that hunting must serve some non-provisioning function — supporting the showing-off / costly-signaling reading.

### Claim 5: Hadza hunter household keeps ≤10% of meat from kill

- **Statement**: "These data are the basis for estimating that a Hadza hunter's household generally keeps 10% *or less* of the meat from his kill (Hawkes et al. 1998)." For carcasses >100 kg, "the household of the responsible hunter held a significantly larger share than other households for carcasses that contained more than 100 kg of meat, about 10% for the hunter and 7% to others."
- **Page**: p. 63
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Hawkes et al. 1998
- **Notes**: The hunter's family does not in any meaningful sense "consume the kill" — most of the carcass goes to others.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Foundational quantitative claim for the non-provisioning argument. Hadza men cannot be hunting to provision their families because they keep so little of what they catch.

### Claim 6: Demand sharing pattern at Hadza kills

- **Statement**: "The style of claims conforms to descriptions from many ethnographers of egalitarian societies (for example, see the summary in Erdal and Whiten 1994). Classic characterizations note that claimants do not say please and thank you (Sahlins 1972) and that, in fact, there is sometimes an edge of threat. Peterson (1993) has labeled this pattern 'demand sharing.'"
- **Page**: p. 63
- **Type**: Interpretive (description of social practice)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Erdal and Whiten 1994; Sahlins 1972; Peterson 1993
- **Notes**: Hawkes uses this framing to argue that the hunter is not a "proprietor" who chooses to share; rather, claimants take.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The "demand sharing" framing supports the tolerated-theft model and undercuts reciprocal-altruism interpretations of meat sharing. Relevant to costly-signaling because it positions sharing as not a debt-creating exchange but as a cost the hunter bears.

### Claim 7: Better hunters do NOT spend less time hunting (and wives work more)

- **Statement**: "Better hunters do not get larger shares from the kills of others; and they do not spend less time hunting. They spend *more*."; "The wives of better hunters spend significantly *more* time foraging (Hawkes et al. 1998)."; "The children of better hunters do better nutritionally, not because they are supported by their fathers but because of the foraging efforts of their mothers and grandmothers. Better hunters are married to harder-working wives."
- **Page**: pp. 64, 67
- **Type**: Empirical (correlation analyses)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Hawkes et al. 1997, 1998
- **Notes**: This is the key empirical finding that destroys the provisioning interpretation. Better hunters do not benefit their families through their hunting *directly*; the correlation between fathers' hunting reputation and children's nutrition runs through some other pathway (likely their wives' increased foraging).
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Most surprising finding in the chapter. The marriage-as-economic-partnership model is wrong for the Hadza: wives of better hunters work more, not less.

### Claim 8: Hadza prey size is ~30× chimpanzee prey size

- **Statement**: "While the success rates of Hadza hunters (.034 prey per hunter-day) are almost identical to the success rates of chimpanzee hunters at Gombe (.037 prey/hunter/day; Wrangham and Bergmann-Riss 1990), Gombe chimpanzees eat very little meat while the Hadza consume large amounts. The extreme difference is entirely due to the difference in prey size... For a Hadza sample of 71 carcasses taken by encounter, intercept, and competitive scavenging over 256 days of observation (2,072 hunter-days), the mean carcass weight (adjusted when scavenged carcasses were not intact) was 143 kg (Hawkes et al. 1991, 1998). Because of the difference in prey size, Hadza hunters with success rates essentially identical to those of chimpanzees make about *thirty times* the amount of meat available for consumption that chimpanzees do."
- **Page**: p. 70
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Wrangham and Bergmann-Riss 1990 for Gombe; Hawkes et al. 1991, 1998 for Hadza
- **Notes**: Critical comparative point. Same hunter success rate, but ~30× the meat per hunter due to prey-size scaling.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the costly-signaling framework: a high-variance bonanza-producing activity (big game) attracts many consumers and therefore creates the conditions for sharing-as-display.

### Claim 9: Sharing as inevitable consequence of carcass size and weapons

- **Statement**: "Hadza men and boys always travel armed. More claimants, plus bows, arrows, and knives, inflate the potential cost of emotional display, favoring self-control and increased emphasis on conventional settlements... With larger prey size there is much more meat for all. Meat is a substantial component of the diets of Hadza of both sexes and all ages, so everyone has good reason to be concerned about hunting success. Successful hunters are themselves valuable resources. Hunting prowess may be among a man's most important characteristics."
- **Page**: p. 71
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence cited by author**: Hadza ethnographic observations
- **Notes**: Sets up the egalitarian-consequence argument.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (theoretical synthesis)
- **Assessment**: Connects prey size, contest theory, and egalitarian outcomes. Conventions-over-conflict is the mechanism.

### Claim 10: Successful hunters are valuable resources / partner-choice premium

- **Statement**: "Among Hadza hunters, as in other ethnographic cases, mean success rates differ, showing the marked effects of skill. Among the Hadza, interview data confirm both that hunters' rankings are of interest to others and that they are quite consistent from year to year. Boys are especially good at ranking those in their own and just older cohorts (though — a matter of some initial surprise — this is not something closely monitored by young women; Blurton Jones et al. 1997). Some men are, and are known to be, much better hunters than others. They should be more desirable neighbors as a consequence."
- **Page**: p. 71
- **Type**: Empirical (skill-ranking data) + interpretive (consequence)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Blurton Jones et al. 1997
- **Notes**: Hawkes does *not* quantify the partner-choice premium in this chapter. The claim is qualitative: better hunters should be more desirable neighbors. **Important**: this chapter does NOT state a "10-20% reproductive success advantage" figure, which is sometimes attributed to Hawkes 2000 in the secondary literature. The 10-20% range, if applicable, comes from Hawkes 1991 ("Showing Off...") or related work, not this chapter.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (qualitative argument; quantitative hunter-ranking data is solid)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the qualitative case that better hunters are valued partners but does not provide the 10-20% reproductive-success number cited in some later papers. **See Verification Note below.**

### Claim 11: Big-game hunting undercuts dominance hierarchies

- **Statement**: "Using the Hadza rates as the example, even the best hunter usually fails to score, and the least skilled and experienced sometimes succeeds. The two-sided consequence, that good hunters cannot be relied on to succeed and that even boys can sometimes provide a bonanza for all, regularly undercuts hierarchical rankings. Any hunter's success on one day will always be followed by failures, limiting the extent to which anyone can maintain superiority over others. The absence of flamboyant self-aggrandizement among hunters has been remarked by many ethnographers... Anyone who brags about his superiority will have his claims soon countered by the success of another (Hawkes 1992)."
- **Page**: p. 72
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence cited by author**: Lee 1969; Hawkes 1992
- **Notes**: Mechanism by which hunting unpredictability undermines hierarchy.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Provides the mechanism connecting hunting variance to egalitarian outcomes. Distinct from sharing-as-leveling: it's the *unpredictability* that does the work, not the redistribution per se.

### Claim 12: Marriage as convention for mate-guarding (not provisioning)

- **Statement**: "If hunting is important because everyone has a stake in the success of any hunter, then *households* are not units of production among hunter-gatherers, and marriage is not an economic partnership... If hunters are not provisioning their wives and children, then what explains the pairing of spouses that distinguishes people from all the great apes?"; "even when paternal investment has large effects on the number or survival of the offspring of mates, pure mate guarding spreads against paternal investment in the models. Marriage is, among other things, a public acknowledgment of a husband-wife relationship. In that sense it is a convention for mate guarding, a convention by which one man's claims are recognized by other men."
- **Page**: pp. 72-73
- **Type**: Interpretive (theoretical)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Hawkes, Rogers, and Charnov 1995 (mate-guarding model)
- **Notes**: Hawkes proposes that human marriage is a convention emerging from the mate-guarding solution to dominance-hierarchy collapse. This is related to but distinct from the costly-signaling argument about hunting itself.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (theoretical model with limited direct empirical support)
- **Assessment**: Marginally relevant to the PP project, but interesting for the broader project of explaining hunter-gatherer social organization.

### Claim 13: Big-game hunting may have lasting paleoanthropological consequences

- **Statement**: "If big-game hunting could undercut male dominance hierarchies and favor a general use of conventions to settle contests, which hominids were big-game hunters?... If big-game hunting appears only with behaviorally modern sapiens, the associated emotional control and regular use of conventions could have allowed moderns to spread into environments previously unoccupied by hominids. The colonization of Sahul, for example, the landmass that includes Australia and the continental islands of New Guinea and Tasmania, was only achieved by substantial water crossings through Wallacea. The construction of serviceable watercraft was a prerequisite, but also the patience to sail in small boats for tens of kilometers... Levels of emotional control that permitted modern humans to make that crossing when other hominids did not (O'Connell and Allen 1998) could be the lasting legacy of big-game hunting."
- **Page**: p. 75
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence cited by author**: O'Connell and Allen 1998; Klein 1989; Stiner 1993
- **Notes**: Speculative paleoanthropological extension; not directly relevant to PP project but indicates the broader scope of the argument.
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (speculative)
- **Assessment**: Tangential to PP project but useful as an illustration of how the chapter generalizes from Hadza ethnography to large-scale human behavioral patterns.

### Claim 14: Australian hunter-gatherer "gerontocracies" as exception

- **Statement**: "Aboriginal Australia has always presented difficulties for ethnological generalizations about egalitarian societies... ethnographers working in Australia described 'gerontocracies' in which old men, no richer than hunters elsewhere, wielded power over young; and polygyny was common (Strehlow 1970)... when people first arrived in Australia, social arrangements might have been much like those seen ethnographically among hunters elsewhere. But in Australia, unlike all other continents occupied by humans, the terrestrial vertebrate populations persisting into the Holocene included no very large-bodied species... With the disappearance of almost all big game, the opportunity for young men to undercut the superiority of their elders by procuring large meat bonanzas would have disappeared as well. Male competition could then take forms that allow steeper, more persistent hierarchies, even in the absence of wealth differences."
- **Page**: pp. 75-76
- **Type**: Interpretive (case-comparison)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Strehlow 1970; Allen and O'Connell 1995; Gould 1980
- **Notes**: Important counterexample handled by the framework: if hierarchies depend on the *absence* of unpredictable big-game bonanzas, then Australian gerontocracies fit the model.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (consistent with the framework but case-specific)
- **Assessment**: Useful for the project's broader claim that environmental conditions (here, prey availability) shape the form of social organization. Parallels the LMV framework's environmental-uncertainty argument.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited; claim is stated as fact without support

## Verification Notes

**Discrepancy with Paper 1 attribution.** Paper 1 (`docs/paper1_theory/Paper1_Theory_Model.md` line 143) cites this chapter as: "Hawkes (2000) summarizes Hadza data showing that successful big-game hunters secure a reproductive success advantage of about 10-20% relative to less successful hunters, mediated by preferential access to mating opportunities and partner choice."

The 10-20% reproductive-success-advantage figure does **not** appear in this chapter. Hawkes establishes that better hunters are valuable resources (Claim 10) and that the partner-choice mechanism is the operative one, but does not provide a quantitative reproductive-success premium in this paper. The numerical 10-20% range is likely from Hawkes 1991 ("Showing Off: Tests of an Hypothesis About Men's Foraging Goals," *Ethology and Sociobiology*) or a related Hawkes paper, not this 2000 chapter. The Paper 1 attribution should either:
(a) cite Hawkes 1991 instead of (or alongside) Hawkes 2000 if the 10-20% figure is the operative claim, or
(b) replace the quantitative claim with a qualitative attribution (e.g., "Hawkes 2000 argues that better Hadza hunters are valuable partners and that hunting prowess is among a man's most important characteristics") that this chapter does support.

**Recommendation**: flag this as a citation-attribution issue in the project's crosscheck log and verify the 10-20% number against Hawkes 1991 before submission.

## Summary Assessment

Hawkes (2000) makes the contrarian case that Hadza big-game hunting is not paternal provisioning but male competitive display, supported by quantitative data showing (i) hunters retain only ~10% of their kills, (ii) better hunters spend more (not less) time hunting, (iii) wives of better hunters work more (not less), and (iv) the geometric mean of hunting return rates is essentially zero. The chapter's broader argument is that big-game unpredictability undercuts dominance hierarchies and favors conventions for settling contests, with implications for the evolution of egalitarian social organization, marriage as a mate-guarding convention, and modern-human cognitive distinctiveness. The quantitative figure of "10-20% reproductive-success advantage" sometimes attributed to this chapter is **not** present in it; the chapter is qualitative on the partner-choice premium. Useful for the PP project as foundational support for the costly-signaling reading of male hunting effort, with the caveat that Paper 1's specific 10-20% citation should be verified against Hawkes 1991 instead.
