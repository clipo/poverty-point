## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "Groundstone plummets of magnetite or hematite are commonly found artifacts of the Late Archaic period in Louisiana. While often assumed to have functioned as weights for fishnets or as thrown objects used to catch waterfowl, relatively little empirical evidence has been generated to explain their form and features relative to hypothesized performance in prehistoric behavior."
- **Page**: p. 84 (abstract)
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the core problem: the functional interpretation of plummets has rested on assumption rather than empirical evaluation. Directly relevant to the project because if plummets are not fishing weights but loom weights, their role in the costly signaling framework changes. They would be components of textile production technology rather than subsistence tools.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "Based on our analyses, we find that plummet form is well explained as a component of weighted looms, supporting the early use weaving technology in the Late Archaic of Eastern North America."
- **Page**: p. 84 (abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The central conclusion of the paper. If plummets are loom weights, their function is technological (textile production) rather than subsistence-related. This reinterpretation has implications for how exotic materials at Poverty Point are understood: the dense minerals (hematite, magnetite) were selected for their physical properties (density, consistent shape) rather than purely for display value.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "In North America, groundstone plummets are often treated as diagnostic artifacts of the Late Archaic period (4000-2500 BP). These objects have a teardrop profile, are circular in cross section and vary from ca. 5-10 cm in length."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Basic morphological description and chronological placement of plummets. The date range of 4000-2500 BP (approximately 2050-550 BCE) encompasses the Poverty Point period (ca. 1700-1100 BCE). Relevant for establishing the temporal context of these artifacts.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "Their shape suggests that plummets acted as weights that were suspended on a line that was attached to the narrow end so that the heavy end pointed down or distally. The proximal ends of plummets bear features for hafting that consist of either sets of fine grooves around the circumference of the proximal end or small, drilled holes."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the two hafting types (grooved and perforated) and the inference that they hung vertically from their proximal end. The distinction between hafting types becomes important in the morphometric analysis, where both types show the same shape constraints.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "In addition to having a consistent shape, plummets are most often fashioned from dense materials such as hematite or magnetite."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The consistent selection of dense materials is a key observation. Hematite (specific gravity 5.3) and magnetite (specific gravity 5.15) are both dense minerals that would need to be acquired through exchange at Poverty Point. The material selection is relevant to the costly signaling framework: these are exotic materials requiring long-distance procurement, but the paper argues they were selected for functional (density) rather than display properties.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "While the English term 'plummet' suggests that there is a recognized function for these objects, there is no consensus as to its environment of use by prehistoric populations. For the most part, speculation has driven discussion for well over 100 years."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Highlights the lack of empirical grounding for functional interpretations of plummets. This methodological criticism motivates the morphometric approach taken in the paper.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "In the Lower Mississippi River valley, plummets are some of the most common artifact classes found in Late Archaic assemblages, second only to Poverty Point Objects and chronologically diagnostic bifaces (Webb, 1968:305)."
- **Page**: p. 85
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes plummets as the third most common artifact class in Late Archaic Lower Mississippi Valley assemblages. Their abundance is relevant to the costly signaling framework: if these are exotic material objects, their sheer quantity implies a large-scale exchange system.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: "At Poverty Point, a large Late Archaic mound center in northeastern Louisiana, for example, Webb (1968:312) reports that over 2300 plummets have been found through excavations and from the efforts of private collectors."
- **Page**: p. 85
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantifies the abundance of plummets at the type site. Over 2,300 plummets represents a substantial investment in acquiring and shaping exotic stone. This quantity is directly relevant to arguments about the scale of exotic material procurement and the economic significance of these objects.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "Roughly eighty percent of these plummets are made from hematite. The remaining twenty percent are made from magnetite with a few examples of limonite, sandstone, galena, clay, steatite, and quartzite (Webb, 1968:312)."
- **Page**: p. 85
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides the material composition breakdown for the Poverty Point plummet assemblage, citing Webb 1968. The overwhelming dominance of hematite (80%) and magnetite (20%) indicates strong material selection for dense minerals, supporting the functional argument that density was the relevant property. All of these materials are non-local to Poverty Point and required exchange to obtain.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "The majority are perforated, some are grooved, and some un-altered. Shapes of the plummets are generally teardrop in form with small numbers of spheres, cubes, rectangles and cones. One hundred and thirty-four of the plummets are decorated, some with multiple grooves, others with an expanded collar below the perforation, others with incisions or gouges, drilled pits, or designs executed by intaglio, engraved, or relief techniques (Webb, 1968:313)."
- **Page**: p. 85
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents morphological and decorative variability. The presence of 134 decorated plummets (out of 2,300+) is noteworthy. Decoration could indicate a signaling or display function for at least a subset of plummets, though this is not the interpretation the authors pursue. The variety of decoration techniques (intaglio, engraving, relief) suggests investment beyond what functional use as loom weights would require.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "We assume the so-called 'plummet shape' is a functional element (sensu Dunnell, 1978), i.e., a portion of the artifact that interacts with the environment, as suggested by both construction and breakage."
- **Page**: p. 85
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Establishes the theoretical framework for the analysis: plummet shape is treated as a functional trait subject to natural selection, following Dunnell's (1978) distinction between function and style. This selectionist framework is the basis for the argument that shape constraints reflect performance requirements.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "Our study included shape characterization, surface roughness measures, three-dimensional laser scans to capture volume, and X-ray fluorescence to measure potential source variability in known raw materials."
- **Page**: p. 86
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the range of analytical techniques applied. XRF analysis for sourcing is relevant to the exchange network dimension of the project, though the paper focuses on shape results rather than XRF findings.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "The results reported here focus on plummet shape and are based on measurements made from 78 complete plummets found in the Poverty Point State Historic Site's museum exhibit and from a number of collections conducted over the years and stored in site's archaeological curation facility."
- **Page**: p. 86
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Sample size of 78 complete plummets, drawn from the site museum and curated collections. A bootstrap analysis later confirms this sample is representative (p. 87).

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "Like the material composition reported by Webb (1968), our samples consisted of plummets composed of 85% hematite, 11% magnetite, and 4% other kinds of stone."
- **Page**: p. 87
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms Webb's material proportions in an independently drawn sample. The consistency between the two samples (decades apart) reinforces the conclusion that material selection was non-random and strongly favored hematite.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "This procedure led us to generalize just 78 plummets (21 grooved, 56 perforated and 1 knobbed) using our shape characterization protocol. At that point, additional plummets provided no new information to the measurement distribution at a 0.99% confidence level."
- **Page**: p. 87
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Bootstrap analysis demonstrates that 78 plummets are sufficient to characterize the full range of shape variability in the Poverty Point collection at 99% confidence. This validates the sample as representative.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "Our study of the shape relative to the haft centroid provides an excellent measure of the shape variability relative to the proximal end of the Poverty Point plummets... The distance between the lines demonstrates that the width of plummets at the haft and height above haft are more tightly constrained than overall length."
- **Page**: p. 87
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Key morphometric finding: width is more constrained than length. This asymmetric constraint is the foundation for the functional argument. If plummets were simple weights (for fishing or bola use), there would be no reason for width to be more constrained than length.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: "Variability in the width of plummets is far more constrained than variability in length."
- **Page**: p. 87
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Restates and reinforces the key finding. Width constraint with length freedom is consistent with objects that must fit together laterally (as in a row of loom weights) while allowing variation in the hanging dimension.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: "The similarities between the results for grooved and perforated plummets suggest that these patterns are consistent across both hafting types."
- **Page**: p. 87
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The same shape constraints apply regardless of hafting type. This suggests a shared functional context rather than different uses for grooved versus perforated plummets.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "It is this property that makes plummets systematically bottom heavy and gives them their 'teardrop' shape."
- **Page**: p. 87
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Explains the teardrop form as a consequence of the widest point being below the length midpoint. The teardrop shape is an outcome of the constraint that width must be consistent while length can vary.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "Our shape studies showed that width is strongly constrained in plummets while length is relatively free to vary. This pattern points to the idea that when hung from their haft, their horizontal orientation in space (potentially in relation to other plummets or additional objects similarly constrained) plays a significant role in their functional context."
- **Page**: p. 88
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The core inference from the morphometric data. The constraint on width but not length implies a functional context where horizontal spacing matters, which is consistent with warp-weighted loom weights that must hang side by side.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "Assuming that the hematite and magnetite source materials are not found in constrained deposits (i.e., found in width-sorted deposits), we can only explain this pattern as caused by the functional environment in which plummets interact."
- **Page**: p. 88
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Rules out raw material constraints as the source of width standardization, attributing it instead to functional selection. This is a reasonable inference but could be strengthened by geological data on source deposits.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: "Shape, then, does not depend on how the plummet is hafted or from what it is made, whether hematite, magnetite or some other hard, dense material."
- **Page**: p. 88
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Shape constraints are independent of both hafting type and raw material. This supports a single functional context for all plummets regardless of material source, which has implications for the exchange network argument: different source materials served the same function.

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: "We noted that hafts for plummets are relatively marginal (i.e., non-robust attachments) whether or not the haft is a perforation or a groove. When perforated, only the smallest hole was required. When perforations were created from two sides, for example, only a minimum opening was created, usually ca. 2.0 mm."
- **Page**: p. 88
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The minimal nature of hafting features is significant. A perforation of only 2.0 mm would accommodate only fine string, ruling out high-stress uses such as bola stones or heavy-duty net weights. This is consistent with loom weights, where the haft need only support the weight's own mass hanging vertically.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: "The incised lines on plummets with grooved hafts are quite shallow and narrow: <1 mm in depth. Given the contracting proximal end, hafting the plummet by wrapping would not be particularly substantial even with the contribution grooves to providing friction."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Further evidence that hafting was minimal. Grooves less than 1 mm deep would not secure a plummet for throwing or any high-energy use. This observation is inconsistent with bola, fishing weight, or hunting interpretations.

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: "This aspect of plummets rules out their use in environments requiring them to thrown via attached lines."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Explicit rejection of the bola hypothesis based on the physical inadequacy of hafting features for high-energy use.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: "Finally, we expect substantial wear/battering on the extreme external surface to have resulted from use. While it is possible that the objects were covered in leather to protect them from battering, catastrophic breakage would appear as shattered plummets broken in the middle. No such pattern of breakage has been observed."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The absence of use-wear or impact damage patterns expected from fishing, bola, or thrown-object use. Negative evidence is inherently weaker than positive evidence, but the complete absence of expected damage across a large assemblage is noteworthy.

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: "Given the lack of robust hafting attachments, lack of wear and distribution in habitation deposits rather than hunting environments, none of the expectations of bola use are met."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Summary rejection of the bola hypothesis on multiple independent lines of evidence: hafting inadequacy, absence of wear, and depositional context. The convergence of multiple negative findings strengthens the rejection.

### Claim 28

- **Statement**: "Expectations for fishing contexts as line or net weights are not consistent with our observations. With no particular constraints placed on the shape of a fishing weight other than having a robust haft, the pattern of shape found among the plummets is not consistent with this functional class."
- **Page**: p. 88
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Rejects the fishing weight hypothesis. Fishing weights do not require standardized width, so the strong width constraint observed in plummets is unexplained under this model.

### Claim 29

- **Statement**: "With depositional distributions of plummet objects in the Late Archaic (as in the case of Poverty Point, in specific) generally restricted to domestic contexts, their use as fishing weights is unlikely. Objects lost in the context of use, such as pointed projectile bifaces, are readily recognizable constituents the archaeological record (e.g., 'isolates')."
- **Page**: p. 88
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Plummets are found in domestic/habitation contexts, not in fishing or hunting contexts. If they were fishing weights, some should be lost in aquatic or riverine settings, analogous to how projectile points are found as isolates in hunting areas.

### Claim 30

- **Statement**: "Our own detailed surveys of large plowed fields in southeastern Missouri conducted over the last 30 years (e.g., Dunnell, 1984), for example, have consistently found plummets to be associated with habitation-related debris rather than isolated locations that one might expect if the plummet was lost in the context of fishing or hunting."
- **Page**: p. 88
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Independent survey data from southeastern Missouri over 30 years corroborating the domestic-context distribution pattern. While this is from a different region, it strengthens the pattern observed at Poverty Point.

### Claim 31

- **Statement**: "As fishing weights are used by populations throughout prehistory in Eastern North America, plummet abundance in the Late Archaic cannot be easily explained with this functional model. Fishing technology does not seem to change much over time once fishing becomes fixed within food procurement technology."
- **Page**: p. 88
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Points out the temporal incongruity: if plummets were fishing weights, their restriction to the Late Archaic is unexplained because fishing continued after plummets disappeared. This is a strong logical argument against the fishing weight hypothesis.

### Claim 32

- **Statement**: "The fallback 'charm stone' functional class, i.e., no interaction with the environment other than hanging on string, has expectations more similar to what is observed, yet again this context lacks a mechanism that would constrain the width of the plummets relative to length."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Rejects the "charm stone" or amulet interpretation because it fails to explain the width constraint. This is relevant to the signaling hypothesis: even if plummets served as personal ornaments or status markers, the morphometric data suggest they were not simply decorative objects but had a functional role requiring standardized width.

### Claim 33

- **Statement**: "Overall, our evaluation leaves us rejecting the traditional functional accounts for plummets. We must look elsewhere, then, to find a context that would permit a marginal haft, provide constraints for variable length but relatively constrained widths, require relatively constrained weights, and which favors a dense material (perhaps to standardize size while maximizing object weight)."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Summarizes the functional requirements that must be met by any viable hypothesis: marginal haft, width constraint with length freedom, weight constraint, and dense material preference. These requirements collectively point to loom weights.

### Claim 34

- **Statement**: "Such a context exists and is one that was suggested by Peabody in 1901, but has never been examined in detail: weights for warp-weighted looms for textiles and/or netting."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Credits Peabody (1901) with the original suggestion of loom weights but notes it was never empirically evaluated. The paper presents the first systematic morphometric test of this hypothesis.

### Claim 35

- **Statement**: "The density of material enables minimal width for a given mass without requiring great increases in length. The similarity in density between hematite (specific gravity 5.3) and magnetite (specific gravity 5.15) supports that plummets are being made from material specifically selected for its density and subsequent ability to create consistent shapes."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides a functional explanation for material selection. Hematite and magnetite have nearly identical densities, which means plummets of either material with the same dimensions would weigh approximately the same. This is critical for loom function, where consistent tension on warp threads requires consistent weight. This reframes the exotic material acquisition: materials were selected for physical properties (density), not for visual display or rarity value.

### Claim 36

- **Statement**: "In general, our observations for plummets meet all of the expectations for a weaving or loom context, specifically for warp-weighted looms. Spatially, we expect to find loom weights in domestic contexts and restricted in time if such looms were later replaced with fixed looms."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Claims that the loom weight hypothesis accounts for all observed properties: domestic deposition, temporal restriction to the Late Archaic, constrained width, marginal hafting, and dense material selection.

### Claim 37

- **Statement**: "The fact that they are frequently found as sets in caches is also consistent with our weaving model."
- **Page**: p. 89
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Cache deposition is consistent with stored loom equipment. Ford and Webb (1956) documented plummets in caches. Sets of weights would be expected if they were components of a loom that was stored or abandoned as a unit.

### Claim 38

- **Statement**: "The earliest evidence of weaving comes from the Arnold Research Cave in Missouri (Kuttruff et al., 1998). Dated to 3025-2440 B.C., fragments of fibrous slip-on shoes exhibit interlacing between fibers as well as weft-twining and plying."
- **Page**: p. 90
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes that weaving technology existed in the region during the relevant time period. The Arnold Research Cave dates (3025-2440 BCE) predate or overlap with early Poverty Point (ca. 1700 BCE), indicating that textile technology was available when plummets were being manufactured.

### Claim 39

- **Statement**: "In Louisiana, evidence of Archaic-age textiles are known from sites on Avery Island. These textiles date to 2900-2460 B.C. (Strickland-Olsen et al., 1999:103). The Avery Island textile fragments consist of interlaced yarns constructed of S-spun and Z-spun yarn in interlaced patterns."
- **Page**: p. 90
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents direct evidence of textile production in Louisiana during the Archaic period, predating Poverty Point. The Avery Island textiles demonstrate that weaving was practiced in the immediate region, making the loom weight hypothesis geographically plausible.

### Claim 40

- **Statement**: "There are certainly implications for other common Poverty Point artifact classes that may be related to textile industries including the so-called Jaketown perforators. These small stone tools may in fact be functionally related to the processing of fibers."
- **Page**: p. 90
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Extends the textile technology argument to other artifact classes. If Jaketown perforators were fiber-processing tools, this would expand the evidence for textile production at Poverty Point. However, this is acknowledged as speculative.

### Claim 41

- **Statement**: "A textile-related industry may help explain the occurrence of Poverty Point populations in this location, representing seasonally intensive weaving activity at a centralized place."
- **Page**: p. 90
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Suggests that textile production could explain seasonal aggregation at Poverty Point. This is highly relevant to the costly signaling project: if aggregation was partly organized around textile production, this adds a cooperative production benefit to the aggregation model alongside fish weirs, information exchange, and mating networks.

### Claim 42

- **Statement**: "The addition of a textile-production dimension in our understanding of Poverty Point might well explain what was being exchanged in the famous long-distance trade networks that brought exotic raw materials to northeastern Louisiana from hundreds to over a thousand miles away (Byrd, 1991; Gibson, 1994; Jackson, 1991; Webb, 1968, 1977)."
- **Page**: p. 90
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Proposes that textiles may have been the goods exchanged through long-distance networks. This would fundamentally reframe the exchange network: rather than exotic raw materials being imported for display, they were imported as functional components of textile production, and the finished textiles may have been the valued exchange goods. This is speculative but would have major implications for the costly signaling framework.

### Claim 43

- **Statement**: "The timing of the occurrence of plummets in the archaeological record of eastern North America may be telling us about the evolution of technology related to clothing and other textile items."
- **Page**: p. 90
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Frames the Late Archaic abundance and subsequent disappearance of plummets as reflecting the adoption and replacement of warp-weighted loom technology.

### Claim 44

- **Statement**: "The late Archaic is known for an increase of site 'permanence' associated with the beginnings of central places that are fixed parts of well-defined seasonal rounds in an increasingly populated environment (Caldwell, 1958). The appearance of plummets in this context may signal the emergence of textiles as an important part of human technology."
- **Page**: p. 90
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Connects the emergence of plummets with increasing sedentism and population in the Late Archaic. The argument is that as populations grew and seasonal rounds became more defined, the demand for textiles (clothing, baskets, fabric) increased. This is relevant to the aggregation dynamics in the project model.

### Claim 45

- **Statement**: "Consider the effect of increased reliance on fish, small game, and plants for subsistence on resources available for late Archaic populations to make clothing and shelters. As there is a relative reduction of the prominence of larger game for subsistence, alternative sources of fabric and material would have been required."
- **Page**: p. 90
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Proposes that the shift away from large-game hunting reduced the availability of hides for clothing, creating demand for woven textiles. This subsistence-technology linkage is relevant to modeling resource uncertainty and technological adaptation at Poverty Point.

### Claim 46

- **Statement**: "The substantial decrease in the prevalence of plummets after the late Archaic may correspond to innovations in frame or fixed loom technology that led to swift elimination of the warp-weighted loom."
- **Page**: p. 90
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Offers a technological replacement explanation for the disappearance of plummets. If correct, the end of the plummet tradition would not mark the end of weaving but rather a technological transition. This is relevant to understanding the "collapse" of Poverty Point: the disappearance of plummets may not indicate the failure of the exchange system but rather technological change.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited; claim is stated as fact without support

## Summary Assessment

Lipo et al. 2012 applies geometric morphometric analysis to 78 groundstone plummets from Poverty Point, finding that plummet width is strongly constrained while length varies freely, a pattern inconsistent with fishing weights, bola stones, or charm stones but consistent with warp-weighted loom weights. The paper systematically rejects traditional functional interpretations (fishing weights, bola, charm stones) based on multiple lines of evidence: inadequate hafting for high-energy use (perforations only ca. 2.0 mm, grooves <1 mm deep), absence of use-wear or impact damage, domestic depositional context, and the temporal restriction of plummets to the Late Archaic. The reinterpretation of plummets as loom weights has significant implications for the costly signaling project: it suggests that the exotic materials (hematite, magnetite) were selected for their physical density properties rather than for visual display, and that textile production may have been an important cooperative activity during seasonal aggregation at Poverty Point. The speculative suggestion that finished textiles may have been the goods exchanged through long-distance networks would, if supported, fundamentally alter the framing of the exchange system. The paper's morphometric evidence is strong, but the loom weight interpretation remains partially circumstantial because no loom remains have been found at Poverty Point.
