# Claims Extraction: Dunnell and Greenlee 1999

**Source**: Dunnell, R.C. and Greenlee, D.M. 1999. Late Woodland period "waste" reduction in the Ohio River Valley. *Journal of Anthropological Archaeology* 18:376-395.
**PDF**: Dunnell and Greenlee_1999.pdf
**Processed**: 2026-03-21
**Pipeline status**: claims

## Claims

### Claim 1
- **Statement**: The "waste" hypothesis "designates the counterintuitive notion that under some specific selective conditions (e.g., unpredictably variable or 'marginal' environments) the diversion of energy into nonreproductive activity (e.g., cultural elaborations termed 'waste'), necessarily at the expense of reproduction, might become fixed in some populations at relatively high levels."
- **Page**: p. 381
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: This is a foundational theoretical claim directly relevant to the Poverty Point costly signaling framework. The "waste" concept (Dunnell 1989) provides the evolutionary logic for why costly, nonreproductive behaviors like monument construction and exotic goods acquisition can be adaptive under conditions of environmental uncertainty. The parallel to costly signaling theory is clear: costly behaviors persist because they enhance fitness under unpredictable conditions.

### Claim 2
- **Statement**: "Under some specific selective conditions... populations at whatever scale waste has become fixed are more likely to survive unpredictable resource crises." A corollary is that "increases in the environmental carrying capacity (or in its predictability) will decrease the fitness of those cultural elaborations."
- **Page**: p. 381
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Central to the Poverty Point collapse question: if environmental carrying capacity increased or became more predictable around 1100 BCE, the signaling system would lose its adaptive advantage, leading to abandonment of monumental construction and aggregation. This provides a testable mechanism for the end of the Poverty Point phenomenon.

### Claim 3
- **Statement**: Middle Woodland Hopewell is "known for its large-scale geometric earthworks, hilltop enclosures, and complex burial mounds with elaborate tombs. Materials interred in the mounds are remarkable for their sheer quantity and their 'aesthetic' qualities (i.e., amount of labor invested)." Exotic materials included "marine Busycon and Cassis shells from the Gulf and southeast Atlantic coasts, mica and quartz crystals from the southern Appalachians, grizzly bear teeth and obsidian from the Rocky Mountains, pottery from the Southeast, galena from northern Illinois, copper and silver from the Upper Great Lakes area, and chalcedony from North Dakota."
- **Page**: p. 377
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the Hopewell exotic exchange network, which provides a direct parallel to Poverty Point's exchange system. Both cases involve long-distance acquisition of exotic materials (copper, galena, mica, etc.) from overlapping source areas. The Hopewell case demonstrates that such exchange networks can develop, persist, and collapse in the Eastern Woodlands, providing comparative context for the Poverty Point system.

### Claim 4
- **Statement**: The Hopewell "collapse" "is not entirely a unilinear path. Different forms of waste persisted differentially. Of course, waste does not disappear completely just as it was not completely absent before the Woodland."
- **Page**: p. 388
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Relevant to understanding the end of Poverty Point. The loss of cultural elaboration was not abrupt or uniform at Hopewell; different elements declined at different rates in different areas. A similar pattern may characterize the decline of Poverty Point, where monument construction may have ceased before exotic exchange networks fully dissolved.

### Claim 5
- **Statement**: "An increase in carrying capacity would select against high levels of waste. Increases in net productivity might arise in one or both of two broadly distinct ways: climatic change toward warmer and/or wetter conditions or a change in subsistence productivity."
- **Page**: p. 381
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides the mechanism for waste/signal reduction. Applied to Poverty Point: if climate change around 1100 BCE improved resource predictability in the lower Mississippi Valley, the adaptive advantage of costly signaling through monument construction would diminish, and the system would be selected against.

### Claim 6
- **Statement**: Evidence from Great Lakes lake-level records and bald cypress tree rings from North Carolina indicates that "the direction of climatic change appears to be toward warmer and wetter conditions (and therefore increased net productivity and carrying capacity) at the end of Hopewell."
- **Page**: p. 386
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Demonstrates that the Hopewell "collapse" correlated with climatic amelioration, not deterioration. This supports the waste hypothesis: improved conditions reduce the adaptive value of costly elaboration. A parallel pattern should be sought for the end of the Poverty Point phenomenon.

### Claim 7
- **Statement**: "We anticipate that population size should increase as waste is reduced" because "when energy is no longer channeled away from reproduction, fertility rates will increase."
- **Page**: p. 381
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: A testable prediction of the waste hypothesis: population should increase after the cessation of "wasteful" activities. Applied to Poverty Point, this predicts that regional population in the lower Mississippi Valley may have increased after the abandonment of monumental construction, even as the aggregation site itself was abandoned.

### Claim 8
- **Statement**: "The particular form that waste takes within a population, in this case earthworks, mounds, mortuary features, grave goods, and so on, is a matter of history and transmission (i.e., homology)."
- **Page**: p. 381
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Important theoretical point: the specific form of costly signaling (moai at Rapa Nui, great houses at Chaco, earthworks at Poverty Point) is historically contingent, but the underlying mechanism (costly investment under uncertainty) is the same. This supports the comparative framework of the project.

### Claim 9
- **Statement**: "Several of the social and/or political hypotheses can be eliminated from consideration as potential explanations of the Hopewell 'collapse' because their empirical implications were not met. There did not appear to be an increase in indicators of warfare toward the end of the Middle Woodland period."
- **Page**: p. 388
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Social/political collapse explanations for the end of elaboration traditions fail empirical testing. This strengthens the case for ecological/evolutionary explanations (waste hypothesis, costly signaling under environmental change) as alternatives to social breakdown narratives.

### Claim 10
- **Statement**: "The 'good and gray' cultures of the Late Woodland period in the Eastern Woodlands are so named because they lack the elaboration of the preceding Middle Woodland. Traditional attempts to explain this loss of elaboration as a consequence of social breakdown or climatic change fail empirically or are untestable."
- **Page**: p. 376
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes that traditional explanations for the end of elaboration traditions are inadequate. The waste hypothesis provides a testable alternative grounded in evolutionary theory. The same critique applies to common explanations for the end of Poverty Point (social collapse, climate deterioration), which typically lack mechanistic specificity.

### Claim 11
- **Statement**: Stable carbon isotope data from the Ohio Valley indicate that "significant maize consumption does not appear until well after the Hopewell 'collapse'" and individual-level data show that "some individuals appear to have been significantly committed to a maize-based diet, while others apparently ate little or no maize."
- **Page**: p. 384
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Demonstrates that subsistence change was not uniform across populations and that maize did not drive the Hopewell transition. Relevant to Poverty Point as a reminder that subsistence explanations must account for individual-level variation and that aggregation/dispersal decisions may have varied among bands.

## Summary Assessment

Dunnell and Greenlee 1999 is directly relevant to the Poverty Point costly signaling project through its development of the "waste" hypothesis. The paper's theoretical framework, where costly nonreproductive activities (earthwork construction, exotic goods acquisition) are adaptive under environmental uncertainty and selected against when conditions improve, closely parallels the costly signaling argument. Key contributions include: (1) a formal evolutionary mechanism for why costly elaboration is adaptive (buffering against environmental unpredictability) and why it ends (increased carrying capacity/predictability); (2) empirical evidence from the Hopewell case that the end of elaboration correlated with climatic amelioration, not deterioration; (3) the principle that the specific form of "waste" is historically contingent while the mechanism is general; and (4) testable predictions (population increase after waste reduction, variable trajectories of different waste forms). Though focused on Hopewell rather than Poverty Point, the theoretical apparatus maps directly onto the Poverty Point case, particularly regarding why monumental construction and exotic exchange would emerge under environmental uncertainty and cease under improved conditions.
