## Claims

### Claim 1
- **Statement**: The stalagmite (PP_nda) was retrieved from War Eagle (WE) cave in Jackson County, Alabama (34 degrees 31'N, 86 degrees 11'W). Mean annual precipitation at the cave locality is 1,446 mm and mean annual temperature is 15 degrees C (1981-2010). The cave is hosted within the Bangor Limestone.
- **Page**: pp. 2-4
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the proxy location at 34.52 degrees N, approximately 2 degrees north of Poverty Point (32.63 degrees N). The close latitude and shared SE US climate regime (coherent in-phase precipitation variability across the SE US; Figure 1) support using this record to infer conditions at Poverty Point, though it is not at the exact site.

### Claim 2
- **Statement**: The PP_nda stalagmite time scale was determined with 22 U/Th dates. Age uncertainties ranged from +/-15 to +/-110 yr with a mean of +/-40 yr. Only one date has an uncertainty of +/-110 yr and the remaining 16 dates have uncertainties lower than +/-70 yr across the 12 ka record.
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The chronological precision is sufficient for correlating with the Poverty Point interval (ca. 3700-3100 BP). Mean age uncertainty of +/-40 years is adequate for assessing centennial-scale climate variability during the occupation.

### Claim 3
- **Statement**: The PP_nda stalagmite grew over three different time intervals separated by two hiatuses. The stalagmite began to grow 12.2 ka BP and stopped at 10.8 ka BP. After an interruption of over 1 ka, the stalagmite resumed growth 9.4 ka BP and stopped growing 4.3 ka BP. After a ~2 ka growth interruption, the stalagmite resumed growth 2.6 ka BP and stopped growing 310 yr BP.
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Critical limitation for the Poverty Point project: the stalagmite has a growth hiatus from 4.3 to 2.6 ka BP. This means there is NO direct record during the core Poverty Point interval (ca. 3700-3100 BP). The 4.2 ka event is captured at the very end of the second growth phase, but the record resumes only around 2600 BP, after Poverty Point has already ended. The record brackets, but does not cover, the Poverty Point occupation.

### Claim 4
- **Statement**: The time resolution of the isotopic records ranges from 7 to 44 yr, decreasing as time progresses from the Early to the Late Holocene. The Early Holocene section has 7-year resolution, the Early to Mid-Holocene section has 12-year resolution, and the Late Holocene section has 44-year resolution.
- **Page**: pp. 5-6 (Figure 2 caption)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The 12-year resolution of the Early to Mid-Holocene section (9.4-4.3 ka BP) would have been excellent for the Poverty Point interval, but the growth hiatus at 4.3 ka BP means this resolution is available only for the period preceding the occupation, not during it. The Late Holocene section (2.6 ka BP to 310 yr BP, 44-year resolution) begins too late to capture Poverty Point.

### Claim 5
- **Statement**: The PP_nda stalagmite long-term average d18O composition is -3.3 per mil, with a range from -4.3 to -2.1 per mil. The most negative isotopic values occur over the intervals 11.8-11, 9.4-8, and 0.5-1 ka BP and the most positive over the intervals 6.5-5.5 and 10.8-11 ka BP.
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The total d18O range of 2.2 per mil provides a measure of total hydroclimate variability magnitude across the Holocene. More negative values correspond to wetter conditions (higher "summer" precipitation). The interval immediately before the hiatus (~5.5-4.3 ka BP) shows a drying trend.

### Claim 6
- **Statement**: A local relationship between annual rainfall amount and its amount-weighed d18O composition exists on interannual timescales, driven mostly by an amount effect during summer and spring seasons. The slope is dP/delta-P = -0.0013 per mil per mm; r-squared = 0.39.
- **Page**: pp. 1, 6
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on instrumental data from 2005-2013)
- **Assessment**: This amount effect calibration is the basis for interpreting d18O variability as precipitation change. The negative relationship means more negative d18O values indicate greater spring-summer precipitation. The r-squared of 0.39 indicates that 39% of interannual d18O variance is explained by precipitation amount, which is a moderate but significant relationship.

### Claim 7
- **Statement**: Stalagmite d18O values become progressively more negative during the evolution of the YD and 8.2 ka cooling events by about ~-1.2 per mil. The stalagmite isotopic shift of ~-1.2 per mil can be explained by an average of 90% increase of "summer" precipitation relative to today's conditions (range 75%-130% based on amount effect uncertainty).
- **Page**: p. 8
- **Type**: Empirical/Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate-Strong (isotopic shift is empirical; precipitation quantification depends on the amount effect model)
- **Assessment**: Provides a quantitative estimate for the magnitude of precipitation change during known abrupt cooling events: approximately 90% increase in spring-summer precipitation. This demonstrates that the SE US experienced dramatically wetter conditions during North Atlantic cooling events, not drought.

### Claim 8
- **Statement**: Precipitation amount in the SE US increases during an interval of 500 yr coeval with the Younger Dryas and during the 8.2 ka and Little Ice Age abrupt cooling events. High precipitation during these events reflects enhancement of spring and summer precipitation by 90% combined, relative to today's conditions, while the contribution of fall and winter rainfall remained unchanged or decreased slightly.
- **Page**: p. 1 (Abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate-Strong
- **Assessment**: The key finding: abrupt North Atlantic cooling events produced wetter, not drier, conditions in the SE US, and the mechanism is specifically through enhanced spring-summer precipitation. If this pattern holds for the climate event at 3.0-2.6 ka BP (which is not directly recorded due to the hiatus), it would imply wetter conditions during the terminal Poverty Point and post-Poverty Point interval, consistent with the flooding mechanism proposed by Kidder (2006).

### Claim 9
- **Statement**: The stalagmite hiatuses at 10.8, 4.3, and 0.3 ka BP occurred when the stalagmite d18O composition had positive trends, suggesting that these hiatuses were associated with drops of precipitation and rainwater infiltration.
- **Page**: p. 10
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The hiatus at 4.3 ka BP is interpreted as reflecting drying conditions. This coincides broadly with the globally recognized 4.2 ka event (a major drought/aridification event). The fact that the stalagmite stopped growing at 4.3 ka BP due to drying suggests that the period immediately preceding Poverty Point (ca. 4300-3700 BP) may have been characterized by reduced precipitation, at least at the Alabama cave latitude. The drying trend leading to the 4.3 ka hiatus is visible in the data.

### Claim 10
- **Statement**: The PP_nda stalagmite suggests a long-term +1 per mil isotopic shift occurring from ~7.5 to ~5.5 ka (Figure 6). Inferred climate evolution from the Early Holocene (9-7 ka BP) to the mid-Holocene (6-5 ka BP) in the SE US coincides with boreal summer insolation reduction, North Atlantic cooling, decrease rainfall in the Caribbean, and weakening of Northern Hemisphere monsoon intensity.
- **Page**: pp. 12-13
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the long-term drying trend through the mid-Holocene that forms the background context for the Poverty Point interval. Annual precipitation decreased from the Early to mid-Holocene as the SE US transitioned from wetter to drier conditions. The +1 per mil shift corresponds to a substantial decline in precipitation over ~2000 years.

### Claim 11
- **Statement**: Spring-summer precipitation in the SE US increases during events of high latitude cooling associated with Atlantic Meridional Circulation slowdown/shutdown, such as during the Younger Dryas, 8.2 ka, and LIA cooling events. The SE US was wet when the North Atlantic was cold and the tropics experienced negative precipitation anomalies.
- **Page**: pp. 11, 15
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate-Strong (consistent pattern across three independent events, supported by model simulations)
- **Assessment**: This is the central conclusion with implications for Poverty Point. The antiphase relationship between SE US and tropical precipitation during AMOC slowdown events establishes a robust mechanism: when North Atlantic deep water formation weakens, the SE US gets wetter (more spring-summer rain) while the tropics get drier. If the climate event at ca. 3.0-2.6 ka BP involved AMOC-related cooling (as suggested by Kidder 2006), this mechanism predicts enhanced precipitation in the SE US during the terminal/post-Poverty Point period.

### Claim 12
- **Statement**: Spatial correlation analyses of the instrumental record of monthly precipitation (from 1901 to 2013) across the SE US suggest coherent in-phase variability within much of the SE US, and a weak anticorrelation with precipitation variability in the broader Caribbean region.
- **Page**: p. 4 (Figure 1)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes that the Alabama cave location is representative of broader SE US precipitation patterns, supporting extrapolation to the Poverty Point latitude (~2 degrees further south). The spatial correlation map (Figure 1) shows strong positive correlations (r > 0.6) extending from Alabama across much of the interior SE US, including the lower Mississippi Valley.

### Claim 13
- **Statement**: The drying trend suggested by the PP_nda stalagmite is interrupted by two prominent negative isotopic excursions between 6 and 4.5 ka BP, indicated by prominent negative isotopic excursions. These inferred climate excursions are also represented, although more subtly, by the stalagmite d18O record from DeSoto Caverns, Alabama.
- **Page**: p. 13
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate-Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents centennial-scale wetting events superimposed on the long-term mid-Holocene drying trend. These excursions between 6 and 4.5 ka BP indicate that even during the overall drying phase, abrupt multi-decadal to centennial wet episodes occurred. This is relevant to understanding the variability regime preceding Poverty Point.

### Claim 14
- **Statement**: The PP_nda stalagmite d18O record during the LIA shows a negative isotopic shift from 1.2 to 0.4 ka BP of ~-0.8 per mil. This stalagmite negative isotopic excursion coincides with the LIA interval, the Holocene's coldest period in the North Atlantic, and the driest in the Caribbean.
- **Page**: p. 13
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides an additional calibration point: during the LIA (another AMOC-related cooling event), the SE US experienced enhanced precipitation (~0.8 per mil shift, somewhat smaller than the 1.2 per mil shift during the YD and 8.2 ka events). This confirms the pattern is reproducible across multiple events of varying magnitude.

### Claim 15
- **Statement**: Results from this study support model simulation results that suggest increased precipitation in the SE US during Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) slowdown/shutdown. AMOC slowdown decreases winter surface temperatures while summer temperatures remain unchanged or increase in the SE US.
- **Page**: pp. 1, 11
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate-Strong (supported by climate model hosing experiments)
- **Assessment**: Connects the empirical SE US precipitation record to the AMOC mechanism. The suggestion that AMOC slowdown produces enhanced summer precipitation in the SE US while not reducing summer temperatures is relevant for understanding resource conditions during climate perturbations at Poverty Point's latitude: wetter summers could enhance both flooding risk (consistent with Kidder 2006) and resource productivity.

## Summary Assessment

Medina-Elizalde et al. (2022) provides high-resolution stalagmite d18O and d13C records from War Eagle Cave, Alabama (34.5 degrees N), spanning the last 12.2 ka. The study establishes that during Holocene abrupt cooling events (YD, 8.2 ka, LIA), spring-summer precipitation in the SE US increased by approximately 90% relative to modern conditions, driven by AMOC slowdown and associated shifts in moisture transport. The critical limitation for the Poverty Point project is the growth hiatus from 4.3 to 2.6 ka BP, which means the stalagmite does not directly record conditions during the Poverty Point occupation (ca. 3700-3100 BP). However, the record provides essential context: (1) the mid-Holocene drying trend leading up to 4.3 ka BP (background conditions preceding Poverty Point), (2) the 4.3 ka hiatus itself, interpreted as drought-related (potentially coinciding with the 4.2 ka event), (3) the consistent pattern across three independent events that AMOC-related cooling produces SE US wetting, which can be applied by analogy to the ca. 3.0-2.6 ka BP climate event documented by Kidder (2006), and (4) spatial correlation analysis showing that Alabama precipitation covaries coherently with the broader SE US including the lower Mississippi Valley. The cave location (~2 degrees north of Poverty Point) and the demonstrated spatial coherence of SE US precipitation make this a relevant, though not directly overlapping, proxy record.
