# Claims Extraction: Sanger 2024

**Source**: Sanger, M.C. 2024. The Archaeology of Awe: Monumental Architecture, Communal Ritual, and Community Formation at Poverty Point, USA. *Journal of Archaeological Method and Theory* 31:1462-1484. DOI: 10.1007/s10816-024-09645-w
**PDF**: s10816-024-09645-w.pdf
**Processed**: 2026-03-28
**Pipeline status**: claims

## Claims

### Claim 1
- **Statement**: Awe is defined as "the feelings that emerge when an individual encounters a phenomenon beyond their ability to easily understand or grasp." Research shows that awe "can promote opportunities to (re)define and strengthen social bonds as it diminishes a person's feeling of self-importance, increases their proclivity to accept others, and reduces their attachment to personal needs."
- **Page**: p. 1463
- **Type**: Theoretical (citing Dai 2023; Ejova et al. 2021; Piff et al. 2015; Schaffer et al. 2023)
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on multiple psychological studies)
- **Assessment**: Central theoretical claim of the paper. Establishes awe as a prosocial emotion with measurable cooperative effects. Directly relevant to costly signaling framework: awe provides a psychological mechanism through which monumental architecture could facilitate cooperation, complementing the economic logic of signaling theory.

### Claim 2
- **Statement**: Awe has two defining components: (1) "vastness," meaning an encounter with something that "surpasses their typical scale of perception," and (2) a "need to accommodate," meaning "a need to intellectually, spiritually, emotionally, or otherwise accommodate for what has been encountered." Vastness "need not pertain only to physical size" but "can refer to any characteristic that challenges an individual's frame of reference" including "complexity, quantity, temporality, ability, emotionality."
- **Page**: pp. 1467-1468 (citing Keltner & Haidt 2003; Shiota et al. 2007)
- **Type**: Theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (established psychological framework)
- **Assessment**: Provides operational criteria for identifying awe-inducing conditions in the archaeological record. Poverty Point's monumental scale, geometric complexity, and the sheer quantity of labor and materials satisfy both the vastness and need-for-accommodation criteria.

### Claim 3
- **Statement**: "The feeling of self-diminishment often results in increasing willingness and ability to cooperate with others." "Cooperation is fostered when feelings of awe create not only a diminishment in self, but also a reduced attachment to personal desires, goals, and even well-being." "This self-transcendence promotes feelings of loyalty and self-sacrifice oriented toward the group the individual felt most connected with during and after the awe-inducing event."
- **Page**: p. 1469 (citing Bai et al. 2017; Stellar et al. 2017; Zining & Mahmud 2023; Jiang et al. 2018; Koh et al. 2019; Naclerio & Van Cappellen 2022; Piff et al. 2015)
- **Type**: Theoretical/Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (multiple independent studies)
- **Assessment**: Key mechanism linking awe to cooperation. For the signaling model, this suggests that awe functions as a proximate psychological mechanism that lowers the cost of cooperation: individuals experiencing awe are more willing to contribute labor and resources to group projects, reducing the free-rider problem that signaling theory addresses through cost imposition.

### Claim 4
- **Statement**: Awe is a "widespread emotion" that "varies across cultural and social groups in terms of magnitude, content, and elicitor." It "elicits a unique universal physiological response, chills and goosebumps, along with vocal and facial expressions that are cross-culturally recognizable." Some primates, including chimpanzees, "exhibit awe-like responses to thunder, waterfalls, and other impressive conditions."
- **Page**: p. 1468 (citing Cordaro et al. 2016; Ejova et al. 2021; Goodall 2005)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (cross-cultural evidence strong; primate evidence contested, citing McPhetres & Shtulman 2021)
- **Assessment**: The cross-cultural and possibly cross-species nature of awe supports its application to past populations. However, Sanger appropriately cautions that modern studies "are based on rather mundane awe-inspiring events and likely grossly underestimate the magnitude of impact created by more monumentous encounters" (p. 1471).

### Claim 5
- **Statement**: "Experiencing awe also makes people more likely to question their preconceived norms and beliefs and promotes the adoption of novel understandings of the world." Awe is described as "an 'epistemic emotion' in which individuals acquire knowledge and increase their understanding of the world." Experiencing awe "decreases an individual's tolerance for uncertainty about why events occurred" and makes individuals "more willing to attribute causality to previously unappreciated agents that were subsequently viewed as intentional and purpose-driven."
- **Page**: pp. 1463, 1470 (citing Dai 2023; Keltner & Haidt 2003; Preston & Shin 2017; Stancato & Keltner 2021; Schaffer et al. 2023; Valdesolo & Graham 2014; Valdesolo et al. 2017)
- **Type**: Theoretical/Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (multiple psychological studies)
- **Assessment**: Relevant to understanding how the Poverty Point aggregation system could generate and maintain shared cosmological beliefs. Awe-induced openness to new worldviews could facilitate the ethnogenesis process that multiple scholars have attributed to Poverty Point.

### Claim 6
- **Statement**: "Virtually every aspect of Poverty Point is awe inspiring." The site "is one of the largest monumental constructions in the Americas as it includes five mounds and six concentric ridges that encircle an expansive plaza." "Mound A, the largest mound at Poverty Point, is one of the most massive pre-Contact constructions in the United States." It measures "more than 20 m tall and 200 m wide."
- **Page**: p. 1472 (citing Ford & Webb 1956; Gibson 2000; Kidder 2002, 2011)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the empirical basis for awe at Poverty Point. The physical scale of the constructions satisfies the "vastness" criterion for awe elicitation. Note: Sanger uses "five mounds" here (p. 1472), which is higher than some counts but consistent with recent work identifying Mounds A through F.

### Claim 7
- **Statement**: "Estimates vary, but the overall creation of Poverty Point may have required the movement of as much as 1,000,000 m^3 of fill." "It also appears that Poverty Point residents may have reworked and redeposited at least some of the earthen ridges at the site, which indicates even more labor investment."
- **Page**: p. 1472 (citing Gibson 2000:109; Kidder 2002, 2011:97; Greenlee & Dalan 2022; Hargrave et al. 2021)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Uses the higher-end estimate of 1,000,000 m^3, consistent with Kidder and Grooms (2025). The reworking/redeposition point strengthens the case that total labor investment exceeded even the volumetric estimates.

### Claim 8
- **Statement**: "Residents of Poverty Point created at least 36 circles of standing poles within the central plaza." "Poles were placed in pits arranged in circles, some measuring more than 60 m across, and many of which were refashioned numerous times." "Most are viewed as open air constructions, perhaps related to calendrics, geometric alignments, sightings, or other unknown uses."
- **Page**: p. 1472 (citing Hargrave et al. 2021; Clay 2023)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The timber circles represent an additional category of monumental construction beyond mounds and ridges. Their recursive construction, renewal, and decommissioning (Clay 2023) indicates sustained ritual investment over time. The 60 m diameter circles would have been visually impressive features contributing to the awe-inducing landscape.

### Claim 9
- **Statement**: "Studies largely suggest that residents created much of the site over relatively small amounts of time." "This includes the creation of Mound A, which looks to be built in less than a year, perhaps over only a few months, by a work crew that numbered in the thousands." "Most of the constructions date to a 100-300 year stretch beginning after ca. 3300 cal B.P., near the end of the site's occupation."
- **Page**: p. 1473 (citing Gibson 2000; Kidder et al. 2021; Kidder 2011; Ortmann & Kidder 2013)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on geoarchaeological analysis)
- **Assessment**: The rapid construction timeline is central to the signaling argument. Construction during aggregation events, rather than gradual accumulation, is consistent with costly signaling during periodic gatherings. The concentration of building activity in the final 100-300 years raises questions about what triggered this intensification.

### Claim 10
- **Statement**: Poverty Point "drew together large numbers of people from across the landscape to attend events that held considerable cosmological, ritual, economic, political, and social value." Some describe these gatherings "as trade fairs, pilgrimages, or large-scale feasting events." "What is clear is that the site was visited by massive numbers of people and that at least some of these visits included group labor projects that resulted in fashioning the mounds."
- **Page**: pp. 1473-1474 (citing Kidder 2010, 2011; Kidder & Sherwood 2017; Kidder et al. 2021; Ortmann & Kidder 2013; Spivey et al. 2015; Jackson 1991; Hays 2019)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (multiple lines of evidence for large gatherings)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the aggregation context central to both the awe and signaling frameworks. The characterization of gatherings as "trade fairs, pilgrimages, or large-scale feasting events" maps onto different functional interpretations that the signaling model can help adjudicate.

### Claim 11
- **Statement**: Poverty Point became a "black hole" that "centralized prior networks with it as the preeminent nexus of social interactions." "Social relations prior to Poverty Point included a wide range of exchange networks, some of which worked in tandem while others crosscut one another, so that there was a relatively high level of redundancy and dispersal."
- **Page**: p. 1474 (citing Sassaman 2010; Spivey et al. 2015:158)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (network reconstruction from material distribution data)
- **Assessment**: The "black hole" metaphor describes how Poverty Point restructured regional social networks by centralizing previously dispersed exchange relationships. This centralization is consistent with the site functioning as a signaling hub where reputation and cooperative commitments could be broadcast to a wide audience.

### Claim 12
- **Statement**: "Archaeologists have proposed various theories regarding the mechanisms through which social bonds were established at Poverty Point with many viewing the labor involved in mound creation as pivotal. They propose that a shared cosmological worldview was embodied in the construction of mounds, serving as a tangible expression of shared beliefs, and this convergence of beliefs and their physical manifestation played a crucial role in forging connections between different communities."
- **Page**: p. 1475 (citing Gibson 2000, 2004; Kidder 2011; Kidder & Sassaman 2009; Spivey et al. 2015)
- **Type**: Interpretive (review of existing interpretations)
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Summarizes the dominant "shared cosmology" interpretation of Poverty Point construction. Sanger positions his awe framework as complementary to but distinct from this view: rather than construction expressing pre-existing shared beliefs, he argues that the act of construction and the resulting monumental landscape generated the emotional states (awe) that then facilitated community formation.

### Claim 13
- **Statement**: "Poverty Point was unlike anywhere else on the planet not only because of its physical structure, but also in terms of its emotional valence and intensity: it was a place built to awe." Approaching the site "would have been an unparalleled experience for visitors, unlike any place they had encountered before."
- **Page**: p. 1476
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (inference from physical evidence to emotional experience)
- **Assessment**: Central interpretive claim. Sanger argues awe was not merely a byproduct of monumental construction but a deliberate objective. This is a strong claim that requires supporting evidence beyond the physical scale of the site. The argument would be strengthened by identifying specific design features that seem intended to maximize perceptual impact rather than serving purely functional purposes.

### Claim 14
- **Statement**: "These group endeavors would have also been awe-inducing events. The scale of work parties may have varied widely, but even a moderately sized crew of 20-40 individuals collaborating would have constituted a remarkable event for someone not accustomed to engaging with such a large number of people, not to mention the truly massive work parties that may have numbered in the thousands."
- **Page**: p. 1477
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Argues that awe was generated not only by the finished product (the monumental landscape) but also by the construction process itself. This is relevant to the signaling model: if construction labor is both a signal and an awe-inducing experience, it creates a positive feedback loop where signaling behavior generates the emotional conditions that facilitate further cooperation.

### Claim 15
- **Statement**: "The material evidence at Poverty Point, including its physical constructions and signs of large gatherings, strongly indicates that inspiring and experiencing awe was a deliberate objective for those who built and attended events. Consequently, I propose that the individuals who gathered at Poverty Point constituted an emotional community, bound together by their shared emotional experiences and the ways in which they defined, displayed, and valued emotional expressions, especially awe."
- **Page**: pp. 1477-1478
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (inference from material evidence to intentionality)
- **Assessment**: Sanger proposes "emotional community" as a framework for understanding Poverty Point social organization. This is distinct from but potentially complementary to costly signaling: signaling theory explains why individuals invest in cooperative behavior, while the awe/emotional community framework describes the proximate psychological mechanism that sustains cooperation.

### Claim 16
- **Statement**: "To the extent that visitors to Poverty Point felt a diminishment of self or self-transcendence will never be known, but the diverse peoples who worked together in unison certainly appeared highly capable of cooperation and toleration of others, including those that may have been giving them orders. This shift in labor allotment and social interactions is precisely the outcomes predicted by modern studies of awe."
- **Page**: p. 1478
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (archaeological evidence consistent with predicted outcomes of awe, but alternative explanations exist)
- **Assessment**: Sanger acknowledges the epistemological limit: we cannot directly observe past emotional states. He argues instead that the behavioral outcomes visible in the archaeological record (large-scale cooperation among diverse groups, acceptance of directed labor) are consistent with the effects of awe documented in psychological research. This is a valid form of inference but not uniquely predicted by the awe framework; costly signaling, reciprocity, and coercion could also produce these outcomes.

### Claim 17
- **Statement**: Keltner (2023) identifies eight categories of awe elicitors: "(1) moral beauty, (2) collective effervescence, (3) natural beauty, (4) music, (5) visual design, (6) spiritual experiences, (7) mortality, and (8) epiphanies." Sanger argues that several of these would have been present at Poverty Point gatherings.
- **Page**: p. 1469 (citing Keltner 2023)
- **Type**: Theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (framework from modern psychology applied to past context)
- **Assessment**: Provides a checklist of awe elicitors against which the Poverty Point evidence can be evaluated. Collective effervescence (large-scale group labor), visual design (geometric earthworks), and potentially music and spiritual experiences are archaeologically plausible at Poverty Point. However, applying modern psychological categories to past populations requires caution that Sanger acknowledges.

### Claim 18
- **Statement**: "People also brought a wide range of materials to Poverty Point, many of which originated from considerable distances including copper, galena, soapstone, pottery, and lithic materials." The distribution of Poverty Point Objects (PPOs) across "broadly contemporaneous sites across much of the region" indicates a shared material culture network.
- **Page**: p. 1474 (citing Gibson 2000; Hill et al. 2016; Hays et al. 2016; Mitch 2023; Walthall et al. 1982; Webb 1982)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the exotic goods exchange network that is central to the signaling model. Sanger does not develop this evidence in relation to signaling theory but it is directly relevant: exotic materials brought from hundreds to over a thousand kilometers would themselves have been awe-inducing objects, and their display at gatherings would have compounded the awe generated by the monumental landscape.

### Claim 19
- **Statement**: There are "notably few archaeological studies of awe." This is "confounding for three key reasons. First, psychologists describe awe as a particularly communal emotion that it is often felt during large gatherings. Second, awe is often inspired by highly visible and long-lasting aspects of the natural and human-made world. Third, awe has been shown to have notable impacts on the formation, maintenance, and strengthening of social bonds."
- **Page**: p. 1472 (citing Keltner & Haidt 2003; Keltner 2023; Guan et al. 2019)
- **Type**: Theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (identifies gap in literature)
- **Assessment**: Sanger's argument that awe is understudied in archaeology despite being well-suited to archaeological investigation is well-supported. The three criteria he identifies (communal, materially visible, socially impactful) describe exactly the conditions present at Poverty Point and at other monumental sites that have been studied through costly signaling frameworks.

### Claim 20
- **Statement**: The Mound C location "was also recursively used as it includes at least sixteen instances in which 'surfaces were cleaned, renewed with selected materials, intermittently used in ritual performance, and then terminated when the final mound cap covered them.'" Other parts of the site "appear to be built rapidly, but with intermittent pauses during which they were used extensively and then capped by further, sequential building episodes."
- **Page**: p. 1476 (citing Clay 2023:196; Kidder et al. 2021)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The recursive construction-use-capping sequence is important for both the awe and signaling frameworks. Each construction episode represented a fresh opportunity for awe-inducing communal labor and for signaling group commitment. The intermittent pauses with ritual use suggest the constructions served ongoing ceremonial functions between building episodes.

## Summary Assessment

Sanger (2024) proposes that awe, understood as a prosocial emotion triggered by encounters with vastness, served as a mechanism for community formation at Poverty Point. Drawing on psychological research demonstrating that awe diminishes self-importance, increases willingness to cooperate, and promotes openness to new worldviews, Sanger argues that Poverty Point's monumental architecture, large-scale communal construction events, and periodic mass gatherings created the conditions for awe, which in turn facilitated cooperation among diverse groups. The paper does not engage directly with costly signaling theory, but the frameworks are complementary: signaling theory explains why individuals invest in costly cooperative behavior (to credibly advertise quality), while the awe framework identifies a proximate psychological mechanism (self-diminishment and increased prosociality) through which participation in monumental construction could lower the barriers to cooperation. The primary limitation is the inferential gap between modern psychological findings about awe and the emotional experiences of people 3,000 years ago, a limitation Sanger acknowledges. The paper provides no new empirical data but offers a novel interpretive framework that could be integrated into the signaling model as a mechanism through which monumental construction generates cooperation benefits beyond the signal itself.
