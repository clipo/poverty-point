## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "The anomaly has primarily been described as abrupt drying in the Northern Hemisphere at ca. 4 ka (ka, thousands of years before present), but evidence of the hydroclimate change is inconsistent among sites both globally and within North America."
- **Page**: p. 1109 (abstract)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the central problem: the 4.2 ka event is widely reported but its expression is spatially inconsistent. For the Poverty Point model, this inconsistency is significant because it indicates that populations in the lower Mississippi Valley may or may not have experienced the drought reported elsewhere.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "Dune-field chronologies and a pollen record in southeastern Wyoming reveal several centuries of low moisture at around 4.2 ka, and prominent low stands in lakes in Colorado suggest the drought was unique amid Holocene variability, but detailed carbonate oxygen isotope (d18Ocarb) records from Colorado do not record drought at the same time."
- **Page**: p. 1109 (abstract)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Demonstrates that even within a single region (the southern Rocky Mountains), different proxy types yield contradictory evidence about the 4.2 ka event. Dune fields and pollen show drought; carbonate isotope records from Colorado do not. This proxy-dependent disagreement complicates attempts to reconstruct regional climate during the period immediately preceding Poverty Point.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "We find new evidence from d18Ocarb in a small mountain lake in southeastern Wyoming of an abrupt reduction in effective moisture or snowpack from approximately 4.2-4 ka, which coincides in time with the other evidence of regional drying from the southern Rocky Mountains and the western Great Plains."
- **Page**: p. 1109 (abstract)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The Highway 130 Lake record provides new isotopic evidence for a ~200-year abrupt drying event centered on 4.2-4.0 ka. This timing (ca. 2250-2050 BCE) places the event approximately 300-500 years before the onset of Poverty Point occupation, making it part of the immediate antecedent climatic context.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "The ostensibly global event has primarily been described as a dry episode at low and midlatitudes (Booth et al., 2005; Nakamura et al., 2016; Di Rita and Magri, 2019; Scuderi et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2018). However, some regions show increased precipitation (Huang et al., 2011; Railsback et al., 2018) or no change (Roland et al., 2014), as is consistent with spatial variation expected from climate variability that shifts atmospheric waves and dynamics."
- **Page**: p. 1109
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The 4.2 ka event was not a uniform global drought. Some regions experienced increased precipitation or no change at all. The spatial heterogeneity is explained by shifts in atmospheric circulation patterns. This is directly relevant to interpreting conditions in the lower Mississippi Valley: the event's expression at Poverty Point's latitude (~32.6 degrees N) cannot be assumed from records in the Rocky Mountains or elsewhere.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "The 4.2 ka event has been used as the benchmark for the stratigraphic division between the middle and late Holocene (Walker et al., 2019)."
- **Page**: p. 1109
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: N/A (definitional)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the formal stratigraphic significance of the 4.2 ka event as a Holocene subdivision boundary. The Poverty Point period (ca. 3700-3100 BP) thus falls entirely within the early late Holocene as defined by this boundary.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "Carter et al. (2013, 2017, 2018) used fossil pollen from Long Lake in the Medicine Bow Mountains, south of the Wyoming dune fields (Fig. 1, Table 1), to identify a 150-year interval of increased temperature and decreased precipitation centered at 4.2 ka."
- **Page**: p. 1110
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides a precise duration estimate for the 4.2 ka event in southeastern Wyoming: approximately 150 years of warming and drying centered on 4.2 ka. The brevity of the event (relative to multi-millennial Holocene trends) indicates it was a distinct climatic episode, not a gradual transition.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "The inferred precipitation reductions were largest in springtime (Carter et al., 2018), when snowfall in the southern Rocky Mountains is highest today (Mock, 1996)."
- **Page**: p. 1110
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The 4.2 ka drought was seasonally concentrated in spring, the primary snowfall season. Spring precipitation reductions would have cascading effects on snowmelt-fed river systems downstream, potentially affecting hydrology in the Mississippi drainage and by extension the lower Mississippi Valley, though the magnitude of downstream effects at Poverty Point's distance is uncertain.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: "The drying event stands out as one of the only multi-centennial features in a summary of low lakes in the Rocky Mountains during the late Quaternary (Shuman and Serravezza, 2017)."
- **Page**: p. 1110
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The 4.2 ka drought was unique in its prominence among late Quaternary lake-level records in the Rocky Mountains. This suggests it was not simply one of many recurring droughts but rather an unusually severe or abrupt event in the context of Holocene climate variability.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "By contrast, the 4.2 ka event does not appear in stable oxygen isotope records from lakes in the same region, such as detailed carbonate d18O (d18Ocarb) records from Bison and Yellow lakes, Colorado (Fig. 1, Table 1; Anderson, 2011, 2012)."
- **Page**: p. 1110
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The absence of the 4.2 ka signal in d18Ocarb records from nearby Colorado lakes highlights the proxy-dependent nature of the evidence. This discrepancy is a central motivation for the paper and demonstrates that the event's detection depends heavily on the type of climate archive and the specific hydrologic setting of each lake.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "Peak d18Ocarb in HL indicates an abrupt decline in effective moisture or at least a decline in the ratio of snowfall to rain in the Medicine Bow Mountains from approximately 4.2-4 ka (Fig. 5) when evidence from additional climate records shows that aridity affected the southern Rocky Mountains and portions of the Great Plains (Carter et al., 2013; Halfen and Johnson, 2013; Stokes and Gaylord, 1993)."
- **Page**: p. 1113
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The Highway 130 Lake d18Ocarb record shows peak values at 4.2-4.0 ka, interpreted as either reduced effective moisture or a shift in the snow-to-rain ratio. The timing aligns with multiple independent lines of evidence for drought across the southern Rocky Mountains and Great Plains. This multi-proxy convergence strengthens the case that the 4.2 ka drought was a regionally significant event.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "d18Ocarb peaks (2.9 standard deviations above the mean) from approximately 4.2-4 ka, for which four calibrated radiocarbon ages constrain the timing and indicate a fast sedimentation rate (Fig. 5)."
- **Page**: p. 1113
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The d18Ocarb peak at 4.2-4.0 ka represents a 2.9 standard deviation excursion above the mean, the largest isotopic anomaly in the Highway 130 Lake record. Four radiocarbon ages constrain the timing, providing good chronological control. The magnitude (2.9 SD) places the event well outside the range of normal Holocene variability at this site.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "The highest d18Ocarb values at HL coincide with the pollen-inferred precipitation and temperature changes at 4.2 ka at Long Lake, which records 2 centuries of severe drought (Long Lake, Fig. 1; Carter et al., 2013)."
- **Page**: p. 1115
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms two centuries of severe drought at the Long Lake pollen site at 4.2 ka, consistent with the isotopic evidence from Highway 130 Lake. The agreement between independent proxies (pollen and carbonate isotopes) at nearby sites strengthens the identification of the drought.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "The excursion also aligns with the long-standing evidence of drought in the Great Plains and southern Rocky Mountains (dune fields, Fig. 1), where a rapid loss of grain-trapping vegetation likely triggered several centuries of increased aeolian transport documented across multiple dune fields (Booth et al., 2005; Forman et al., 2001; Halfen et al., 2010; Stokes and Gaylord, 1993)."
- **Page**: p. 1115
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Dune reactivation across multiple dune fields in the Great Plains and southern Rocky Mountains provides independent geomorphic evidence for the drought. The loss of stabilizing vegetation severe enough to trigger aeolian activity indicates ecologically significant aridity. The "several centuries" of increased dune activity suggests the drought's ecological impacts were prolonged.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: Table 1 summarizes changes at ca. 4.2 ka inferred from North American climate records near Highway 130 Lake. Dune fields in southeastern Wyoming (Casper, Ferris/Seminoe) show drying. Long Lake fossil pollen shows warming/drying. Lake-level records from Colorado (Upper Big Creek, Little Molas, Emerald) show drying. But Bison Lake and Yellow Lake d18Ocarb records show "No prominent change."
- **Page**: p. 1114 (Table 1)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: This table provides a systematic comparison of proxy evidence for the 4.2 ka event across the southern Rocky Mountains. Five of seven records show drying; two carbonate isotope records from Colorado lakes show no prominent change. The pattern demonstrates both the regional scope of the drought and the inconsistency among proxy types.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "Sedimentological changes in Little Molas, Emerald, and Upper Big Creek lakes, all high-elevation lakes in Colorado (Fig. 1, Table 1), show substantial hydrological transformation at around 4 ka matching the timing and scale of drought inferred from HL's record (Fig. 7). The sediment stratigraphies in these three lakes record low water levels that shifted shoreline sands to the locations of cores collected in 1-5 m water depth today and thus indicate reduced effective moisture at ca. 4.2-4 ka."
- **Page**: p. 1116
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Lake-level evidence from three high-elevation Colorado lakes independently confirms drought at 4.2-4.0 ka. Shoreline sands found in sediment cores from locations now under 1-5 m of water indicate substantial lake-level declines. This is strong physical evidence that the drought was severe enough to dramatically lower lake levels in the southern Rocky Mountains.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "The rapid transition from deep-water muds to shallow-water sands as water levels dropped in the Colorado lakes at around 4.2 ka corresponds to changes in pollen assemblages in central Colorado (Jimenez-Moreno et al., 2019) and southeastern Wyoming (Carter et al., 2013), as well as other evidence for drought in North America (Fig. 1, Table 1; Booth et al., 2004). Similar sedimentological features found in lakes along the Atlantic margin from Maine to Pennsylvania date to around 4.2 ka, for example, where the drought appears as one of multiple events linked to circulation changes over the North Atlantic (Li et al., 2007; Marsicek et al., 2013; Newby et al., 2014; Nolan, 2020; Shuman et al., 2019; Shuman and Burrell, 2017)."
- **Page**: p. 1116
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The 4.2 ka drought extended from the Rocky Mountains to the Atlantic margin, with lake-level evidence from Maine to Pennsylvania. Along the Atlantic margin, the event appears as "one of multiple events linked to circulation changes over the North Atlantic." This broad geographic extent is significant for the Poverty Point context: if the drought affected areas from the Rockies to the eastern seaboard, it likely affected the lower Mississippi Valley as well, though the paper does not directly document this.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: "The sequences in the southern Rocky Mountains, however, include uniquely prominent sedimentological changes from ca. 4.2-4 ka, which align with the single large positive d18Ocarb excursion at HL."
- **Page**: p. 1116
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: In the southern Rocky Mountains specifically, the 4.2 ka drought was "uniquely prominent" compared to other Holocene events. While the drought was also detected on the Atlantic margin, it may have been especially severe in the Rocky Mountain region.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: "Stratigraphic features in Hidden Lake, located in northern Colorado just south of Upper Big Creek Lake (Fig. 1) but several hundred meters lower in elevation, document a rapid increase in effective moisture at around 4 ka (Shuman et al., 2009), the opposite response of the surrounding lakes at higher elevations (Fig. 7). The wet phase was abrupt in onset and termination and lasted from around 4.4-3.7 ka based on multiple radiocarbon ages, and it stands out amid an otherwise gradual trend towards higher water levels since 6 ka (Shuman et al., 2009)."
- **Page**: p. 1116
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: A low-elevation lake in Colorado showed the opposite response to the 4.2 ka event: wetting rather than drying, lasting from 4.4 to 3.7 ka. The elevation-dependent antiphased response indicates that the drought at high elevations may have been accompanied by increased summer rainfall or fall precipitation at lower elevations. This complicates the picture significantly: the climatic response to the 4.2 ka forcing was not spatially uniform even at local scales. The wet phase ending at 3.7 ka nearly coincides with the onset of the Poverty Point period.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "The low-elevation location of Hidden Lake may indicate an important role for increased summer or fall rainfall when high-elevation lake levels declined in response to low winter snowfall. Low winter snow can create favorable surface energy conditions for strong summer convective precipitation (Zhu et al., 2005)."
- **Page**: p. 1116
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The proposed mechanism for the antiphased response at lower versus higher elevations involves seasonal precipitation redistribution: reduced winter snow but increased summer convective rainfall. If analogous dynamics operated across the Mississippi Valley during the 4.2 ka event, the lower Mississippi Valley (at low elevation) might have experienced different or mitigated drought impacts compared to upland regions.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "Given the potential prominence of the 4.2 ka drought at HL and in other southern Rocky Mountain records, it may have been uniquely severe in this region even if it had a complex regional expression at broader spatial scales. The lake-level reconstructions from Colorado contain evidence of other Holocene hydrologic changes (Fig. 7), but the records lack evidence for multiple recurrent, multi-century hydroclimate changes recorded with the 4.2 ka event in places like the Atlantic margin (Shuman et al., 2019)."
- **Page**: p. 1116-1117
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: In the southern Rocky Mountains, the 4.2 ka event stands out as uniquely severe, unlike the Atlantic margin where it is one of several recurrent multi-century events. This suggests that different regions experienced different modes of centennial-scale hydroclimate variability during the middle-to-late Holocene. For the Poverty Point model, this means the frequency and recurrence of drought episodes may have depended strongly on location within the exchange network.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "Elsewhere, aridity at 4.2 ka may represent just one of several repeated drying events consistent with climate records and simulations from around the world that show drought as a regular feature of late Holocene climate variability (Arz et al., 2006; Bradley and Bakke, 2019; Mayewski et al., 2004; Wanner et al., 2008; Wanner et al., 2015; Yan and Liu, 2019). The midlatitude Rocky Mountain records may suggest that the mid-continent was insulated from some of the abrupt late Holocene climate changes, possibly due to its isolation from the ocean-atmosphere dynamics proposed to play key roles in Holocene variability."
- **Page**: p. 1117
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The mid-continent may have been partially insulated from some abrupt late Holocene climate events due to distance from oceanic driving mechanisms. This is relevant for Poverty Point: the site is located in the interior of the continent but connected to the Gulf of Mexico, placing it in a transitional position between oceanic-influenced and continental climates.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: "Consequently, a prolonged 'megadrought' at 4.2 ka was likely a significant feature of the hydroclimate history in the midlatitude Rocky Mountains even if that is not true globally."
- **Page**: p. 1120
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The paper's conclusion is that the 4.2 ka event was regionally significant as a prolonged megadrought in the southern Rocky Mountains, even though its global expression is debatable. The qualifier "even if that is not true globally" reflects the paper's nuanced position on the event's significance.

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: "Inconsistencies among the North American records, particularly in d18Ocarb trends, thus show that site-specific factors can prevent identification of the patterns of multi-century drought. However, the prominence of the drought at ca. 4 ka among a growing number of sites in the North American interior suggests it was a regionally substantial climate event amid other Holocene variability."
- **Page**: p. 1109 (abstract)
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Summarizes the paper's balanced conclusion: site-specific factors can obscure the drought signal in some records, but the growing body of evidence from the North American interior confirms the drought was regionally substantial. The event was real and important, though its detection depends on the right proxy and the right hydrologic setting.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: "The timing and magnitude of hydroclimate change in our record agrees with the perspective of a widespread megadrought at around 4.2 ka (Weiss, 2016), but inconsistencies among climate records suggest that (1) site-specific factors can prevent identification of the patterns of abrupt hydroclimate changes, particularly in d18Ocarb records; (2) the hydrologic response in North America and likely elsewhere around the world was spatially complex; and (3) the abrupt hydroclimate changes in the North American mid-continent were more pronounced against background Holocene variability than in many regions such as the Atlantic margin."
- **Page**: p. 1120
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The three-part conclusion is the paper's most important synthesis. The 4.2 ka drought was (1) detectable but proxy-dependent, (2) spatially complex rather than uniform, and (3) relatively more prominent in the mid-continent than along the Atlantic margin. For the Poverty Point model, this means the 4.2 ka event was a real antecedent perturbation, but its expression at any particular location was contingent on local factors.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: Average net sediment accumulation rates at Highway 130 Lake were "18 cm kyr-1 (thousand years) from 11.7-4.4 ka and 45.5 cm kyr-1 from 4.4 ka to present. High rates of net sedimentation correspond to intervals of high carbonate flux into the lake."

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Based on direct measurement, isotopic analysis, sedimentological observation, or compilation of proxy data from paleoclimate records.
- **Interpretive**: Inference about causes, mechanisms, spatial patterns, or significance of observed climate features.
- **Methodological**: Claims about analytical approaches, proxy interpretation, or calibration of isotope records.

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Based on multiple independent proxy records or well-replicated measurements with robust chronological control; consistent across analytical approaches.
- **Moderate**: Supported by data but involving interpretive inference about mechanisms or spatial extrapolation; or based on fewer replicated records with acknowledged uncertainty.
- **Weak**: Speculative or poorly constrained; acknowledged limitations in proxy sensitivity or geographic extrapolation.

## Summary Assessment

Liefert and Shuman (2022) investigate the expression of the 4.2 ka event in the southern Rocky Mountains using a new carbonate oxygen isotope record from Highway 130 Lake in southeastern Wyoming, combined with comparison to existing lake-level, dune-field, pollen, and isotope records from the region. The key finding is that the 4.2 ka event produced a prominent isotopic excursion (2.9 SD above mean) at Highway 130 Lake spanning approximately 4.2-4.0 ka, consistent with reduced effective moisture or decreased snowfall. This aligns with dune reactivation in Wyoming, pollen-inferred drought at Long Lake (~150 years, centered on 4.2 ka), and lake-level declines in Colorado. However, d18Ocarb records from Bison and Yellow lakes in Colorado do not show the event, demonstrating that site-specific hydrologic factors can mask or alter the climate signal.

For the Poverty Point costly signaling model, this paper contributes in three ways. First, it refines the timing and severity of the 4.2 ka drought in the North American interior to approximately 4.2-4.0 ka (2250-2050 BCE), placing it 300-500 years before the onset of Poverty Point occupation. The drought was among the most prominent Holocene events in the Rocky Mountain region, though its expression was spatially complex. Second, the paper demonstrates that different elevations and hydrologic settings within the same region can experience opposite responses to the same climate forcing (drying at high elevations, wetting at low elevations), cautioning against assuming a uniform drought effect across the Poverty Point landscape. Third, the geographic reach of the drought from the Rocky Mountains to the Atlantic margin (documented by lake-level evidence from Maine to Pennsylvania) suggests it likely affected the lower Mississippi Valley, though direct evidence from that region is not presented. The 4.2 ka drought represents a plausible antecedent environmental stressor that could have set the conditions for the subsequent development of cooperative aggregation and signaling systems at Poverty Point, consistent with the model's prediction that environmental uncertainty favors costly signaling.
