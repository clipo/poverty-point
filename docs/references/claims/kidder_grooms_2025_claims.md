## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "Between 4000 and 3000 cal BP, the Lower Mississippi Valley experienced significant climatic and environmental changes, including increased rainfall, cooler temperatures, and high-frequency flooding. These shifts affected regional environmental stability, leading to landscape disruptions across much of eastern North America."
- **Page**: p. 1 (abstract)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (supported by multiple citations throughout the paper)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the environmental uncertainty context (sigma) that is central to the costly signaling model. Climate disruption is the key driver the authors invoke for why Poverty Point was built.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "Indigenous peoples responded to these disruptions by coming together at the Poverty Point site in northeast Louisiana and performing elaborate rituals over brief periods that included constructing earthworks and deploying bundles of diverse raw materials and unique or intricately wrought artifacts, both aimed at cosmological revitalization."
- **Page**: p. 1 (abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (this is the paper's central thesis)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the signaling framework. Aggregation and cooperative construction are reframed as ritual performance rather than political economy, but the behavioral pattern (aggregation + costly cooperative investment during environmental stress) aligns with costly signaling predictions.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "In much of the literature, Poverty Point is described as an economic center, permanently occupied for hundreds of years, built slowly, and serving as home to a political elite that organized the regional economy and institutionalized power over time. Instead, we contend that the Poverty Point site was occupied over a shorter time than previously asserted, and that the earthworks were built rapidly over a span of months (or just a few years) by a large voluntary labor force drawn from across eastern North America."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (supported by Kidder and Grooms 2024 chronological modeling)
- **Assessment**: Critical for the signaling model. Rapid construction by a large, voluntary, geographically dispersed labor force is consistent with costly signaling through cooperative monument building during aggregation events. Contradicts gradualist models of slow accumulation.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "Widespread climatic and environmental changes across the eastern United States circa 4000-3000 cal BP are manifest as increased rainfall and cooler temperatures, increased storminess along parts of the Gulf coast, high-amplitude and high-frequency flooding with increased sediment discharge, and relative sea-level shifts."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Kidder 2010; Kidder et al. 2018)
- **Assessment**: Specifies the nature of the environmental uncertainty that triggered the behavioral response. Multiple simultaneous stressors increased sigma across the region.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "In the Lower Mississippi River Valley (LMV), there was an abrupt alteration in regional landscape stability after circa 3300 cal BP and before 3000 cal BP. Meander belt relocation caused frequent flooding throughout the alluvial valley, resulting in channel switching, extensive crevassing, environmental disruption, and site burial."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Kidder 2010; Kidder et al. 2018)
- **Assessment**: Narrows the timing of maximum environmental disruption to ca. 3300-3000 cal BP, overlapping with the period of monument construction at Poverty Point.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "Indigenous responses included sudden diminished use or abandonment of many areas of the LMV and tributaries; discontinuities in settlement, subsistence, and technologies in the midcontinent and Northeast; an occupational hiatus on the Gulf coast; and cessation of shell ring construction on the lower Atlantic/eastern Gulf coasts, with settlement dispersion."
- **Page**: pp. 1-2
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (aggregated from multiple regional studies)
- **Assessment**: Documents widespread population displacement and cultural disruption contemporaneous with Poverty Point's construction. This regional depopulation and disruption is the context in which aggregation at Poverty Point becomes adaptive.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "In the century following 3300 cal BP, the Poverty Point site was abandoned, and contemporary sites ceased to be occupied or were buried by flood sediments. There were no identifiable occupations in the LMV until after circa 2800-2600 cal BP."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Kidder 2006)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the termination of the Poverty Point phenomenon and the subsequent occupational hiatus. Relevant to modeling why the signaling system collapsed.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: "There are no obvious indicators of hierarchy at Poverty Point, unless one mistakes the engineering and labor complexity suggested by the mounds as de facto evidence of hierarchical social organization."
- **Page**: p. 3
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (based on absence of evidence for hierarchy)
- **Assessment**: Supports modeling Poverty Point as a non-hierarchical, cooperation-based system. Absence of hierarchy is consistent with signaling models where authority (not coercive power) organizes collective action.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "There is no evidence of institutionalized leaders, no economic specialization beyond the presence of skilled artisans, no houses expected of a population center, no evidence of prolonged, generational construction or occupation, and no burials."
- **Page**: p. 4 (citing Gibson 2010:35)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (multiple lines of negative evidence)
- **Assessment**: Critical for modeling: confirms Poverty Point lacks the standard markers of permanent, hierarchical settlement. No burials, no elite residences, no economic specialization. Consistent with a seasonal aggregation model driven by signaling rather than political coercion.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point material culture and architectural/settlement complexity appear as if out of thin air"
- **Page**: p. 4 (paraphrasing Gibson 2010:35)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Supports the interpretation of rapid, unprecedented emergence rather than gradual development. Consistent with the revitalization movement framework and with a threshold-crossing event in the signaling model.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: The authors distinguish between power ("an entity's or individual's ability to control or direct others") and authority ("influence that is predicated on perceived legitimacy, that is, power that people agree to follow").
- **Page**: p. 5 (citing Angelbeck and Grier 2012)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (theoretical distinction applied to the archaeological case)
- **Assessment**: Relevant to modeling leadership in the aggregation system. Authority-based organization is more consistent with costly signaling (where followers voluntarily respond to credible signals) than coercion-based hierarchy.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "Humans, as custodians of creation, are morally required to manage a world of infinitely complex relationships, especially at times of great disruption. Ritual is one tool to intervene in and to reset the easily torn fabric of the universe."
- **Page**: p. 5 (emphasis in original; citing Howe 2000:67)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (ethnographic and philosophical argument)
- **Assessment**: Provides the motivational framework for why people would voluntarily bear the costs of aggregation and construction. In signaling terms, the "moral requirement" functions as the cooperation benefit that makes costly signaling adaptive.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point (Figure 1) is located on the eastern edge of Macon Ridge, a 5 m high Pleistocene landform at the edge of the LMV floodplain in northeast Louisiana. This location provided inhabitants with security from flooding, with immediate access to upland and lowland plant/animal communities and ready communication with contemporaries."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms the ecotone positioning hypothesis. The location provides access to multiple ecological zones (upland and lowland), security from the flooding that was disrupting the broader region, and communication advantages.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point is a 3 km^2 earthwork complex of two enormous mounds, five smaller mounds, and six elliptical ridges; roughly 750,000-1,000,000 m^3 of fill was moved to create the earthworks at the site."
- **Page**: p. 7 (citing Gibson 2000:109)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the scale of cooperative labor investment. The range of 750,000-1,000,000 m^3 is a key parameter for modeling construction costs and the minimum labor force required.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "There are 36 timber circles beneath the plaza."
- **Page**: p. 7 (citing Hargrave et al. 2021)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents pre-mound post-circle structures in the plaza area, indicating organized ceremonial/gathering activities predating mound construction.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "The site is dated circa 3535 to 3135 cal BP with two phases of occupation, one before construction and one associated with the building of mounds/earthworks and their occupation (Figure 2). The mounds/earthworks were erected late in the site's history."
- **Page**: p. 7 (citing Kidder and Grooms 2024)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on Bayesian chronological modeling)
- **Assessment**: Key chronological parameter. The total site occupation spans approximately 400 years (3535-3135 cal BP), but monument construction occurs only in the later phase. This constrains when the signaling system became fully active.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: "Artifacts include 'millions' of purposefully shaped fired-clay objects (Hays 2018:3), as well as thousands of kilos of unworked minerals, unfinished and finished ground and chipped stone, the remains of hundreds of soapstone vessels, and a diverse assemblage of ground/polished lithic materials, sourced from across eastern North America."
- **Page**: p. 7 (citing multiple sources)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the massive scale of material brought to the site from across the continent. The quantity and diversity of exotic materials are key parameters for modeling individual-level signaling through exotic goods acquisition.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: "Despite the quantities of exotic goods at Poverty Point, the evidence for subsidized craft production, specialized economic activities, or wealth concentration is inconclusive or lacking (Webb 1982:65-67). No definitive residential structures have been identified, despite intensive searching; burials are also absent."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Webb 1982)
- **Assessment**: Reinforces the absence of economic specialization and wealth concentration. Exotic goods were not produced or accumulated by elites; this is consistent with a signaling model where goods function as individual-level honest signals rather than accumulated wealth.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "The mounds were not used as summits for perishable structures, as containers for burials, or as locations for wealth concentration. Therefore, there is no indication that any occupants of the site commanded 'differential access to power or resources involving institutionalization of status hierarchies by hereditary privileges or positions' (Mattison et al. 2016:185)."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Mounds served neither residential, mortuary, nor wealth-storage functions. This eliminates several alternative explanations for monument construction and supports the signaling/ritual performance interpretation.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "While lithic materials measured by the metric ton come into Poverty Point, nothing visible goes out. There are more than 10,000 projectile points from Poverty Point, many made on lithic material that is found hundreds of kilometers distant, encompassing stylistic diversity beyond that commonly found at contemporary or earlier sites."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Critical observation: material flows into Poverty Point but does not visibly flow out. This is inconsistent with redistribution/trade-center models but consistent with ritual deposition/signaling where materials are consumed in performances rather than traded.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "This variety of finished chipped stone projectile points was in place from the beginning of the site's occupation to its end. The considerable stylistic variability coupled with the breadth of raw and finished materials suggests that Poverty Point drew large numbers of pilgrims from a wide geographic catchment."
- **Page**: p. 7 (citing Spivey et al. 2015)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Stylistic diversity of projectile points as evidence for geographically diverse visitors throughout the site's occupation. Supports the aggregation model: people came from across eastern North America bringing their own tool traditions.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: "A single cache pit yielded fragments from an estimated 200 to 300 soapstone vessels sourced to the southern Appalachian Mountains (Webb 1944). Similarly, there are more than 2,800 plummets at Poverty Point. These objects are mostly made of hematite or magnetite, but some were crafted from soapstone, limonite, sandstone, galena, fired clay, or copper."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Webb 1944)
- **Assessment**: Quantifies exotic goods at the site. 200-300 soapstone vessels from Appalachia in a single cache and 2,800+ plummets provide scale parameters for modeling exotic goods as signals. The variety of materials and distant sourcing establishes the costliness of acquisition.

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: "Outside of Poverty Point and the contemporary Claiborne and Jaketown sites (Ford et al. 1955; Gagliano and Webb 1975), plummets are rare at Late Archaic sites in the midcontinent and Southeast (Goldstein 2004; Perino 1961)."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Concentration of plummets at Poverty Point (and a few contemporary sites) versus rarity elsewhere supports the interpretation of Poverty Point as a unique aggregation center where these objects were deployed.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: "Some of these objects, notably, potbelly owl beads (Figure 5) and engraved perforated gorgets, are widely distributed in a brief temporal horizon; we hypothesize that these may be tokens of membership in ritual sodalities."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (hypothesis without direct evidence)
- **Assessment**: If correct, sodality membership tokens would function as group-identity signals, relevant to the signaling model. Widespread but temporally restricted distribution suggests rapid dissemination through a social network.

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: "In comparison with contemporary sites, exceptional quantities of unprocessed/unworked minerals are present at Poverty Point (e.g., ocher, quartz crystals, magnetite, hematite, galena, fluorite, mica, feldspar, slate, shale, pumice, asphaltum, cannel coal, polished pebbles, crinoid fossils, and kaolin [Webb 1982:Table 17])."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Webb 1982)
- **Assessment**: The presence of unworked raw minerals in large quantities suggests materials were valued for their inherent properties (color, origin, symbolic associations) rather than utilitarian function, consistent with ritual/signaling use.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: "Webb (in Walthall 1981:8-9) extrapolates from known data that there could be between 500 and 1000 kg of galena, sourced most likely to east-central Missouri or farther north in the Mississippi Valley, at Poverty Point."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (extrapolation from partial data)
- **Assessment**: 500-1000 kg of galena from sources 800+ km distant. Establishes the massive scale of long-distance material transport and provides a quantitative parameter for modeling transport costs.

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: "While in some contemporary instances these artifacts are found in human burials, this is not the case at Poverty Point. Some see the placement of exotic objects from long distances with the dead during the Middle and Late Archaic as a sign of expanding economic exchange and political integration (Lambert et al. 2024; Sanger et al. 2019). An Indigenous perspective, though, interprets these objects as elements of an integrated moral economy, where, in ritual contexts, including but not limited to burials, they were offered to the ancestors."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: At Poverty Point, exotic objects are not associated with burials (unlike contemporary sites), suggesting their deployment was in communal ritual contexts rather than individual mortuary display. This is relevant for distinguishing between individual-level and group-level signaling.

### Claim 28

- **Statement**: "At Poverty Point red, white, and black are predominant colors for pigments (raw ocher, galena, limonite) and beads. Most stone beads were made of red-hued materials, while many of the remaining ones are 'white' (e.g., quartz and fluorite crystal beads, ones of golden micaceous schist, galena, copper, and possibly shell)."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Color symbolism (red, white, black) in material selection connects to broader Southeastern symbolic systems and supports intentional, ritually meaningful selection of materials rather than purely economic acquisition.

### Claim 29

- **Statement**: "The quantity, variety, and recognizable ritual importance of these items singularly and in combination suggest to us the residues of discard/replenishment associated with performing bundle ceremonies."
- **Page**: p. 11 (citing Foster 1994; Hanson 1980; Murie 1981; Wildschut 1960; Wissler 1912; Zedeño 2008)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (ethnographic analogy)
- **Assessment**: Interprets the exotic artifact assemblage as residues of bundle ceremonies. If correct, the "consumption" of these goods in ritual performances makes them costly signals: they are permanently removed from circulation through ritual deposition.

### Claim 30

- **Statement**: "In our view these extraordinary objects brought to or made at the site in exceptional quantities indicate that Poverty Point was a locus of historically unprecedented ritual performances, where people from across the East brought and opened their personal and communal bundles, perhaps even to abandon them, as a component of rituals intended to renew the physical and spiritual world."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Core interpretive claim. People traveled long distances carrying costly materials to Poverty Point and deposited/abandoned them in ritual performances. This maps onto the signaling model: the cost of acquisition and the irreversibility of deposition make these honest signals.

### Claim 31

- **Statement**: "The total earthmoving may be somewhere around 1,000,000 m^3, a remarkable amount of work for people who foraged for subsistence. In the absence of compulsory labor, we see this as evidence that the community agreed that there was an urgent moral requirement to expend such effort."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (volume estimate empirical; voluntariness is interpretation)
- **Assessment**: Voluntary, non-coerced labor investment of approximately 1,000,000 m^3 of earthmoving by foragers. The "urgent moral requirement" functions in signaling terms as the cooperation benefit that makes costly investment adaptive. This is a key parameter: total earthmoving volume.

### Claim 32

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point was occupied before there were mounds or ridges. Monumental construction started with the erection of multiple single-set wooden post structures within the area identified as the plaza; several buildings are greater than 60 m across."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing archaeological excavation data)
- **Assessment**: Documents a pre-monumental occupation phase with large post-circle structures. The sequence (post circles then mounds/ridges) suggests escalation of cooperative investment over time, consistent with a signaling arms race or threshold-crossing event.

### Claim 33

- **Statement**: "Following their use, the post structures were dismantled, and the plaza surface was filled and leveled, which was a labor effort involving perhaps more sediment movement and deposition than is accounted for in most of the mounds."
- **Page**: p. 11 (citing Clay 2023; Greenlee and Dalan 2022)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Deliberate dismantling and burial of the pre-mound structures before building the mounds/ridges. This represents a significant labor investment that is often uncounted in total earthmoving estimates. The act of burying the old order before constructing the new is consistent with the revitalization interpretation.

### Claim 34

- **Statement**: "The ridges and mounds were built immediately after some of the post structures were erected and then dismantled. Building the ridges and mounds buried the earlier occupation and imposed a new social order constructed to a unified plan."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The construction sequence (build post structures, dismantle, bury, then build mounds/ridges to a unified plan) suggests a deliberate reordering event rather than gradual accumulation. Relevant to modeling construction as a punctuated, coordinated event.

### Claim 35

- **Statement**: "The new plan was a radical reconfiguration of existing spatial practices that required the builders to terraform a roughly 200 ha landscape and disregard existing topographic obstacles, including clearing and entombing wetlands (Ortmann and Kidder 2013), leveling the roughly 17 ha plaza, and filling massive gullies (Gibson 2019:54-56), to impose a singular vision on the landscape in exceptionally short order."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing specific archaeological investigations)
- **Assessment**: The scale of landscape modification (200 ha) and the deliberate overcoming of topographic obstacles demonstrate coordinated planning and massive labor mobilization. "Exceptionally short order" reinforces rapid construction.

### Claim 36

- **Statement**: "The ridges and mounds were built at the end of the site's history, and we measure the construction duration in weeks, months, or perhaps a few years, but not decades or centuries."
- **Page**: p. 11 (citing Kidder and Grooms 2024)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on Bayesian chronological modeling)
- **Assessment**: Critical temporal parameter. Construction measured in weeks to years, not decades or centuries. This constrains the labor mobilization rate and implies a very large workforce assembled for a short duration, consistent with aggregation-based construction.

### Claim 37

- **Statement**: "Unlike earlier and later sites with multiple earthworks that were arrayed with mounds constructed around a central plaza, Poverty Point's layout is inverted; it is an amphitheater with the C-shaped ridges open to the east and all but one of the mounds on the outside of the ridges."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The amphitheater-like layout is unique and consistent with a performance-oriented design. Open and outward-looking architecture supports the interpretation of the site as a venue for communal ritual performance rather than an enclosed, exclusionary settlement.

### Claim 38

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point's design is open and outward looking, whereas earlier, and later, mound sites were inward looking and architecturally exclusionary."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The open, inclusive design distinguishes Poverty Point from other mound sites and supports the aggregation model. An open design is consistent with costly signaling: performances must be publicly visible to function as signals.

### Claim 39

- **Statement**: "The monuments at Poverty Point were erected in rapid episodes requiring exceptional cooperative labor efforts. Unprecedented quantities of finished and raw lithic materials were brought to the site at the same time from a wide catchment that encompassed much of eastern North America."
- **Page**: p. 11 (conclusion)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (synthesizing multiple lines of evidence)
- **Assessment**: Summary claim combining rapid construction with wide-catchment material importation. Both elements are key inputs for the signaling model: cooperative labor and costly material acquisition occurring during brief aggregation episodes.

### Claim 40

- **Statement**: "We contend that these remarkable efforts and the imposition of a new spatial and monumental configuration were elements of a multifaceted performance guided by the authority of prophetic visionaries and intended to reinstate harmony, balance, and equilibrium in the universe."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (the "prophetic visionaries" component lacks direct evidence)
- **Assessment**: Interpretive claim about prophetic authority guiding construction. While the "prophetic visionaries" claim lacks archaeological evidence, the characterization of construction as "performance" is consistent with the signaling framework. Authority-based (not coercive) leadership aligns with signaling models.

### Claim 41

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point is at the extreme margin of known human behavior within both forager societies worldwide and Native American pre-Invasion societies."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (the scale of the site relative to other forager sites is well-documented)
- **Assessment**: Poverty Point as an extreme outlier among forager societies underscores the explanatory challenge and makes it a strong test case for costly signaling models.

### Claim 42

- **Statement**: "Performances at Poverty Point were not attempts to distract from reproduction (Hamilton 1999; Peacock and Rafferty 2013), symbols of power (Earle 2021; Gibson 2019), displays of magic (Gibson 2000, 2004), or ritualized economic practices, in which 'individuals acquire prestige, ritual status, and greater access to material goods by sponsoring important events for the community' (Stanish 2017:10)."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (argument against competing explanations)
- **Assessment**: Directly rejects several interpretive frameworks, including Stanish's prestige-through-sponsorship model, which is close to but distinct from the costly signaling framework. The authors reject individual prestige accumulation as the driver, favoring collective moral obligation.

### Claim 43

- **Statement**: "Ritual performance here did not hide an agenda of people aspiring to rule or manage their brethren (e.g., Lekson 2009:132-133, 2012:598). Performing at Poverty Point in a moment when creation was threatened by rapid climate and environmental change across the Southeast was part of the affirmative responsibility humans have within the relational world."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Rejects hidden-power models of ritual performance. The emphasis on collective responsibility during environmental crisis aligns with the costly signaling model's prediction that cooperation increases when environmental uncertainty (sigma) is high.

### Claim 44

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point was performed into existence by a cosmopolitan Late Archaic community that came to the site to discharge a moral obligation to mend and renew the torn fabric of creation, which was manifest by climatic perturbations measured on human time scales."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Final interpretive statement. "Cosmopolitan Late Archaic community" implies diverse, geographically dispersed groups aggregating at one location. "Climatic perturbations measured on human time scales" specifies that environmental changes were perceptible within individual lifetimes, making adaptive behavioral responses possible.

### Claim 45

- **Statement**: Revitalization movements "are usually stimulated by people's recognition of a critical situation or decline, which is perceived to demand urgent action, often driven by factors such as rapid acculturation, economic hardship, environmental disruptions, disease, or cultural erosion."
- **Page**: p. 6 (citing Wallace 1956:269)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (well-established concept in anthropology)
- **Assessment**: Connects the theoretical framework of revitalization movements to the environmental disruption context. Environmental disruption as a trigger for collective action is parallel to the signaling model's prediction that high sigma triggers cooperative investment.

### Claim 46

- **Statement**: "Revitalization requires the rejection of symbols of the existing order (Liebmann 2008:363; Mooney 1896:675-679) and the constitution of novel expressions of the identities and affiliations of a better future."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (ethnographic and historical evidence)
- **Assessment**: If applied to Poverty Point, the novel artifact forms (PPOs, figurines, owl beads, perforated gorgets) and the unprecedented architectural plan represent rejection of old symbolic orders and creation of new ones. The novelty of the signaling system is itself predicted by revitalization theory.

### Claim 47

- **Statement**: "Revitalization movements are mobilized through adherents (Liebmann 2008:361), often organized in ritual or religious sodalities. Sodalities are nonkin cultural groups that mediate and facilitate the transmission of knowledge, material, and culture."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (ethnographic analogy)
- **Assessment**: Sodalities as the organizational unit for revitalization movements is relevant to modeling the social structure of aggregation. Non-kin groups organized around ritual participation could function as the agents in a signaling model, distinct from the band-level agents currently modeled.

### Claim 48

- **Statement**: "Among foragers, membership in these sodalities is likely composed of younger males who inhabit social contexts that are 'dominated by links between non-relatives, where residential group membership is fluid and supports large-scale social networks of interaction' (Bird et al. 2019:96)."
- **Page**: pp. 6-7
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (ethnographic analogy)
- **Assessment**: Suggests the demographic composition of those most actively participating in the signaling system. Young males in fluid, non-kin networks is consistent with costly signaling theory's predictions about who benefits most from signaling (those seeking to establish cooperative relationships).

### Claim 49

- **Statement**: "Religious sodalities, 'as communities of ritual practice, typically focus on visions of a culture hero, deity, spirit, or transcendental being' (Dye 2022:148). These groups require novel material expressions such as dress, icons, dances, songs, architecture, and body modifications that signal membership and enhance a sense of group unity."
- **Page**: p. 7 (citing Dye 2022:148)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Sodality membership signaled through novel material culture. This maps directly onto the signaling model: distinctive material items (owl beads, gorgets) function as group membership signals, while novel architecture (the ridges) signals group commitment.

### Claim 50

- **Statement**: Table 1 provides comparative artifact counts showing Poverty Point has dramatically more of nearly every category of artifact and exotic material than any contemporary site. For example: ~34,000 ground stone tools, 2,790 plummets/fragments, 1,214 stone beads, 175 crinoid beads, 155 copper beads/objects, 296 pendants, 546 perforated stone gorgets/fragments, 29 bannerstones, ~50+ steatite objects/pieces, 2,221 galena objects/fragments, 702 clay figurines/fragments, and 133 "atlatl weights."
- **Page**: p. 8 (Table 1)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (compiled from published sources)
- **Assessment**: Quantitative data for modeling. These counts provide empirical baselines for artifact density and diversity at Poverty Point versus contemporary sites. The orders-of-magnitude differences between Poverty Point and all other sites reinforce its unique role as a regional aggregation center.

### Claim 51

- **Statement**: "People living in the region exclusively used wild-caught or foraged foods, even though some contemporaries in the East were harvesting domesticated Eastern Agricultural Complex plants."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms hunter-gatherer subsistence at Poverty Point with no agriculture, despite its availability elsewhere. This is a key parameter for modeling: resource uncertainty derives from wild food variability, not crop failure.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented

## Summary Assessment

Kidder and Grooms (2025) argue that Poverty Point was a site of cosmological revitalization, not a political-economic center: people from across eastern North America aggregated voluntarily during a period of severe climatic and environmental disruption (ca. 3500-3200 cal BP) to perform rituals of world renewal, constructing earthworks rapidly (weeks to years, not centuries) and depositing vast quantities of exotic materials as components of bundle ceremonies. The paper's empirical contributions most relevant to the signaling project include: (1) refined chronology showing rapid construction late in the site's history, (2) quantitative artifact data demonstrating orders-of-magnitude differences between Poverty Point and contemporary sites (Table 1), (3) comprehensive documentation of the absence of hierarchy, wealth concentration, burials, and economic specialization, and (4) the one-directional flow of materials into the site with nothing visible going out. While the authors explicitly reject prestige-competition and political-economy frameworks, their characterization of voluntary aggregation, cooperative labor investment during environmental crisis, public performance, and costly material deposition aligns closely with the behavioral predictions of costly signaling models, even though their interpretive framework (Indigenous moral economy and revitalization) differs from an evolutionary ecology framing.
