## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "A study of 70,000 artifacts from the type-site is reported; the known cultural content is increased by numerous new traits."
- **Page**: p. 297 (Abstract)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the scale of the artifact assemblage studied from Poverty Point: approximately 70,000 objects. This massive collection size is relevant to understanding the intensity and duration of occupation at the type-site and the volume of material culture produced and accumulated there.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "The thesis is advanced that Formative elements of Mesoamerican origin, including ceremonial organization, massive mound construction, village planning, ceramics, figurines, advanced lapidary industry, and probable agriculture, enriched the basic Archaic culture and contributed to subsequent cultural developments in the valley."
- **Page**: p. 297 (Abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Webb's central interpretive thesis attributes key Poverty Point traits to Mesoamerican Formative influence. While the Mesoamerican connection is no longer widely accepted, the specific traits listed (massive mound construction, village planning, advanced lapidary industry) remain central to understanding Poverty Point's anomalous complexity. These traits are the same ones that require explanation under the costly signaling framework.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "Late Archaic developments along the Gulf Coast and up the Mississippi Valley, after 2000 B.C., contributed a substantial base for the Poverty Point culture. New coastal and inland discoveries bring the total number of Poverty Point sites to 34, with many additional possibilities."
- **Page**: p. 297 (Abstract)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the expansion of known Poverty Point sites from the original 5 to 34 definite sites by 1968. This regional extent is directly relevant to understanding the scale of the exchange network and aggregation system.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "No longer are the original five sites -- Poverty Point, Insley, Calion, Jaketown, and Paxton Brake -- scattered in virtual isolation. A picture of the Poverty Point complex is unfolding which indicates it to have been a major, long lasting and widespread cultural manifestation which was part of the American Formative."
- **Page**: p. 297
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Characterizes Poverty Point as a widespread, long-lasting cultural phenomenon rather than a few isolated sites. The shift from 5 known sites to 34 fundamentally changed the understanding of Poverty Point's regional scope, relevant to modeling the network of participating communities.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "It now appears that the Poverty Point culture was ancestral to the Adena-Hopewellian cultural climax, to the extent that it transmitted a number of key non-Archaic traits which provided the necessary cultural stimuli to trigger this explosion."
- **Page**: p. 297
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Webb positions Poverty Point as ancestral to Hopewell, a claim that remains debated. However, the suggestion of cultural continuity between these two major exchange-oriented traditions is relevant to the broader question of how signaling systems develop and persist across archaeological periods.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: On the Gulf Coast near Lake Pontchartrain, Gagliano and Saucier (1963) described three sites: "Bayou Jasmine, Linsley, and Garcia." Additionally, Gagliano (1963; personal communication, 1966) listed two more: "Rabbit Island and Avery Island, on the western fringe of the Mississippi Delta."
- **Page**: p. 297
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents five coastal Poverty Point sites around Lake Pontchartrain and the Mississippi Delta, expanding the known extent of the culture to coastal environments. Relevant to understanding the ecological diversity of the Poverty Point network.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: The Bayou Jasmine and Linsley sites near Lake Pontchartrain "were buried 6 to 12 ft. below present mean Gulf level." The Linsley site radiocarbon dates (discarding the oldest as anomalous) averaged 1740 B.C., "as valid and consistent with a date of 2090 +/- 140 (G-581) from peat directly beneath the natural levee of the Mississippi River, on which the site is located."
- **Page**: p. 298
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides early radiocarbon dates for coastal Poverty Point occupations, with an average of ca. 1740 B.C. The submergence of sites 6-12 feet below present Gulf level indicates significant landscape change and suggests many coastal sites may be inaccessible. These dates help bracket the temporal span of the Poverty Point phenomenon.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: At the Garcia site on the eastern end of Lake Pontchartrain, "A full chipped-stone assemblage reproduces Poverty Point lithic types; and plummets of stone and hematite, boatstones, bar weights, sandstone saws, celts, and a full complement of microflint tools are represented. A high percentage of materials is imported; these include quartz, novaculite, metamorphic rocks, magnetite, and hematite."
- **Page**: p. 298
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the presence of a full Poverty Point assemblage, including imported exotic materials, at a coastal site far from the type-site. The "high percentage" of imported materials at this remote location demonstrates the reach of the exchange network, directly relevant to the costly signaling argument about long-distance exotic goods acquisition.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: At the Pearlington site on the Pearl River Delta, "The lowest 6 in. of the 2.5-ft. midden had stone tools, animal bones and bone awls, steatite vessel fragments, hematite plummet fragments, a red jasper bead, and gorget fragments." Above this level, "baked clay balls in quantity accompanied fiber-tempered pottery."
- **Page**: pp. 298-299
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents stratigraphic association of Poverty Point materials with exotic items (steatite, hematite, red jasper) at a Pearl River Delta site. The stratigraphic sequence shows Poverty Point materials at the base, relevant to understanding the temporal and spatial extent of the exchange network.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "The Mississippi Delta is bordered by coastal and upland late Archaic sites." These include "Copell site (Collins 1927; Ford and Quimby 1945) and Avery Island (Gagliano 1964) on the western margin, and the Amite and Pearl River phases (Gagliano 1963) north of Lake Pontchartrain." The upland Amite River phase is "represented by small sites, with no discovered burials but with small conical earth mounds 3 to 5 ft. in height and 70 to 150 ft. in diameter."
- **Page**: p. 299
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents a range of related late Archaic sites bordering the Mississippi Delta, including small mound sites on the Amite River. The presence of small conical mounds (3-5 ft. high) at these secondary sites contrasts with the massive construction at Poverty Point and is relevant to understanding the hierarchical structure of the site network.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "In the inland river valleys, there are three clusters of Poverty Point sites. The Tensas Basin Survey by Williams (1966) established Poverty Point components at the Head, Mott, and Neeley mound sites on Macon Ridge above and below the Poverty Point village."
- **Page**: p. 301
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents clustering of Poverty Point sites along Macon Ridge, the same landform as the type-site. Multiple sites in close proximity suggests a network of settlements centered on the main Poverty Point site, relevant to modeling the aggregation system and its catchment area.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "The second cluster of sites is along the Ouachita River in north-central Louisiana and southern Arkansas." Sites include one near Monroe with "steatite vessel fragments and Archaic lithic items in addition to the clay balls previously reported," and the Lea site in Ouachita Parish which "produced a cache of 43 polished hematite perforated plummets, along with flake scrapers and late Archaic projectile points."
- **Page**: p. 302
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents a second cluster of Poverty Point sites along the Ouachita River, featuring exotic materials (steatite, hematite plummets). A cache of 43 polished hematite perforated plummets represents a substantial investment in exotic material acquisition and curation, directly relevant to the costly signaling argument.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: At the Terrell Lewis site, "A dense midden, 3 ft. in depth, was loaded with whole and fragmentary clay balls of all the major types found at Poverty Point except cylindrical grooved. Typical projectile points and chipped flint tools, celts, steatite vessel fragments, slate and limonite ornaments and gorgets, hematite and magnetite plummets, lamellar blades, and a few clay-tempered pottery sherds have also been found. We consider this to be a pure Poverty Point village site."
- **Page**: p. 301
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents a "pure" Poverty Point village site with a full complement of diagnostic artifacts including exotic materials (steatite, slate, hematite, magnetite). The characterization as a "village site" rather than a temporary camp suggests sustained occupation and is relevant to understanding the settlement pattern associated with the Poverty Point network.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "The third cluster of Poverty Point and related sites is in the Yazoo Basin in the northwestern portion of the state of Mississippi, east of the present Mississippi River channel. Formerly only the Jaketown site and the nearby Paxton Brake site were known."
- **Page**: p. 306
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents a third geographic cluster of Poverty Point sites in the Yazoo Basin of Mississippi, east of the Mississippi River. The expansion from two known sites to many in this region demonstrates the widespread nature of the Poverty Point phenomenon across the lower Mississippi Valley.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: At the Teoc Creek site in Carroll County, Mississippi, "the dark midden forms a semicircle, 900 ft. in diameter, from the levee crest to the backslope, and a deep midden 10 ft. below the levee crest indicates the existence of aboriginal occupation throughout the period of levee deposition. This is a village site covering 10 to 12 acres, with numerous fire-blackened areas where clay ball fragments and other cultural debris are abundant. The cultural complex (Table 2) is very similar in objects and materials to the Poverty Point complex at the Jaketown site, except that microflints are less numerous."
- **Page**: p. 306
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents a substantial Poverty Point village site of 10-12 acres in the Yazoo Basin, with a semicircular midden layout 900 ft. in diameter. The site layout and long occupation through levee deposition are relevant to understanding settlement patterns. While much smaller than the Poverty Point type-site, this village represents a significant secondary settlement in the network.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "Diagnostic Criteria: Poverty Point and Related Sites -- Mention should be made of the diagnostic criteria employed in assigning sites to the Poverty Point complex." Initial consideration is given to "the presence, in the village, of clay balls or fragments distributed through the midden, indicating use of this method of cooking. In the present survey, all sites assigned with certainty to the Poverty Point period have exhibited this trait."
- **Page**: p. 303
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes baked clay balls as the sine qua non diagnostic trait for Poverty Point site identification. All definite Poverty Point sites share this cooking technology. The universal presence of clay balls across the network is relevant to understanding shared cultural practices that united dispersed communities.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: Primary diagnostic traits beyond clay objects include: "tubular pipes of clay or stone, clay figurines of Poverty Point type (so far confined to the type-site), fragments of steatite or sandstone vessels, hematite plummets (especially if perforated), microflints such as lamellar blades or Jaketown perforators, rough greenstone celts or hoes, and beads or polished ornaments and problematical objects of red jasper."
- **Page**: pp. 303-304
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Defines the primary diagnostic trait list for Poverty Point culture. Several of these items involve exotic materials (steatite, hematite, red jasper, greenstone) requiring long-distance acquisition, directly relevant to the costly signaling framework. The restriction of figurines to the type-site is notable and suggests certain cultural activities were concentrated at the primary center.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: "Of these traits, the figurines, rough greenstone celts and hoes, red jasper owl beads, and narrow-end jasper tablets have been found only on known Poverty Point sites and are highly diagnostic."
- **Page**: p. 304
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Identifies certain artifact types as exclusive to confirmed Poverty Point sites, suggesting these items functioned within the specific cultural system rather than being general late Archaic traits. Red jasper owl beads and narrow-end jasper tablets are particularly relevant as highly diagnostic exotic items.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: Secondary traits "are those which are present generally in late Archaic cultures and often carry over into the Tchefuncte-Tchula or Marksville-Baytown periods. They are regularly present in the Poverty Point complex, often in greater abundance or variety than in other complexes in which they occur." These include "late Archaic projectile points, usually in a sufficient variety to include 8 to 10 types (Motley, Delhi, and Epps types made of Indiana gray flint become primary diagnostics)."
- **Page**: p. 304
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents that Poverty Point sites have a greater "abundance or variety" of Archaic traits than comparable sites. The use of Indiana gray flint for specific projectile point types (Motley, Delhi, Epps) documents long-distance lithic raw material acquisition from sources in Indiana, relevant to the exchange network analysis.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "Tertiary diagnostic traits are frequent at Poverty Point sites but not significantly diagnostic because of their presence in other complexes, older or more recent. These include galena or quartz, plummets other than hematite or magnetite, food-preparing equipment like manos, mortars, and pitted stones, ground stone celts, and sherds of fiber-tempered pottery."
- **Page**: p. 304
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The inclusion of galena and quartz as tertiary traits indicates these exotic materials are present but not unique to Poverty Point. Their classification as "tertiary" does not diminish their relevance to exchange network analysis, as galena sources are in Missouri/Illinois (~800 km) and quartz crystals from Arkansas (~300 km).

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: At the Kelly site on the north bank of the Ohio River near Clarksville, Clark County, Indiana, "Several hundred baked clay balls, found in groups, were reported from an area about 12 by 24 ft. in diameter; of the 82 remaining in collections... the types are: biconical plain, 4; biconical grooved, 58; cross-grooved, 2; melon-shaped, 8; melon-shaped, twisted, 1; amorphous, 6; spherical, dimpled, 1; biconical with multiple notches, 1; spherical with grooves, 1."

- **Evidence strength**: Strong

This site is approximately 900 km from the type-site, demonstrating the extraordinary geographic reach of the cultural system or its exchange connections.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: "Knowledge of the internal content of the Poverty Point site has been enhanced by a number of new collections during the past decade, especially that of Carl Alexander, who lives near the site and has collected extensively. In all, James A. Ford and the author have studied and tabulated 18 collections from the Poverty Point site, totaling approximately 70,000 objects, tripling the numbers available for our 1956 publication."

- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms the scale of artifact study: 70,000 objects from 18 collections. The tripling of the available sample from the 1956 publication substantially strengthened the empirical basis for characterizing Poverty Point material culture.

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: "A hallmark of Poverty Point culture is the profusion of baked clay balls. Eighty-seven per cent of the 12,000 which we have classified from the type-site fall into six major types; in order of frequency these are: cylindrical with grooves, cross-grooved, melon-shaped with end grooves, melon-shaped, biconical grooved, and biconical plain."
- **Page**: p. 308
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantifies the clay ball assemblage: 12,000 classified specimens, 87% falling into six types. The enormous quantity and typological standardization of these cooking objects testify to the intensity of food preparation activity at the site, relevant to understanding the scale of aggregation events and communal activities.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: "Four minor types add another eight per cent: amorphous, spheroidal, melon-shaped twisted, and biscuit-shaped. The remaining five per cent are atypical or unusual, including perforated, miniature, and decorated forms."
- **Page**: p. 308
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the full typological range of clay balls. The existence of decorated forms (5%) is notable because it suggests individual or group identity markers on utilitarian cooking objects, which could be relevant to understanding social organization within the aggregation.

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: "The previous suggestion that clay balls were not only 'hot rock' substitutes in a cooking process but that they were probably used in pit baking, rather than boiling, is strengthened by the discovery of a half dozen pits at both the type-site and the Terrell Lewis site; these were also reported by Gagliano (1963) from Linsley and Bayou Jasmine sites on Lake Pontchartrain. These pits are usually 12 to 21 in. in diameter and 15 to 20 in. in depth; they sometimes show traces of fire or small amounts of ash and contain from a dozen to more than 200 clay balls."
- **Page**: p. 308
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides physical evidence for the function of clay balls as pit-baking implements, with documented pit features containing 12-200 balls at multiple sites. The pit dimensions and contents across geographically dispersed sites demonstrate standardized cooking technology. Relevant to understanding communal food preparation during aggregation events.

### Claim 28

- **Statement**: "Most of the clay balls in a given pit are of a single type, indicating the individual housewife's or family's preference for a certain shape or decoration."
- **Page**: p. 308
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Suggests that clay ball type preferences were maintained at the household or family level, implying individual or family-level identity within the larger community. This is relevant to understanding social organization within the aggregation, where distinct social units maintained their own practices.

### Claim 29

- **Statement**: "Persistence of the trait of using stones in cooking, despite the huge numbers of fired clay balls, is indicated at Poverty Point and several other sites by the finding of moderate numbers of fire-darkened or fire-cracked cobbles. Fragments of stone vessels or pottery have not been associated with the baking pits nor with a large fire hearth, 4.5 ft. in diameter, found by Alexander."
- **Page**: p. 308
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents that traditional stone-boiling persisted alongside the clay ball technology and that no vessels were associated with cooking features. This confirms that clay balls functioned as heat-retention devices for earth-oven cooking, not for stone boiling in vessels.

### Claim 30

- **Statement**: "The previous report (Ford and Webb 1956) listed only 53 pottery sherds from the Poverty Point site. Subsequently more than 500 sherds have been found, the majority being typologically earlier than Marksville. Eighty-two of these 362 early sherds are fiber-tempered, 33 are sand-tempered, and 247 are clay-tempered."
- **Page**: p. 308
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents a tenfold increase in pottery recovery at the Poverty Point site (from 53 to 500+ sherds). The small total relative to 70,000 artifacts confirms that pottery was a minor component of Poverty Point material culture. The fiber-tempered component establishes temporal connections to early ceramic traditions along the Gulf Coast.

### Claim 31

- **Statement**: "Other clay objects found at Poverty Point include tubular pipes and human figurines. The latter are solid, always female, sometimes pregnant, with modeled faces and torsos, but with mere suggestions of extremities."
- **Page**: p. 310
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the presence of clay figurines restricted to the type-site. The exclusively female and sometimes pregnant form is relevant to understanding ritual or symbolic activities at the primary center.

### Claim 32

- **Statement**: "Whole and fragmentary projectile points from the Poverty Point site, in the collections tabulated to date, total 10,214. Nearly 3,000 of these are broken to the extent that typing is not possible, and an additional 263 are untyped; 94 had been reworked into drills, spokeshaves, or stemmed scrapers. One hundred and one (1.46%) are arrow points, presumably from the late occupation on the immediately adjoining Neal site."
- **Page**: p. 311
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantifies the projectile point assemblage at over 10,000 specimens. The massive quantity of dart points, combined with the exclusion of arrow points as intrusive, provides important data on the scale of lithic production and consumption at the site. Relevant to understanding the intensity of activities during aggregation periods.

### Claim 33

### Claim 34

- **Statement**: "A differential preference for certain materials, suggested previously, is confirmed in the large collection: Gary and lozenge-shaped points were made preferably from Arkansas novaculite and quartzites; Motley points from Harrison County, Indiana, gray flint; Pontchartrain and Kent points of local tan and brown cherts."
- **Page**: p. 311
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents material-specific projectile point manufacturing preferences. Motley points from Indiana gray flint represent long-distance raw material acquisition (~900 km to Harrison County, Indiana). Gary and lozenge-shaped points from Arkansas novaculite (~250 km) also indicate systematic exchange. This differential sourcing of lithic raw materials is directly relevant to understanding the Poverty Point exchange network.

### Claim 35

- **Statement**: "The microflint industry is one of the outstanding traits at Poverty Point. The collections we tabulated amassed more than 23,000 of these objects: unused and slightly used lamellar blades, Jaketown perforators, needle and double-end forms, and cores."
- **Page**: p. 312
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantifies the microflint industry at over 23,000 pieces. This is the largest concentration of microlithic tools in eastern North America and represents a distinctive technology, potentially of Mesoamerican origin according to Webb. The scale of production is relevant to understanding specialized activities during aggregation.

### Claim 36

- **Statement**: "A survey of the tradition of making microlithic lamellar bladelets from cores prepared with a sloping platform indicates that this tradition is present, in any significant amounts, in any United States area, only in Poverty Point and Hopewell cultures."
- **Page**: p. 312
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Links the microblade tradition exclusively to Poverty Point and Hopewell in eastern North America. This shared technological tradition between two major exchange-oriented cultural systems is relevant to understanding continuities in cultural practices associated with long-distance interaction networks.

### Claim 37

- **Statement**: "It has previously been noted that Archaic sites in the lower valley and along the coast have microflint tools made by a different bipolar technique; to date, the typical microflints which are so abundant at Poverty Point and Jaketown have not occurred in presumably earlier Archaic sites, suggesting that they were introduced with other Poverty Point traits."
- **Page**: p. 312
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Distinguishes the Poverty Point microblade technology (prepared platform cores) from earlier Archaic bipolar techniques, arguing the former was an introduced innovation. Relevant to understanding the suite of technological innovations that characterize the Poverty Point phenomenon.

### Claim 38

- **Statement**: "Ground and polished stone objects are abundant at Poverty Point, reflecting the culture's major dependence on late Archaic technology. There were more than 2,300 whole or fragmentary plummets in the studied collections. Most were made of hematite and magnetite, in the ratio of 80:20 per cent, respectively, but some are of limonite, sandstone, galena, clay, steatite, quartzites, and other stones."
- **Page**: p. 312
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantifies over 2,300 plummets, predominantly of hematite (80%) and magnetite (20%). These materials required importation: hematite and magnetite sources are non-local. The sheer number of plummets and the predominance of exotic raw materials are directly relevant to the costly signaling argument, as acquiring and displaying these objects represented significant investment.

### Claim 41

The possible presence of obsidian, if confirmed, would extend the exchange network even further.

### Claim 44

- **Statement**: "Peculiar to this complex is the narrow-end tablet of red jasper or of banded red, yellow, and gray jasper. A few of these are roughed-out to the desired shape by chipping and are not ground; more often, they are partially or completely ground. Broken fragments are smooth, indicating breakage during use."
- **Page**: p. 314
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents narrow-end jasper tablets as a diagnostic artifact type unique to the Poverty Point complex. These objects, made from exotic red jasper, show evidence of use and breakage during use, suggesting functional rather than purely decorative purpose. Their restriction to Poverty Point sites makes them a potential marker of participation in the signaling system.

### Claim 45

- **Statement**: Regarding copper at Poverty Point: "In December, 1961, Carl Alexander found several copper beads washing out of the midden; subsequently, other beads, including 19 beads in a double row suggesting a burial placement, and small copper objects were found in the same place. There is a total of 34 rolled tubular beads, 1 solid globular bead, perforated, a copper awl or pin, and 15 flat fragments."
- **Page**: p. 317
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents native copper artifacts at Poverty Point, including 34 rolled tubular beads and other objects. Copper would have been sourced from the Great Lakes region (~1,600 km), making it the most distant exotic material at the site. The burial-associated placement of 19 beads in a row suggests these exotic items held special significance, directly relevant to the costly signaling interpretation.

### Claim 46

- **Statement**: "The entire lapidary industry in Poverty Point culture has striking resemblances to Olmec; only in these two cultures, at this time period, had there appeared such variety and virtuosity in lapidary work, with the penchant for seeking exotic materials from distant sources. Moreover, there are numerous parallels or close similarities (see Drucker 1952: 162-72, Pls. 53-58)."
- **Page**: p. 317
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Webb draws a direct comparison between Poverty Point and Olmec lapidary traditions, noting shared characteristics of variety, virtuosity, and preference for exotic materials. Whether or not the Mesoamerican connection is valid, the parallel with Olmec highlights Poverty Point's anomalous investment in lapidary craft and exotic material acquisition compared to other contemporary North American cultures.

### Claim 47

- **Statement**: "No other site of the Poverty Point period shares with the type-site the massive mound building, the extensive geometric village construction, and the evidence of solar orientation."
- **Page**: p. 317
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes Poverty Point's uniqueness in combining massive mound construction, geometric village layout, and solar orientation. No other Poverty Point period site has all three features, positioning the type-site as the singular primary center in the regional network. This primacy is relevant to the costly signaling framework, which predicts a hierarchical structure with the most costly signals concentrated at the main aggregation center.

### Claim 48

- **Statement**: "Mound A is 640 ft. in N-S length and 70 ft. in height; it is terraced and has a ramp on the east side which descends to a platform, 23 ft. high and 240 by 300 ft. in diameters, which connects the mound to the outermost village terrace on its western side. Mound B is north of Mound A, northwest of the village; it is conical in shape and has a height of 21 ft. and a basal diameter of 195 ft."
- **Page**: p. 317
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides detailed dimensions of Poverty Point's two primary mounds. Mound A at 70 ft. (21 m) high and 640 ft. (195 m) long is among the largest Pre-Columbian mounds in North America. The scale of these constructions represents an enormous labor investment by hunter-gatherers, central to the costly signaling argument about monumental construction.

### Claim 49

- **Statement**: "The Motley Mound, 1.5 mi. north of the village center, was reported by Clarence B. Moore's (1913) survey to be 51 ft. in height, 560 ft. in E-W diameter and 400 ft. N-S, with an added low (6 ft.) southward terrace which was connected with the apex by an incomplete ramp."
- **Page**: p. 317
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the Motley Mound, 1.5 miles north of the main village, as another massive construction at 51 ft. (15.5 m) high. Its location separate from but near the main village center suggests it served a distinct function within the Poverty Point monumental landscape. The presence of multiple massive mounds amplifies the labor investment argument.

### Claim 50

- **Statement**: "The Poverty Point village, 3,960 ft. in diameter, is on a series of six concentrically arranged ridges or terraces, shown by aerial photography to be octagonal in shape and separated by gaps which radiate from center like wheel spokes. Each ridge is 4 to 6 ft. in height, and they are placed about 150 ft. apart, from crest to crest. Moved earth involved in construction of the three mounds and the terraces totals nearly one million cubic yards."
- **Page**: p. 318
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides critical measurements of the Poverty Point earthworks: 3,960 ft. (1,207 m) diameter village with six concentric ridges, each 4-6 ft. high, spaced 150 ft. apart. Total earth moved: nearly one million cubic yards (~765,000 cubic meters). This labor estimate is central to the costly signaling argument, as it quantifies the enormous collective investment in monumental construction by a hunter-gatherer population.

### Claim 51

- **Statement**: "Ford (personal communication) has established from aerial photographs that the orientation at Poverty Point is 7 degrees west of magnetic north. This would mean that persons standing on the crest of Mound A and looking eastward down the ramp and across the village would face the rising sun at the time of the equinoxes."
- **Page**: p. 318
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents solar orientation of the site layout, with equinox sunrise alignment from Mound A looking east. This planned astronomical orientation adds to the evidence of community planning and deliberate design, suggesting the earthworks served ceremonial or calendrical functions beyond simple habitation.

### Claim 52

- **Statement**: "The Teoc Creek village site, in Carroll County, presented an interesting arrangement of the habitation areas. These were indicated by dark midden soil, 160 to 180 ft. in width, which appeared in a semicircle down the backslope of the levee. The base of the semicircle along the levee crest was approximately 900 ft. in diameter."
- **Page**: p. 318
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents a semicircular village layout at Teoc Creek in Mississippi (900 ft. diameter), echoing the geometric arrangement at the Poverty Point type-site. This suggests that planned geometric village layouts were a feature of the broader Poverty Point cultural system, not unique to the primary center.

### Claim 53

- **Statement**: "Two sites have shown evidence of structures built during Poverty Point times: Jaketown contained an oval structure 22 ft. at the greatest diameter, and at the Monte Sano mound, a square structure, 20 to 21 ft. in diameter, was revealed beneath the mound."
- **Page**: p. 318
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents architectural remains at two Poverty Point sites: an oval structure (22 ft.) at Jaketown and a square structure (20-21 ft.) at Monte Sano. These are among the earliest known structures in the lower Mississippi Valley and demonstrate that Poverty Point peoples constructed formal buildings, not just earthworks.

### Claim 54

- **Statement**: "It is believed that the previous suggestion of 800-600 B.C. for the Poverty Point period (Ford and Webb 1956) is too late and too brief. We hope to secure additional radiocarbon dates for the inland sites... It is probable that Poverty Point culture was established on the coast and in the valleys between 1500 and 1000 B.C., antecedent to Hopewell and Adena and in time to contribute to Eastern Woodland origins in Burial Mound 1 times. The Poverty Point-Tchefuncte transition probably occurred at about 500 B.C."
- **Page**: p. 318
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Revises the chronology for Poverty Point from 800-600 B.C. to 1500-1000 B.C., roughly doubling the time depth. The extended chronology (now generally accepted as ca. 1700-1100 B.C.) is relevant to modeling the duration of the signaling system and the timeframe over which monumental construction accumulated.

### Claim 55

- **Statement**: "Ford and the author advance the thesis that Poverty Point culture was formed when increments of Mesoamerican Formative culture -- not all necessarily in one package -- were introduced into a well-developed Southeastern Archaic forest economy to enrich and permanently alter the valley cultures."
- **Page**: p. 318
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: The Mesoamerican diffusion thesis, while historically influential, is no longer the dominant interpretation of Poverty Point origins. However, the underlying observation that Poverty Point represents a dramatic enrichment of the regional Archaic cultural system remains valid and requires explanation. The costly signaling framework offers an alternative mechanism for the emergence of this complexity.

### Claim 56

- **Statement**: "Some of those recognized or presumed Mesoamerican increments are: strong religious and civil organization in ceremonial centers, with satellite villages; planned construction with solar orientation; massive mounds with ramps and terraces; pottery making. Agriculture or horticulture is not proved, but it is implied by the riverine settlement patterns; by the usual association of agriculture with large year-round villages and ceremonial centers; by the known presence of maize, beans, squash, and other cultigens in contemporary Mesoamerican centers; and by the occurrence, at the Poverty Point site, of artifacts like trough metates, loaf-shaped manos, and clay figurines, which are associated with agriculture in Mesoamerica."
- **Page**: pp. 318-319
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Webb speculates that agriculture accompanied the other Formative traits at Poverty Point, acknowledging it is "not proved." This claim has been largely rejected by subsequent research showing Poverty Point subsistence was based on hunting, gathering, and fishing. The absence of agriculture is central to the project's framework: the anomaly of monumental construction by non-agricultural peoples is precisely what the costly signaling model seeks to explain.

### Claim 57

- **Statement**: "Sixteen of the 34 Poverty Point sites listed in Table 1 are village sites with no mound construction." And "Many other sites which have Poverty Point components, but also evidences of later occupation, have from one to eight mounds -- most of which are thought, and in some instances demonstrated, to have been built by later peoples."

- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents that nearly half (16 of 34) of definite Poverty Point sites lack mound construction, establishing a clear hierarchy within the site network. This distinction between mound sites and non-mound village sites is relevant to the signaling model: monumental construction was concentrated at certain locations while many settlements in the network lacked it.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited

## Summary Assessment

This is a foundational synthesis of Poverty Point culture as understood in 1968, expanding the known site inventory from 5 to 34 sites and tripling the analyzed artifact assemblage to approximately 70,000 objects from the type-site alone. Webb's most significant contributions for the costly signaling project are: (1) the quantification of exotic material acquisition at an industrial scale (2,300+ plummets, 1,344 lapidary ornaments, 23,000+ microflints, copper from the Great Lakes); (2) the documentation of a regional site network spanning from Indiana to the Gulf Coast with clear hierarchical differentiation; (3) the earth-moving estimate of nearly one million cubic yards for the type-site earthworks; and (4) the establishment of diagnostic criteria demonstrating shared cultural practices (especially clay ball cooking technology) across the entire network. Webb's Mesoamerican diffusion thesis is largely rejected today, but his empirical observations about the anomalous scale of exotic material acquisition, lapidary production, and monumental construction by a non-agricultural society remain central problems that the costly signaling framework addresses.
