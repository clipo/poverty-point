## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: The Poverty Point Site consists of 2-3 km^2 of dense occupation debris surrounding a central mound and earth ridge complex that covers over 1 km^2. The concentric ridges have an outside diameter of nearly 1 km.
- **Page**: pp. 17, 37-38
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides basic site dimensions relevant to modeling the scale of monumental construction and population estimates.

---

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: Ford and Webb (1956) interpreted the construction of Poverty Point as the fulfillment of a master blueprint, arguing that "probably all of it was built and inhabited at about the same time" and that the site "was constructed according to a integrated plan that probably would not have prevailed if the town had grown by accretion over a long span of time."
- **Page**: p. 18
- **Type**: interpretive (Jackson reporting Ford and Webb's interpretation)
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (Jackson critiques this reasoning as circular)
- **Assessment**: Relevant to understanding whether construction was rapid or episodic, which affects signaling model parameters.

---

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: The stratigraphy in the ridge excavations consisted of thin midden deposits separated by loaded fill, which suggested that occupation coincided with construction. "The presence of only thin accumulations of living debris was interpreted as evidence of a span of site use on the order of 200 years" (Ford and Webb 1956:124).
- **Page**: p. 18
- **Type**: empirical / interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to whether occupation was continuous vs. episodic. Thin midden between construction episodes is consistent with seasonal aggregation rather than permanent settlement.

---

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: Ford and Webb argued that "without a stable basic food so large a population could not have been concentrated in one locality, and certainly the surplus labor necessary to undertake construction on the scale accomplished by people would not have been available" (Ford and Webb 1956:129). They assumed an agricultural economy was necessary.
- **Page**: p. 19
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (Jackson notes this was subsequently undermined)
- **Assessment**: This assumption that agriculture was required is directly challenged by the signaling model, which provides an alternative explanation for labor mobilization among hunter-gatherers.

---

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: Radiocarbon dates from Poverty Point originally ranged between 1200 and 389 B.C. (uncorrected, old half-life). Ford and Webb selected an 800-600 B.C. occupation period, but the dates required "manipulation" to support this interpretation.
- **Page**: pp. 19-20
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (Jackson is critical of the date selection)
- **Assessment**: Provides the radiocarbon chronology context. The dates suggest a longer occupation than originally assumed.

---

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: Table 1 presents a compilation of published radiocarbon dates from Poverty Point and related sites. For Poverty Point proper, dates range from 2339+/-200 B.P. to 3340+/-60 B.P. (uncorrected). Related sites yield even older dates: Teoc Creek 3020+/-150 to 3650+/-160 B.P.; Linsley 3540+/-120 to 4400+/-140 B.P.; Claiborne 3100+/-110 to 3990+/-80 B.P.
- **Page**: pp. 20-21
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (compiled radiocarbon data)
- **Assessment**: Key chronological data. Dates from related sites extend well before the proposed 800-600 B.C. florescence at the type site, supporting a longer cultural tradition.

---

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: Gregory, Davis, and Hunter (1970:44) interpreted the Terral Lewis Site as a spring-summer agricultural camp and suggested a Poverty Point interaction sphere "based upon seasonal production with the maintenance of a large nucleated center, Poverty Point proper, by in and out moving groups. That is, no one group need be resident at the large site all year round, but the site maintained a series of local contributive units which were occupied seasonally by some groups, probably extended families."
- **Page**: p. 28
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Highly relevant to the aggregation model. This is an early articulation of a seasonal aggregation interpretation for Poverty Point that aligns closely with the signaling framework.

---

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: At Teoc Creek in the Yazoo Basin, radiocarbon dates range between 1700+/-160 and 1070+/-150 B.C. Even for these early dates, "there is evidence for the trade that characterizes the whole Poverty Point Period; it is reflected at the Teoc Creek site in a wide array of exotic lithic and mineral materials." A decided preference for eastern and northern sources in Alabama, northeastern Mississippi, Tennessee, and the Midwest is suggested by their proportional representation.
- **Page**: p. 29
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Demonstrates that long-distance exchange networks operated early in the Poverty Point Period, supporting the idea that exotic goods exchange was foundational to the system, not a late development.

---

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: Gibson (1973) argued for a chiefdom-type social formation during the Poverty Point period, distinguishing two broad strata of society based on proportional distribution of artifact classes including "restricted access paraphernalia" such as stone beads and certain types of Poverty Point Objects and Motley points (Gibson 1973:156).
- **Page**: p. 32
- **Type**: interpretive (Jackson reporting Gibson's claim)
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (Jackson subsequently critiques this)
- **Assessment**: Gibson's chiefdom model is the principal alternative interpretation to a signaling/aggregation framework. Understanding its basis and weaknesses is important.

---

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: Gibson's population estimates for Poverty Point during the Florescent Phase were approximately 4000 people. This was calculated three ways: (1) using clay object estimates of 12,000,000 at the site, a use rate of 80/family/year, yielding 777 families (4662 persons); (2) using total ridge crest length (11,270 m) with 15.2 m house-and-yard spacing, yielding 740 houses (4440 people); (3) using earth volume in ridges to calculate 1350 people working at any construction phase (one-third of 4050 total).
- **Page**: p. 54
- **Type**: empirical / interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (Jackson critiques all three methods as requiring problematic assumptions)
- **Assessment**: Population estimates are critical for the simulation model. Jackson's critique suggests these estimates may be inflated by assuming year-round permanent occupation rather than seasonal aggregation.

---

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: Jackson critiques Gibson's population estimates by noting that "neither the possibility that this assumption might imply that the site might have been utilized in the summer only, nor the impact on the population estimate of year-round clay object cooking, are adequately explored." Gibson assumed clay objects were employed for baking fish only during summer months (Gibson 1973:131).
- **Page**: p. 55
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (valid critique)
- **Assessment**: If clay objects were used only seasonally, the population present during that season could differ from a year-round estimate. This supports a seasonal aggregation model.

---

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: Jackson argues that the evidence presented by Gibson for social ranking "relies heavily upon an intrasite spatial analysis of provenienced surface collections from Poverty Point provided by Webb and Alexander." Evidence for a high-ranking elite is derived from the distribution of lapidary artifacts, specifically stone beads, since they "represent a tremendous investment of time, labor, and skill" and constitute "restricted access paraphernalia" (Gibson 1973:156). However, only 25 beads of the more than 1000 known from the site had source information.
- **Page**: pp. 56-57
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (valid methodological critique)
- **Assessment**: Undermines the chiefdom interpretation. If exotic goods were not restricted to elite zones, they may have functioned as signals rather than markers of inherited status.

---

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "Stone beads, other lapidary items, Motley points, and other exotic materials are also present in supposedly 'non-elite' areas of Poverty Point, at sites constituting the low end of the settlement hierarchy, and throughout the geographic distribution of the cultural complex" (Goad 1980; Johnson 1980; Webb 1982). Johnson (1980) demonstrated that for the Yazoo Basin sites, "exotics" are not restricted to larger sites.
- **Page**: p. 58
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Highly relevant to the signaling model. If exotic goods are widely distributed rather than restricted to elites, they may function as signals accessible to all participants in the exchange system, consistent with costly signaling theory rather than chiefly redistribution.

---

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: Jackson argues that "the basis for this assumption [that Poverty Point was a complexly organized, class-structured society] has not been adequately established." The interpretation "rests largely on the assumption that the scale of both earthwork construction and movements of exotic materials into and through the system is indicative of a complex, centrally organized society."
- **Page**: p. 39
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (identifies unsupported assumption)
- **Assessment**: Directly supports the need for alternative explanations of monumental construction and exchange, such as the signaling model.

---

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: New evidence suggests ridge construction "occurred at intervals. As new ridges were constructed, borrow pits from previous ones had to be filled" (Goad 1980; Gibson and Hillman 1982). There is also evidence that the main mound was constructed over a filled, 4-m deep borrow pit. Jackson argues this duplication of effort would have been avoided "if the site plan had been conceived at the outset and implemented in a rapid and intensive program of earth-moving."
- **Page**: p. 41
- **Type**: empirical / interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Episodic construction is consistent with seasonal aggregation rather than permanent occupation. Supports the idea that construction was a recurring collective activity, possibly signaling-related.

---

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "While in terms of size the earthworks at Poverty Point are certainly an impressive demonstration of cooperative effort, there is no a priori reason to conclude that they represent a qualitatively different social phenomenon from that emerging elsewhere in eastern North America at about the same time." Circular shell rings on the South Carolina and Georgia coastline date as early as 3000 B.C., and conical mound construction in the Lower Mississippi Valley appears to have commenced by the middle of the third millennium B.C.
- **Page**: pp. 41-42
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Important context. Places Poverty Point monumental construction within a broader pattern rather than treating it as anomalous, which is relevant to understanding signaling as a general adaptive strategy.

---

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: Jackson argues that "at the root of much of what I am suggesting to be the present interpretive uncertainty is poor control over the internal chronology of the period. Few of the over one hundred known sites have been adequately dated." A widely shared assumption of a short "florescent" phase during which major construction and commerce occurred has been maintained, but "dates from other sites tend not to support the short 'florescence' model."
- **Page**: pp. 42, 44
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: If the Poverty Point period was longer than 200 years, construction could have been more episodic and the population per episode smaller, consistent with seasonal aggregation.

---

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: Jackson argues that much of the variation in clay object (P.P.O.) types at Poverty Point and related sites "is not a function of chronology." The shape of objects and combinations in which they were used "reflects primarily functional considerations." Gibson's (1975:215) cooking experiments indicated that "the shape of the objects used serves to regulate the intensity and duration of cooking temperatures."
- **Page**: pp. 47-48
- **Type**: empirical / interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: P.P.O. variation as functional rather than chronological has implications for how we interpret site contemporaneity and seasonal use.

---

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: If the bulk of the population at Poverty Point were transient, "their stay at the site was brief, and their primary purpose was to participate in the exchange of readily transportable commodities, then carrying pots for cooking or manufacturing them at the site may have been an inefficient luxury. The alternative, fashioning clay balls and using them for baking, may reflect a more expedient cooking technology applied in a temporary visitation."
- **Page**: p. 52
- **Type**: speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (acknowledged as speculative)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to aggregation model. The dominance of clay cooking objects over pottery at Poverty Point is consistent with temporary/seasonal occupation rather than permanent settlement.

---

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "The allocation of labor in public works projects does not necessarily imply a redistributive function of a ruling elite, since such projects could be undertaken within the context of periodic ritually compelled participation by a large segment of society (or by members of several otherwise independent social groups)."
- **Page**: p. 61
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (based on comparative examples)
- **Assessment**: Central to the signaling framework. Jackson explicitly identifies the possibility that monumental construction could result from periodic participation by independent groups, not top-down direction.

---

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: Johnson (1980) examined intersite raw material type and artifact variability in the Yazoo Basin and demonstrated that "access to exotic materials depended on distance from the source, rather than proximity to the proposed regional redistributive center (Jaketown)." Status-defining articles' abundance at sites was not conditioned by site size. Johnson concludes "there is little evidence that a chiefdom level of society was attained in the Yazoo Basin."
- **Page**: pp. 64-65
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Strong evidence against a redistributive chiefdom model. Distance-dependent access to exotics is more consistent with a network exchange system that could operate through individual signaling decisions.

---

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: Ford and Webb (1956) assumed that a permanent residential population at Poverty Point implied a stable productive agricultural base, "probably involving maize." Webb (1968:319, 1970b:32) later reiterated this view. Gibson (1973:314ff) offered an alternative, arguing that the natural abundance of the Lower Mississippi Valley, "specifically, of the juxtaposition of the Macon Ridge Pleistocene terrace and the active floodplain, could be efficiently exploited through a broad spectrum strategy."
- **Page**: pp. 65-66
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (Gibson's ecological model)
- **Assessment**: Gibson's ecotone exploitation argument is relevant to the model's ecological diversity hypothesis. The juxtaposition of terrace and floodplain resources is central to understanding why Poverty Point is located where it is.

---

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: Thomas and Campbell (1978:274), relying primarily on plant remains, "suggest that the Poverty Point components were occupied during the fall and winter. They suggest that the nut masts on Macon Ridge may have provided a suitably productive resource to promote a seasonal aggregation of people, which during the middle ranges of the Poverty Point Period, swelled beyond the confines of the Poverty Point earthworks."
- **Page**: pp. 73-74
- **Type**: empirical / interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly supports seasonal aggregation model. Fall-winter occupation tied to nut masts is consistent with an aggregation triggered by seasonal resource availability.

---

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: Systematic flotation from Poverty Point features produced four nut and ten fruit, seed, or bean taxa. "Hickory nutshells and acorn shells and meats constituted the major portion of the samples." Hickory nuts were identified from 30 of 35 samples, acorn parts from 21 samples. "A greater number of acorn parts were identified (7778 versus 3712 hickory nutshell fragments)." Only one specimen each of Polygonum sp. and Chenopodium sp. were identified, suggesting "a minor role at best in the diet."
- **Page**: p. 68
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (quantified archaeobotanical data)
- **Assessment**: Key subsistence data. Heavy reliance on hickory and acorn with negligible evidence for cultigens supports a hunter-gatherer subsistence model, not agriculture. The nut-dominated assemblage is consistent with fall-season occupation.

---

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: "The Poverty Point fauna is dominated by fish." Faunal materials from Poverty Point contexts at localities on the periphery included primarily freshwater drum (17 and 15 fragments at two localities), catfish (1 and 22), and mud-musk turtle (3 and 9, plus 4 and 10 probable). Only 552 bones were associated with Poverty Point components, of which only 141 could be identified taxonomically.
- **Page**: pp. 70, 72-73
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (small sample, preservation issues with calcined bone)
- **Assessment**: Fish-dominated faunal assemblage, combined with nut-dominated botanical remains, points to a mixed aquatic/terrestrial foraging strategy consistent with ecotone positioning.

---

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: Woodiel's (1981) excavation of the plaza midden produced floral and faunal patterns similar to Thomas and Campbell (1978). "Hickory, walnut, and acorn shell fragments, as well as persimmon, grape, and honey locust, comprised the plant food remains." No tropical cultigens or weedy plants were recovered. The few animal bones identified were of turtle and freshwater fish taxa.
- **Page**: p. 74
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Consistent with claim 24. No cultigens found in the plaza midden further supports a non-agricultural subsistence base.

---

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: Jackson defines sedentary settlement systems, following Rafferty (1985:115) and Reed (1977), as "those in which at least part of the population remains at the same location throughout of the year." He modifies this definition "to include the provision that the overall adaptive system of sedentary groups is one that resolves problems of environmental variability within the context of permanent settlement locations."
- **Page**: pp. 82-83
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (definitional, well-sourced)
- **Assessment**: Important for distinguishing between true sedentism and seasonal aggregation. Under this definition, seasonal aggregation at Poverty Point would not qualify as sedentism.

---

### Claim 28

- **Statement**: "Sedentarization, referring to increasing settlement permanence, does not necessarily produce a uni-directional reduction in overall mobility, but rather changes the kind of mobility patterns followed. Movements that are part of logistically organized procurement activities may actually increase as a consequence of a reduction in the frequency of residential moves."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The distinction between residential and logistical mobility is relevant to interpreting the Poverty Point settlement system. A shift toward logistical mobility from a base camp is compatible with the aggregation model.

---

### Claim 29

- **Statement**: "Settlement permanence suggests conditions under which alternatives for community location are reduced. Specifically, responses to resource fluctuations do not or cannot involve settlement movement." This may be a consequence of demographic conditions, environmental structure, or "an effort to manage risks produced by the interaction of these variables."
- **Page**: p. 92
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The connection between settlement permanence and constrained locational choices is relevant to understanding why Poverty Point occupied a specific ecotone location.

---

### Claim 30

- **Statement**: "In the absence of independently pursued locational responses to periodic shortfalls, it is predictable that social relations extending beyond the local groups must provide the 'safety net' to mediate resource variability." The archaeological record of the latter half of the Archaic in eastern North America "simultaneously provides evidence for increasing settlement permanence and social integration."
- **Page**: pp. 93-94
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Highly relevant to the signaling model. Jackson argues that as mobility decreases, social networks replace residential movement as the primary mechanism for coping with resource uncertainty. This is precisely the role that costly signaling could fill, facilitating the formation and maintenance of cooperative networks.

---

### Claim 31

- **Statement**: Jackson argues that as a consequence of sedentarization, intergroup networks "become extended by the formation of alliances through, for instance, marriage, trade, or ritual relations" while "intragroup relations become more formalized through kinship, sodality, and ritual systems." There is pressure to develop "social and ideological sanctions" to manage inter-group stress and select decision-makers.
- **Page**: pp. 95-96
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the signaling framework. The formalization of inter-group relations through exchange and ritual is the context in which costly signaling would operate.

---

### Claim 32

- **Statement**: "An important consequence of the transition toward hunter-gatherer sedentism is the extension of the social and ritual networks as a fundamental means of coping with environmental and social variability." The expectation for regularization, intensification, and increase in scale of social interactions is predicted on the basis of: "(1) the necessity of maintaining social ties that extend geographically beyond the spatial extent of resource shortfalls (Minnis 1981); and (2) the necessity of maintaining these ties during years when they are not needed to mediate shortages (O'Shea 1981:171)."
- **Page**: pp. 96, 98
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Central theoretical claim. The need to maintain social networks even when not immediately needed for risk-buffering mirrors the logic of costly signaling as ongoing investment in social capital.

---

### Claim 33

- **Statement**: O'Shea (1981) suggested that "converting food value into durable goods provides the necessary solution" to the problem of direct food exchange being unreliable during simultaneous shortfalls. "Social storage is accomplished through exchange transactions which convert food into physical tokens which may later be re-exchanged for foodstuffs" (O'Shea 1981:169). Increasing reliance on conversion of durable items into food "results in the regularization of exchange relations."
- **Page**: pp. 99-100
- **Type**: interpretive (Jackson synthesizing O'Shea)
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The concept of "social storage" through exchange of durable goods parallels the signaling model's mechanism. Exotic goods at Poverty Point could function as a form of social storage, with their exchange creating obligations redeemable during shortfalls.

---

### Claim 34

- **Statement**: "Ritual activities, then, are suggested to take on a more primary role in long-term subsistence success. This role includes not only the direct redistribution of foodstuffs among local kin-related communities, but also the regularized opportunity to forge and validate more socially distant ties through the incorporation of exchange activities within the context of solidarity enhancing ceremonies and festivities."
- **Page**: p. 104
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the aggregation and signaling model. Jackson sees ritual activities (which could include monumental construction) as having a primary role in subsistence success through network maintenance, paralleling the signaling framework.

---

### Claim 35

- **Statement**: "Items of greater material and symbolic value may become increasingly important in the context of the more formalized interactions of socially distant individuals and groups, creating a demand for exotic materials or goods" (citing Braun and Plog 1982). "Moreover, the ritual activities themselves may stimulate demand for exotically derived paraphernalia" (citing Ford 1972b).
- **Page**: p. 104
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Explains why exotic goods become increasingly important in the context of inter-group interactions, consistent with a signaling interpretation where costly goods demonstrate commitment and capacity.

---

### Claim 36

- **Statement**: Ethnographic data on aggregations among hunter-gatherers: Flood (1980:81) noted gatherings of 500-700 individuals lasting as long as two months in the Alpine regions of southeastern Australia. Other multiband gatherings estimated at 1000 individuals were recorded during eel migrations in the Lake Bolan region (Lourandos 1977). In southeastern Australia, cooperative maintenance of a 6-hectare drainage system was required (Lourandos 1985:408).
- **Page**: p. 102
- **Type**: empirical (ethnographic data)
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides ethnographic scale analogies for the kind of seasonal aggregation proposed for Poverty Point. Gatherings of 500-1000 people for resource exploitation are documented among hunter-gatherers.

---

### Claim 37

- **Statement**: "Among mobile hunter-gatherers, ritual aggregations irregularly take place under favorable conditions that permit groups to cluster for a period of time (Yengoyan 1972:6). Aggregations of this sort, therefore, are responsive to environmental potential. They serve the important functions of facilitating opportunities to mark solidarity through collective activities, and to reinforce social and ideological constructs, as well as to exchange information, goods, and personnel."
- **Page**: p. 103
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (well-sourced from ethnographic literature)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the aggregation model. Aggregations among mobile groups serve multiple functions (solidarity marking, exchange, information sharing) that parallel the proposed functions of Poverty Point gatherings.

---

### Claim 38

- **Statement**: "In contrast, regularity of calendrically defined rituals is characteristic of settled societies." Among settled groups, "rituals create a regularized opportunity for counteracting potential shortages" (Ford 1972a; Bean 1972). "In contrast to responding to high resource availability as is suggested for mobile populations, rituals create a regularized opportunity for counteracting potential shortages."
- **Page**: pp. 103-104
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The shift from irregular to regular ritual aggregation during sedentarization is relevant to modeling the frequency and scale of aggregation events at Poverty Point.

---

### Claim 39

- **Statement**: Jackson notes that trade relations extending beyond social boundaries "may provide a high order response to severe subsistence stress, by creating places for the families of trading partners to go" (Minnis 1981; Waddell 1975). These contacts "may involve expeditions to each other's territories or meeting within the context of intertribal trade fairs" (Spielmann 1982).
- **Page**: p. 101
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (ethnographic basis)
- **Assessment**: Exchange relations as an insurance mechanism during severe subsistence stress is directly compatible with the signaling model's mechanism for risk-pooling.

---

### Claim 40

- **Statement**: In the Lower Mississippi Valley floodplain environment, "technology is likely to be greatly constrained by the lack of immediately available lithic raw materials." For groups living at sites like J.W. Copes, "direct acquisition of stone would have required procurement forays to sources at distances of 50 km or more." This made raw material acquisition "a more important aspect of the logistical procurement system" and exchange for stone tools "would have become increasingly important."
- **Page**: p. 116
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The lithic raw material poverty of the Lower Mississippi Valley helps explain why long-distance exchange was not merely a luxury but a functional necessity, providing context for why exotic goods could serve as costly signals.

---

### Claim 41

- **Statement**: Altschul (1978) "suggested the possibility that the dichotomy of terrace edge and floodplain settlement could reflect seasonal differences in settlement location."
- **Page**: p. 67
- **Type**: interpretive (Jackson reporting Altschul)
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (presented as one of several alternatives)
- **Assessment**: Seasonal settlement differences between ecological zones are consistent with the aggregation model's ecotone positioning.

---

### Claim 42

- **Statement**: Geomorphological evidence suggests that "at the time of the Poverty Point occupation a rather substantial lake was located in the lowlands through which Bayou Macon now flows" (Gibson 1984). The deposits above the lake-edge sediment "consist of basket-loaded clays interspersed with midden and occupation debris."
- **Page**: p. 34
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The presence of a substantial lake adjacent to the Poverty Point Site during occupation would have significantly enhanced the local resource base, particularly for aquatic resources, and is relevant to understanding site location choice.

---

### Claim 43

- **Statement**: Thomas and Campbell (1978) investigated the periphery of the main center and revealed "a variety of site types related to the middle ranges of the Poverty Point Period. Three small, but intensively occupied linear sites were situated along the bluff edge north of the earthworks." Activity specialization was suggested by assemblages, with one locale appearing to be a steatite vessel processing site and another a locus of stone tool manufacture from exotic white and grey cherts.
- **Page**: pp. 35-36
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Specialized activity areas adjacent to the main site are consistent with aggregation-period task specialization, where different groups or activities occupied distinct areas.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited

## Summary Assessment

Jackson's dissertation is a foundational critique of the Poverty Point chiefdom model and a thorough examination of hunter-gatherer sedentism in the Lower Mississippi Valley. His central argument is that the assumptions underlying interpretations of Poverty Point as a complexly organized, class-structured society have not been adequately established: the evidence for social ranking is weak, the chronology is poorly controlled, and the subsistence data (dominated by hickory nuts, acorns, and fish) do not support the agricultural base that was originally thought necessary for monumental construction. Jackson develops a theoretical framework in which sedentary hunter-gatherers cope with environmental variability through the extension and regularization of social and ritual networks, including exchange of exotic goods as a form of "social storage" against future resource shortfalls. This framework aligns closely with the signaling model's core mechanism: seasonal aggregation, monumental construction as collective ritual activity, and exotic goods exchange as network maintenance. Key empirical findings include plant remains suggesting fall-winter occupation (Claims 23-24), the absence of cultigens (Claim 26), episodic rather than continuous construction (Claim 15), and the wide distribution of exotic goods not restricted to elite contexts (Claims 13, 21). These findings collectively support an interpretation of Poverty Point as a seasonal aggregation center rather than a permanently occupied chiefdom capital.
