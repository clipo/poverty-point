## Claims

### Claim 1: Definition of Culture

- **Statement**: "The Poverty Point culture is a prehistoric manifestation in the southern United States, transitional in nature, that participates in the American Formative shift from Archaic band existence to a village-regional center-great ceremonial center complex with ranked societal organization on a chiefdom level (Gibson, 1974b)."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Webb frames Poverty Point as transitional between Archaic and Formative stages, attributing to it ranked societal organization at the chiefdom level following Gibson's model. This characterization is central to understanding whether Poverty Point represents emergent complexity among hunter-gatherers or a fundamentally different social formation. The chiefdom attribution is contested in more recent literature.

### Claim 2: Environmental Setting and Ecological Zones

- **Statement**: "The environmental setting of the upriver sites combines the general southeastern coastal plain context of oak-hickory-dominated uplands with the river valley ecological zones. The latter include large streams, broad natural levees, backwater swamps, or mud flats and shallow cutoff lakes."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the multiple ecological zones available to Poverty Point inhabitants, directly relevant to the ecotone diversity hypothesis in the project framework. The combination of upland and riverine environments would have provided diverse and partially independent resource bases.

### Claim 3: Seasonal Resource Availability

- **Statement**: "The annual cycle on the southeastern coastal plain exhibits seasonal characteristics: fall and winter provide acorns and other nuts, game animals, turkey and pigeon, oysters, and conch; spring and summer offer roots and berries, the alligator, and most varieties of fish and crustaceans."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the seasonal structure of resource availability. The seasonal patterning is directly relevant to modeling aggregation dynamics, as different seasons would have offered different cooperation benefits and foraging opportunities.

### Claim 4: Diverse Floral Food Resources

- **Statement**: Gibson (1970a) "has collated the reports of ethnohistorians and native Louisiana informants to show the great variety of floral food resources in the Lower Mississippi Valley. Outstanding examples are wild rice, chufa (Cyperus esculentus), cane, palmetto seeds, lotus or yoncopin (Nelumbo lutea), cattails (Typha latifolia), persimmon, and many roots and tubers. Nuts include hickory, walnut, chestnut (chinquapin), pecan, and acorns of many oak varieties."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Details the rich diversity of plant foods available in the Lower Mississippi Valley, supporting the argument that hunter-gatherer subsistence in this region could sustain substantial populations without agriculture.

### Claim 5: Large Faunal Resources

- **Statement**: "Of the larger animals, deer, bear and beaver were numerous; alligators and turtles were ubiquitous. The large 'rough' fish of major rivers afforded generous quantities of protein food, for species such as catfish, buffalo fish, and garfish weigh from 10 kg to more than 45 kg."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the availability of large protein sources. The very large fish (up to 45 kg) are particularly relevant to the aggregation model because communal fishing operations (e.g., weirs) would have provided returns to scale.

### Claim 6: Migratory Waterfowl as Seasonal Resource

- **Statement**: "And most important, millions of migratory waterfowl, ducks, geese, swans, pigeons, filled the Mississippi central flyway, cutoff lakes, and the coastal areas. Coots, gallinules, and wood ducks were year-round inhabitants."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents a massive seasonal resource pulse from migratory waterfowl along the Mississippi flyway. This seasonal abundance could have been a key trigger for aggregation, as collective fowling (using hematite-weighted bolas) would have provided returns to scale during migration seasons.

### Claim 7: Chronology and Dating

- **Statement**: Radiocarbon dates from Poverty Point site range from 1050 +/- 90 B.C. (TX-680) to 865 +/- 255 B.C. (L.N.S.C.-73-057), with a thermoluminescence date of 750 B.C. Teoc Creek dates range from 1700 +/- 160 B.C. (M-2395) to 1070 +/- 150 B.C. (M-2394). Claiborne dates are 1150 +/- 110 B.C. (I-3705) and 1520 +/- 160 B.C. (TX-1404), with a thermoluminescence date of 650 B.C. Dragline dates are 2550 +/- 70 B.C. (U. Ga. 2280) and 2825 +/- 90 B.C. (U. Ga. 2281).
- **Page**: p. 3 (Table 1)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Key chronological data compiled from multiple sources. The dates indicate Poverty Point culture began on the Gulf Coast and in the Mississippi River basin by 2000 B.C., was fully developed and widely distributed between 1200 and 1000 B.C., and declined by 800-500 B.C. The Dragline site dates of ca. 2550-2825 B.C. are notably early.

### Claim 8: Poverty Point Culture Began by 2000 B.C.

- **Statement**: "The indications are that Poverty Point culture began on the Gulf Coast and in the Mississippi River basin by 2000 B.C., and that it was fully developed and widely distributed between 1200 and 1000 B.C."
- **Page**: p. 3
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Provides a summary temporal framework for the culture's development. The 800-year span from inception to florescence is relevant to modeling the emergence and growth of the signaling system.

### Claim 9: Rapid Growth of Known Sites

- **Statement**: "By 1960, only five sites had been discovered; today more than a hundred are known, together with an equal number of related sites."
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents that by 1982 more than 100 Poverty Point sites and an equal number of related sites were known, indicating a widespread regional phenomenon rather than an isolated occurrence.

### Claim 10: Site Location Ecological Pattern

- **Statement**: "Most Poverty Point sites are found in four kinds of settings: on terraces or old land masses overlooking major river courses, relict or active; on levees of major river channels, generally relict; at river-lake junctions; and on the Gulf Coast, at estuaries or on old lands in marsh areas. The ecological constant is the location of all sites in contact zones, which were strategic ecotones that permitted exploitation of combinations of upland forest, lowland swamp, riverine, lacustrine, or paludal environments, with diverse plant and animal resources."
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: This is a critical claim for the project. Webb identifies ecotone positioning as the "ecological constant" across all Poverty Point sites, directly supporting the ecological diversity hypothesis in the CLAUDE.md framework. The strategic positioning at environmental boundaries maximized access to diverse resource types.

### Claim 11: Settlement Hierarchy

- **Statement**: "The various sizes of Poverty Point sites, therefore, seem to be consistent with a settlement pattern consisting of a great ceremonial center, lesser regional centers, modest hamlets, seasonal or activity camps, and single family abodes."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Webb posits a five-tier settlement hierarchy, with Poverty Point at the apex. This hierarchical model implies centralized organization and redistribution, relevant to evaluating whether the site system reflects signaling-based cooperation or centralized political control.

### Claim 12: Village Construction at Poverty Point

- **Statement**: "The village construction at Poverty Point consists of 6 concentric elevated ridges, each forming five-eighths of an octagon, with aisles left at the angles."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Core descriptive claim about the site's most distinctive architectural feature. The geometric precision of the ridge construction is relevant to evaluating labor investment and coordinated planning.

### Claim 13: Outer Ridge Diameter and Ridge Dimensions

- **Statement**: "The diameter of the outer ridge (Fig. 7a) is 1208 m (3964'); that of the innermost ridge is 594 m (1950'). The north-south ridges, therefore, average 51.2 m (168') wide from midswale to midswale. The total length of ridge construction is approximately 11.2 km (7 mi)."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Precise measurements of the ridge system. The total ridge construction length of 11.2 km is a key metric for estimating labor investment. These dimensions establish the massive scale of the earthwork construction.

### Claim 14: Rapid, Planned Construction

- **Statement**: "There is evidence that construction was rapid, according to an integrated plan, and not by accretion over a prolonged period."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: If construction was indeed rapid and planned rather than incremental, this has major implications for the signaling model. Rapid, coordinated construction of earthworks on this scale would require substantial collective labor mobilization, consistent with costly signaling through monumental architecture.

### Claim 15: Mound A Dimensions

- **Statement**: Moore's "surveyor Miller determined the height of Mound A to be 21.3 m (70'), with basal dimensions of 207.3 m (680') north to south and 210.3 m (690') east to west, including the platform. Measurements of Mound B were 6.6 m (21.5') high and 59.4 m (195'), basal diameter."
- **Page**: p. 15
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Key measurements of the two major mounds. Mound A at 21.3 m height is one of the largest pre-Columbian mounds in North America at its time, representing enormous labor investment. Mound B at 6.6 m is a substantial conical mound.

### Claim 16: Motley Mound Dimensions

- **Statement**: "Motley Mound, and the cone now known as Mound B. He was the first to state that the great mounds were constructions, not geological remnants. ... Mound was 15.5 m (51') high and 170.7 m (560') east to west, 121.9 m (400') north to south, basal dimensions."
- **Page**: p. 15
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the Motley Mound as another major construction, 15.5 m high, located 2.4 km (1.5 mi) south of Mound A. The presence of multiple monumental mounds strengthens the case for coordinated, large-scale construction effort.

### Claim 17: Mound A Platform and Solar Orientation

- **Statement**: Ford (1954) "stressed the geometric accuracy of the construction and the compass (solar) orientation of the village and mounds. The ramp and projecting platform of Mound A, directed toward the center of the village, are within 7 degrees of due east, those of Motley are similarly near due south."
- **Page**: p. 15
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents solar/cardinal orientation of the major constructions. The geometric precision and consistent orientation suggest deliberate planning and astronomical awareness, relevant to the argument that construction reflected coordinated community investment.

### Claim 18: Poverty Point Site Situated on Macon Ridge Escarpment

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point site is situated (Fig. 8, 9) on the edge of the Macon Ridge escarpment, 4.5 m to 6.1 m (15' to 20') above the Mississippi flood plain to the east. Macon Ridge is an early alluvial fan of the Arkansas River, trimmed along its eastern face by Fisk's (1944) C Stage of the Mississippi, and, later, by the Arkansas Stage H."
- **Page**: p. 16
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the precise geological and topographic setting. The elevated position on the Macon Ridge escarpment, overlooking the Mississippi flood plain, provided both defensive advantage and access to diverse ecological zones, directly relevant to the ecotone positioning argument.

### Claim 19: Extensive Habitation on Ridges

- **Statement**: "Hearths, pits, and individual post molds were found, but no structures were identified. There was no doubt that there had been extensive occupation of the ridges' tops."
- **Page**: p. 16
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms that the ridges served as habitation areas, with evidence of domestic activities (hearths, pits, post molds) but no formal structures. The absence of identified house structures despite intensive occupation is notable.

### Claim 20: Mound A Platform Dimensions

- **Statement**: "Terraces and ramps were identified, and the Mound A platform was shown to be 7 m (23') high and 73.1 x 91.4 m (240' x 300'), in basal dimension."
- **Page**: p. 16
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Updated measurements of the Mound A platform from the 1955 investigations. The platform alone is a massive construction requiring substantial coordinated labor.

### Claim 21: Year-Round Occupation Suggested

- **Statement**: "The author believes that studies in the village center will eventually show year-round occupation to some extent and suggests that, at all times, special occasions like ceremonies, festivals, market days, and trading activities, probably combined, as seen at many native Latin-American cities like Chichicastenango, swelled the population considerably."
- **Page**: p. 19
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Webb speculates about year-round occupation with periodic population increases for special events. This is directly relevant to the seasonal aggregation model in the project framework, though Webb favors permanent occupation with seasonal swelling rather than seasonal aggregation of otherwise dispersed groups.

### Claim 22: Population Estimates from Thomas and Campbell

- **Statement**: Thomas and Campbell "described five settlement types of the Poverty Point period at and around the site: (1) the arcuate or semioctagonal arrangement of the center; (2) linear strips along the terrace edge, with almost continuous occupation northward from the site to a point about 1 km beyond the Motley Mound; (3) small habitation sites situated on topographic highs; (4) small temporary occupations or activity sites, at distances of 2 to 15 km from the village; and (5) mounds without habitation evidences, as exemplified by Motley Mound."
- **Page**: p. 18-19
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents five distinct settlement types in and around the site, suggesting a complex spatial organization that extends well beyond the geometric earthworks. The variety of site types supports a model of differentiated activities and occupations.

### Claim 23: Postulated Town Size

- **Statement**: "A postulated town of 600 houses and a population of 4000 to 5000 persons at the site, in an Archaic milieu that elsewhere supported only small groups, appeared inexplicable."
- **Page**: p. 13
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Webb cites the earlier Ford and Webb (1956) population estimate of 4,000-5,000 persons with 600 houses, while noting it appeared inexplicable for an Archaic context. These numbers should be treated with caution as they predate modern methods of population estimation. However, they indicate that even early researchers recognized the anomalous scale of the site.

### Claim 24: Evidence for Regional Interaction and Redistribution

- **Statement**: "Answers to this 'enigma' are now at hand. They are: (1) evidence of a widespread population in the valleys of five major river systems and near their Gulf Coast effluents; (2) an organization with regional centers and satellite occupations, conceived at a chiefdom level (Gibson, 1974b) of sociopolitical structure, in a half-dozen areas around Poverty Point site (Fig. 2), on the Ouachita River (Fig. 4), in central Louisiana (Fig. 5), on the eastern and western flanks of the lower delta (Fig. 6), and in the Yazoo Basin (Fig. 3) in two or three clusters."
- **Page**: p. 13
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Webb summarizes the evidence for a multi-center regional system centered on Poverty Point, with satellite sites organized in distinct geographical clusters. This distributional pattern is consistent with a hub-and-spoke exchange network relevant to the signaling model.

### Claim 25: Considerable Trade in Favored Materials

- **Statement**: "A considerable trade for favored materials existed during the Late Archaic period in the Southeast."
- **Page**: p. 13
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes that long-distance trade predates Poverty Point, providing the context within which the Poverty Point exchange network developed and expanded.

### Claim 26: Expansion of Trade Contacts

- **Statement**: "As Poverty Point culture developed, trade contacts expanded rapidly, first in lithics for pragmatic use, then in a wide variety of materials for esoteric, as well as mundane, purposes."
- **Page**: p. 13-14
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Webb argues for a temporal sequence in trade expansion, from utilitarian to esoteric/ceremonial materials. If accurate, this trajectory is consistent with a signaling model where practical exchange networks were co-opted for or supplemented by the acquisition of costly signal goods.

### Claim 27: No Burials Attributable to Poverty Point Until Recently

- **Statement**: "Until recently, no burials attributable to Poverty Point period had been found, even at near-coastal sites where faunal remains were well preserved."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The near-absence of identified Poverty Point burials is significant for understanding ritual behavior and social organization. Subsequent discoveries at Cowpen Slough (flexed burials and cremation) expanded this picture.

### Claim 28: Burials at Cowpen Slough

- **Statement**: "In 1977, William S. Baker discovered human bone fragments, confirmed by Neitzel and Webb, eroding from the transected Poverty Point midden at Cowpen Slough site (Fig. 5), west of Larto Lake. Subsequent excavations demonstrated three tightly flexed adult burials, the skull of another, and a pit containing parts of a cremated juvenile and cranial fragments of an unburned adult."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents some of the first identified Poverty Point burials. The combination of flexed inhumation and cremation at the same site indicates diverse mortuary practices. The presence of burial offerings (p. 12: "Placements of choice objects have been found and imply burial offerings; these include a double row of 40 copper beads at Poverty Point, fine projectile points or plummets at Poverty Point and Claiborne, and a cache of steatite vessels at Claiborne") is relevant to signaling through mortuary ritual.

### Claim 29: Burial Offerings with Exotic Materials

- **Statement**: "Placements of choice objects have been found and imply burial offerings; these include a double row of 40 copper beads at Poverty Point, fine projectile points or plummets at Poverty Point and Claiborne, and a cache of steatite vessels at Claiborne."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents the association of exotic and high-value objects with burials. A row of 40 copper beads is a substantial exotic goods deposit. The deposition of costly imported materials in burial contexts is consistent with signaling status through conspicuous disposal.

### Claim 30: Earth Oven Cooking and Baked Clay Objects

- **Statement**: "The association of Poverty Point baked clay balls and earth ovens is firmly established in dozens of locations at excavated sites. The earth oven pits are 30 cm to 60 cm (12''-24'') in diameter and 23 cm to 50 cm (9''-20'') deep. They often contain a dozen to more than a hundred baked clay objects and sometimes ash or charcoal."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the primary function of the hallmark Poverty Point baked clay objects as cooking stones for earth ovens. The widespread, standardized association across many sites confirms this was a systematic cooking technology.

### Claim 31: Approximately 500,000 Baked Clay Objects at Poverty Point

- **Statement**: "Allowing for diminished densities at the periphery, the projection for the entire midden approximates 500,000 baked clay objects."
- **Page**: p. 25
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Extrapolated estimate from controlled excavation at the Terral Lewis site (a small satellite), but the same method applied to Poverty Point would yield even larger numbers. At the main site, the total was based on study of 70,000 artifacts from eighteen collections (p. 37), with six major types constituting 86% to 87% of the 29,000 classified objects (p. 37). The sheer volume of production is relevant to modeling labor investment and the intensity of occupation.

### Claim 32: Total Artifact Count and Collection Size

- **Statement**: "Webb's report (1968) of the cultural content of Poverty Point site was based on 70,000 artifacts studied by him and Ford, from eighteen collections. Subsequently, some 22,000 objects collected from the site by Carl Alexander and Webb, most with intrasite provenience designation, were analyzed by Webb, Gibson, and Gregory. Artifacts from other sites of the culture bring the total to approximately 120,000 objects."
- **Page**: p. 37
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the massive scale of the artifact assemblage: approximately 120,000 objects from across the culture, with roughly 92,000 from the Poverty Point site alone. This enormous collection provides the empirical basis for Webb's analyses of artifact distributions and trade networks.

### Claim 33: Classification of Artifact Assemblage

- **Statement**: "Classification of the more than 90,000 collected artifacts shows that 32% of all collected artifacts are lamellar cores, blades, and blade tools; 22% are Poverty Point baked clay objects; 25% are conchoidally flaked core and flake tools and byproducts, half of them projectile points; and 5% to 6% each are stone vessel sherds and raw materials. The remaining 10% incorporates plummets, ground stone tools, problematicals, beads and other lapidary items, potsherds, pipes, figurines, copper, bone and antler objects, and miscellaneous artifacts."
- **Page**: p. 65
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides the overall composition of the assemblage. The dominance of microflint blade technology (32%) and baked clay objects (22%) defines the technological signature of the culture. The 10% category of exotic and ornamental objects (plummets, lapidary items, copper, etc.) represents the material most relevant to the signaling argument.

### Claim 34: Most Materials Imported from Distant Sources

- **Statement**: "It should be noted that all lithic material that the alluvial soils of the lower valley are devoid of stones, that even 'local' gravel sources were not nearer than 25 miles from the site, and that most material was imported from sources 100 to 700 miles distant."
- **Page**: p. 53
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Critical claim for the signaling argument. The complete absence of local stone meant that all lithic materials, not just exotics, required transport. Even "local" gravels were 40+ km away, and most raw materials came from 160 to 1,120 km distant. This fundamentally changes the cost structure: all stone tool manufacture required participation in exchange networks.

### Claim 35: Source Map of Raw Materials

- **Statement**: "The extensive trade network is illustrated in Figure 32, which shows the known or assumed sources of many imperishable materials found at Poverty Point. Sources of other items have not been identified, and undoubtedly there was equally extensive trade in perishables such as salt, furs and hides, feathers, wood products, shells, and exotic foods. Much of this transport must have been by water over the central valley network."
- **Page**: p. 72
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Webb argues that the documented trade in imperishable materials represents only a fraction of total exchange, with perishable goods also circulating. The emphasis on water transport through the central valley network supports the model of Poverty Point as a hub site at a strategic river confluence.

### Claim 36: Plummets of Hematite and Magnetite

- **Statement**: "Plummets of polished hematite and magnetite (Fig. 27) were unmistakably important in Poverty Point activities. The presence and large numbers of these objects at nearly all sites of the culture (Table 10, 18) bespeak utilitarian rather than ornamental purpose. There are 2790 plummets and fragments from Poverty Point, approximately 3% of all collected objects. Fewer than 100 were of materials other than the heavy iron ores, 78% of which are hematite and 22% magnetite."
- **Page**: p. 56
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents 2,790 plummets at Poverty Point, with 78% hematite and 22% magnetite. The sheer number and the use of heavy iron ores sourced from distant locations make these significant for the signaling model. Webb interprets them as utilitarian (bola weights for fowling), but their prevalence and the labor invested in sourcing, shaping, perforating, and decorating them are also consistent with a signaling function.

### Claim 37: Plummet Dimensions

- **Statement**: "Plummets range from 3.2 cm to 12.7 cm long, averaging 6.7 cm; from 2.1 cm to 5.2 cm wide, averaging 3.3 cm; the average weight in a sample is 150 g."
- **Page**: p. 56
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides precise metric data for plummet dimensions and weight. The average weight of 150 g is relevant to evaluating their function as bola weights or as display objects.

### Claim 38: Decorated Plummets with Elaborate Designs

- **Statement**: "Sixteen are decorated with incisions, drilled pits (Fig. 27:20, 21), gouges or punctations, and designs executed by engraving, intaglio, or bas-relief techniques. ... A remarkable group from Poverty Point, in the Lemley collection at the Gilcrease Museum (Tulsa) (Fig. 27:15-19, 23, 26) shows stylized human, owl, deer mask, serpent, and webbed feet, often in reversed duplication on opposite sides of the plummet, beautifully symmetrical in execution."
- **Page**: p. 56
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents elaborate artistic decoration on a subset of plummets. The high skill evident in the symmetrical stylized designs (human figures, owls, deer masks, serpents) on imported stone is consistent with these objects serving as costly signals of artisan skill and access to exotic materials.

### Claim 39: Plummets as Bola Weights

- **Statement**: "The general belief is that plummets were weights for bolas used for capturing small animals or waterfowl, as suggested by Ford and Webb (1956), although use as net weights or ornaments has been proposed."
- **Page**: p. 56
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Webb favors the bola weight interpretation, which would give these objects a practical hunting function. However, the multiple competing interpretations (bola weights, net weights, ornaments) indicate the function is uncertain. The dual practical-plus-signaling interpretation is plausible.

### Claim 40: Steatite Vessels Widespread

- **Statement**: "Vessels or fragments of steatite (Fig. 22) have been found at nine Poverty Point sites in Louisiana, at eleven sites in Mississippi, and at three in Arkansas (Table 18). Sandstone vessel fragments have been found at six of these sites."
- **Page**: p. 44
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the wide distribution of steatite vessels across at least 23 Poverty Point sites. Steatite was sourced from the Appalachian region (see Claim 42), making these vessels significant exotic trade goods.

### Claim 41: Steatite Cache at Poverty Point

- **Statement**: "At Poverty Point site, 2221 steatite fragments came from all sectors and levels of midden, in addition to 2724 fragments in the cache found west of Mound A (p. 15), a total of 4945. One-sixth of these are rim fragments. Several hundred vessels were thought to be represented by the cached fragments, and many more are likely from the general collections."
- **Page**: p. 45
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The cache of 2,724 steatite fragments west of Mound A, plus 2,221 from general midden contexts, totaling 4,945 fragments, represents an enormous investment in exotic material from the Appalachian region. The cache near Mound A could represent a communal storage or ritual deposit.

### Claim 42: Steatite Source Identification

- **Statement**: "Recent mineralogical studies by Alden B. Carpenter, University of Missouri, of steatite vessel fragments from Poverty Point and from several Late Archaic sites in southern Arkansas (submitted by Frank Schambach and Webb) showed that none of the materials was from steatite deposits in Arkansas and confirmed the previous opinion that the source is the southern Appalachians, specifically northeastern Alabama and northwestern Georgia."
- **Page**: p. 44
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Mineralogical analysis confirmed Appalachian (NE Alabama and NW Georgia) as the source for steatite, ruling out closer Arkansas deposits. This confirms transport distances of approximately 900 km, making steatite vessels costly exotic goods.

### Claim 43: Copper Objects at Poverty Point

- **Statement**: From Table 14, there are 155 copper objects at Poverty Point and 7 at Claiborne, including 42 tubular rolled beads, 3 globular or barrel-shaped beads, 4 disc beads, 1 needle or awl, 61 sheet fragments, and various other forms.
- **Page**: p. 62 (Table 14)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents 155 copper objects at Poverty Point, primarily beads and sheet fragments. Native copper from the Great Lakes region (approximately 1,600 km distant) represents one of the longest-distance trade items in the assemblage and a clear exotic material for signaling purposes.

### Claim 44: Galena Abundance and Distribution

- **Statement**: "Galena, in raw crystals or nodules and in manufactured products, is more abundant at Poverty Point and more widespread in other sites of the culture than is copper. There have been 702 galena masses, varying from 30-gram crystals to a nodule of 4430 grams, and 38 finished objects found at Poverty Point site (Table 15)."
- **Page**: p. 60
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents 702 galena masses plus 38 finished objects at Poverty Point, plus occurrences at 16 other sites. The range from small crystals to a 4.43 kg nodule indicates substantial quantities were being transported from distant sources. Galena is more widespread across Poverty Point sites than copper.

### Claim 45: Galena Sources Identified as Potosi, Missouri

- **Statement**: "Of the 22 specimens from Poverty Point site, 16 were allocated to the Potosi deposits in southeastern Missouri, 6 to the Upper Mississippi Valley sources in northern Illinois and Iowa."
- **Page**: p. 62
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Trace element analysis identified the primary galena source as Potosi, Missouri (approximately 800 km distant), with a secondary source in the Upper Mississippi Valley. This sourcing data provides concrete evidence for the long-distance exchange network.

### Claim 46: Lapidary Industry Using Exotic Materials

- **Statement**: "The lapidary industry (Fig. 29; Table 13) at Poverty Point is characterized by a great variety of objects, excellent workmanship, new techniques, and the use of such exotic materials as red jasper, red and green talc, banded slate, quartz, quartzites, hematite, magnetite, limonite, galena, feldspar, amethyst, fluorite in yellow, green, and purple shades, and many unidentified colored or translucent stones. ## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited; claim is stated as fact without support

## Summary Assessment

Webb 1982 is the most comprehensive single-source reference on Poverty Point culture available for the period of its publication. As a 100-page monograph with 32 figures and 18 tables based on decades of fieldwork and analysis of approximately 120,000 artifacts, it provides foundational data on virtually every aspect of the Poverty Point phenomenon relevant to the costly signaling project.

**Key contributions to the signaling model:**

1. **Ecotone positioning**: Webb explicitly identifies "contact zones" and "strategic ecotones" as the ecological constant across all Poverty Point site locations (p. 5), directly supporting the project's ecological diversity hypothesis.

2. **Exchange network scope**: The monograph documents in detail the extraordinary range and volume of exotic materials imported to Poverty Point from sources spanning the central and eastern United States: copper from the Great Lakes (~1,600 km), steatite from the Appalachians (~900 km, confirmed by mineralogical analysis), galena from Potosi, Missouri (~800 km, confirmed by trace element analysis), northern gray flint from Illinois/Indiana, novaculite from the Ouachita Mountains, and numerous other materials. All lithic materials were imported at least 40 km, since the alluvial valley is devoid of stone.

3. **Monumental construction**: Detailed measurements of earthworks (6 concentric ridges, 11.2 km total length, outer diameter 1,208 m; Mound A at 21.3 m height with 207 x 210 m base; Mound B at 6.6 m height), with evidence of rapid, planned construction on solar orientation. The semicircular/horseshoe architectural template was replicated at multiple sites.

4. **Intrasite artifact distribution**: Analysis of 18,727 provenience-designated artifacts reveals spatial patterning consistent with functional and possibly social differentiation: cooking activities in N and S sectors, microflint craft production in SW sector, ceremonial/exotic materials in S sector, and Motley (status?) points concentrated in N sector.

5. **Seasonal aggregation indicators**: Thomas and Campbell's analysis suggests peak population in fall and winter (nut harvest and hunting seasons), which is also proposed as the probable time for construction activities (p. 19). Webb himself suggests year-round occupation with periodic population swelling for ceremonies, festivals, and trading activities (p. 19).

6. **Decline**: Webb documents the simultaneous collapse of construction, trade networks, exotic material acquisition, and ornamental elaboration between 800 and 500 B.C., with no obvious environmental cause (p. 72).

**Limitations**: Webb interprets the evidence through a chiefdom model (following Gibson 1974b), which may overstate the degree of political centralization. His population estimates (4,000-5,000) are acknowledged to be speculative. The chronology has been significantly refined by subsequent radiocarbon work. The assumption of ranked, hierarchical social organization is challenged by more recent interpretations emphasizing egalitarian or heterarchical organization among Poverty Point peoples.
