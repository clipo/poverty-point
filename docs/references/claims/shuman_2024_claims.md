## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "Noise in Holocene paleoclimate reconstructions can hamper the detection of centennial to millennial climate variations and diagnoses of the dynamics involved. This paper uses multiple ensembles of reconstructions to separate signal and noise and determine what, if any, centennial to millennial variations influenced North America during the past 7000 years."
- **Page**: p. 1703 (abstract)
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: N/A (statement of approach)
- **Assessment**: Frames the paper's contribution as a signal-versus-noise problem. The analytical strategy of comparing independent multi-record ensembles at four spatial scales is directly relevant to evaluating the reliability of centennial-to-millennial climate signals during the Poverty Point period.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "In all cases, the potential centennial to millennial variations had small magnitudes. However, at least two patterns of centennial to millennial variability appear evident."
- **Page**: p. 1703 (abstract)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the key finding: cen-mil variability existed but was small in magnitude. For the Poverty Point model, "small" magnitudes of climate variation are still ecologically significant (as demonstrated in the paper) and relevant to environmental uncertainty.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "First, large-scale variations included a prominent Mid-Holocene anomaly from 5600-5000 yr BP that increased mean effective moisture and produced temperature anomalies of different signs in different regions. The changes shifted the north-south temperature gradient in mid-latitude North America with a pattern similar to that of the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO)."
- **Page**: p. 1703 (abstract)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The Mid-Holocene anomaly at 5600-5000 yr BP (3650-3050 BCE) overlaps temporally with the beginning of the Poverty Point period (ca. 1700 BCE / 3650 BP). The NAO-like spatial pattern, with opposing temperature anomalies at different latitudes, means that the climate signal experienced at Poverty Point's latitude (32.6 degrees N) may have differed in sign from the mid-latitude records used in the analysis.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "Second, correlated multi-century (~350 years) variations produce a distinct spectral signature in temperature and hydroclimate records along the western Atlantic margin."
- **Page**: p. 1703 (abstract)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: A ~350-year periodicity in Atlantic-margin hydroclimate would have produced approximately two full cycles during the Poverty Point period (~600 years), contributing to environmental variability experienced by populations in the lower Mississippi Valley.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "Analytical or calibration uncertainties of ~2 degrees C (e.g., Russell et al., 2018; Williams and Shuman, 2008; Martinez-Sosa et al., 2021) often dwarf the expected magnitudes of cen-mil temperature variation during the Holocene (~0.5 degrees C in CCSM3 TRACE simulations; Marsicek et al., 2018; Wan et al., 2019)."
- **Page**: p. 1704
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides a quantitative scale for expected centennial-to-millennial temperature variability: approximately 0.5 degrees C in model simulations. This is relevant for parameterizing sigma in the Poverty Point model. The signal is real but small relative to reconstruction uncertainty, which is why multi-record ensembles are needed to detect it.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "Detrended temperatures vary only 0.25-0.5 degrees C, which is consistent with Common Era fluctuations (PAGES 2k Consortium, 2013). The rarity of significant anomalies affirms the weak signal-to-noise ratio at cen-mil scales."
- **Page**: p. 1709
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantifies the magnitude of centennial-to-millennial temperature variability after removing long-term trends: 0.25-0.5 degrees C. This is a key number for calibrating sigma in the Poverty Point model. While small in absolute terms, variations of this magnitude produced detectable ecological and geomorphic responses.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "A Mid-Holocene wet phase from 5600-4500 yr BP is the most prominent anomaly. It departs significantly from the mean trends in the mid-latitude, central, and northeastern coastal moisture reconstructions (Fig. 4b-d), although the duration only extends from 5500-5000 yr BP along the northeastern coast (Newby et al., 2014)."
- **Page**: p. 1709
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The most prominent centennial-to-millennial anomaly in the record is a wet phase at 5600-4500 yr BP (3650-2550 BCE). The early portion of this wet phase overlaps with the beginning of the Poverty Point period. This anomalous moisture increase would have enhanced aquatic and terrestrial resource productivity in the lower Mississippi Valley, potentially facilitating the aggregation and construction activities at Poverty Point.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: "Additional multi-century departures appear at 900-700 yr BP in the central moisture ensemble (Fig. 4c) and at 4400-4200 and 3400-3100 yr BP in the northeastern coast (Fig. 4d)."
- **Page**: p. 1709
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The 3400-3100 yr BP departure in the northeastern coastal record (ca. 1450-1150 BCE) falls squarely within the late Poverty Point period. This multi-century climate departure on the Atlantic coast may reflect regional expressions of broader climate dynamics affecting the Poverty Point system, including potential disruptions to exchange networks extending toward the Atlantic seaboard.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "Both [mid-latitude temperature and moisture ensembles] include repeated multi-century cool and wet fluctuations, particularly at 5500-5000, 4400-4200, 3400-3100, and 1200-0 yr BP in the northeastern coast (Fig. 4d). (Note that the features recorded in the northeastern coast have been replicated in multiple reconstructions; they are not specific to the site or method (Shuman et al., 2019) and are confirmed by detailed radiocarbon dating across multiple cores and sites (Newby et al., 2014; Shuman and Burrell, 2017))."
- **Page**: p. 1709
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The repeated multi-century cool/wet fluctuations at 5500-5000, 4400-4200, and 3400-3100 yr BP define a recurrence interval of roughly 1000-1300 years. The 3400-3100 yr BP event (1450-1150 BCE) corresponds to the late Poverty Point occupation and potentially the beginning of the system's decline. These events are replicated across multiple independent records, strengthening their reliability.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "The strongest correlation among two independent time series of cen-mil residuals comes from the mid-latitude scale (Fig. 4b). One time series representing this scale derives from fossil pollen (MTWM; Fig. 4b) and the other from sedimentary evidence of lake level and dust deposition changes (moisture z scores; Fig. 4b).... Pearson's product-moment correlation between the two ensembles equals -0.60 (95% range from -0.42 to -0.73; Table 1), and the correlations exceed those of random surrogates at timescales of 300-1600 years (Fig. 5b)."
- **Page**: p. 1709
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The temperature-moisture anti-correlation (r = -0.60) at the mid-latitude regional scale, significant at timescales of 300-1600 years, confirms that cooling coincided with wetting over the centennial-to-millennial band. This corroborates the r = -0.90 correlation reported in Shuman and Marsicek (2016) for the full (non-detrended) time series. For the Poverty Point model, this means that centennial-scale climate perturbations systematically coupled temperature and moisture changes, amplifying their ecological impact.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "In the coastal northeastern sub-region, the cen-mil variation in the SST and lake level records also correlate significantly (Fig. 4d). Both include repeated multi-century cool and wet fluctuations, particularly at 5500-5000, 4400-4200, 3400-3100, and 1200-0 yr BP. These events alternate with warm and dry departures from the long trend at 4900-4600, 4200-3900, 2900-2100, and 1300-1200 yr BP (Newby et al., 2014; Shuman and Marsicek, 2016). Pearson's product-moment correlation coefficient (r = -0.51; 95% range from -0.66 to -0.31; Table 1) and the slope of the generalized least squares model (-36 plus/minus 8 mm per degrees C) both differ significantly from zero (Fig. 4d)."
- **Page**: p. 1709
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The northeastern coastal record provides a detailed chronology of alternating warm/dry and cool/wet multi-century events. The warm/dry event at 4200-3900 yr BP (2250-1950 BCE) immediately precedes Poverty Point's fluorescence, consistent with the drought documented in Shuman and Marsicek (2016). The cool/wet event at 3400-3100 yr BP (1450-1150 BCE) corresponds to the late Poverty Point period. The warm/dry phase at 2900-2100 yr BP (950-150 BCE) postdates the Poverty Point decline.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "Multitaper spectral analyses of the detrended time series (Fig. 4) show that maximum spectral power exists at the scale of multiple millennia for most of the scales, regions, and variables (Fig. 6). The multi-continent spectra based on land and water temperatures both indicate increasing power from 500- to 5000-year periods, as do the spectra representing the mid-latitude region and central sub-region, which both include substantially more power at near-2000-year (4000-1500 years) periods than in the multi-century band."
- **Page**: p. 1709
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Spectral analysis shows that most climate variability during the Holocene operated at multi-millennial scales (1500-5000 year periods), with less power in the multi-century band. For the Poverty Point model, this means that the dominant climate fluctuations operated on timescales longer than the Poverty Point occupation (~600 years), but shorter-period variability (multi-century) was still present and significant, particularly on the Atlantic coast.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "The spectra from the NE coastal sub-region differs from the others, but the temperature and moisture spectra from the sub-region are like each other (Fig. 6). Both contain peaks in power at 500-year periods, which is a timescale of variation evident in the physical stratigraphy of lakes in the region (Newby et al., 2014) and is consistent with the 100-600-year timescale of the significant correlations between the SST and lake level records (Fig. 5d)."
- **Page**: p. 1709
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The ~500-year spectral peak in northeastern coastal records is distinct from the dominant multi-millennial variability seen elsewhere. This ~500-year periodicity is consistent with the ~350-year multi-century variation identified in the abstract and the 100-600-year timescale of significant correlations. Over the Poverty Point period (~600 years), this periodicity would have produced approximately one full cycle of moisture variability.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "The most prominent steepening of the temperature difference between areas that warmed or cooled at ca. 5200-4700 yr BP (after the prominent anomaly from 5600-5000 yr BP) developed from ca. 4800-3800 yr BP (Shuman et al., 2023), possibly indicating different millennial-length phases of negative versus positive NAO states."
- **Page**: p. 1713
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: A steepening of the north-south temperature gradient from 4800-3800 yr BP (2850-1850 BCE) spans the period immediately before and into the early Poverty Point occupation. If driven by NAO-like dynamics, this would imply shifting atmospheric circulation patterns during the period when the Poverty Point aggregation system was developing.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "The Mid-Holocene anomaly from 5500-5000 yr BP, like a possible anomaly of the opposite sign after 4800 yr BP (Fig. 7b) (Shuman et al., 2023), would not be expected to appear in all records or regions. Spatial heterogeneity in detecting the pattern would be consistent with the small magnitude of cen-mil variations (Fig. 4), interactions with other trends (Fig. 3), and the inherent spatial variability in such dynamics (Fig. 7)."
- **Page**: p. 1714
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Acknowledges that the spatial expression of centennial-to-millennial variability is heterogeneous, with different regions experiencing different expressions of the same large-scale dynamics. This spatial heterogeneity is important for the Poverty Point model because it means the climate conditions at Poverty Point's latitude may not have been identical to those documented in the mid-latitude records.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "The pattern of change reflects a steepening in the north-south temperature gradient at ca. 5200-4700 yr BP (Fig. 7), which additional sites in the northeastern USA confirm, plausibly also including early cooling in the north before warming in the south (Shuman et al., 2023)."
- **Page**: p. 1712
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The spatial pattern of Mid-Holocene change included cooling in the north and warming in the south, consistent with a negative NAO-like pattern. For Poverty Point (32.6 degrees N, south of the study region), this suggests the Mid-Holocene anomaly period (5200-4700 yr BP) may have been experienced as warming rather than cooling. This is relevant to interpreting what environmental conditions preceded the Poverty Point fluorescence.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: "The weak latitudinal temperature gradient before the transition at ca. 5000-4700 yr BP was followed by a steep gradient until ca. 3500 yr BP, particularly in the northeastern US and the adjacent Atlantic (Shuman et al., 2023), and could be consistent with either a shift from negative- to positive-dominated NAO regimes or an analogous low-frequency change in the Atlantic pressure field."
- **Page**: p. 1714
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The steep north-south temperature gradient from 5000 to 3500 yr BP (3050-1550 BCE) encompasses most of the Poverty Point period. An NAO-like pressure gradient shift during this interval would have affected precipitation patterns across eastern North America, including the lower Mississippi Valley. The timing of the gradient relaxation at ca. 3500 yr BP (1550 BCE) falls within the Poverty Point occupation.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: "Cen-mil variations rarely departed significantly from the long trends with respect to reconstruction uncertainties in the eight time series examined here (Fig. 3); detrended series remain near zero throughout the Holocene (Fig. 4). Detrended temperatures vary only 0.25-0.5 degrees C."
- **Page**: p. 1709
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Reiterates the small magnitude of centennial-to-millennial temperature variability. For the Poverty Point model, this means that sigma (environmental uncertainty) at centennial-to-millennial scales was modest in absolute terms: temperature perturbations of 0.25-0.5 degrees C. However, the ecological impact of these perturbations was detectable in lake levels, dust records, and vegetation.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "The mid-Holocene anomaly from 5500-5000 yr BP includes a strong east-west contrast but repeated warm-dry events at 4900-4600, 4200-3900, 2900-2100, and 1300-1200 yr BP do not follow the same east-west pattern."
- **Page**: p. 1713
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The warm/dry events identified at 4200-3900 and 2900-2100 yr BP bracket the Poverty Point period (3700-3100 yr BP). The Poverty Point occupation thus falls in the interval between two major warm/dry events, consistent with a period of relative climatic stability or modest variability. The 2900-2100 yr BP warm/dry phase beginning shortly after Poverty Point's decline may have contributed to the system's end.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "Events that have previously attracted focused attention, such as at 9300, 8200, 4200, and 2700 yr BP, do not stand out as the most distinct features of the ensembles of data compiled here."
- **Page**: p. 1716 (conclusions)
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The 4.2 ka event (4200 yr BP), often cited as a major Holocene climate event, does not stand out as particularly distinct in these multi-record ensembles. This suggests the environmental uncertainty experienced during the Poverty Point period was not dominated by a single catastrophic event but rather by the cumulative effect of repeated, modest perturbations.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "Instead, two major patterns of variability appear evident. One appears expressed across a broad range of spatial scales, which is similar to unforced global variability in transient climate simulations (Marsicek et al., 2018; Wan et al., 2019). The pattern includes a set of millennial fluctuations with important anomalies and rapid shifts in both temperature and moisture gradients at ca. 5000 and 2100 yr BP."
- **Page**: p. 1716 (conclusions)

The 5000 yr BP shift (3050 BCE) falls near the onset of the Poverty Point occupation, while the 2100 yr BP shift (150 BCE) is well after its decline. The Poverty Point period occupies a window between these two major climate reorganizations.

### Claim 23

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Based on direct measurement, statistical analysis, or data reconstruction from paleoclimate proxy records.
- **Interpretive**: Inference about causes, mechanisms, spatial patterns, or significance of observed climate features.
- **Methodological**: Claims about analytical approaches, data processing, or signal detection strategies.

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Based on multiple replicated proxy records with statistical validation; robust comparison against null expectations from random surrogates.
- **Moderate**: Supported by data but involving interpretive inference about mechanisms or spatial patterns; or based on fewer replicated records with acknowledged uncertainty.
- **Weak**: Speculative or poorly constrained; acknowledged limitations in detection or attribution.

## Summary Assessment

Shuman (2024) advances the signal-detection problem for centennial-to-millennial (cen-mil) Holocene climate variability by comparing independent multi-record ensembles across four spatial scales in mid-latitude North America. The paper confirms that cen-mil temperature variability was small (0.25-0.5 degrees C after detrending) but detectable above noise in correlated independent datasets. Two patterns emerge: (1) a broad-scale millennial pattern including a prominent Mid-Holocene anomaly at 5600-5000 yr BP with NAO-like spatial characteristics, and (2) a multi-century (~350-500-year) pattern of hydroclimate variability along the Atlantic coast.

For the Poverty Point costly signaling model, this paper refines the environmental uncertainty (sigma) parameter in several ways. It confirms that centennial-to-millennial climate variability was real but modest, with temperature perturbations of 0.25-0.5 degrees C and coupled temperature-moisture variations at timescales of 300-1600 years. The Poverty Point period (3700-3100 yr BP) falls between the two major climate reorganizations at ca. 5000 and 2100 yr BP, in a window following the prominent Mid-Holocene anomaly. Multi-century warm/dry events at 4200-3900 and 2900-2100 yr BP bracket the Poverty Point period, while a cool/wet fluctuation at 3400-3100 yr BP corresponds to the late occupation. The spatial heterogeneity of cen-mil signals (NAO-like north-south gradient changes, east-west contrasts) means that different regions within the Poverty Point exchange network may have experienced different climate perturbations, supporting the model's prediction that inter-regional cooperation and information exchange would have been adaptive.
