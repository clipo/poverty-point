## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "We present a global compilation of quality-controlled, published, temperature-sensitive proxy records extending back 12,000 years through the Holocene. Data were compiled from 679 sites where time series cover at least 4000 years, are resolved at sub-millennial scale (median spacing of 400 years or finer) and have at least one age control point every 3000 years, with cut-off values slackened in data-sparse regions."
- **Page**: p. 1 (abstract)
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (defines the scope and selection criteria of the database)
- **Assessment**: The database covers the full Holocene, including the Poverty Point period (~3700-3100 BP). The sub-millennial resolution (median spacing 400 years or finer) and minimum 4000-year duration requirements mean records spanning the Poverty Point interval are well represented. This database provides a systematic resource for calibrating environmental variability (sigma) parameters for the signaling model.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "The data derive from lake sediment (51%), marine sediment (31%), peat (11%), glacier ice (3%), and other natural archives. The database contains 1319 records, including 157 from the Southern Hemisphere."
- **Page**: p. 1 (abstract)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific counts and percentages)
- **Assessment**: The dominance of lake and marine sediment records (82% combined) is relevant for the North American Southeast where lake sediments would be the primary archive type. The multi-proxy approach means that temperature estimates for the Poverty Point region are not dependent on a single proxy type.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "Previous syntheses of Holocene temperature records have typically focused on specific time horizons (mostly 6000 years ago), and are based nearly entirely on pollen assemblages from terrestrial archives, or are dominated by sea-surface temperatures near continental margins. Few global datasets have been compiled based on evidence from a wide variety of proxy types, including ecological, geochemical and biophysical evidence from both marine and terrestrial archives."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (characterizes the state of prior work)
- **Assessment**: Establishes that this database fills a gap in prior work by incorporating multiple proxy types and providing continuous time series rather than single time-slice reconstructions. This is relevant because continuous time series are needed to assess temporal variability (sigma) across the Poverty Point occupation span, not just a snapshot at a single moment.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "This data descriptor presents version 1.0.0 of the Temperature 12k database (ref. 7, with additional supporting information and updates at: www.ncdc.noaa.gov/paleo/study/27330)."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific version and repository identified)
- **Assessment**: The database is publicly available in multiple machine-readable formats (LiPD, MATLAB, Python, R), enabling direct extraction of North American paleotemperature records for the Poverty Point period. Data accessible at DOI: 10.25921/4RY2-G808.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "Only data published in the peer-reviewed literature were considered. This restriction helps to assure that the data are high quality, intended for scientific reuse, and are supported by vetted, complete and often nuanced paleoclimate and geochronological interpretations."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (clear quality criterion)
- **Assessment**: The peer-review restriction increases confidence in the underlying proxy records. For calibrating the environmental variability parameter in the Poverty Point model, it matters that the temperature data used have been independently vetted.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "Only those proxy records that have a demonstrated relation with temperature were included. Because most of the paleotemperature time series are not sufficiently resolved temporally to meaningfully compare with instrumental-based observations, this demonstration of temperature sensitivity is typically based on accepted understanding of the proxy systems."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (explains inclusion criterion)
- **Assessment**: Temperature sensitivity is established through proxy-system understanding rather than direct instrumental calibration for most records. This introduces a layer of interpretive uncertainty, but is the standard approach for Holocene-scale paleotemperature reconstruction.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "The Temperature 12k database gathers paleotemperature data from every continent and ocean. The geographical distribution of the records, however, is uneven. Latitudinally, 51% of the sites are located within the zone of 60-30 degrees N, and only 16% are located in the Southern Hemisphere. The spatial density of sites is comparatively high in North America and Europe."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (quantified spatial distribution; supported by Fig. 2a and Fig. 3)
- **Assessment**: North America has comparatively high site density, which is directly relevant for extracting paleotemperature data near Poverty Point. The 30-60 degrees N latitude band, which includes the Poverty Point site at 32.63 degrees N, contains the highest concentration of records. This means the database should provide reasonable spatial coverage for the lower Mississippi Valley region, though exact site locations near Poverty Point would need to be checked in the database itself.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: "The average record length within the Holocene is 9813 years for this database (n = 816, where only one season (or annual) is counted for each proxy from each site). All sites are represented by records that include at least some data between 8.5 and 3.5 ka."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (quantified with specific values)
- **Assessment**: The requirement that all sites include data between 8.5 and 3.5 ka (8500-3500 BP) guarantees that every record in the database covers the Poverty Point period (~3700-3100 BP). This is a critical finding: the database is structurally complete for the time interval of interest.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "Most of the records (91%) extend back at least 6000 years, thereby encompassing the '6 ka time slice', which is an on-going and long-standing target for paleoclimate modeling."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (quantified)
- **Assessment**: The 6 ka (6000 BP) time slice includes the period approximately 2,300 years before the start of Poverty Point occupation. Records extending to at least 6 ka would capture the full climatic context leading into the Poverty Point period.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "The median record resolution ('Resolution') of individual time series in the database is 164 years over the Holocene (n = 816, where only one season (or annual) is counted for each proxy from each site). Overall, 15% of these records have 50-year resolution or finer, 39% have 51- to 150-year resolution, and 21% are coarser than 250-year resolution."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (quantified resolution distribution)
- **Assessment**: Median resolution of 164 years means that for the ~600-year Poverty Point occupation (1700-1100 BCE), most records would provide roughly 3-4 data points spanning the occupation. This is sufficient to identify multi-century temperature trends (warming vs. cooling) during the Poverty Point period, but insufficient to detect decadal-scale variability that might drive year-to-year resource uncertainty. For calibrating sigma in the signaling model, this resolution constrains estimates to centennial-scale temperature variability.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "Most of the sites (74%) include at least one record that was interpreted as mean annual temperature, or was calculated as the average of summer and winter values for this data product to represent annual temperature. Most of the sites (64%) have records that are interpreted by the original authors as representing summer temperature, and 39% of the sites include winter paleotemperature estimates."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (quantified seasonal coverage)
- **Assessment**: The availability of seasonal temperature records (summer vs. winter) is relevant for the Poverty Point model because resource uncertainty for hunter-gatherers would have varied seasonally. Summer temperatures may correlate with growing-season productivity, while winter temperatures relate to cold-season stress, a key variable for aggregation decisions.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "For sequences that rely mainly on radiocarbon (n = 613), the average number of ages for records in this database is 1.0 per 1000 years, including the tephra ages."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (quantified chronological control)
- **Assessment**: An average of 1.0 radiocarbon age per 1000 years means that the Poverty Point period (~3700-3100 BP) would typically have at most one independent age control point within the occupation interval. Chronological uncertainty at this level introduces potential timing errors on the order of several centuries, which limits the ability to precisely correlate temperature changes with specific archaeological events at Poverty Point.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: The compositing procedure: "all time series were standardized to z-scores, with a mean of zero and variance of 1 SD over the entire record length (except for the global composite, as specified below). Individual data points were binned by averaging the measurements within 500-year intervals."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific procedure described)
- **Assessment**: The z-score standardization and 500-year binning define the resolution at which composite temperature trends are presented. For the Poverty Point period, 500-year bins would produce at most one or two composite data points (~3500-3000 BP and ~4000-3500 BP). Finer-scale variability within the occupation is smoothed out by this binning.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "The composites all show warming trends during the early Holocene, some reaching peak warmth as early as around 10,000 years ago (e.g., chironomids), and others not until around 6000 years ago (e.g., pollen). By 6000 years ago, all of the composites show a cooling trend."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (supported by Fig. 4 showing composites for 8 proxy types with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals)
- **Assessment**: This is the key climate finding relevant to Poverty Point. By 6000 BP, all proxy types show a cooling trend. The Poverty Point period (3700-3100 BP) falls squarely within this post-6 ka cooling phase. The Neoglacial cooling trend would have increased environmental uncertainty and resource unpredictability for hunter-gatherer populations in the lower Mississippi Valley, potentially creating the conditions that favored aggregation-based signaling strategies.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: Comparison among seasonal and annual records (Fig. 5): "Nearly all composites show the general pattern of warming during the early Holocene then are either constant or cooling thereafter." In the 30-60 degrees N latitude band, summer, winter, and annual composites all show a decline from peak values around 6000-5000 BP through the present.
- **Page**: pp. 7-8 (supported by Fig. 5)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (composite trends with bootstrap confidence intervals shown for six latitude bands)
- **Assessment**: The 30-60 degrees N band is most relevant for Poverty Point (32.63 degrees N, near the boundary of the 30-60 degrees N and 0-30 degrees N bands). In this band, the cooling trend is evident in summer, winter, and annual composites from approximately 5000-6000 BP onward, confirming that the Poverty Point occupation (3700-3100 BP) occurred during a period of declining temperatures. The confidence intervals widen during this period, reflecting greater inter-record variability, which itself could indicate increased spatial heterogeneity in climate.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "Nearly all composites warm during the early Holocene, then are either flat or cool thereafter, with a suggestion that tropical land records cooled more than for tropical sea surfaces."
- **Page**: p. 8
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (comparison between marine and terrestrial composites in Fig. 6)
- **Assessment**: The differential cooling between terrestrial and marine records in tropical latitudes indicates that land-based temperature changes may have been more pronounced than ocean-surface changes. For the Poverty Point region, which is terrestrial but influenced by Gulf of Mexico moisture, this distinction could affect how temperature variability translates into resource uncertainty.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: "The millennial-scale trends of the high- and low-resolution records generally track each other. The high-resolution composites also exhibit greater variability than the low-resolution composites, as expected because the high-resolution composites are based on fewer records and because those records capture higher-frequency variability."
- **Page**: p. 8 (supported by Fig. 7)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (systematic comparison with consistent results)
- **Assessment**: High-resolution records (median spacing less than 100 years) capture more centennial-scale variability than low-resolution records. For calibrating sigma in the Poverty Point model, high-resolution records from the region would better capture the frequency of temperature fluctuations experienced by Poverty Point populations. Approximately 39% of sites have records with resolution finer than 100 years (p. 6).

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: The global composite (Fig. 8) shows that calibrated temperature records display a warming trend from the early Holocene reaching peak warmth around 6500 BP, followed by a long-term cooling of approximately 0.5 degrees C through the late Holocene until the recent warming. The composite was "placed on a temperature scale (left-side x-axis) by aligning the 500 to 1500 CE mean of the composite with the mean of the global temperature reconstruction from the PAGES 2k Consortium."
- **Page**: p. 8 (Fig. 8 and caption, p. 15)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (area-weighted global composite with multiple calibration approaches)
- **Assessment**: The global cooling of approximately 0.5 degrees C from 6500 BP to the late Holocene (before industrial warming) provides a quantitative estimate of the temperature decline during and after the Poverty Point period. This Neoglacial cooling trend is the backdrop against which Poverty Point populations operated. A cooling of 0.5 degrees C over several millennia translates to environmental shifts in growing season length, species distributions, and resource availability, all factors that would have increased uncertainty and potentially favored cooperative strategies.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "The proxy locations explain between 93% and 100% of the temperature variance in the latitudinal bands" when compared with instrumental temperature data from HadCRUT4 and ERA20C datasets (Fig. 9). R-squared values range from 0.93 to 1.00 across all six 30-degree latitude bands.
- **Page**: pp. 8-9 (Fig. 9, p. 16)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (quantitative validation against instrumental records)
- **Assessment**: This validation demonstrates that the proxy network is spatially representative of zonal temperature variability. The high explanatory power (93-100%) means that temperature trends derived from the database are unlikely to be artifacts of uneven site distribution. For the 30-60 degrees N band containing Poverty Point, R-squared values are 0.98 (HadCRUT4) and 0.96 (ERA20C).

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "The proxy locations generally explain 94% of the variance in the latitudinal averages of mid-Holocene minus preindustrial changes across the multiple models. This number increases to 98% for global means computed from area-weighted means of the six latitude bands."
- **Page**: p. 9 (supported by Fig. 10, p. 17)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (validated against 12 PMIP3 climate models)
- **Assessment**: Validation against climate model output for mid-Holocene conditions confirms that the proxy network captures the spatial pattern of Holocene temperature change. This is directly relevant because it means the database can reliably represent mid-to-late Holocene temperature trends for the latitude band that includes Poverty Point.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "Among the important uncertainties to consider when using this database are those related to (1) calibration and proxy biases, (2) chronology, (3) spatiotemporal coverage. Depending on the scientific goal, uncertainties related to these and other factors can be substantial."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (authors' own assessment of limitations)
- **Assessment**: These three categories of uncertainty are relevant for any application of this database to the Poverty Point model. Calibration uncertainties affect the magnitude of temperature changes; chronological uncertainties affect the timing; and spatiotemporal coverage affects how well the database represents the lower Mississippi Valley specifically.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: "Calibration uncertainties are often large relative to the small amplitude of Holocene temperature changes, but these uncertainties become less important when investigating the relative magnitude of temperature changes rather than the absolute temperature."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (appropriate caveat)
- **Assessment**: For the Poverty Point model, the relevant quantity is not absolute temperature but rather the magnitude and direction of temperature variability (sigma). The authors note that calibration uncertainties are less problematic for relative magnitude assessments, which is what the signaling model requires.

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: "The 3000-year-maximum spacing between age-control points was chosen as a relatively inclusive screening criterion. The accuracy of the time scales depends mainly on (1) how well the ages represent the true age of the proxy sensor itself... and (2) the uniformity of the accumulation rate of the archival medium."
- **Page**: p. 13
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (describes chronological uncertainty sources)
- **Assessment**: Chronological uncertainty is a fundamental limitation for correlating temperature changes with archaeological events at Poverty Point. The 3000-year maximum spacing between age controls means that some records may have poorly constrained chronologies during the Poverty Point period. Bayesian age modeling (available for many records) helps address this uncertainty.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: "Some regions are covered more densely than others, and the number of records available decreases as the demands for temporal resolution increase. At finer spatial and temporal scales, the number of records with sufficient resolution and geochronological control is limited and typically based on more recent studies. For example, only about 39% of the sites have records with resolution finer than 100 years."
- **Page**: p. 13
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (quantified limitation)
- **Assessment**: The reduction in available records at finer resolution is relevant for Poverty Point analyses. To assess centennial-scale variability during the ~600-year Poverty Point occupation, only the subset of records with sub-centennial resolution would be useful, and these represent only 39% of all sites. The actual number of such high-resolution records near the lower Mississippi Valley would need to be determined from the database itself.

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: The Temperature 12k database includes proxy time series from "470 terrestrial and 209 marine sites." By archive type: "715 records from lake sediments, 359 records from marine sediments, and 245 from other terrestrial archives (e.g., glacier ice and speleothem)."
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific counts)
- **Assessment**: The 470 terrestrial sites are the most relevant for the Poverty Point region. Lake sediment records (715 total, the single largest category) would be the primary archive type available for the lower Mississippi Valley. The database can be filtered by archive type, latitude/longitude, and other parameters to isolate records most relevant to the Poverty Point setting.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: "The most frequent proxy type is pollen." Most pollen-based paleotemperature time series from North America and Europe "are from the synthesis of Marsicek et al., which used the modern analogue technique to calculate paleotemperatures back to around 11 ka, and screened records using the PaleoSig package ('randomTF' function with a 95% confidence level)."
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: empirical / methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (identifies the primary data source for North American pollen records)
- **Assessment**: The Marsicek et al. synthesis is the backbone of the North American terrestrial paleotemperature record in this database. This is significant because Marsicek et al. (2018, Nature 554:92) demonstrated that Holocene temperatures showed millennial-scale cooling after approximately 5500 BP, a finding that has been influential in understanding long-term Northern Hemisphere climate trends during the period that includes Poverty Point.

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: The database is formatted in "Linked Paleo Data (LiPD)" structure, comprising "JSON formatted, standardized files that are machine-readable in multiple programming languages for querying and data extraction." Database files are available in LiPD, MATLAB (.mat), Python (.pkl), and R (.Rdata) formats.
- **Page**: p. 17 (Table 2, p. 17)
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (multiple format availability confirmed)
- **Assessment**: The machine-readable format enables systematic extraction of paleotemperature records near Poverty Point for direct use in calibrating the model's environmental variability parameter. Records can be filtered by location, proxy type, resolution, and time period to identify the most relevant subset for the lower Mississippi Valley.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited

## Summary Assessment

Kaufman et al. 2020 presents the Temperature 12k database, a comprehensive global compilation of 1319 quality-controlled, multi-proxy Holocene paleotemperature records from 679 sites. The database is structured for machine-readable access and covers the past 12,000 years at sub-millennial resolution.

For the Poverty Point costly signaling project, this database is relevant in three ways.

First, it provides the empirical basis for calibrating environmental variability (sigma) during the Poverty Point period (~3700-3100 BP). All records in the database include data between 8500 and 3500 BP (Claim 8), guaranteeing coverage of the Poverty Point interval. The dominant climatic signal across all proxy types is a cooling trend from peak Holocene warmth (~6500-5000 BP) through the late Holocene (Claims 14, 15, 18), with a global cooling of approximately 0.5 degrees C. The Poverty Point occupation falls squarely within this Neoglacial cooling phase, a period of declining temperatures that would have increased environmental uncertainty and resource unpredictability for hunter-gatherer populations.

Second, North America has comparatively high site density in the database (Claim 7), and the 30-60 degrees N latitude band that includes or borders the Poverty Point site is the most data-rich. Pollen-based records from the Marsicek et al. synthesis form the backbone of North American terrestrial coverage (Claim 26). The database can be queried by location to identify records nearest to the lower Mississippi Valley.

Third, the database documents important methodological limitations for this application. Median temporal resolution of 164 years (Claim 10) means the ~600-year Poverty Point occupation would be represented by only a few data points per record. Chronological uncertainty (Claim 12, average 1.0 radiocarbon date per 1000 years) limits the precision of correlations between temperature changes and archaeological events. Calibration uncertainties are large relative to the small amplitude of Holocene temperature changes, but are less problematic for relative variability assessments (Claim 22), which is what the signaling model requires.

The database does not provide direct evidence about Poverty Point; it provides the paleoclimate context within which the signaling system operated. For the model, the key inference is that the Poverty Point period was characterized by long-term Neoglacial cooling, which, combined with the inherent stochasticity of hunter-gatherer resource systems, may have created the environmental uncertainty that made aggregation-based costly signaling adaptive.
