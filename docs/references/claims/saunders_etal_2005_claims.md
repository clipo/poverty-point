# Claims Extraction: Saunders et al. 2005

**Source**: Saunders, J.W., Mandel, R.D., Sampson, C.G., Allen, C.M., Allen, E.T., Bush, D.A., Feathers, J.K., Gremillion, K.J., Hallmark, C.T., Jackson, H.E., Johnson, J.K., Jones, R., Saucier, R.T., Stringer, G.L., and Vidrine, M.F. 2005. Watson Brake, A Middle Archaic Mound Complex in Northeast Louisiana. *American Antiquity* 70(4):631-668.
**PDF**: Saunders_etal_2005.pdf
**Processed**: 2026-03-28
**Pipeline status**: claims

## Claims

### Claim 1: Site Description and Scale
- **Statement**: "Watson Brake is an oval arrangement of 11 earthen mounds with connecting ridges. These form two curved rows of earthworks called the north and south mounds. The level enclosure between them measures about 300 m long by 200 m across, and the tallest mound (Gentry Mound, A in Figure 2) is 7.5 m high."
- **Page**: p. 632
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Core dimensional data for comparison with Poverty Point. Watson Brake is substantially smaller (300 x 200 m enclosure vs. Poverty Point's ~160 ha constructed landscape; tallest mound 7.5 m vs. 22 m) but still a significant construction for Middle Archaic hunter-gatherers.

### Claim 2: Chronology of Initial Occupation
- **Statement**: "The initial occupation of the terrace surface was sporadic, first at ca. 4000 B.C. (K/A-tr, At2b1), again at ca. 3700 B.C. (B-tr? shell midden), then at ca. 3600 B.C. (E-tr?). At ca. 3500 B.C., terrace occupation became widespread (J-tr, Ap-tr, C-tr, and D-tr). The last dated terrace occupation is at ca. 3350 B.C. (Ap-tr, top)."
- **Page**: p. 648
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on accepted radiocarbon dates)
- **Assessment**: Establishes initial occupation at ca. 4000 B.C., roughly 2,300 years before Poverty Point's construction began. Watson Brake demonstrates mound-building was not a Poverty Point innovation.

### Claim 3: Chronology of Mound Construction
- **Statement**: "The construction of the first minor earthworks began around 3500 B.C. with Mounds K and B (and possibly A) followed by midden accumulations where Mounds D and C, and to the south I, J, and E were subsequently built... Major building projects then commenced ca. 3350 B.C., and existing earthworks may have been heightened and extended along the north mound row. Mound J was erected on the south side at around 3000 B.C."
- **Page**: p. 665
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on 18 accepted chronometric dates)
- **Assessment**: Construction spanned at least 500 years (ca. 3500-3000 B.C.), with construction in stages rather than a single episode. This multi-stage pattern is relevant for comparison with Poverty Point's construction chronology.

### Claim 4: Construction Was Staged, Not Continuous
- **Statement**: "Six, perhaps seven, of the mounds and at least two ridges were constructed in stages. The degree of soil development in the multistage mound and ridge fills suggests periods of 200+ years between construction phases."
- **Page**: p. 665
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on soil stratigraphy and pedogenesis)
- **Assessment**: The long hiatuses (200+ years) between construction phases argue against sustained, continuous labor mobilization. This is consistent with episodic aggregation events rather than permanent, directed labor.

### Claim 5: Evidence Against Recycled Premound Charcoal
- **Statement**: "The four accepted charcoal dates in Mound B are in correct stratigraphic order (older with depth), as are the two in ridge K/A. Such orderly sequences of stratified dates would not occur if premound charcoal was incorporated randomly as the earthworks grew."
- **Page**: p. 644
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Addresses a potential criticism that radiocarbon dates do not actually date construction. The stratigraphic ordering of charcoal dates within individual mounds confirms that the charcoal dates the fills in which it occurs.

### Claim 6: Minor Earthworks Preceded Substantial Moundraising
- **Statement**: "Minor earthworks were begun here at ca. 3500 B.C. in association with an oval arrangement of burned rock middens at the edge of a stream terrace. The full extent of the first earthworks is not yet known. Substantial moundraising began ca. 3350 B.C. and continued in stages until some time after 3000 B.C. when the site was abandoned."
- **Page**: p. 631 (abstract)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Distinction between "minor earthworks" (ca. 3500 B.C.) and "substantial moundraising" (ca. 3350 B.C.) suggests an escalation in labor investment over time, consistent with a signaling model where initial small-scale construction grew into larger commitments.

### Claim 7: Oval Layout Was Deliberate
- **Statement**: "The shape of the complex was deliberately laid out by 3500 B.C. Major building projects then commenced ca. 3350 B.C."
- **Page**: p. 665
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (inferred from spatial pattern of earliest features)
- **Assessment**: If correct, the deliberate layout implies some degree of planning or communal agreement on the form of the complex before major labor investment began. This has implications for the degree of coordination required.

### Claim 8: Ecotone Location
- **Statement**: "The site is on an ecotone between riverine and upland mosaics, both extremely rich in edible plants. The upland sector of the site's catchment supports deer and a full range of small mammals and birds, while the riverine sector carries a diverse aquatic fauna."
- **Page**: p. 633
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Directly parallel to the ecotone hypothesis for Poverty Point's location. Watson Brake, like Poverty Point, was positioned at an ecological intersection, maximizing access to diverse resources. This strengthens the argument that ecotone positioning was a deliberate strategy for aggregation sites.

### Claim 9: All-Season Occupation Indicated by Fauna
- **Statement**: "Faunal and floral remains from a basal midden indicate all-season occupation, supported by broad-spectrum foraging centered on nuts, fish, and deer."
- **Page**: p. 631 (abstract)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The abstract claims all-season occupation, but the detailed discussion on p. 661 is more cautious: "While support for all-season occupation of the terrace is quite strong, it would be premature to suppose that it reflects year-round (permanent) residence here. That would require many different case studies using multiple lines of evidence."

### Claim 10: Seasonal Indicators from Fish Otoliths
- **Statement**: "Only 39 otoliths had sufficiently well-defined annuli to determine season of death, of which most (37) were drum. All seasons are represented with remarkably even distribution, given the modest size of the samples: 17.9 percent winter (n = 7); 25.6 percent spring (n = 10); and 28.2 percent each for summer (n = 11) and fall (n = 11)."
- **Page**: p. 661
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (small sample size: n = 39)
- **Assessment**: All four seasons are represented, but the small sample and absence of modern controls for seasonal drum availability limit the strength of the all-season inference. The even distribution could reflect year-round presence or multiple seasonal visits over many years of accumulation.

### Claim 11: Fish Dominated the Faunal Assemblage
- **Statement**: "Fish contribute over half of the total bone weight, while whitetail deer about 30 percent by weight. Others (small mammals, birds, reptiles/amphibians) each make up less than 10 percent. Thus fish and deer contributed the bulk of the meat in the diet."
- **Page**: p. 657
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on 12,927 total faunal specimens from Mound B)
- **Assessment**: Fish dominance is consistent with a riverine-focused subsistence economy exploiting the Watson Brake channel. The dominance of drum (freshwater drum) specifically indicates heavy exploitation of the main river channel.

### Claim 12: Drum Were Small, Suggesting Fishing Pressure
- **Statement**: "The lengths of drum otoliths indicate that they were much smaller than the modern drum average in Louisiana, namely 54 cm long and 2.3 kg in weight. By contrast, the average Watson Brake drum is only 19-cm long and weighs only .2 kg. Only one otolith (20.2 mm long) came from a drum approaching the modern average."
- **Page**: p. 657
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The very small size of drum (roughly 10% of modern average weight) suggests either heavy fishing pressure depleting larger individuals or selective exploitation techniques (e.g., nets with small mesh). This indicates intensive aquatic resource use at the aggregation site.

### Claim 13: Broad-Spectrum Faunal Exploitation
- **Statement**: "At least 56 taxa are present. As expected in this floodplain setting, riparian and bottomland taxa dominate the assemblage: small mammals (beaver, raccoon, muskrat, and otter); waterfowl (mostly ducks and geese); aquatic turtles (snapping and soft-shell, also emydids); watersnakes; amphibians; and abundant fish."
- **Page**: p. 657
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The taxonomic richness (56+ taxa) confirms broad-spectrum foraging. This is the expected subsistence pattern for a hunter-gatherer aggregation site where diverse resources are exploited simultaneously by multiple groups.

### Claim 14: Plant Remains Limited but Include Potential Cultigens
- **Statement**: "Charred nutshell fragments of hickory (Carya spp.) were most common, with only a few charred seeds of grape (Vitis spp.), hackberry/sugarberry (Celtis occidentalis/laevigata), goosefoot (Chenopodium berlandieri), and possibly sumpweed/marshelder (Iva annua) in the lower levels of Mound B."
- **Page**: p. 661
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (limited preservation and small sample)
- **Assessment**: The presence of goosefoot and possible marshelder is notable because both species were eventually domesticated in eastern North America. The specimens show no signs of domestication (no increased seed size or reduced coat thickness), but their presence at 3500 B.C. documents early use of these taxa.

### Claim 15: No Evidence of Trade in Non-Local Materials
- **Statement**: "Local gravel was the lithic source for both the bifaces and stone beads; evidence of trade for nonlocal material does not exist."
- **Page**: p. 665
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on raw material sourcing)
- **Assessment**: A critical contrast with Poverty Point. Watson Brake has no evidence of long-distance exchange networks, while Poverty Point's exchange system spanned 1,600+ km. This suggests the exotic goods acquisition system at Poverty Point was a later innovation, not inherited from the Middle Archaic mound-building tradition.

### Claim 16: Manuports Are All Local
- **Statement**: "Manuports include a quartz crystal, fossils (mostly crinoid stems), two red jasper pebbles, and lumps of hematite and limonite (a few possibly shaped). They are randomly distributed in the fills. All of these originate in the terrace gravels beneath the earthworks. There are also pieces of slate not found locally, but certainly sourced to the Ouachita River valley (R. De Hon, personal communication 2003), so they probably came from only a few kilometers away."
- **Page**: p. 654
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Even the "non-local" items (slate) came from a few kilometers away, not hundreds. This confirms Watson Brake's exchange radius was essentially local, unlike Poverty Point's long-distance network.

### Claim 17: Fired Earthen Objects Present
- **Statement**: "A total of 34.2 kg of fired earthen objects and fragments was recovered. These are relatively small and roughly finished in several shapes that invite comparisons with solid geometry. Most specimens are broken, and none has been found to refit... Sizes are quite standardized, typically 4 cm x 4 cm x 3.5 cm for a whole cuboidal (n = 7), and 4 cm x 4.5 cm x 3 cm for a whole rectanguloid (n = 2), while fragments of both (n = 178) are of similar dimensions."
- **Page**: p. 654
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Fired earthen objects (similar to Poverty Point Objects) are present at Watson Brake, establishing this artifact class as part of the Middle Archaic tradition, not a Poverty Point invention. However, the Watson Brake specimens are much smaller and less varied in form than the elaborated PPOs at Poverty Point.

### Claim 18: Enclosure Kept Clean, Suggesting Ritual Use
- **Statement**: "The area enclosed by the mounds was kept clean of debris, suggesting its use as ritual space... the enclosure is almost bereft of lithics and fire-cracked rock. By contrast, densities rise steeply on the flanks of Mounds D and K where auger samples were taken. Occupation was restricted to the mounds and ridges, and the enclosure was not inhabited."
- **Page**: pp. 631, 656
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on systematic auger survey of enclosure)
- **Assessment**: The clean enclosure is consistent with a communal plaza or gathering space, paralleling the central plaza at Poverty Point. The deliberate maintenance of an open space within the mound complex suggests communal activities requiring space for assembly.

### Claim 19: No Burials Found
- **Statement**: "No burials were encountered. However, the B-tr? shell midden under Mound B produced three adult hand phalanges and a radius or ulna shaft fragment, possibly all from a single individual. An adult cranium fragment was identified in the 2Ab4 horizon at the top of the B-tr? shell midden. Among the fauna from the overlying B1 fill was the humerus of a child about three years old, and a deciduous molar, possibly from the same individual."
- **Page**: p. 662
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: No formal burials were found, and the isolated human remains were scattered in midden contexts. This argues against the mounds serving a mortuary function and against the site being interpreted through a hierarchical burial-mound model.

### Claim 20: No Continuity Between Middle Archaic and Poverty Point Moundbuilding
- **Statement**: "As mysteriously as the Middle Archaic mounds appeared 6,000 years ago, moundbuilding disappeared 1,000 years later, and for 1,300 years mound construction stopped. It did not resume until Poverty Point times (2700-2300 B.P.). At present, there is virtually no evidence of continuity between the Middle Archaic and Poverty Point cultures."
- **Page**: p. 663
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (based on absence of evidence)
- **Assessment**: The 1,300-year gap between the end of Middle Archaic moundbuilding (ca. 3000 B.C.) and the beginning of Poverty Point (ca. 1700 B.C.) is a critical observation. If there is no cultural continuity, then Poverty Point's moundbuilding represents an independent development, not a direct inheritance from the Watson Brake tradition.

### Claim 21: Building Bursts Correlate with ENSO Events
- **Statement**: "Some building bursts covary with very well-documented increases in El Nino/Southern Oscillation events. During such rapid increases in ENSO frequencies, rainfall becomes extremely erratic and unpredictable. It may be that early moundraising was a communal response to new stresses of droughts and flooding that created a suddenly more unpredictable food base."
- **Page**: p. 631 (abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (correlation, not causation demonstrated)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the bet-hedging/costly signaling framework. If mound construction correlates with periods of increased environmental unpredictability (via ENSO), this supports the hypothesis that monumentality emerged as a cooperative response to resource uncertainty.

### Claim 22: Mounds Built During Stable Periods Between ENSO Pulses
- **Statement**: "We propose that the mounds were built during stable conditions, not unstable ones... all dated submound occupations and all building hiatuses (with buried soils) at Watson Brake occurred during calm, stable periods between pulses... it is more likely that the unstable Pulse 3 caused a halt in building activity and that the devastating Pulse 4 put a stop to it altogether."
- **Page**: p. 664
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (depends on correlation between Watson Brake dates and Laguna Pallcacocha ENSO record)
- **Assessment**: This claim actually contradicts Hamilton's (1999) bet-hedging model. Saunders et al. argue mounds were built during calm periods, not during crises. If correct, this challenges a purely reactive "costly signaling under uncertainty" model and suggests moundbuilding may have been proactive, occurring when surplus labor was available during stable conditions.

### Claim 23: Hamilton's Bet-Hedging Model Rejected
- **Statement**: "For now, bet hedging does not explain mound building at Watson Brake, unless folk memory of past calamities or fear of future ones was invoked to get it started. In our view this is an untestable proposition."
- **Page**: p. 664
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (their rejection is based on the timing argument)
- **Assessment**: Saunders et al. explicitly reject the bet-hedging hypothesis for Watson Brake on the grounds that construction occurred during stable, not unstable, periods. This is an important counterpoint that any application of the costly signaling/bet-hedging framework to Poverty Point must address. The situation at Poverty Point may differ if its construction coincides with different ENSO/climate conditions.

### Claim 24: Moundbuilding Was Not a Package Deal
- **Statement**: "The only trait common to all Middle Archaic mound sites is the mounds themselves. This limited sample does nevertheless show that moundbuilding was not a monolithic cultural expression. Sites varied not only in size, number of mounds, and site layout, but also in the presence/absence of artifact types, and perhaps, standing architecture."
- **Page**: p. 663
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (based on comparison of 13+ sites in Louisiana and Mississippi)
- **Assessment**: Variability among Middle Archaic mound sites argues against a single diffusing cultural template. Instead, moundbuilding appears to have been independently adopted by different groups, consistent with a model where the practice spread because it was adaptive in specific ecological contexts.

### Claim 25: Number of Middle Archaic Mound Sites
- **Statement**: "The number of Middle Archaic mound sites has grown to 13 in Louisiana and one in Mississippi... One- (n = 2), two- (n = 3), three- (n = 1), and eight-mound (n = 1) sites make up the seven southern Louisiana sample; one- (n = 1), two- (n = 1), four- (n = 1) five- (n = 1), six- (n = 1), and eleven-mound (n = 1) sites compose the northern Louisiana mounds."
- **Page**: p. 662
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Watson Brake (11 mounds) is the largest of the known Middle Archaic mound complexes, analogous to how Poverty Point is orders of magnitude larger than its contemporary sites. Both sites appear to be regional centers within broader traditions.

### Claim 26: Middle Archaic Mound Dates Range 6000-5000 B.P.
- **Statement**: Watson Brake is "the largest and most complex of these early mound sites" in the earthen moundbuilding tradition that "thrived in the Lower Mississippi Valley between ca. 6000 and 5000 B.P."
- **Page**: p. 662
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the temporal range of Middle Archaic moundbuilding as ca. 4000-3000 B.C. (6000-5000 B.P.), with Watson Brake spanning most of this interval.

### Claim 27: Labor Investment in Fill Sources
- **Statement**: "About 90 percent of the gravels recovered from all test units (1,133 kg) came from the north mounds (TU 1-8). The obvious source of this material is the exposed terrace edge immediately next to each mound and/or ridge. Evidently the scarp face was systematically quarried for material."
- **Page**: p. 639
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Fill was obtained locally from the adjacent terrace scarp, requiring minimal transport distance. This contrasts with Poverty Point where borrow pits have been identified at greater distances. The local fill sourcing at Watson Brake suggests lower per-unit labor costs for construction.

### Claim 28: Soil Acidicity Limits Preservation
- **Statement**: "Extreme acidity of the structural fills (pH 4.0-5.0) precludes survival of bone and shell, but fragments were encountered at or near the bases of some earthworks where pH rises to weakly acidic or neutral levels. A total of 95.5 kg of bone and shell was recovered under the north mounds D, C, B, A, and ridge K/A and below mound J in the south mounds."
- **Page**: p. 657
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The acidic matrix severely limits organic preservation, meaning that the subsistence data come primarily from the basal midden beneath Mound B. The absence of organics in the overlying fills is taphonomic, not cultural.

### Claim 29: Microdrills Prefigure Poverty Point Technology
- **Statement**: "Some (n = 39) [bladelets] were converted into drill preforms and drills, a pattern that prefigures the Poverty Point period when bladelets were routinely transformed into Jaketown perforators to be used as drills (Yerkes 1983)."
- **Page**: p. 652
- **Type**: Empirical/Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (technological parallel, not proven continuity)
- **Assessment**: Microdrill technology at Watson Brake is a potential precursor to the Poverty Point microdrill/Jaketown perforator tradition. However, given the 1,300-year gap and the authors' own statement of no cultural continuity (Claim 20), this may represent convergent technological solutions rather than direct transmission.

### Claim 30: Site Abandonment Linked to Hydrological Changes
- **Statement**: "Complex shifts in major local drainages between 3000 and 2000 B.C. (Saucier 1994; Washington 2001) placed natural levees across the mouth of Watson Brake, thereby converting it to a swampy backwater. These events may be implicated in the abandonment of the site at this time, but the relationship has not yet been fully investigated."
- **Page**: p. 633
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (explicitly stated as not fully investigated)
- **Assessment**: If abandonment was caused by local hydrological changes rather than social dynamics, this limits the applicability of purely social models for explaining the end of the Watson Brake system. Environmental degradation of the ecotone could have made the location no longer viable for aggregation.

## Summary Assessment

Saunders et al. (2005) provides the definitive report on Watson Brake, the largest and most complex Middle Archaic mound site in the Lower Mississippi Valley, dated to ca. 3500-3000 B.C. The site predates Poverty Point by roughly 2,000 years and demonstrates that hunter-gatherer mound construction in the region has deep roots. For the costly signaling model applied to Poverty Point, the key findings are:

1. Watson Brake establishes that monumental construction by hunter-gatherers was possible without long-distance exchange networks, formal hierarchy, or agricultural surplus; all materials are local and there is no evidence of trade in non-local goods.

2. The ecotone location (riverine/upland intersection) parallels Poverty Point's positioning, supporting the ecological diversity hypothesis for aggregation site placement.

3. The 1,300-year gap between Watson Brake's abandonment and Poverty Point's emergence, with "virtually no evidence of continuity," means Poverty Point's signaling system was likely an independent development.

4. The authors explicitly reject Hamilton's (1999) bet-hedging model for Watson Brake, arguing mounds were built during stable periods between ENSO pulses, not during crises. This is a direct challenge that must be addressed when applying costly signaling/bet-hedging to Poverty Point.

5. Seasonal indicators suggest all-season site use, though the authors caution against equating this with permanent year-round residence; the evidence is consistent with prolonged or repeated seasonal visits.
