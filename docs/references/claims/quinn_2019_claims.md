## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "The earliest known evidence for humans using material culture to signal can be traced back to the use of ochre and other pigments as far back as 300,000 years ago (Kuhn 2014). By 75,000 years ago, there is evidence of objects being intentionally manufactured, such as the perforated shell beads and ochre from Blombos Cave, with the primary purpose of sending messages (d'Errico et al. 2005; Henshilwood et al. 2009, 2011)."
- **Page**: p. 275
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (cites specific archaeological evidence with dates)
- **Assessment**: Establishes deep evolutionary time-depth for material signaling, grounding CST in human evolutionary heritage rather than treating it as a culturally emergent phenomenon.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "Originally developed in biology, costly signaling theory (CST) provides an evolutionary explanation for why people engage in seemingly wasteful behaviors and invest in extravagant material displays. Due to the unique opportunities and limitations in the archaeological record, archaeologists employing CST have transformed the initial theory into a new approach. Archaeologists have broadened CST's focus on individual reproductive fitness to community-scale phenomena, such as the creation, maintenance, and subversion of social hierarchy."
- **Page**: p. 275
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (accurately characterizes the disciplinary trajectory)
- **Assessment**: Key observation for the Poverty Point project: archaeological CST has already moved beyond individual fitness to community-scale phenomena, which is exactly the scale needed for monumental construction by hunter-gatherer aggregations.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: CST is premised on the following basic elements: "(1) There is variation between individuals in a particular trait (e.g., genes, wealth); (2) Individuals signal information about personal qualities and interpret signals from others; (3) The underlying information being signaled is not immediately obvious to others; (4) Signals impose a cost on the signaler above and beyond the energetic costs of staying alive and reproducing; (5) Signals must have an audience and must provoke a response from the recipients; (6) The cost of signaling information should be substantial enough to either ensure honesty or pose a significant debt on those attempting to fake the signal; (7) Because both the signaler and recipient benefit from the exchange of information, there is long-term evolutionary stability selecting for the capacity for signaling; (8) The signal that is used to communicate underlying information may change without changing the nature of the information being exchanged."
- **Page**: p. 276
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (synthesis from Bliege Bird and Smith 2005; Boone 1998; Johnstone 1997; Smith 2010; Wright 2017, p. 548)
- **Assessment**: Comprehensive eight-element definition of CST. Elements 4-6 are critical for archaeological operationalization: signals must be costly, must have an audience, and must be honest (or at least partially honest). Element 8 is important for understanding signal change over time, relevant to the Poverty Point temporal trajectory.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "Hawkes and Bliege Bird (2002) distinguish altruistic signaling from more wasteful signals. Altruistic signals, such as hunters sharing meat or throwing feasts, benefit signal recipients in more than one way. In addition to information, the audience receives other gains (e.g., additional calories) that wasteful signals (e.g., peacock tails, conspicuous leisure) do not convey."
- **Page**: p. 277
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific distinction from the literature)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to Poverty Point. Monumental construction could be an altruistic signal if the constructed features provided direct benefits to the audience (e.g., flood protection, delineation of social space, ceremonial infrastructure), not merely information about group capacity.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "Rather than wasting resources, costly signalers receive a social benefit as a return. While the social benefit may not match the resources put in, in some cases the return may exceed what was originally signaled (e.g., using a costly signal to secure an ally that may provide assistance in times of life and death). As such, signals, even costly ones, should not necessarily be seen as wasteful."
- **Page**: p. 277
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (nuanced restatement of the theory)
- **Assessment**: Important corrective: "costly" does not mean "wasteful." The returns from signaling (alliance formation, cooperation networks) can exceed the investment. This is central to the Poverty Point argument, where monumental construction costs are offset by cooperation benefits during aggregation.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "In biology, CST individual organisms are the signalers and recipients of signals. Humans live in multi-scalar social systems, where collections of individuals (e.g., households, villages, polities) have the ability to make decisions (have agency) that may or may not reflect the choices of individuals within those collective social units. In several cases of signaling, such as large monuments (e.g., Neiman 1997), the signaler is not always a single individual. Roscoe (2009) has argued that signaling co-occurs at the individual and group levels."
- **Page**: p. 281
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (cites specific examples and scholars)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to Poverty Point. The signaler for monumental construction is necessarily a collective (the aggregated group), not an individual. Quinn identifies this as an unresolved issue: "archaeologists will have to confront how group-level signaling might work, especially as it relates to broader concepts of who is incurring the costs and reaping the benefits of signaling."

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "Glatz and Plourde (2011) also hint at polities being capable of contesting territory through costly monumental architecture."
- **Page**: p. 281
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (reference to one case study)
- **Assessment**: Monumental architecture as a territorial signal is relevant to Poverty Point's potential role as a regional marker, though territorial competition among mobile hunter-gatherers operates differently than among polities.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: "In archaeological contexts, it is difficult, if not impossible, to measure individual reproductive fitness to see if it was increased by signaling. Madsen et al. (1999), building on Dunnell's (1989) discussion of the role of waste in cultural elaboration, suggest that fitness can be conceptualized as a statistical summary of real-world interactions of individual fitness (Madsen et al. 1999, p. 258)."
- **Page**: p. 281
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (identifies a fundamental challenge and a proposed solution)
- **Assessment**: Acknowledges a core limitation for archaeological CST. Rather than measuring individual fitness, archaeologists must work with proxies and population-level patterns. This supports modeling approaches like the one used for Poverty Point.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "Human signaling can provide returns in social prestige, an alternative to reproductive or inclusive fitness (see Hamilton 1964). While the ultimate benefit of added social capital is to be able to differentially survive and reproduce (e.g., social networks to buffer against subsistence failures), the proximate benefit of signaling may not result in that ultimate benefit."
- **Page**: p. 281
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (distinguishes proximate from ultimate benefits)
- **Assessment**: Critical for the Poverty Point model. Social prestige and network formation are the proximate benefits of signaling that can be archaeologically modeled, even if the ultimate fitness consequences cannot be directly measured. The parenthetical about "social networks to buffer against subsistence failures" aligns precisely with the risk-buffering hypothesis for Poverty Point.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "One of the most important aspects in identifying costly signals in the archaeological record is to be able to distinguish costly and non-costly signals... Previously, I have distinguished costly signals and non-costly signals based on how they relate to reproductive fitness (Quinn 2015). Costly signals are more directly related to reproductive fitness than others, and some signals do not impact reproductive fitness at all. The power of a signal varies along a continuum based on how significant they are in broadcasting and enhancing reproductive fitness."
- **Page**: pp. 281-282
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (presents an analytical framework)
- **Assessment**: Quinn proposes a continuum model (Fig. 14.1) rather than a binary costly/non-costly distinction. Non-costly signals convey social information (age, gender, group membership) without fitness consequences. Costly signals are at the high end of "signaling power" where fitness is actively affected. This is relevant to distinguishing PPOs (likely non-costly identity signals) from exotic materials and monumental construction (potentially costly signals) at Poverty Point.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "Whether a signal is costly and provides a social or biological benefit is dependent on its context. Each geographic, temporal, cultural, and sociopolitical context will have different information that is important to signal. A costly signal that enhances reproductive fitness in one context may neither be costly nor provide a positive return in another context."
- **Page**: p. 282
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (logical argument)
- **Assessment**: Context-dependence is important for the Poverty Point case. What constitutes a costly signal for mobile hunter-gatherers in the Lower Mississippi Valley during 1700-1100 BCE is not the same as what would be costly in a state-level society or even in a different environmental setting.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "Socio-ecological variation impacts the cost, information, and necessity of signals. The ecological differences among environments affect the predictability of access to resources, ability to restrict access to resources, and the ability to amass surplus resources. Archaeologists have long recognized that environmental predictability and uncertain access to key economic resources significantly impact cooperation and competition among communities."
- **Page**: p. 282
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (synthesis of established principles)
- **Assessment**: Directly supports the environmental uncertainty mechanism in the Poverty Point model. Resource unpredictability creates the conditions where signaling (and the cooperation it facilitates) becomes adaptive.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "In a case study comparing Rapa Nui and Rapa Iti, DiNapoli et al. (2017) argue that costly signaling may explain the divergent histories of communal investment in large-scale construction projects. People living on Rapa Nui invested in the construction of stone statues (*moai*) and large ritual monuments (*ahu*), while people on Rapa Iti constructed fortifications amidst evidence of intergroup conflict (DiNapoli et al. 2017, p. 208). DiNapoli et al. predict that signaling should increase in contexts where key resources are more evenly distributed, as it is only through costly signals that groups can stand out from each other."
- **Page**: pp. 282-283
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific case study with testable predictions)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant as a parallel case from the same theoretical lineage as the Poverty Point project. The prediction that signaling increases where resources are evenly distributed is particularly interesting for Poverty Point's ecotone location, where access to multiple resource zones may have been relatively even across groups.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "The consequences for archaeologists are clear. There are no types of objects, raw materials, or behaviors that are inherently costly signals. Archaeologists must demonstrate, and not assume, that something is a costly signal based on characteristics of the object; its context of manufacture, use, and deposition; as well as the broader social context. Archaeologists employing CST must allow for signals to gain or lose signaling power and for signals to be either costly or non-costly."
- **Page**: p. 283
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (clear analytical directive)
- **Assessment**: A key methodological warning. For the Poverty Point manuscript, we must demonstrate rather than assume that monumental construction and exotic material acquisition functioned as costly signals. This requires showing the costs, the audience, and the benefits, not merely asserting that expensive things are signals.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "Behavioral signals are more honest and more difficult to fake than material signals. In biology, costly signals such as peacocks' tails are honest signals that the individual has the caloric resources and can withstand a high parasite load to grow a large tail. In ethnographic contexts, such as torch fishing and turtle hunting, other individuals witness the behaviors, ensuring that the individual must have had the skill to complete the task. Material culture represents an additional layer between signal and the underlying attribute being signaled that decreases a material culture's signaling power."
- **Page**: p. 283
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (logical distinction between behavioral and material signals)
- **Assessment**: Important for Poverty Point. The monumental construction itself may be a more honest signal than the exotic goods, because participation in construction is directly witnessed, whereas the provenance and acquisition costs of exotic materials are not directly observable to the audience. This distinction matters for modeling the relative signaling power of construction vs. exotic goods.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "We cannot assume that every signaling behavior will be materialized in the archaeological record. The challenges of identifying signaling behaviors in the past are expanded in the next section."
- **Page**: pp. 283-284
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (appropriate caution)
- **Assessment**: A limitation that must be acknowledged in the Poverty Point manuscript. Behavioral signals (e.g., communal fish drives, public feasting) that left little material trace may have been as important as the archaeologically visible signals.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: "The strongest criticism of CST in archaeology has been that it is too often proposed as a 'just-so-story,' an underdeveloped theoretical approach with an underdeveloped suite of predictive and testable models to be able to identify when costly signaling is and is not occurring (Codding and Jones 2007; Quinn 2015)."
- **Page**: p. 280
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (cites the primary critique)
- **Assessment**: The central methodological challenge for the Poverty Point project. The manuscript must provide testable predictions, not merely a plausible narrative. This motivates the formal modeling approach.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: "The prestige hunting hypothesis cast as CST by McGuire and Hildebrandt (McGuire and Hildebrandt 2005; McGuire et al. 2007) predicts that an increase in group size or frequency of social aggregations will spur individuals to target larger game to receive a social benefit for showing off their hunting skill (Codding et al. 2010, p. 56)."
- **Page**: p. 284
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific prediction cited)
- **Assessment**: Relevant as a parallel case: aggregation increases the audience for costly signals, which increases the incentive to signal. At Poverty Point, seasonal aggregation would similarly increase the returns to signaling by expanding the audience.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "Codding and Jones (2007; Jones and Codding 2010) challenged how McGuire and Hildebrandt applied costly signaling theory. Rather than expecting the relative portion of large taxa to increase when costly signaling in hunting was occurring, Codding and Jones suggest that prestige hunting may actually lead to an overall decrease in the archaeological abundance of large prey as only those able to incur the costs of procurement could take large game."
- **Page**: p. 284
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (presents the counter-prediction)
- **Assessment**: Illustrates how CST can generate counter-intuitive predictions, and how different operationalizations of the same theory can produce opposite archaeological expectations. The Poverty Point manuscript must specify which archaeological patterns would confirm vs. disconfirm the signaling hypothesis.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "Codding and Jones draw attention to ethnographic examples of costly hunting behaviors (e.g., Bliege Bird and Smith 2005; Bliege Bird et al. 2001; Smith and Bliege Bird 2000; Smith et al. 2003; Sosis 2000). Ethnographic studies show that while costly signaling does occur, these events are rare and do not contribute significantly to the diets of communities. Only a few individuals engage in these high-risk hunting strategies and, even then, only rarely (Codding and Jones 2007, p. 350)."
- **Page**: p. 284
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (ethnographic evidence cited)
- **Assessment**: The rarity of costly signaling behaviors in ethnographic contexts is an important constraint. For Poverty Point, this suggests that the massive scale of monumental construction may be a cumulative effect of many small costly contributions rather than a few dramatic events.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "Codding and Jones suggested restraint in extolling CST's value in archaeology until more work to model CST in archaeological contexts was completed, lest it become a 'just-so-story' with no predictive or explanatory power."
- **Page**: p. 284
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (direct quote of the critique)
- **Assessment**: Reinforces the need for the formal modeling approach taken in the Poverty Point project. Narrative plausibility is insufficient; the model must generate specific, testable predictions.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: "Galle (2010, p. 27-30) provides a template for the archaeological expectations of costly signaling in a study on material culture in eighteenth-century Chesapeake-enslaved communities... Galle proposes an abundance index that can be used to compare the distribution of imported goods across houses and between plantations... By combining testable hypotheses with quantitative measures designed to evaluate signaling strategies, Galle demonstrates how archaeologists can identify costly signaling in the archaeological record."
- **Page**: pp. 284-285
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific analytical method described)
- **Assessment**: Galle's abundance index approach could be adapted for Poverty Point: comparing the distribution and abundance of exotic materials across areas of the site and between Poverty Point and contemporary sites provides a quantitative test of signaling predictions.

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: "I have argued that the signaling power of material culture items will necessarily drop over time as they become more easily available to individuals who did not incur the cost of their acquisition or production (Quinn 2015). In such a situation, it is not that medium of costly signaling *can* change over time; it is that it *must* change if they are to continue to be honest signals."
- **Page**: p. 285
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (logical argument with testable implication)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the temporal dynamics at Poverty Point. If exotic goods became more widely available over the site's 600-year history, their signaling power would have decreased. This could drive escalation to monumental construction as the primary signal, or it could contribute to the system's eventual collapse if no replacement signal emerged.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: "As DiNapoli et al. (2017) have highlighted, there is a strong link between signaling strategies and the environment. In the uncertain and risky environment of Rapa Nui, signaling took the form of communal monument construction that emphasized cooperation (DiNapoli et al. 2017). As a consequence, less risky and unpredictable environments should diminish the payoff of communal signaling."
- **Page**: p. 285
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific prediction with cited support)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the environmental-signaling link that is central to the Poverty Point model. Environmental uncertainty drives communal signaling; environmental stability reduces its payoff. This provides the mechanism for both the emergence and potential collapse of the Poverty Point signaling system.

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: "The first application of CST in archaeology, and a key example in Bliege Bird and Smith (2005), was Neiman's (1997) examination of monumentality in Mesoamerica. Neiman developed a Darwinian theory of wasteful advertising, built on the work by Zahavi and Grafen, which sought to explain the spatial and temporal pattern of the Maya collapse. During the Maya collapse, an ecological disaster impacted the persistence of monumental architecture by spurring migration and increasing population density and audience sizes for signals."
- **Page**: p. 279
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (describes a foundational application)
- **Assessment**: Neiman's 1997 work is the first archaeological CST application and deals directly with monumental construction. The mechanism (ecological stress drives signaling through population dynamics) is parallel to what may have occurred at Poverty Point, though with different social organization.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: "One archaeological example, Kantner and Vaughn (2012) developed a model of religious adherence as a costly signal. Kantner and Vaughn postulate that (1) the strength of adherence to a religious system is an underlying attribute that is not easily visible; (2) devotees benefit from reliable information about who is, and who is not, committed to the religion; and (3) devotees benefit from broadcasting their adherence but that these signals would be ideal if they were not able to be faked by non-devotees. Using case studies from Chaco Canyon and Nasca, Kantner and Vaughn argue that devotees can signal their commitment by undertaking costly pilgrimages to religious centers."
- **Page**: p. 286
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (specific archaeological model described)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant as a parallel case: pilgrimage to a central site as a costly signal. For Poverty Point, traveling to the aggregation site from distant home ranges is analogous to pilgrimage, and the cost of travel signals commitment to the cooperative network.

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: "Monumentality and mortuary practices, which combine material and behavioral aspects of CST, continue to be an important topic for employing costly signaling in archaeology (see Neiman 1997; Church 2012). Wandsnider (2013, 2015) has explored the roles of monumental civic architecture and signaling within and between Hellenistic Greek and Greco-Roman cities. O'Driscoll (2017) has argued that highly fortified defensive hillforts in Ireland were constructed as costly signals to convey the power and strength of a community to others."
- **Page**: pp. 286-287
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (cites multiple case studies)
- **Assessment**: Establishes that community-level monumental construction as costly signaling is a recognized pattern across diverse archaeological contexts, from Mesoamerica to Ireland to the Hellenistic world. Poverty Point fits this pattern.

### Claim 28

- **Statement**: "Several authors have explored the significance of *moai* from the Easter Islands (Rapa Nui and Rapa Iti) as costly signals of island communities' competitive ability to cooperate and defend its limited resources (DiNapoli et al. 2017; Graves and Ladefoged 1995; Hunt and Lipo 2011)."
- **Page**: p. 287
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (multiple sources cited)
- **Assessment**: The Rapa Nui case is the closest theoretical parallel to the Poverty Point project, as it comes from the same research lineage. Both involve communal investment in monumental construction as a signal of group cooperative capacity.

### Claim 29

- **Statement**: Quinn presents a "general framework for assessing material culture signaling power" based on the following premises: "(1) The underlying attributes that impact reproductive fitness vary based on the social and environmental setting; (2) The costs of signals are the product of the physical attributes of the object and the social, economic, and technological systems that influence its procurement, production, distribution, and use; (3) The visibility of the signal is a product of its physical characteristics and the audiences that can see it in the situations where it is displayed; (4) The signaling power of an object is dynamic and subject to constant negotiation; (5) Signals will decrease in power the longer they stay in circulation, especially if they are able to be transferred easily between people."
- **Page**: pp. 287-288
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (systematic analytical framework)
- **Assessment**: This five-premise framework provides concrete analytical criteria for assessing signaling power in the archaeological record. Premise 5 is particularly relevant for exotic goods at Poverty Point: items that can be easily transferred (traded, gifted, inherited) lose signaling power faster than items requiring personal investment (like participation in construction).

### Claim 30

- **Statement**: Quinn identifies "several first-order variables that impact the signaling power of material culture: (1) the audience of the signal, (2) the contexts of use and discard of the signal, (3) the physical attributes of the signal, and (4) how the signal is procured, produced, and distributed." Second-order variables include: artifact attributes (size, color, sheen, raw material distance to source), production (skill level, time to manufacture, producer/consumer relationship, cost of maintenance), audience (relationship between signaler and recipient, population size/density, visibility of material culture), and context (use context: daily life, ritual, event; deposition context: caches, graves, votive offerings, loss, discard).
- **Page**: pp. 288-289 and Table 14.1
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (comprehensive analytical framework with Table 14.1)
- **Assessment**: Table 14.1 provides a checklist of variables that can be applied to Poverty Point materials. For exotic goods: raw material distance to source is a key cost variable; population size and density during aggregation is the audience variable; use context (daily vs. ritual) is a context variable. For monumental construction: skill level and time to manufacture are production variables; visibility and population density during aggregation are audience variables.

### Claim 31

- **Statement**: "The costliest signals will be difficult to produce or acquire, made of exotic or very rare raw materials, highly visible, and used in social contexts with large audiences."
- **Page**: p. 288
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (logical synthesis of the framework)
- **Assessment**: This characterization maps onto both Poverty Point's exotic goods (difficult to acquire, rare materials) and monumental construction (difficult to produce, highly visible, constructed during large aggregation events with maximum audience size).

### Claim 32

- **Statement**: "Costly material signals should rarely enter the archaeological record through loss, as their immense social value would lead people to search and recover them (see Schiffer 1987). The intentional deposition of costly signals, such as in graves, ritual caches, and other votive deposits, would provide additional benefits to the signaler by demonstrating to audiences that they are able to withstand the loss of a valuable signal."
- **Page**: pp. 288-289
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (testable prediction with supporting logic)
- **Assessment**: Generates a testable prediction for Poverty Point: if exotic materials functioned as costly signals, they should appear disproportionately in intentional depositional contexts (caches, ritual deposits, burials) rather than in general midden debris. This can be checked against the excavation data.

### Claim 33

- **Statement**: "The abundance of material signals in circulation will significantly impact the overall signaling power of material culture. As objects become more common, it is more likely that individuals who do not have the underlying attribute being signaled could come to possess the signal. As audiences encountered these non-honest signalers, the link between the material signal and the underlying attribute would be weakened. The signaling power of all objects, even those possessed by honest signalers, would be lowered. At a certain point, the increasingly abundant objects would no longer be effective costly signals."
- **Page**: p. 289
- **Type**: theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (clear causal chain)
- **Assessment**: Predicts signal inflation. For Poverty Point, if exotic goods became more abundant over time (through expanding exchange networks or increasing production), their signaling power would decrease. This could explain either escalation to larger/more costly signals or collapse of the signaling system.

### Claim 34

- **Statement**: "CST represents a dynamic frontier in evolutionary approaches to the archaeological record... CST provides a unique way of understanding (1) how this capacity first evolved, (2) how signaling fosters both cooperation and competition, and (3) how signals change over time."
- **Page**: p. 290
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (concluding synthesis)
- **Assessment**: Quinn's three dimensions of CST research align with the Poverty Point project's concerns: the cooperation-competition tension within the aggregation system, and the temporal dynamics of signaling over the site's 600-year history.

### Claim 35

- **Statement**: "In order to explain human behavior through its material consequences, archaeologists must develop theoretical models to frame research questions and identify the data necessary to answer them (Codding and Bird 2015, p. 9). The continued development of CST will require additional conceptual models as well as new case studies."
- **Page**: p. 290
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (clear directive for future work)
- **Assessment**: The Poverty Point project directly addresses this call by developing a formal model (not just a narrative) and applying it to a new case study (hunter-gatherer monumentality).

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Theoretical**: Statement about how a theory works or should be applied
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited

## Summary Assessment

Quinn provides the most comprehensive review of CST in archaeology as of 2019, tracing the theory's origins in biology, its adoption through human behavioral ecology, and its transformation by archaeologists. Five key issues are identified: (1) the challenge of translating biological CST (individual organisms as signalers) to human multi-scalar social systems where groups can also be signalers; (2) the difficulty of measuring reproductive fitness in the archaeological record; (3) distinguishing costly from non-costly signals; (4) differentiating costly behavioral signals (more honest, less archaeologically visible) from material signals (less honest, more archaeologically visible); and (5) understanding how signaling changes over time.

For the Poverty Point project, Quinn's chapter provides several essential contributions. First, it establishes the "just-so-story" critique as the central challenge, motivating the formal modeling approach. Second, the analytical framework (Table 14.1, Fig. 14.2) provides concrete variables for assessing signaling power that can be applied to Poverty Point materials. Third, the discussion of group-level signaling (Roscoe 2009; Glatz and Plourde 2011) legitimizes the community-scale signaling proposed for Poverty Point. Fourth, the emphasis on environmental context and the DiNapoli et al. (2017) case study connecting environmental uncertainty to communal monumental signaling provides direct theoretical support for the Poverty Point hypothesis. Fifth, the signal inflation mechanism (signals lose power as they become common) provides a testable prediction for the temporal dynamics at Poverty Point.
