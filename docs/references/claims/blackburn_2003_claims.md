# Claims Extraction: Blackburn 2003

**Full citation**: Blackburn, Candler. 2003. The Use of Magnetite and Hematite in the Poverty Point Culture. *Central States Archaeological Journal* 50(1):24-26.
**PDF filename**: Blackburn-2003-The use of magnetite and hematite in the Poverty Point Culture.pdf
**PDF version**: scan; page numbers correspond to journal pagination (pp. 24-26)
**Date extracted**: 2026-03-21
**Extractor**: Claude Code

**Note**: This article appears in an avocational/collector-oriented journal, not a peer-reviewed academic outlet. The author writes from French Camp, Mississippi, and appears to be an amateur archaeologist/collector. Claims should be evaluated accordingly.

## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "Along the lower Mississippi River and its tributaries there exists a little understood culture identified by Webb, 1968: The Poverty Point Culture." There are "some 30 Poverty Point sites known in Mississippi and Louisiana."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (the number 30 is stated without specific enumeration and differs from other published counts)
- **Assessment**: Provides a rough estimate of the number of Poverty Point-period sites. Other sources give different counts, so this figure should not be treated as authoritative.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "Two rather important and significant sites of this culture are the Poverty Point site mentioned above, on Bayou Macon (Ford, Phillips, and Haag, 1955), and the Jakestown Site in Humphreys County, Mississippi, near Belzoni, Mississippi, on the Yazoo River in west central Mississippi."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Identifies two major Poverty Point-period sites. Jakestown is relevant as a secondary node in the exchange network.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "The Jakestown Site was occupied off and on for more than 3,000 years, up to and including the Mississippian Cultural Period. The Poverty Point Site in Louisiana appears to have been occupied only during the Archaic Period with radiocarbon dates covering the 3,300 to 2,200 BP span."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (the radiocarbon range of 3,300-2,200 BP is broader than currently accepted calibrated dates and differs from modern chronological assessments; the claim that Poverty Point was occupied "only during the Archaic Period" oversimplifies)
- **Assessment**: The date range is roughly consistent with the Late Archaic occupation but imprecise by current standards. More recent work (e.g., Kidder et al.) provides tighter chronological control.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "Both sites have a rich and standard complex of stone tools and show traditions with the presence of conical burial mounds."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (the characterization of mounds as "conical burial mounds" conflates Poverty Point mound forms; Mound A at the Poverty Point site is not conical)
- **Assessment**: The description of mounds is imprecise. The presence of conical mounds at both sites does suggest shared cultural practices, but the characterization oversimplifies the mound architecture.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "At the Louisiana site there exists not only different earthen works than those usually found during this time period but also an effigy mound in the shape of an eagle or some other bird of prey with wings out stretched."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (the bird effigy mound interpretation is widely accepted, though the specific species identification remains uncertain)
- **Assessment**: Confirms the distinctive and unusual nature of Poverty Point's monumental architecture, which is relevant to the signaling argument: the earthworks are unlike those at any contemporary site.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "Objects from the site include tubular pipes of clay and stone, sandstone whetstones and saws, adzes, small celts of greenstone and granite, atlatl weights (bannerstones) of several types, boatstones made from soapstone (steatite), hematite and magnetite plummets, steatite and sandstone vessels, numerous heavy stemmed and notched projectile points (Motley, Gary, Pontchartrain Types I and II, and Pelican), assorted scrapers, clay 'cooking balls,' the unusual Poverty Point objects such as owl effigies exquisitely carved and polished from jasper, and micro-flint industry tools: micro-blades and drills."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (this is a general inventory; individual items are well-documented in other sources)
- **Assessment**: Provides a comprehensive list of artifact types found at the Poverty Point site, including multiple exotic materials (greenstone, granite, steatite, hematite, magnetite, jasper). The diversity of materials, many from distant sources, is directly relevant to the costly signaling argument regarding exotic goods acquisition and display.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "Many of the items found at Poverty Point sites, especially some of the stone, are not indigenous to the areas in which they are found, i.e. galena or lead ore, jasper, greenstone, hematite, magnetite, steatite, granite, as well as clear silica or quartz crystals."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (the non-local character of these materials is well-established in the literature)
- **Assessment**: Core claim for the signaling argument. The range of non-local materials at Poverty Point sites confirms an extensive exchange network. The costliness of acquiring these distant materials is central to their potential function as honest signals.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: "Projectile points have been found at the Jakestown Site fashioned from Tallahata quartzite brought from one of the main sources for such stone, Lauderdale County near Meridian, Mississippi, which is well over 100 miles southeast of Jakestown."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents one specific long-distance material procurement link (Tallahata quartzite, >100 miles). Relevant to the exchange network argument.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "Points made from the same stone have also been found in non-Poverty Point sites throughout the Mississippi Delta."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (no specific sites or quantities cited)
- **Assessment**: Suggests that long-distance stone procurement was not exclusive to Poverty Point sites, which could complicate the argument that exotic materials served as distinctive signals within the Poverty Point system.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "The stones and subsequent artifacts that are of particular interest to this investigator are hematite and magnetite specimens and the plummets. Some are scored with a top with a single circle designed to be the fastening point for the artifact, and others are drilled with a single hole near the top, with many having concentric circles etched below the hole, sometimes 2, 3, or more."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (based on personal observation of specimens)
- **Assessment**: Describes the physical characteristics of hematite and magnetite plummets. The investment in scoring, drilling, and engraving concentric circles on these objects suggests they were more than simple utilitarian items.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "Hematite is a relatively hard substance, having a hardness factor of between 5.5 and 6.5 on a scale of 10, with the hardest naturally occurring substance known being a diamond."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (standard mineralogical data)
- **Assessment**: The hardness of hematite is relevant to the costly signaling framework: shaping and drilling hematite plummets required substantial labor investment due to the material's hardness, making these objects genuinely costly to produce.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "Hematite is 70% metallic iron, a composition of Fe2O3. It was brought to Poverty Point sites from somewhere in Missouri, Arkansas, Kentucky or other states."
- **Page**: p. 24
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (the chemical composition is accurate; the source attributions are vague)
- **Assessment**: Documents both the composition and non-local origin of hematite, supporting its classification as an exotic material requiring long-distance acquisition.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "Magnetite has basically the same hardness factor of hematite, 5.5 to 6.5. It, too, is metallic iron, 73.4%, with a chemical composition of Fe3 O4."
- **Page**: p. 24-25
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (standard mineralogical data)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the material properties of magnetite, relevant to understanding the labor cost of working this material into plummets and other artifacts.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "Magnetically polarized magnetite is also known as lodestone. However, in open flame it is oxidized to non-magnetic hematite."
- **Page**: p. 25
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (standard mineralogical fact)
- **Assessment**: The transformation of magnetite to hematite through heating is a minor point but indicates that the Poverty Point people would have needed to understand the material properties of these minerals if using them near fire.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "The source for magnetite east of the Mississippi River is Pennsylvania and the Adirondacks of New York. It can also be found in the west in Utah. But chances are that the source for magnetite utilized by these people was east of the Mississippi. The Poverty Point people most probably secured their magnetite from the same source."
- **Page**: p. 25
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (the source attribution is speculative; no sourcing analysis is cited)
- **Assessment**: If magnetite was sourced from Pennsylvania or the Adirondacks, this would represent one of the longest-distance exchange connections in the Poverty Point system (comparable to Great Lakes copper). However, the claim lacks supporting geochemical sourcing data and should be treated as a hypothesis, not an established fact.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "The unique characteristic of magnetite is that it is magnetic, hence the name. A piece of magnetite has a north and south polarity, the same as any industrially made magnet. If one were to cut magnetite in half, it would automatically re-polarize itself."
- **Page**: p. 25
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (standard mineralogical property)
- **Assessment**: The magnetic property of magnetite is central to the author's speculative argument about compass use, but is also relevant as a distinctive material property that would have made magnetite objects unusual and potentially valued as exotic items with remarkable physical characteristics.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: The author proposes that Poverty Point people may have "known the magnetic properties of magnetite and used it as a primitive compass." He argues: "It is well known that they had elaborate trade routes throughout the country. A magnetite compass could very well explain how they moved along these routes and found their way to such far away places as the Adirondacks, Missouri and Yellowstone."
- **Page**: p. 25
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence (no archaeological evidence is cited to support compass use; the argument is purely inferential)
- **Assessment**: This is the central interpretive claim of the article and is highly speculative. There is no archaeological evidence that Poverty Point people used magnetite as a compass. The author himself acknowledges this is a theory to be debated: "Whether there can be any credence to a theory that Poverty Point people, as well as others east of the Mississippi River, employed magnetite as a compass is open to debate." This claim should not be used in the signaling manuscript.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: Regarding an article by Fraser (2002) on "A Cache of Archaic Plummets From Christian County, Kentucky," the author notes that "the plummets pictured most certainly do bear a great resemblance to Poverty Point plummets. As mentioned, the traits of the Kentucky plummets are of Poverty Point style with some differences: the Kentucky plummets are made of hematite, and the perforations as well as the size, diameter and weight are different."
- **Page**: p. 25
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (based on comparison of published photographs and descriptions)
- **Assessment**: Documents Poverty Point-style plummets found outside the core Poverty Point region (Christian County, Kentucky), suggesting the spread of artifact styles through exchange networks. The differences in material and dimensions between Kentucky and Louisiana/Mississippi plummets could reflect local production emulating Poverty Point forms.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point Culture plummets found in the Mississippi site and examined by this researcher have perforations drilled from one side only without the countersinking seen in the Kentucky plummets, the drilling being much like that which is seen in bannerstones. Bannerstone drilling is believed to have been done with a swamp cane drill."
- **Page**: p. 25-26
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (based on personal examination of specimens)
- **Assessment**: Documents a specific manufacturing technique (unidirectional drilling) for Poverty Point plummets from Mississippi, and a probable tool (swamp cane drill). The labor required to drill hard hematite/magnetite with organic tools is relevant to the costly signaling argument: these objects required significant time and skill investment.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "The drilled or perforated plummets from Mississippi and Louisiana sites are almost exclusively fashioned from magnetite, while the grooved plummets such as the Snyder type pictured in the Fraser article, are almost exclusively made from hematite."
- **Page**: p. 26
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (based on personal observation; no quantitative data provided)
- **Assessment**: Documents a material-form correlation: drilled/perforated plummets tend to be magnetite, while grooved plummets tend to be hematite. This pattern of material selection suggests intentional choice based on material properties or cultural convention.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "These differences in Mississippi and Louisiana plummets and the Kentucky ones constitute the impetus for the article written by this contributor. The differences also give rise to the theory that the plummets from the Southern Poverty Point sites held dual purposes: The grooved plummets were used as weights; the perforated magnetite ones, perhaps, as compasses."
- **Page**: p. 26
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence (the dual-purpose hypothesis is presented without supporting evidence beyond the material difference)
- **Assessment**: The proposed dual function is speculative. However, the underlying observation that grooved (hematite) and perforated (magnetite) plummets may have had different functions is worth noting, even if the specific compass hypothesis is unsupported.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: "The plummets' duality of purpose seems to be in keeping with the thoughts of Gregory Perino and David Lutz, for these gentlemen suggest that plummets were used as burial offerings as well as weights."
- **Page**: p. 26
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (attributed to Perino and Lutz without specific citation)
- **Assessment**: The use of plummets as burial offerings would be relevant to the signaling framework, as burial goods often function as status markers. However, the claim is not directly sourced.

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: "The fact that the Kentucky plummets were found outside their 'known distribution zone and buried with care along with another ornamental object' perhaps suggests that these plummets reached Kentucky via trade routes established by the Poverty Point and/or other Archaic peoples. This would give some credence to their having been trade items."
- **Page**: p. 26
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (the contextual information about burial with care and outside the distribution zone supports the interpretation, though it comes from Fraser 2002, not primary data)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the signaling argument. If plummets were trade items that moved through exchange networks and were deposited in burial contexts with care, they functioned as valued prestige goods. Their appearance outside the core distribution zone suggests they carried social value beyond utilitarian function.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: "Another possible use for the perforated plummets, is they fashioned from hematite or magnetite, may have in the construction of homes, temples, and other structures built by these people."
- **Page**: p. 26
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence (no evidence provided for use in construction)
- **Assessment**: This claim is presented without support and is not useful for the signaling argument.

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: "The bird effigy mound and the semi-circular earthen works built at the Poverty Point Site in northeast Louisiana" are described, and the author notes that "Plummets do bear a strong resemblance to modern plumbobs employed in the construction business today."
- **Page**: p. 26
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence (resemblance to modern plumb bobs does not demonstrate that function)
- **Assessment**: The comparison to modern plumb bobs is an untested analogy. Not useful for the signaling manuscript.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited; claim is stated as fact without support

## Summary Assessment

This article, published in an avocational journal, provides limited but useful information for the Poverty Point costly signaling project. Its primary contributions are: (1) confirming the range of non-local exotic materials found at Poverty Point sites, including magnetite and hematite; (2) documenting specific material properties of magnetite and hematite (hardness 5.5-6.5 on Mohs scale, 70-73.4% metallic iron) that make them costly to work; (3) noting a geographic pattern where drilled/perforated plummets are magnetite while grooved plummets are hematite; and (4) reporting that Poverty Point-style plummets appear outside the core region (Kentucky), suggesting exchange network distribution. The article's central hypothesis that magnetite plummets served as compasses is entirely speculative and unsupported by evidence. The source should be used cautiously given its non-peer-reviewed status and the author's amateur standing, but the descriptive observations about material properties and plummet characteristics can supplement more rigorous sources.
