## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "At 1200 B.C., the Poverty Point site was an important cultural center with trade connections extending over much of what is today the eastern United States." (Byrd, Foreword)
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Establishes the basic characterization of Poverty Point as a trade hub, directly relevant to the signaling model's premise about exchange networks.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: The Poverty Point culture "is a prehistoric manifestation in the southern United States, transitional in nature, that participates in the American Formative shift from Archaic band existence to a hamlet-village-regional center-great ceremonial center complex with ranked societal organization possibly on a chiefdom level." (Webb)
- **Page**: p. 3
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Webb's characterization of Poverty Point as potentially chiefdom-level organization. The costly signaling model offers an alternative explanation for the apparent complexity without requiring permanent hierarchical organization.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point culture seems to have had its beginnings by 2000 B.C., to have been well developed by 1500 B.C., and to have reached its climax at about 1000 B.C., then declined, to be followed by Tchefuncte-Tchula about 600 B.C." (Webb)
- **Page**: p. 3
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Provides the chronological framework. The 1400-year span and eventual decline are relevant to modeling the rise and collapse of the signaling system.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "The locations of Poverty Point sites are most often on natural levees and terraces that border major rivers in the lower central United States from the Missouri boot-heel to the Gulf of Mexico. It is essentially a riverine culture." (Webb)
- **Page**: p. 3
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The riverine focus supports the ecotone-positioning hypothesis in the project model. Site placement along waterways is relevant to understanding trade routes and resource access.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "The major known sites are in clusters on relict channels of the Mississippi-Ohio and Arkansas rivers." (Webb)
- **Page**: p. 3
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Site clustering on relict channels is important for understanding aggregation dynamics. These geomorphic settings provided rich ecotonal environments.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "Trade contacts from Oklahoma to the Appalachians and from the Great Lakes to the Gulf of Mexico have been emphasized previously." (Webb)
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms the vast geographic scope of the exchange network, central to the argument that exotic goods acquisition functioned as costly signals.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "Yet to be answered are the questions of how much of this trade was direct, how much through intermediaries, how much was centrally controlled and how much was regionally independent. Probably there is some of all of this involved." (Webb)
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence
- **Assessment**: Acknowledges that the structure of the trade network remains poorly understood. Whether exchange was centrally controlled or independently organized has implications for whether goods functioned as individual-level signals or group-level signals.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: Bruseth proposes that Claiborne, located "at what was then the confluence of the Mississippi River with the Gulf of Mexico, may have been a deliberate attempt to control the trade of exotic materials between the gulf and the Mississippi River." (Bruseth)
- **Page**: p. 7 (abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The gateway model suggests strategic positioning for trade control, relevant to understanding how exotic materials were channeled through the system.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: Bruseth proposes "a two-phased, multiple gateway model for Poverty Point development in the Lower Mississippi Valley. According to this model, the initial phase consisted of several Poverty Point site clusters, each located at the confluence of major arteries of trade. At this time, the Poverty Point site would have been of equivalent importance to Claiborne." (Bruseth)
- **Page**: p. 7 (abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The multiple gateway model suggests that Poverty Point's dominance was not inevitable but emerged from competition among sites. This is consistent with a signaling model where the most successful aggregation site attracted the most participation.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "During the next phase the type site became the preeminent gateway community controlling trade throughout the Lower Mississippi Valley, and the Claiborne site (and perhaps other regional gateways) declined in importance or were abandoned." (Bruseth)
- **Page**: p. 7 (abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The consolidation phase, where Poverty Point becomes dominant after 1500-1200 B.C., could represent a "winner-take-all" dynamic in the signaling system.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "Gateway communities develop either as a response to increased trade or to the settling of sparsely populated frontier areas. They generally are located along natural corridors of communication and at the critical passages between areas of high mineral, agricultural, or craft productivity." (Bruseth, quoting Hirth 1978)
- **Page**: p. 21 (vol. pagination)
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (as a theoretical framework)
- **Assessment**: The gateway model provides a complementary framework for understanding Poverty Point's location at the convergence of multiple trade routes.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "The Poverty Point site is positioned where the northern trade routes (Ouachita, Arkansas, and Mississippi rivers) may have been closest together and thus would have been situated to dominate much of the flow of critical raw materials in the Lower Mississippi Valley." (Bruseth, citing Gibson 1980)
- **Page**: p. 22 (vol. pagination)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the project's question of why this location became the regional center. The convergence of trade routes at this location is consistent with the ecotone/aggregation hypothesis.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "There are two major and two minor Poverty Point site clusters in northeast Louisiana." Locality A centered around Poverty Point, Locality B along Bayou Bonne Idee, Locality C along lower Big Creek, and Locality D along the Ouachita near Monroe. (Kidder)
- **Page**: p. 30
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The clustering pattern supports a model of multiple semi-independent social groups that periodically aggregated at the primary center. Each cluster had its own regional center.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "It is possible, however, to imagine that the Poverty Point settlement system could revolve around seasonal movements of short spatial and chronological duration, from floodplain to the edge of Macon Ridge. Such movements would be very difficult to detect archaeologically." (Kidder)
- **Page**: p. 35
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Directly supports the seasonal aggregation model proposed in this project. Kidder suggests that seasonal mobility between floodplain and ridge locations was plausible.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: Neimeyer-Dare "served as the focal point for the redistribution of two material classes, magnetite and novaculite" and "there is a positive relationship between novaculite quantity and distance from the Neimeyer-Dare site." (Kidder)
- **Page**: pp. 37, 40
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Provides evidence for hierarchical redistribution of exotic materials from regional centers, consistent with a signaling model where centers served as nodes for displaying and exchanging signal goods.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "Magnetite was apparently brought in as pre-worked nodules. At Neimeyer-Dare these nodules were further worked into plummets or plummet blanks. Presumably these tools were then introduced into the local exchange network." (Kidder, from Discussion section)
- **Page**: p. 197
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Demonstrates craft specialization at regional centers for exotic materials. The production of finished signal goods (plummets) at specific centers suggests organized manufacture of items with display/status value.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: "The basic economy of the Poverty Point culture was on an Archaic level" and "the basic form and content of Poverty Point culture is essentially Archaic. Although the culture has elaborations that are spectacular in the context of southeastern and midwestern prehistory, the economic base was apparently little changed from preceding cultures." (Kidder)
- **Page**: p. 46
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Critical for the project's argument: the subsistence base was hunter-gatherer, meaning that the monumental construction and long-distance exchange represent "add-ons" rather than products of agricultural surplus. This is exactly what the costly signaling model predicts.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: "There was clearly a process of social specialization and the need for establishing social identity. Long-distance trade may have served to provide individuals or groups with items that set them apart from other people or cultures." (Kidder)
- **Page**: p. 47
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Kidder directly suggests that exotic goods functioned to establish social identity, aligning with the costly signaling framework where exotic goods serve as honest signals of capacity or status.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "Given the Archaic economic base of the Poverty Point culture it seems unlikely that any one individual or group could have maintained enough economic power to perpetuate the social cohesion of such a large and disparate culture." (Kidder)
- **Page**: p. 47
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Kidder questions whether chiefdom-level control was sustainable on a hunter-gatherer base. This supports the project's alternative model where cooperation was maintained through signaling rather than centralized political authority.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "I suggest that the territories evolved into their form as a result of autonomous indigenous developments" and "the adaptations manifested in the Poverty Point period were distinctly local." (Kidder)
- **Page**: p. 47
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The autonomy of regional clusters is consistent with a model of voluntary aggregation rather than coerced participation. Each locality maintained its own cultural base.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "Ultimately, for reasons that we do not yet comprehend, the society could not be sustained. Thus, in a historical sense, Poverty Point culture was a failed experiment." (Kidder)
- **Page**: p. 48
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence
- **Assessment**: The eventual collapse of the system is a key question for the project model. Understanding what changed (environmental conditions, diminishing returns to signaling, etc.) is essential.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point does not have, with perhaps the addition of squash, a really revolutionary subsistence base." (Kidder, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 195
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Reinforces the fundamental puzzle: how did hunter-gatherers sustain the level of investment evident at Poverty Point without an agricultural surplus?

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: "When Poverty Point begins to function, you start seeing intensification of trade. Then you see a lot of different trade materials and ideas and clay ball types and things like that apparently moving, or ideas moving. Something has been stimulated along these trade lines." (Gibson, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 196
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The intensification of trade coincident with the site's operation supports the idea that the aggregation site catalyzed exchange networks.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: "We know that this trade is crossing ethnic boundaries and probably crossing language boundaries. These are certainly groups of people that operate mostly unto themselves most of the time. There are strangers involved." (Gibson, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 196
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Highly relevant to the signaling model. Inter-group exchange among "strangers" is exactly the context where costly signals would be needed to facilitate cooperation and reduce conflict.

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: "The patterns of exchange are what I think are so different. Whereas, during the Late Archaic you are getting a down-the-line exchange, it starts to be more of a directional exchange with Poverty Point." (Lehmann, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 196
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The shift from down-the-line to directional exchange suggests increasing centralization and organization of trade networks during the Poverty Point period, consistent with the emergence of a signaling system.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: "Trade networks were in existence during Archaic time. Poverty Point peoples may have maximized or expanded these existing networks." (Williams, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 196
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The expansion of pre-existing networks suggests that Poverty Point built on earlier exchange infrastructure, which is consistent with a gradual emergence of costly signaling behaviors.

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: Jackson finds that at the Copes site, "the subsistence remains point to a highly selective use of the floodplain environment" with "plant procurement centered on nut resources." Pecan occurs more frequently than acorn (74% vs. 52% of samples), and "pecans, fish, and deer provided the backbone of the subsistence system." (Jackson)
- **Page**: pp. 133, 139, 149
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides detailed subsistence data from a satellite site 13 km from Poverty Point. The selective, high-quality diet argues against population pressure, which is relevant to understanding why people aggregated (for cooperation benefits rather than subsistence stress).

### Claim 28

- **Statement**: "Multiple lines of seasonal data point to site use from early spring (March) through late fall (November or December). Evidence for mid-winter occupation of the site is weakest." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 139
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides the best available seasonality data for a Poverty Point satellite community. Near year-round occupation with possible winter abandonment is consistent with seasonal aggregation patterns.

### Claim 29

- **Statement**: Jackson identifies significant variability in nut production: "average yields about 30-45% maximum yields and poorest yields between 2 and 7%." During poor production years, "sweetgum-loamy ridge oak forests might produce so little mast that, in essence, no nuts would have been available in a given stand." (Jackson)
- **Page**: pp. 144-145
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the project's environmental uncertainty parameter (sigma). The high variability in nut yields (a primary caloric resource) provides empirical support for the kind of environmental uncertainty that the signaling model requires to make cooperation adaptive.

### Claim 30

- **Statement**: "Synchronized high production of all oaks probably occurs no more often than once in every 10 years." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 144
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (based on forestry studies, not direct archaeological measurement)
- **Assessment**: The infrequency of high-production years means that bad years are common, supporting the argument that resource uncertainty was a persistent challenge requiring cooperative risk-pooling.

### Claim 31

- **Statement**: "Alternative collecting areas, a shift to less nutritious, 'second line' resources, or reliance on the productive capacity of other social groups would be required during these years." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 145
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Jackson explicitly identifies inter-group reliance as a response to resource failure, directly supporting the project's argument that cooperation and risk-pooling were adaptive strategies.

### Claim 32

- **Statement**: "The highly selective nature of plant resource use is relevant in regards to previous interpretations of the Poverty Point system. Such a strategy seems possible only under conditions of low regional population density, yet such a strategy was employed by a community only 13 km from Poverty Point." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 149
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The low population density inferred from the selective subsistence strategy challenges models of Poverty Point as a densely populated center with several thousand permanent residents.

### Claim 33

- **Statement**: "The independence of the Copes site community, at least with respect to subsistence provisioning, implies that if the Poverty Point phase represents a chiefdom, it did not serve a redistributive role." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 150
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The absence of redistributive provisioning contradicts chiefdom models and supports the signaling model where cooperation was voluntary rather than politically coerced.

### Claim 34

- **Statement**: A total of 6,352 steatite sherds and 18 steatite vessels are known from 39 prehistoric sites. 6,081 sherds (95.7%) and five vessels are from the Poverty Point site alone. Other sites with significant quantities include Claiborne (117 sherds, 10 vessels), Jaketown (23 sherds), and J.W. Copes (27 sherds). (Smith)
- **Page**: pp. 173-174
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The extreme concentration of steatite at the Poverty Point site (95.7% of all known sherds) suggests that this exotic material was concentrated at the primary center, consistent with a model where display/signaling occurred predominantly at the aggregation site.

### Claim 35

- **Statement**: Neutron activation analysis confirms the southern Appalachians as the source of steatite found at Poverty Point sites, with specific quarry locales identified: "Green Quarry, North Carolina; Jackson Hill Quarry, Georgia; Soapstone Ridge Quarry, Georgia; and the ITA15 Quarry site, Alabama." (Smith)
- **Page**: p. 177
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms the long-distance transport of steatite (approximately 800-900 km), establishing the costliness of acquisition that is central to the signaling argument.

### Claim 36

- **Statement**: "Presumably, based on the REE curve similarities and the geographic distribution of sites with steatite, Late Archaic and Poverty Point peoples were utilizing the Tennessee, Ohio, and Mississippi rivers to transport steatite from prehistoric quarries." (Smith)
- **Page**: p. 177
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Identifies the river routes used for long-distance transport of exotic materials.

### Claim 37

- **Statement**: A distributional study of galena "indicates a disproportionate occurrence at the Poverty Point site. Out of a total of 724 galena cubes or modules at all Poverty Point sites, 702 (97%) were from the Poverty Point site." (Smith, citing Walthall et al. 1982)
- **Page**: p. 177
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Like steatite, galena is overwhelmingly concentrated at the primary site. The pattern of extreme concentration of exotics at Poverty Point supports the aggregation-and-display model.

### Claim 38

- **Statement**: "Although the Poverty Point site clearly functioned as a regional distribution center for some exotic lithic materials, little galena or steatite was distributed to other sites." (Smith)
- **Page**: p. 177
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The lack of redistribution contradicts a centralized redistribution model and is more consistent with a display/signaling function where exotics were accumulated and displayed at the central aggregation site rather than redistributed to outlying communities.

### Claim 39

- **Statement**: Johnson finds that prepared core (microlith) technology in the Yazoo Basin is restricted to Jaketown and Paxton Brake, two sites separated by less than 6.5 km. "The relative abundance of the microlith technology at the major centers suggests a direct relationship to the factors that made these two sites prominent among all known Poverty Point components." (Johnson, quoting Webb and Gibson 1981)
- **Page**: pp. 182-183
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The concentration of specialized craft production at regional centers supports the model of centers as nodes for specialized activities, including production of signal goods.

### Claim 40

- **Statement**: "At Jaketown many magnetite and hematite blanks were discovered while at the neighboring smaller sites blanks were relatively rare, although many finished magnetite and hematite plummets were found. This suggests that plummet manufacture probably took place at Jaketown." (Byrd, Future Research Directions)
- **Page**: p. 202
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Craft specialization in plummet manufacture at regional centers indicates organized production of items that may have functioned as signal goods.

### Claim 41

- **Statement**: "Poverty Point holds a fascination for professional archaeologists and non-archaeologists alike" because "this important site, located near the town of Epps in northeast Louisiana, had extensive earthworks and a plan site of most unusual proportions. In fact, the main site is so large that its true dimensions and spatial configurations were not recognized until noted on aerial photographs in the early 1950s." (Byrd, Foreword)
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The sheer scale of the earthworks, not fully recognized until aerial photography, underscores the enormous labor investment, a central concern for the signaling model.

### Claim 42

- **Statement**: "It is not only the clusters and intervening areas that differ. The artifact assemblages from the various clusters show considerable diversity. Artifacts, exotic trade materials, and the type of craft specialization vary from area to area." (Byrd, Future Research Directions)
- **Page**: p. 201
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Regional diversity in exotic materials and specialization supports a model of semi-independent groups rather than a monolithic culture controlled from a single center.

### Claim 43

- **Statement**: "Evidence indicates that material from as far away as Oklahoma, Virginia, the Great Lakes, and the Gulf of Mexico arrived at the Poverty Point site." (Byrd, Future Research Directions)
- **Page**: p. 202
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms the geographic extent of the exchange network spanning approximately 1,600 km.

### Claim 44

- **Statement**: "Have we ever considered that maybe the Poverty Point people weren't getting anything from the Gulf in return? I mean the returns might not have been very material." (Gibson, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 196
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence
- **Assessment**: Gibson's suggestion that the trade was asymmetric (material goods flowing one direction, possibly non-material returns the other) is relevant to signaling theory, where the value of exchange may be in the social relationship rather than material reciprocity.

### Claim 45

- **Statement**: "I think we can see that the technology is similar; but the societies are so localized and so tradition-ridden with their roots back in the local antiquity, that we are always going to find local peculiarities." (Gibson, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 196
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The persistence of local traditions despite participation in a shared interaction sphere supports a model of voluntary aggregation where distinct groups maintained their identities.

### Claim 46

- **Statement**: The fishing component of the Copes subsistence system "seems to be the key to the evolution of a basically sedentary settlement system in the first place. The high productivity of fishing is central to overall system structure, since fish represent an abundant and localized source of meat in the backwater lakes and oxbows of the Mississippi River floodplain." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 149
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Fish as an anchor for sedentism is relevant to understanding what made the aggregation site viable. Productive fisheries could have supported seasonal concentrations of people.

### Claim 47

- **Statement**: "It now appears that the Poverty Point people were mainly hunters and gatherers with some emphasis on horticulture (squash). Evidence seems to suggest that at least some Poverty Point people were sedentary during most of the year, although this may vary to some extent from area to area." (Byrd, Future Research Directions)
- **Page**: p. 202
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Confirms the hunter-gatherer base with some variability in sedentism, consistent with a fission-fusion model where some groups were more sedentary and others more mobile.

### Claim 48

- **Statement**: "One could say 'Let's make steatite one of the important things to define Poverty Point,' but is it? ... you get this large amount of steatite here at Poverty Point, but you go 30 miles west into the Boeuf Basin, and we get only three pieces of steatite after four years of survey." (Williams, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 193-194
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The extreme drop-off in steatite away from the primary site reinforces the interpretation that exotic materials were concentrated at the aggregation center, not broadly distributed.

### Claim 49

- **Statement**: "I look at my Late Archaic groups and I can see basically what amounts to a base camp and a number of scattered hamlets or extraction camps. However, by the time we get into what can be considered Poverty Point, we have a hierarchical organization." (Kidder, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 195
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The emergence of site hierarchy during the Poverty Point period, in the absence of economic transformation, suggests that social organization (potentially including signaling mechanisms) was the primary driver of change.

### Claim 50

- **Statement**: "Non-local lithic raw materials were traded, including quartz, novaculite, galena, white chert, hematite, magnetite, dover chert, northern gray chert, argillite, bigfork chert, fluorite, copper, banded slate, red banded chert, hornblende, mica, chlorite schists, brown sandstone, orthoquartzite, and steatite or soapstone." (Smith)
- **Page**: p. 173
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Enumerates the full range of exotic lithic materials in the trade network. The diversity of materials from multiple distant sources reinforces the costliness of acquisition.

### Claim 51

- **Statement**: "Webb (1977) has stated that there also must have been an equally extensive trade in perishables such as salt, furs and hides, feathers, wood products, shells, and exotic foods." (Smith, citing Webb)
- **Page**: p. 173
- **Type**: Speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence
- **Assessment**: The likely invisible perishable trade means the archaeological record underrepresents the actual volume and diversity of exchange. The signaling system may have been even more elaborate than the lithic evidence suggests.

### Claim 52

- **Statement**: The Copes site "covers approximately 0.5-0.75 ha" and "is located on a remnant natural levee ridge segment, adjacent to a low-lying arcuate brake" interpreted as "an abandoned channel segment of the Arkansas River Joe's Bayou meander belt which flowed through the area between 4000 and 3000 B.C." and "was likely a large open oxbow lake at the time the site was occupied." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 132
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The small size and specific geomorphic setting of Copes provides a baseline for understanding the scale of satellite communities relative to the massive Poverty Point site.

### Claim 53

- **Statement**: Three AMS radiocarbon dates from the Copes site: "3040 plus or minus 100 years: 1090 B.C. (Beta-9580, Carya sp. nutshell); 3170 plus or minus 90 years: 1220 B.C. (Beta-9581, acorn nutshell); and 3440 plus or minus 90 years: 1490 B.C. (A-4176, squash seed fragment)." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 133
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The dates (1490-1090 B.C.) place the Copes occupation during "the postulated period of increased interregional interaction and presumably also earthwork building activities at the Poverty Point site."

### Claim 54

- **Statement**: "Does Poverty Point exist as a chiefdom or as a culture on the local level, with the exchange of high status trade objects, which had little effect on the average individual?" (Byrd, Future Research Directions)
- **Page**: p. 203
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: N/A (research question)
- **Assessment**: This question, posed as a future research direction in 1991, is precisely what the costly signaling model attempts to address: was Poverty Point a chiefdom, or was the apparent complexity a product of exchange and display behaviors without centralized political authority?

### Claim 55

- **Statement**: "Novaculite trade started early and it looks like it may have run itself independent of the whole Poverty Point trade net." (Gibson, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 198
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The possibility of multiple overlapping trade networks, some predating Poverty Point, suggests that the signaling system built on pre-existing exchange relationships.

### Claim 56

- **Statement**: "Some materials fall off so sharply south of Neimeyer-Dare, especially novaculite, may be because the Neimeyer-Dare site is interdicting that trade." (Kidder, from Discussion)
- **Page**: p. 197
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Evidence for trade interdiction by regional centers suggests competitive dynamics in the exchange network, consistent with signaling theory where controlling access to signal goods provides advantages.

### Claim 57

- **Statement**: At the Copes site, the faunal assemblage includes "representatives of 17 mammalian species, 28 different birds, 18 reptilian taxa, two amphibian genera, and 26 mutually exclusive fish taxa" but "the overall assemblage composition indicates a decided emphasis on the procurement of fish and large mammals (deer). Small mammals, birds, reptiles, and amphibians comprise only 15% of the NISP." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 134
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The high faunal diversity but selective procurement pattern supports the interpretation that Poverty Point communities operated in a resource-rich environment without significant demographic pressure.

### Claim 58

- **Statement**: Exotic materials at the Copes site include "Arkansas novaculites, Dover, Pickwick and other Ft. Payne Formation chert varieties, possibly Big Fork chert from Oklahoma, and gray cherts from midwestern sources" as well as "quartz crystal, fluorite, galena, steatite, hematite, magnetite, slate, bitumen, a red jasper bead, and a crinoid fossil bead." (Jackson)
- **Page**: p. 133
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The presence of diverse exotic materials at a small satellite site 13 km from Poverty Point indicates that even small communities participated in the broader exchange network.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Summary Assessment

This edited volume provides foundational data on multiple dimensions of the Poverty Point system directly relevant to the costly signaling argument. Its most valuable contributions are: (1) Jackson's detailed analysis of nut mast variability, showing average yields at only 30-45% of maximum and poor years at 2-7%, which provides empirical grounding for the environmental uncertainty parameter (sigma) in the signaling model; (2) Smith's neutron activation sourcing of steatite to specific Appalachian quarries 800-900 km distant, and the demonstration that 95.7% of all known steatite and 97% of all known galena concentrate at the Poverty Point site itself rather than being redistributed; (3) Kidder's identification of semi-autonomous site clusters with independent regional centers, supporting a model of voluntary aggregation rather than coerced participation; and (4) Jackson's finding that the Copes site community operated independently of centralized redistribution, contradicting chiefdom models and supporting the signaling framework. The Discussion transcript is particularly valuable for capturing the participants' collective recognition that Poverty Point's organization cannot be explained by subsistence change alone, that trade crossed ethnic and linguistic boundaries among groups who were "strangers" to one another, and that the system ultimately "could not be sustained," all observations consistent with the costly signaling/bet-hedging framework.
