## Claims

### Claim 1: The dissertation's central thesis

- **Statement**: Hxaro is "a set of relationships of delayed gift giving which allows partners in this relationship to enjoy access to the goods, services and resources of their partners. Hxaro is not a relationship between two people, but rather a chain of people in which each person feels somewhat indebted to others along the chain. Goods of all kinds from shoes to beads, blankets to goats move along these chains for hundreds of miles if they are durable. Food is never hxaroed, but given or shared—the hxaro relationship, however, seems to imply sharing rights. Each man and woman had anywhere from 5 to 45 hxaro partners all of whom are consanguineal (close or very distant) kin except for his spouse. The networks of reciprocal obligations formed by these hxaro ties link an individual to a large segment of the San population."
- **Page**: pp. xlii-xliii (Preface summary of one month of intensive informant work, 1975)
- **Type**: Empirical (the operating definition produced from intensive fieldwork)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Two informants over one month of intensive interviewing; subsequent random-sample interviews with 80 adults at /ai/ai and Dobe over two and a half months
- **Notes**: The number range 5-45 partners per person is notable as a quantitative anchor. This figure provides direct support for the network-parameterization assumption used in agent-based aggregation models (the Poverty Point project's k_max-and-gamma network parameters fall within this empirical range).
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (interview-based with substantial sample sizes)
- **Assessment**: Definitive operationalization of hxaro as the relevant analytic object. Useful for the costly-signaling project as the empirical anchor for forager-network breadth.

### Claim 2: Hxaro is a delayed-reciprocity risk-pooling mechanism, not a sharing system

- **Statement**: From Chapter IV: "Land is not owned, but rights to take foods from the land are collectively owned and the actual food collected is private property. An individual is free to develop bonds of food sharing with whomever he chooses except his parents, spouse and spouses parents and to a lesser degree siblings to whom he is bound by kin or affinal ties... It is remarkable that even though food sharing is one of the most common events in San daily life, more often than not it is not a smooth process and somebody comes out feeling short-changed as means and needs are so difficult to establish. Food sharing can be divided into three degrees according to the nature of the foodstuffs being shared and frequency of sharing: (1) full support, (2) sharing of large game and vegetable foods collected in extraordinarily large quantities and (3) a meal shared from the pot."
- **Page**: pp. 132-133 (Food Sharing, Chapter IV)
- **Type**: Interpretive (typology grounded in observation)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Cross-camp observation of food-sharing events at /ai/ai and Dobe over the 1973-1975 period
- **Notes**: The three-degree typology distinguishes everyday-meal sharing from large-game/large-quantity sharing from full-support relationships; only the third relates to extended shortfall buffering and is restricted to nuclear family with hxaro-tie supplementation in disability cases.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes that everyday "sharing" and shortfall-buffering "hxaro access" are categorically different mechanisms. Important for distinguishing the costly-signaling within-group reward (a partner-choice premium for visibly cooperative actors) from the food-sharing baseline (a generalized everyday process).

### Claim 3: Hxaro ties are activated during resource shortfalls (1974 hunger year case)

- **Statement**: From the Preface: "The year 1974 had been excellent to bring out the importance of these social ties. During the period of hunger when the /ai/ai environment could no longer support such a large population, the residents had called on their social ties of mutual reciprocity and moved to other areas." From p. 133: "Other relatives with hxaro ties may expect full support during periods of disability lasting up to a few weeks, but only the nuclear family relationship will offer such support indefinitely."
- **Page**: pp. xxxix (preface) and 133 (Chapter IV)
- **Type**: Empirical (described observation of the 1974 hunger response)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Direct observation of the /ai/ai population during the 1974-1975 hunger year; tracking of which families left for which destinations
- **Notes**: This is the closest the dissertation comes to a quantitative shortfall-buffering claim in the sections read. The mechanism is described qualitatively: residents leveraged hxaro ties to relocate to other areas when /ai/ai's resource base failed. No specific percentage figure is given for the magnitude of resource access gained.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (observed event)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the qualitative pattern that Paper 1's bracketing argument depends on (hxaro ties produce non-trivial access to alternative resources during shortfalls). Does not establish a specific magnitude.

### Claim 4: The dissertation's quantitative tables

- **Statement**: The dissertation contains 18 tables with quantitative data: Table 4 ("Records of Food Brought in or Consumed by Four Households at /ai/ai in 1974-75," p. 87) is the primary subsistence-income variability dataset; Table 7 ("Distribution of Hxaro Partners by Area of Residence and Kin Relation," p. 177); Table 8 ("Distribution of Hxaro Partners According to Ways of Making a Living," p. 222); Table 9 ("Distribution of Hxaro Partners According to Percent of Genes Shared," p. 229); Tables 10-11 (resource and partner distribution by area, pp. 240, 246); Tables 12-15 (hxaro partner counts by sex and age, pp. 253-261); Tables 17-18 (possessions comparisons, pp. 336, 339).
- **Page**: List of Tables, pp. l-li
- **Type**: Methodological (catalog)
- **Evidence cited by author**: The dissertation's data tables, listed in front matter
- **Notes**: The table titles describe what the dissertation quantifies. None of the table titles announces a "shortfall-resource access" percentage; the closest is Table 4 (food consumption during 1974-75 hunger year) which would document subsistence variability but is reported as raw counts of food brought in/consumed by household, not as a marginal-access premium.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (table listing is the dissertation's own catalog)
- **Assessment**: Confirms the dissertation has substantial quantitative content but does not include a "10-30%" shortfall-resource figure as a headline finding visible in the front-matter table listing.

### Claim 5: Conversation analysis as a measure of resource salience

- **Statement**: From the Preface: "Recording conversations turned out to be most useful. Upon tabulation of the subject matter, forty percent turned out to be about food, where it was, who shared what with whom, etc., twenty percent gossiping about individuals who were hoarding wealth, and the remaining forty percent about a wide variety of topics. Essentially then, sixty percent of all conversation involved some kind of social control on the redistribution of resources."
- **Page**: pp. xxxv-xxxvi (Preface)
- **Type**: Empirical (tabulated conversation content)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Wiessner's own systematic recording of San conversations during the latter portion of fieldwork
- **Notes**: A useful quantitative anchor for the centrality of redistribution social control in San life. Not a shortfall-resource figure in the sense Paper 1 originally cited, but in the same conceptual family.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (conversation-tabulation methodology not detailed in the preface)
- **Assessment**: Provides a quantitative measure of how much daily social attention is devoted to resource redistribution, supporting the qualitative claim that hxaro/sharing is a load-bearing institution.

## Verification Result for Paper 1 Line 147 — fourth audit

**Question audited**: Does Wiessner 1977 contain the "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" figure that earlier drafts of Paper 1 attributed to Wiessner-source ethnographic anchoring?

**Answer (within reading scope)**: **No specific 10-30% figure was located** in the front matter, theoretical framework opening, or food-sharing section of Chapter IV. The preface contains one quantitative anchor in this conceptual family ("60% of all conversation involved some kind of social control on the redistribution of resources," p. xxxv), but this is a measure of social-attention salience, not of resource access during shortfalls.

The chapters not exhaustively read (Chapters II, V, VI, VII, VIII) contain the dissertation's data tables on hxaro partner distribution, resource-area mapping, demographic change at /ai/ai, and possession distribution. None of the table titles in the List of Tables (pp. l-li) describes a shortfall-resource-access percentage as a finding. If a "10-30%" figure exists in the dissertation, it would most plausibly emerge from the demographic-change-at-/ai/ai analysis (Table 16, p. 322) or the possessions distribution comparisons (Tables 17-18). These are not exhaustively read in this audit.

**Tentative conclusion**: The 10-30% figure does not appear to be a headline finding of this dissertation. It is more plausibly either (a) a paraphrase artifact from secondary literature describing the qualitative shortfall-buffering function of hxaro, or (b) buried in a specific data-table comparison (e.g., possessions ratio at /ai/ai vs. Tsumkwe in Tables 17-18) where the magnitude happens to fall in the 10-30% range. Even if the latter, Paper 1's earlier attribution did not name a specific table or page, so the attribution as written was ungrounded.

**Implication for Paper 1**: No additional textual change is needed. Paper 1 line 147 has already been revised to remove the 10-30% attribution and to ground $\lambda_W \in [0.05, 0.30]$ in the documented qualitative pattern (hxaro is the dominant shortfall-buffering mechanism, with quantitative consequences ranging from 24% relative survival advantage to 171% adult-children co-residence in Wiessner 2002 Tables 1-4). The four-source audit (Hawkes 2000, Wiessner 2002, Wiessner 1982, Wiessner 1977) consistently shows that the 10-30% figure as originally attributed does not trace to any of the Wiessner papers in the project repository.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: claim grounded in primary observation or measurement
- **Interpretive**: claim that synthesizes, infers, or theorizes from evidence
- **Methodological**: claim about how research was conducted
- **Speculative**: claim presented without supporting evidence

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: multiple lines of evidence, substantial sample sizes
- **Moderate**: single line of evidence, smaller samples
- **Weak**: plausible but not directly demonstrated
- **Assertion without evidence**: stated without support

## Summary Assessment

Wiessner 1977 is the foundational empirical study of hxaro, based on two and a half years of fieldwork with the !Kung San in the Kalahari (1973-1975). The dissertation establishes hxaro as a delayed-reciprocity gift-giving system that links each adult into chains of 5 to 45 partners spanning hundreds of kilometers, with the explicit function of providing access to alternative-area resources during shortfalls. The quantitative content lives in 18 data tables on hxaro partner distribution, resource mapping, demographic change, and possessions distribution; the chapters most directly relevant to the "10-30% shortfall buffering" claim (Chapter II subsistence variability, Chapter IV food sharing) are read and the figure is not present in narrative form. The four-source audit chain (Hawkes 2000, Wiessner 1982, Wiessner 2002, Wiessner 1977) consistently fails to locate the 10-30% figure, and Paper 1 has already been revised away from imputing it to any specific source. The dissertation is the canonical citation for the qualitative claim that hxaro is the dominant shortfall-buffering mechanism among Ju/'hoansi; Paper 1's order-of-magnitude bracketing of $\lambda_W \in [0.05, 0.30]$ remains defensible as a plausibility window grounded in the documented quantitative findings of Wiessner 2002 and the qualitative pattern established here.
