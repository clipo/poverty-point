# Claims Extraction: Sanger 2023

## Claims

### Claim 1
- **Statement**: "Monumental architecture has long been associated with the rise of the State and societal inequality, yet recent studies have shown some small and relatively egalitarian societies also built large-scale architecture." Some of these groups utilized "institutional flexibility," "a strategy of creating and then dismantling hierarchical power systems during limited periods of time, as a means of harnessing group labor, establishing ritual cycles, and policing behavior during periods of gathering, but then reverting to more autonomous power relations for the remainder of the year when groups were dispersed."
- **Page**: p. 555 (abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive (core thesis)
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (theoretical framework applied to archaeological case)
- **Assessment**: Central argument of the paper. Institutional flexibility is drawn from Graeber and Wengrow (2021). The concept provides an alternative to both the elite-directed and egalitarian-communal interpretations of Poverty Point. Directly relevant to the costly signaling framework: institutional flexibility describes a mechanism by which authority is temporally bounded, paralleling the signaling model's treatment of aggregation as a distinct behavioral context.

### Claim 2
- **Statement**: Poverty Point "is a massive series of earthworks spanning 750 hectares, including five mounds, six concentric ridges that encircle an expansive plaza, and dozens of timber circles." The site was "created by hunter-gatherer-fisher peoples over a 500-year period (ca. 3600-3100 cal B.P.)"
- **Page**: p. 556
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Hargrave et al. 2021; Gibson 2000; Kidder et al. 2021)
- **Assessment**: Standard site description. Note 750 hectares (7.5 km^2) for the full site spread including timber circles, which is larger than earlier estimates focused on the earthwork core. Date range of ca. 3600-3100 cal B.P. (ca. 1650-1150 BCE) is consistent with current consensus.

### Claim 3
- **Statement**: "Despite engaging in complex social activities over many generations, there is no evidence for an emergent elite or entrenched hierarchy at Poverty Point or anywhere else in the region."
- **Page**: p. 556
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Gibson 2000; broad consensus)
- **Assessment**: Key negative finding. The absence of hierarchy despite monumental construction is the central paradox the paper addresses. Directly supports the costly signaling interpretation: if hierarchy is absent, then monumental construction must serve a function other than elite aggrandizement.

### Claim 4
- **Statement**: Community members "control and limit the efforts of these aggrandizers through a range of actions and practices, including by defining times and places in which aspirant leaders can acquire prestige and hold authority, while ensuring that these acquisitions are ineffectual outside of these contexts." Such shifting power dynamics are described as "heterarchical" (Crumley 1995) and reliant on "institutional flexibility" in which "individuals and groups adopt social parameters, power dynamics, and cultural norms based on context" (Graeber and Wengrow 2021).
- **Page**: p. 556
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (theoretical argument drawing on ethnographic analogy)
- **Assessment**: Defines the mechanism of authority containment. Communities actively define the temporal and spatial boundaries within which aggrandizers can operate. This is conceptually parallel to the signaling model's treatment of aggregation sites as specialized contexts where costly signaling occurs, distinct from dispersed foraging life.

### Claim 5
- **Statement**: Sanger argues Poverty Point "offers an opportunity to study the flipside of inequality, namely, how do communities achieve more balanced power structures and preserve autonomy, often in the face of mounting pressures to instead become more hierarchical and unequal?" He concludes that "people built Poverty Point not as a platform for human elites, nor through undirected labor, but rather as a means of compartmentalizing and containing the efforts of aggrandizers into a context that could be controlled by the broader community."
- **Page**: p. 557
- **Type**: Interpretive (core thesis)
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Reframes the Poverty Point paradox from "why did egalitarian people build monuments?" to "how did communities prevent monumentality from producing lasting hierarchy?" This reframing is compatible with the costly signaling model: if signals are honest and costly, they function within a competitive context that need not produce permanent hierarchy.

### Claim 6
- **Statement**: Anarchist theory "prioritizes the ability of groups to self-organize, often with situational leadership based on justified authority, and to create social networks and cultural practices that actively combat aspiring elites and their attempts to impose coercive power systems." Despite the focus on non-elite actors, "Anarchist theory assumes that power-hungry individuals are a near universal aspect of human society and that coercive power, centralized authority, and threat of inter-personal domination occurs within all communities, no matter how small or seemingly simple."
- **Page**: p. 559
- **Type**: Theoretical
- **Evidence strength**: N/A (theoretical framework)
- **Assessment**: The assumption that aggrandizers are universal provides the theoretical basis for expecting active containment mechanisms in all societies. This parallels signaling theory's assumption that individuals will seek to manipulate signals when possible, requiring honest (costly) signals as a check.

### Claim 7
- **Statement**: Graeber and Wengrow (2021, 111) describe "institutional flexibility" as "a conscious creation and dismantling of hierarchical relationships based on temporal, spatial, and social context." They document that "a wide range of societies of societies flip from living in groups with few if any leaders, to living under 'unequivocal authoritarianism' (109). Often, this flip relates to a time or season in which large numbers of people came together, often based on a shared ritual calendar."
- **Page**: p. 559
- **Type**: Theoretical/Ethnographic
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (ethnographic compilation by Graeber and Wengrow)
- **Assessment**: Defines the key concept. The seasonal flip between autonomous and hierarchical organization maps directly onto the aggregation-dispersal cycle at Poverty Point. The conscious, deliberate character of this institutional shift is important: it implies active social management of power dynamics, not mere structural byproduct.

### Claim 8
- **Statement**: Lowie's (1948) observations of the Mandan-Hidatsa and Crow in the Great Plains provide an example: "These groups lived in small familial units, or slightly larger unions created through intermarriages, for much of the year during which decisions were made by consensus or head of the family who held little disciplinary or coercive authority. In late summer and early fall, smaller groups formed larger social units engaged in buffalo hunting, at which time they established leadership roles, strict taboos and rules, and a 'police force.' Leaders of the group retained absolute authority over the group through the police force which was able to coerce, detain, harm, or even kill transgressors. Once the buffalo hunt was over, the larger group disbanded, the police force dissolved, and the leaders who once held so much authority returned to their much-diminished role as titular leader of their own familial group."
- **Page**: pp. 559-560
- **Type**: Ethnographic
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (direct ethnographic observation by Lowie 1948)
- **Assessment**: Key ethnographic analog for institutional flexibility. The Mandan-Hidatsa/Crow example demonstrates that temporary hierarchical authority during aggregation events, followed by reversion to autonomous relations, is ethnographically documented. The police force mechanism shows that coercive authority can exist during aggregation without becoming permanent. Directly relevant to modeling aggregation dynamics at Poverty Point.

### Claim 9
- **Statement**: The Green Corn Ceremony among southeastern Indigenous peoples "includes dances, singing, feasting, preparation of a plaza, and a range of other activities that generate logistic, spiritual, and social demands on the group. These demands require coordinated efforts and so are typically administered by individuals granted high levels of authority during the ceremony, but then revert to their original status once the event has concluded."
- **Page**: p. 560
- **Type**: Ethnographic
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Jackson 2003, pp. 59-62 and 206-240; Witthoft 1949)
- **Assessment**: Southeastern U.S. ethnographic analog for institutional flexibility during ceremonial aggregation. Particularly relevant given the geographic proximity to Poverty Point. Demonstrates that temporary authority during ceremonial events is a well-documented pattern in the region.

### Claim 10
- **Statement**: At Kolomoki (Middle Woodland, ca. 100-500 AD, Georgia), the site "was distinctly divided between a ceremonial sphere where elite individuals held power, displayed wealth, and ruled over their neighbors, and a domestic sphere where power was far more dispersed, there was no centralization of goods, and there were few, if any leaders." This division was "not an accident, but rather a purposeful sequestering of elite authority into a place and time that was 'bounded and removed from domestic contexts' so that 'the segmentation of space and activities could have simultaneously limited the privileges these leaders enjoyed to specific contexts, making the emerging differentials in power and prestige more palatable to those of lesser station' (Pluckhahn 2010, 114)."
- **Page**: p. 560
- **Type**: Empirical/Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (archaeological interpretation by Pluckhahn 2010)
- **Assessment**: Archaeological precedent for spatial containment of authority. Kolomoki demonstrates that the ceremonial/domestic division can be identified archaeologically through spatial patterning of goods and architecture. Relevant to interpreting spatial organization at Poverty Point, where the ridges, plaza, and mounds may similarly have segmented ceremonial from domestic space.

### Claim 11
- **Statement**: "Archaeological evidence for institutional flexibility comes in many forms, the most prominent of which is a radical spatial and/or temporal variation in the scale of activities taking place within a larger social group. Lacking the ability to recreate fine-grained contextual details, archaeological examples of institutional flexibility will often appear as paradoxes in which 'simple' groups engaged in 'complex' activities, to the degree that deaggrandizers successfully contained the efforts of aggrandizers with groups establishing increasingly elaborate practices during events that nonetheless had little impact on the power relations that structured their daily lives."
- **Page**: pp. 560-561
- **Type**: Interpretive (methodological)
- **Evidence strength**: N/A (interpretive framework)
- **Assessment**: Identifies the archaeological signature of institutional flexibility: apparent paradoxes where "simple" societies produce "complex" outcomes. This is precisely the situation at Poverty Point and is the central puzzle the costly signaling model also addresses. The resolution offered here (containment of aggrandizers) is complementary to rather than competing with the signaling model.

### Claim 12
- **Statement**: The plaza at Poverty Point, "one of the least conspicuous features of the site, required the clearing of a 17.4 hectare area that was then leveled and flattened, in some places by the addition of more than a metre of fill." The six concentric ridges "each measures 2-3 metre tall, 30-40 metre wide, and are almost 10 km long when laid end-to-end." Mound A is "a massive three-part construction that includes a 21 metre tall steep-sided conical structure, a roughly rectangular flat-topped mound measuring 10 metre tall, and ramp connecting the two."
- **Page**: p. 561
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Hargrave et al. 2021; Kidder et al. 2021; Ortmann and Kidder 2013)
- **Assessment**: Provides detailed construction dimensions. Plaza = 17.4 ha cleared and leveled. Ridges = 2-3 m tall, 30-40 m wide, ~10 km total length. Mound A = 21 m tall conical + 10 m flat-topped mound + ramp. These are consistent with other sources and useful for labor estimates.

### Claim 13
- **Statement**: "According to one estimate, Poverty Point peoples had to move roughly three-quarters of a million cubic meters of earth to erect the above-ground constructions at the site." The scale is "made that much more notable as virtually all of it was conducted by hunter-gatherer-fisher peoples over a 500-year period (ca. 3600-3100 cal B.P.)"
- **Page**: p. 561
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Clark, Gibson, and Zeidler 2010, p. 228)
- **Assessment**: Earth-moving estimate of ~750,000 m^3 for above-ground constructions. Note this is for above-ground features only; total earth-moving including plaza leveling and other activities would be greater. Consistent with other estimates in the literature (Kidder and Grooms 2025 estimate ~1,000,000 m^3 total).

### Claim 14
- **Statement**: Poverty Point peoples "engaged in acquiring massive amounts of chert, novaculite, and other lithic materials, resulting in as much as 63,500 kilograms being deposited at the site, some of which originated hundreds of kilometers away." They also exchanged steatite from the southeastern Piedmont, pottery from the mid-Atlantic, and copper from eastern Canada.
- **Page**: p. 561
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Carr and Stewart 2004; Gibson and Griffing 1994; Hill, Greenlee, and Neff 2016)
- **Assessment**: The 63,500 kg (63.5 metric tons) figure for lithic materials deposited at the site is a key quantitative measure of exchange volume. Combined with the multi-directional source areas, this demonstrates the unprecedented scale of the exchange network.

### Claim 15
- **Statement**: "While there are very few, if any, other contemporaneous mound sites near Jaketown and Poverty Point, there are a significant number of smaller residential, specialized use-areas, and other more ephemeral occupations in the surrounding environs that are evidence of a dispersed, but possibly sizeable population base."
- **Page**: p. 561
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (citing Clark, Gibson, and Zeidler 2010; Ward et al. 2022)
- **Assessment**: Documents the settlement pattern of a dispersed population base surrounding the main aggregation sites. This supports the aggregation model: Poverty Point functioned as a central gathering point for otherwise dispersed bands.

### Claim 16
- **Statement**: Early work described Poverty Point as North America's "first chiefdom" (Gibson 1974). "Over time, archaeologists began to question whether Poverty Point was indeed led by elite actors, in part because decades of excavations failed to uncover any evidence for an entrenched ruling class." While the site "was rich with trade items, exotic materials, and other rare objects, it also held massive amounts of more mundane items (Gibson 2000) all of which were largely intermixed with one another (Gibson and Griffing 1994)." The near absence of skeletal remains is noted, with acidic soils partially responsible, but "excavations also failed to encounter burial shafts or remnant funerary goods, resulting in most archaeologists assuming few, if anyone was buried at the site."
- **Page**: p. 563
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing multiple excavation reports)
- **Assessment**: Enumerates the negative evidence against hierarchy: no elite class, exotic and mundane items intermixed (not restricted), no burials or funerary goods. The intermixing of exotic and mundane items is particularly relevant to the signaling model: it suggests exotic goods were not monopolized by elites but were broadly distributed, consistent with a signaling system rather than a political economy of elite control.

### Claim 17
- **Statement**: "Mound A, which includes roughly 238,500 m^3 of fill, or 15.5 million baskets of soil, appears to have been constructed in a single episode lasting less than a year, perhaps only three months." Construction crews "created the mound in layers, each made up of different soils, some of which were mined from relatively distant or deep deposits." The labor "is thought to require thousands of people, perhaps as many as ten thousand people, all present on the site for several months."
- **Page**: p. 563
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Ortmann and Kidder 2013)
- **Assessment**: Critical evidence for the aggregation model. Mound A = 238,500 m^3, 15.5 million baskets, built in under a year (possibly 3 months), requiring up to 10,000 people. The rapid construction timeline demands a large aggregated labor force, not gradual accumulation by a small resident population. This is consistent with seasonal aggregation for construction events.

### Claim 18
- **Statement**: "Poverty Point peoples built at least one of the concentric ridges in several rapid stages with relatively little time between each." "Recent geophysical research shows that the overall amount of labor required to create Poverty Point was even greater than originally assumed as they found evidence of previously unknown terraforming activities, including the reworking of parts of the site, particularly the ridges, as well as the formation of less visible constructions, including numerous timber circles."
- **Page**: pp. 563-564
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Kidder et al. 2021; Hargrave et al. 2021)
- **Assessment**: Extends the rapid-construction evidence beyond Mound A to the ridges. The reworking of ridges and discovery of timber circles indicates labor investment was greater than previously estimated. Multiple rapid construction episodes are consistent with repeated seasonal aggregation events.

### Claim 19
- **Statement**: "Currently, there is an emerging consensus that leadership roles were ritual or religious in nature and that these individuals recruited and mobilized groups of laborers drawn from diverse and dispersed communities spread across the region by promoting cosmological worldviews and ritual actions designed to create and maintain a shared cultural identity." "While capable of organizing and deploying massive amounts of labor, many researchers now agree that these leaders had little authority beyond the events leading up to and occurring at Poverty Point."
- **Page**: p. 564
- **Type**: Interpretive (emerging consensus)
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (citing Anderson 2004; Kidder 2011; Kidder et al. 2021; Ortmann and Kidder 2013)
- **Assessment**: Documents the current scholarly consensus on leadership at Poverty Point: ritual/religious leaders who could mobilize labor but held no lasting authority. This is the institutional flexibility argument in archaeological form. For the signaling model, this consensus supports treating Poverty Point as a context-dependent signaling arena rather than a seat of permanent political power.

### Claim 20
- **Statement**: "Research at broadly contemporaneous sites near Poverty Point shows that the landscape was populated by a diverse constellation of local social groups that did not form a broader 'Poverty Point culture,' but rather were autonomous units with their own identities that each drew at different levels on the events held at Poverty Point."
- **Page**: p. 564
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (citing Ward et al. 2022)
- **Assessment**: Challenges the concept of a unified "Poverty Point culture." Instead, autonomous social groups with distinct identities participated in Poverty Point events to varying degrees. This supports the aggregation-signaling model: independent bands converge at an aggregation site but maintain distinct identities, consistent with signaling within a multigroup context.

### Claim 21
- **Statement**: "The social configuration found at Poverty Point emerged, at least in part, as an outcome of the constantly shifting social and political dynamics created by competition between aggrandizers and deaggrandizers. Living in a highly productive natural environment where food and other resources were abundant and at time when populations were adopting less mobile residential strategies and living in larger groups (Gibson 2000) there would have been ample opportunities for aspiring elites to acquire greater degrees of authority."
- **Page**: p. 564
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Identifies the conditions that created pressure for authority containment: productive environment, reduced mobility, larger group sizes. These are also the conditions under which costly signaling becomes adaptive in the model: resource abundance enables aggregation, but the shift toward sedentism creates new social tensions that must be managed.

### Claim 22
- **Statement**: "A compromise that benefitted both aspiring elites and their fellow community members would include the establishment of a ritual calendar in which aggrandizers could begin to acquire some level of power, albeit contextual, and the broader group would be able to build more expansive social networks."
- **Page**: p. 564
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Frames institutional flexibility as a compromise between aggrandizers and the community. Both parties benefit: leaders gain contextual authority, community gains expanded social networks. This compromise structure is compatible with the signaling model: costly signals benefit both the signaler (prestige, mating opportunities) and the audience (information about cooperator quality, expanded networks).

### Claim 23
- **Statement**: "Deaggrandizers likely used a wide range of leveling mechanisms to keep emergent elitism at bay and probably instilled core concepts of egalitarianism into their broadly held cosmological ideology." The practices at Poverty Point, "including mound building, feasting, and ceremonies, were another means of containing aspiring elites as they offered an opportunity for individuals to wield remarkable levels of authority, perhaps including coercion and violence." After returning home, aggrandizers found their authority dissolved. "These symbols of power and perhaps authority were likely viewed as dangerous to bring back to a life characterized by autonomy and were therefore left behind."
- **Page**: p. 565
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Proposes that exotic goods and authority were deliberately left at Poverty Point rather than taken home. If true, this has implications for the signaling model: the signals (exotic goods, monumental labor) are context-bound, functional only within the aggregation setting. The "dangerous to bring back" interpretation suggests social norms actively prevented the transfer of aggregation-context status to daily life.

### Claim 24
- **Statement**: "While some people may have gone home with PPOs as mementos of their time at the mounds, the truly valuable and exotic goods, like copper and galena, remained at Poverty Point."
- **Page**: p. 565
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (citing Spivey et al. 2015; Ward et al. 2022)
- **Assessment**: Proposes a material distinction between PPOs (carried away as low-value mementos) and high-value exotics (copper, galena, which stayed at the site). If supported, this spatial restriction of exotic goods to the aggregation site would be consistent with institutional flexibility: symbols of authority are contained within the ceremonial context. However, the evidence base for this claim needs verification; some exotic materials are found at outlying sites.

### Claim 25
- **Statement**: "The study of monuments built by small-scale groups is plagued by simplistic, often binary, understandings of how and why people devoted energies to making long-lasting, highly visible, pieces of architecture beyond what they needed for their daily lives." "In one view, small scale societies are naturally egalitarian and therefore monumental constructions were created during periods of enhanced social solidarity and the formation of more cohesive communal identities. In the other, we assume leaders were able to transfer their roles from one context to another and thereby utilize ritual prestige and authority to wield social and political power with little resistance by community members."
- **Page**: p. 565
- **Type**: Interpretive (critique)
- **Evidence strength**: N/A (critique of existing frameworks)
- **Assessment**: Identifies the binary framing that the paper seeks to transcend. The costly signaling framework also avoids this binary: signals are produced by individuals seeking to advertise quality, but the system as a whole functions to facilitate cooperation without requiring permanent hierarchy. Sanger's institutional flexibility framework and the signaling model converge on this point.

## Summary Assessment

Sanger (2023) applies anarchist political theory and the concept of "institutional flexibility" (from Graeber and Wengrow 2021) to Poverty Point, arguing that the site represents a context in which communities deliberately contained the authority of aspiring elites within bounded temporal and spatial contexts. The paper does not present new archaeological data but synthesizes existing evidence within a novel theoretical framework. The key contribution is the reframing of the Poverty Point paradox: rather than asking why egalitarian people built monuments, Sanger asks how communities prevented monumental construction and ceremonial aggregation from producing permanent hierarchy. The institutional flexibility concept, where societies oscillate between autonomous and hierarchical organization based on seasonal and ritual context, is well-supported ethnographically (Mandan-Hidatsa buffalo hunting police force; southeastern Green Corn Ceremony) and has archaeological precedent (Kolomoki). For the costly signaling model, this paper provides a complementary framework: institutional flexibility describes the social mechanism by which signaling is contained within aggregation contexts, preventing costly signals from converting into permanent political power. The two frameworks are not competing but address different aspects of the same phenomenon: signaling theory explains why individuals invest in costly displays, while institutional flexibility explains how communities prevent those displays from producing lasting inequality.
