## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "The shift at 5.5 ka initiated widespread cooling and increases in effective moisture, which culminated in the coolest, wettest millennia in most areas after 2.1 ka."
- **Page**: p. 38 (abstract)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Based on a network of 40 well-resolved paleoclimate datasets. The 5.5 ka shift (~3550 BCE) precedes the Poverty Point period by roughly 1,800 years, but it establishes the long-term climatic trajectory (cooling and moistening) within which the Poverty Point occupation developed. The shift initiated a new multi-millennial climatic regime that persisted through and beyond the Poverty Point period.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "Replicated evidence from multiple records also shows a spatially-varied set of multi-century fluctuations including 1) low temperatures and high effective moisture at 5.5-4.8 ka in the mid-continent and 2) repeated phases of low SSTs, cool summers, and drought superimposed upon long cooling, moistening trends in eastern North American since 5.5 ka."
- **Page**: p. 38 (abstract)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents multi-century climate fluctuations superimposed on long-term trends. The "repeated phases of... cool summers, and drought" in eastern North America after 5.5 ka directly encompass the Poverty Point period (~3700-3100 BP). This pattern of recurrent drought episodes is directly relevant to environmental uncertainty (sigma) in the costly signaling model.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "The mean rate of change across all datasets exceeded 85% of the rates (0.26 sd/200 yrs) seven times: at 10.9-10.5, 9.4, 8.3, 5.5, 5.2, 4.7, and 0.9-0.6 ka."
- **Page**: p. 41
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Identifies specific intervals of abrupt climate change. None of these peak rates of change fall within the Poverty Point period itself (3.7-3.1 ka), suggesting the Poverty Point occupation occurred during a period without major abrupt climate shifts, though it was situated within the longer moistening/cooling trend initiated at 5.5 ka.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "Warming combined with widespread drought at 4.7 ka to end the anomalously cool, wet conditions that began after 5.5 ka."
- **Page**: p. 42
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The 4.7 ka event (~2750 BCE) represents the end of a cool, wet interval and the onset of warming and drought. This event occurs approximately 1,000 years before the Poverty Point florescence and may have helped establish baseline conditions for the subsequent period. The drought at 4.7 ka is a significant environmental perturbation.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "Afterwards, mean regional temperatures changed little until 2.9 ka, except for a minimum from 3.9 to 3.5 ka and a maximum at 3.0 ka; both events shifted the distribution of boot strapped temperatures (gray lines, Fig. 2A). The extensive drought after 4.7 ka persisted until 4.0 ka (with moisture levels reaching a minimum from 4.2 to 3.9 ka), but the drought was followed by a progressive increase in effective moisture to present."
- **Page**: p. 42
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: This is the most directly relevant claim for the Poverty Point period. Mean regional temperatures were relatively stable from 4.7 to 2.9 ka but with a notable temperature minimum at 3.9-3.5 ka, which overlaps with the early Poverty Point period (~1950-1550 BCE). The drought that began at 4.7 ka persisted until 4.0 ka, with moisture levels at their lowest from 4.2 to 3.9 ka. After 4.0 ka, effective moisture progressively increased, meaning the Poverty Point period (3.7-3.1 ka) occurred during a transitional phase of increasing moisture following a major drought. This provides direct context for environmental uncertainty during the Poverty Point occupation.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "Additional drought episodes at 3.6-3.3 and 3.0-2.4 ka had sufficient severity across records to also temporarily decrease the median and mean effective moisture z-scores relative to the long-term increase, but the events did not achieve the magnitude or extent of the aridity reconstructed at 4.7-4.0 ka."
- **Page**: p. 42
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: This claim is critical for the Poverty Point signaling model. Drought episodes at 3.6-3.3 ka (~1650-1350 BCE) and 3.0-2.4 ka (~1050-450 BCE) fall squarely within and immediately after the Poverty Point period. The 3.6-3.3 ka drought overlaps with what may be the peak of Poverty Point activity. The 3.0-2.4 ka drought begins near the end of the Poverty Point period and extends well past it, potentially contributing to the system's decline. These drought episodes, superimposed on a general moistening trend, would have created the kind of environmental uncertainty (variable sigma) that the costly signaling model predicts should favor cooperative signaling systems.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "Changes after 5.7 ka marked the beginning of cooling and moistening trends in many areas, which persisted until present and were punctuated by a series of multi-century events."
- **Page**: p. 46
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms that the long-term climate trajectory during and after the Poverty Point period was one of cooling and moistening, but punctuated by multi-century perturbations. The "punctuated" character of the climate history is directly relevant to modeling environmental uncertainty: it was not steady-state but involved episodic disruptions within a broader trend.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: "Warm/dry and cool/moist deviations from the long-term trends appear to represent the dominant modes of variability. The warm, dry phases became progressively cooler and wetter toward present, such that the most recent warm, dry periods (e.g., 1.3-1.2 ka) had absolute temperatures and moisture levels equal to the earliest of the intervening cool, wet phases (e.g., 4.6-4.2 ka)."
- **Page**: p. 46
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Identifies the dominant mode of multi-century climate variability as alternating warm/dry and cool/moist phases. This covariance structure between temperature and moisture is important for modeling resource uncertainty at Poverty Point: warm/dry intervals would reduce aquatic resources and some terrestrial productivity, while cool/moist intervals would favor different resource configurations. The opposing relationship between temperature and moisture means these variables would have co-varied in ways that amplified environmental uncertainty for populations dependent on multiple resource types.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "The three different data types from nine different sites in the eastern sub-region contain a well-replicated signal of drought and warming at 4.9-4.6, 4.2-3.9, 2.9-2.1, and 1.3-1.2 ka."
- **Page**: p. 46
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides a specific chronology of warm/dry episodes in the eastern sub-region (which includes sites closest to the lower Mississippi Valley and Poverty Point). The 4.2-3.9 ka drought episode (~2250-1950 BCE) immediately precedes the Poverty Point florescence. The gap between the 4.2-3.9 ka and 2.9-2.1 ka drought episodes corresponds roughly to the Poverty Point period (3.7-3.1 ka), suggesting the Poverty Point occupation may have developed during a relatively favorable interval between major drought episodes, although Claim 6 notes intermediate drought at 3.6-3.3 ka.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "Sharp cooling at 2.1-1.8 ka of 0.25-0.5 degrees C coincided with a rapid rise in water levels (>0.25 sd), and produced the dominant climate of the Common Era (0-1950 AD)."
- **Page**: p. 46
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The 2.1 ka step change occurred well after the Poverty Point decline (~3.1 ka). This claim is less directly relevant to the Poverty Point period itself, but it places the post-Poverty Point climate trajectory in context. The climate system continued to evolve after Poverty Point's abandonment.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "Stepwise transitions at ca. 5.7-5.2 ka, particularly in effective moisture, terminated extensive mid-continent aridity and the 'Holocene thermal maximum' across much of our study region."
- **Page**: p. 48
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The end of the Holocene thermal maximum at 5.7-5.2 ka established fundamentally different climatic baseline conditions approximately 1,500-2,000 years before Poverty Point. The termination of "extensive mid-continent aridity" meant that the Poverty Point period developed under generally wetter conditions than the preceding mid-Holocene, though with episodic drought perturbations.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "Within the context of sub-regional variation, widespread, multi-parameter replication of the structure of the climate history indicates that abrupt events had magnitudes equal to about 25-50% of the long-term changes. They included both positive and negative interference with the long trends, and despite their modest magnitudes, must have produced significant ecological (vegetation/pollen) and geomorphic (lake/dust) consequences to enable detection by the records examined here."
- **Page**: p. 48
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantifies the magnitude of abrupt climate events relative to long-term trends. Abrupt events were 25-50% the magnitude of multi-millennial changes, meaning they were substantial enough to produce "significant ecological" consequences. For hunter-gatherer populations at Poverty Point dependent on diverse ecological zones, such events would have been sufficient to alter resource availability and predictability, supporting the case for meaningful environmental uncertainty during this period.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "Over the last 7 ka, the mean regional temperature and moisture time series correlate significantly (r = -0.90; 1 sd range = -0.53 to -0.95)."
- **Page**: p. 42
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents a strong negative correlation between temperature and moisture over the last 7,000 years in mid-latitude North America. Warm periods tended to be dry; cool periods tended to be wet. This inverse relationship has implications for resource uncertainty at Poverty Point: warm/dry conditions would favor certain foraging strategies while cool/wet conditions would favor others. The strong coupling means environmental shifts would have affected multiple resource domains simultaneously rather than independently.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "Insolation and ice sheet area explain 24.0% and 27.7%, respectively, of the total variance in our spatially distributed, multivariate dataset."
- **Page**: p. 43
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Roughly half (51.7%) of total Holocene climate variance is explained by orbital insolation and ice sheet dynamics. This means the remaining ~48% of variance derives from other factors, including the multi-century events and regional variability that would have generated environmental uncertainty for Poverty Point populations. The substantial unexplained variance underscores that Holocene climate was not smoothly predictable from orbital parameters alone.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "Rapid transitions from ca. 9.3-8.2 ka coincided with the declining influence of the Laurentide Ice Sheet and its meltwater, but additional step changes and acceleration of long-term trends at ca. 5.5 and 2.1 ka were equally as important and may have resulted from far-field feedbacks related to Northern Hemisphere cooling."
- **Page**: p. 48
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Identifies major climate step changes at 5.5 and 2.1 ka as potentially driven by Northern Hemisphere feedbacks rather than local ice sheet effects. The 5.5 ka step change established the climatic regime under which Poverty Point developed; the 2.1 ka step change occurred well after Poverty Point's decline. The mechanism (Northern Hemisphere cooling feedbacks) suggests these were large-scale, regionally coherent shifts rather than local perturbations.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Claims based on direct measurement, statistical analysis, or data reconstruction from paleoclimate proxy records.
- **Interpretive**: Claims involving inference about causes, mechanisms, or significance of observed climate patterns.
- **Methodological**: Claims about analytical approaches, data processing, or reconstruction techniques.

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Based on multiple replicated proxy records with statistical validation across sub-regions; robust age control.
- **Moderate**: Supported by data but involving some interpretive inference about mechanisms, or based on fewer replicated records.
- **Weak**: Speculative or poorly constrained by available data; acknowledged uncertainties in age models or proxy interpretation.

## Summary Assessment

Shuman and Marsicek (2016) provide a high-resolution, multi-proxy, multi-site reconstruction of Holocene climate change across mid-latitude North America. Their network of 40 paleoclimate records spans from the Rocky Mountains to the Atlantic coast, including sites in the upper Mississippi Valley, Great Lakes, and northeastern United States. While no records come from the lower Mississippi Valley or Gulf Coast (the immediate Poverty Point region), the regional climate patterns they identify are relevant to understanding broad-scale environmental conditions during the Poverty Point period.

**Key findings relevant to the Poverty Point costly signaling model:**

1. **Climate regime**: The Poverty Point period (3.7-3.1 ka / 1700-1100 BCE) fell within a long-term cooling and moistening trend initiated at 5.5 ka, following the end of the Holocene thermal maximum. Conditions were generally wetter than mid-Holocene averages.

2. **Temperature stability with perturbations**: Mean regional temperatures were relatively stable from 4.7 to 2.9 ka, but a temperature minimum at 3.9-3.5 ka overlaps with the early Poverty Point period.

3. **Drought episodes**: A major drought at 4.2-3.9 ka immediately preceded the Poverty Point florescence. Additional drought episodes at 3.6-3.3 ka fall within the Poverty Point period, and a later drought at 3.0-2.4 ka overlaps with its terminal phase. These droughts were less severe than the 4.7-4.0 ka drought but were ecologically significant.

4. **Multi-century variability structure**: Warm/dry and cool/moist phases alternated as the dominant mode of variability. Temperature and moisture were strongly inversely correlated (r = -0.90). Abrupt events had magnitudes of 25-50% of long-term trends, sufficient to produce significant ecological consequences.

5. **Environmental uncertainty (sigma)**: The pattern of episodic drought superimposed on a moistening trend supports the modeling of intermediate environmental uncertainty during the Poverty Point period. Conditions were neither stable (which would reduce incentives for cooperative signaling) nor catastrophically volatile (which would make aggregation impossible). The 3.6-3.3 ka drought occurring during peak Poverty Point activity is particularly relevant.

**Limitations for the Poverty Point context**: The study's records are concentrated north of 39 degrees N latitude, with no sites in the lower Mississippi Valley, Gulf Coast, or southeastern United States south of Virginia. The relevance of these mid-latitude patterns to conditions at Poverty Point (32.6 degrees N) requires cautious extrapolation. The authors note that sub-regional differences account for more than 30% of variance, and the same details do not apply to all locations.
