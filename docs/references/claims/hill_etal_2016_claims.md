## Claims

### Claim 1: Six PP copper artifacts assigned to eastern (Appalachian / Maritime) sources, not Lake Superior

- **Statement**: From the Abstract: "Results indicate that these copper artifacts most likely originated in the eastern sources of the Maritimes or Appalachian Mountains, rather than the Lake Superior region as long proposed." From Conclusions (p. 11): "Of the six Poverty Point copper artifacts examined in this study, none were consistent with the chemical composition of sources in the Great Lakes in any of the three tests. Instead, the composition of these artifacts associates them most closely with sources in eastern North America, primarily in the Appalachian Mountains and Nova Scotia."
- **Page**: pp. 1 (Abstract), 11 (Conclusions)
- **Type**: Empirical (LA-ICP-MS analysis result)
- **Evidence cited by author**: 6 PP copper samples vs. 62 native copper source samples; three discriminant function analyses (2-region, 3-region, 4-region tests)
- **Depends on**: Source sample collection (Rapp et al. 2000); LA-ICP-MS methodology (Hill 2012a, 2012b)
- **Notes**: The headline finding. Overturns the longstanding "Lake Superior copper" assumption for Poverty Point that had circulated since the 19th century (e.g., Foster and Whitney 1850).
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate — based on a small sample (6 of ~188 PP copper objects, ~3.2% of the assemblage) and a sparsely sampled Appalachian source region. Authors flag the "preliminary nature of this study" (p. 15) and acknowledge the result is a "test of concept" (p. 6).
- **Assessment**: The claim is methodologically defensible within its sample limits. The qualitative result (PP copper comes from eastern sources, not Lake Superior) is robust across all three tests; the specific Nova Scotia attribution may reflect undersampling of southern Appalachian sources rather than actual Maritime provenance.

### Claim 2: Two-region discriminant test — 100% of PP samples to eastern sources

- **Statement**: "In this simple test, the elemental composition of all six Poverty Point copper artifacts is most consistent with the eastern sources (Table 4). The probabilities that Poverty Point copper was most consistent with the eastern macro region ranged from 0.73 to 0.97 (Figure 4a)."
- **Page**: pp. 9-10 (Table 4)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Discriminant function analysis (Wilks' Lambda = 0.618; p = 0.011); 85.5% reclassification success
- **Notes**: Of the 62 source samples, 91.3% of Lake Superior sources were correctly classified, while 31.3% of eastern sources were misclassified as Lake Superior. The authors note this asymmetry suggests "any provenance errors are more likely to falsely indicate a Lake Superior source rather than falsely indicate an eastern source" (p. 9), which strengthens the headline finding.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (within the data analyzed)
- **Assessment**: The probability range (0.73-0.97) and the asymmetric error structure both support the claim that none of the 6 samples derives from Lake Superior.

### Claim 3: Three-region test — 4 Maritime + 2 Appalachian assignments

- **Statement**: "In this test, four of the six Poverty Point samples were found most similar to sources in the Maritimes, while two were compositionally consistent with the Appalachians (Table 5)... none of the Poverty Point samples were found to be most consistent with the Lake Superior macro region. Probabilities for the Maritimes associated samples ranged from 0.56 to 0.80, while the Appalachian sources were associated with probabilities of 0.68 and 0.69 (Figure 4b)."
- **Page**: p. 10 (Table 5)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Discriminant function analysis (Wilks' Lambda = 0.392; p = 0.001); 82.3% reclassification success
- **Notes**: Splitting "eastern" into Appalachian + Maritime regions still excludes Lake Superior entirely.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Consistent with Claim 2; refines the "eastern sources" finding into a Maritime-dominant pattern.

### Claim 4: Four-region test — All 6 PP samples assigned to Nova Scotia

- **Statement**: "All six of the Poverty Point copper samples were assigned to sources in Nova Scotia (Table 6, Figures 2 and 3). Three of the artifacts exhibited their second highest source probabilities in the Lake Superior region while the remaining three showed their second highest probabilities in the Appalachians, though the Lake Superior probabilities were quite low (Figure 4c)."
- **Page**: pp. 10-11 (Table 6, Figures 2-3)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Discriminant function analysis (Wilks' Lambda = 0.302; p ≤ 0.003); 82.3% reclassification success
- **Notes**: Probabilities for Nova Scotia: 0.54-0.99 (per Figure 4c — values 0.59, 0.99, 0.85, 0.54, 0.44, 0.76 for samples GC-1 through GC-6). Sample GC-5 has a near-tie with Appalachians (0.43 vs 0.44), so the assignment is least confident for that sample.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate — strong on the headline (no Lake Superior) but the specific Nova Scotia attribution depends on the assumption that southern Appalachian sources are well-sampled.
- **Assessment**: The Nova Scotia clustering may be an artifact of sparse Appalachian sampling. Authors explicitly raise this possibility on p. 15: "the surprising similarities with copper sources of Nova Scotia are logically consistent with potential unsampled areas in the Appalachians."

### Claim 5: Six PP copper samples, ~3.2% of known PP copper assemblage

- **Statement**: "Six samples of copper materials from Poverty Point (Table 1) were compared with the compositional profiles of 62 copper source samples"; "These samples were selected from existing collections housed at the Poverty Point Curatorial Facility and represent approximately three percent of the known copper from the site."
- **Page**: pp. 6-7 (Table 1; Section 4)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Sample inventory; Table 1 lists provenience and form (1 bead, 2 flat sheet fragments, 1 nugget, 2 rolled sheet/tube fragments) with weights ranging 0.18-0.84 g
- **Notes**: Sample size limitation is acknowledged. The total PP copper assemblage is reported as 188 objects on p. 4 (the Methods section refers to 6 samples as "approximately three percent" of "the known copper from the site"). 6/188 = 3.2%.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (factual sample description)
- **Assessment**: The small sample is a real constraint on inference. Authors explicitly acknowledge it: "The current study cannot rule out the use of Lake Superior copper at Poverty Point, rather it can only demonstrate that there is little indication of such source utilization in the samples examined here" (p. 16).

### Claim 6: 62 source samples spanning Lake Superior, Appalachians, Maritimes

- **Statement**: "Source samples (Table 2) were obtained from the extensive copper collection developed by George Rapp (see Rapp et al. 2000 for additional details) and currently housed at Michigan Technological University. A total of 62 samples, representing sources around Lake Superior, Nova Scotia, Newfoundland, and the central to southern Appalachians were used in this analysis."
- **Page**: p. 7 (Table 2)
- **Type**: Empirical (sample description)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Rapp et al. 2000 collection; Table 2 lists 7 Maritime samples, 9 Appalachian samples, 46 Lake Superior samples
- **Notes**: Sampling is heavily biased toward Lake Superior (46/62 = 74%). Appalachian sources are sparsely represented (1 from each of: Cornwall PA, Greenstone PA — 5 samples, Gold Hill NC; total 9 from Appalachians). The asymmetry matters because it limits the discriminative power for Appalachian-versus-Maritime questions.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate — sample size differs substantially across source regions
- **Assessment**: Authors acknowledge this is a major limitation: "the poorly sampled nature of Appalachian sources, particularly those in the southern Appalachians and adjacent Piedmont, potentially leading to the similarities seen here between Poverty Point copper and sources in the Maritimes" (p. 11).

### Claim 7: Lake Superior assumption was uncritically accepted historically

- **Statement**: "It has been widely assumed to originate in the deposits of the upper Great Lakes" (p. 1). "Archaeologists often assume that much or most of this copper originated from the extensive native copper deposits found in the vicinity of Lake Superior (e.g., Carr 2005; Fogel 1963; Griffin 1961; Walthall et al. 1982). Lake Superior hosts the largest native copper deposits in the world (Rapp 2000) and the distribution of copper in eastern North America is not inconsistent with a Lake Superior origin (Fogel 1963; Goad 1978, 1980), leading to a widespread and often uncritical acceptance of this assumption" (p. 4).
- **Page**: pp. 1-2, 4
- **Type**: Interpretive (historiographic)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Carr 2005; Fogel 1963; Griffin 1961; Walthall et al. 1982
- **Notes**: Frames the longstanding archaeological assumption that Hill et al. then test and partially reject.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (synthesis of prior literature)
- **Assessment**: Useful framing for understanding why the eastern-source finding is methodologically significant.

### Claim 8: Galena (separately) does come from Upper Mississippi Valley sources

- **Statement**: "The presence of Upper Mississippi galena fits with an established narrative that Poverty Point exchange materials often originated in the Mississippi, Ohio, and tributary valleys (Gibson 1994), with an implicit suggestion of transport to Poverty Point along those rivers... assumption strengthened by research which has determined that some of the galena found at Poverty Point originates from sources in the Upper Mississippi Valley (Ghosh 2008; Walthall et al. 1982)" (p. 11; pp. 1-2).
- **Page**: pp. 1-2, 11
- **Type**: Empirical (cited from prior work)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Walthall et al. 1982; Ghosh 2008
- **Notes**: Important distinction: galena is a confirmed northern outlier in PP exotic materials, while copper (per this paper) is not. The galena finding had been used to support the assumption that copper followed the same Mississippi River transport pathway, but Hill et al. argue the new copper finding undercuts that linkage.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (cited from established literature)
- **Assessment**: Useful for the costly-signaling framework: it shows that the multi-source exchange pattern at PP includes both northern (galena) and eastern (copper) sources, with no single dominant geographic vector.

### Claim 9: Steatite from southern Appalachians as parallel exchange route

- **Statement**: "It would be equally logical to argue the copper followed pathways of steatite transport from the southern Appalachians to Poverty Point (Truncer 2004; Yates 2009), as these steatite sources are equally coincident with copper sources in the Appalachians (Rapp 2000; Watson 1923). The lack of direct river access from these locations to Poverty Point appears not to have hampered the movement of steatite across the southeast to the Lower Mississippi Valley based on the large numbers of steatite bowl fragments at Poverty Point (Truncer 2004). Indeed, most of the materials imported to Poverty Point originated in the southeast and the northern galena source is a distinct northern outlier (Figure 1)."
- **Page**: p. 15
- **Type**: Interpretive (synthesis of multi-material exchange)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Truncer 2004; Yates 2009; Rapp 2000; Watson 1923
- **Notes**: Authors propose that copper followed the steatite transport route from Appalachians, which is the same as the Paper 2 framework's argument that geographic source sets the magnitude of long-distance investment.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (consistent with multi-material co-routing)
- **Assessment**: Directly supports the costly-signaling framework's claim that PP exotic materials are unfakeable indicators of long-distance acquisition, regardless of which specific source region is identified.

### Claim 10: Implications for Claiborne and Cedarland coastal pair

- **Statement**: "If future work reveals that the copper came from southern Appalachian locations that were coincident with other materials found at Poverty Point, like steatite and schist, this would suggest that the acquisition network was less extensive, and more intensive, than previously believed. The presence of copper at Claiborne and Cedarland would be consistent with a coastal transport pathway from the southern Appalachians to Poverty Point, and that could have implications for the direction of redistribution."
- **Page**: p. 15
- **Type**: Speculative (acknowledged future-research implication)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Implicit — Gibson 1994 reports copper at Claiborne and Cedarland
- **Notes**: Authors explicitly tie the eastern-source finding to the coastal Pearl River pair (Claiborne and Cedarland) as a possible coastal-transport route.
- **Evidence strength**: Speculative (a hypothesis raised for future work)
- **Assessment**: Relevant to the LMV regional analysis in Paper 2 but not used as a source for any specific Paper 2 claim. Useful background for the §4.4 / §4.5 argument that the coastal Pearl River sites are structurally distinct.

### Claim 11: Limitations explicitly acknowledged

- **Statement**: "We must acknowledge the preliminary nature of this study. Only six copper samples from Poverty Point were analyzed here (3.2 percent of the currently known total), and while the sample of Great Lakes copper source materials is quite large, far fewer samples of Appalachian sources were available for analysis... Future research into Poverty Point copper sourcing should attempt to resolve these two shortcomings by first procuring additional samples from native copper sources in the southern Appalachians... If Nova Scotia continues to be well represented in Poverty Point copper sources even after an exhaustive sampling of southern Appalachian copper, models of Poverty Point exchange and eastern Archaic economies will need to be reassessed." Also: "The current study cannot rule out the use of Lake Superior copper at Poverty Point, rather it can only demonstrate that there is little indication of such source utilization in the samples examined here."
- **Page**: pp. 15-16
- **Type**: Methodological (limitations)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Self-assessment
- **Notes**: The authors are unusually careful in framing this as a tentative result.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (methodological transparency)
- **Assessment**: Reinforces that the Paper 2 framing — "what matters for the signaling argument is that geochemical sourcing identifies the materials as non-local and unfakeable" (Paper 2 line 39) — is the appropriate level of inference. The exact source region remains uncertain; the long-distance non-local origin does not.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited; claim is stated as fact without support

## Verification Notes

**This paper supersedes the unpublished `hill_etal_2010` claims file.** The 2010 entry in the project INDEX is for the SAA symposium-paper precursor ("Sourcing Poverty Point Copper: Testing the Lake Superior Hypothesis using LA-ICPMS Analysis") which is marked "DO NOT CITE WITHOUT PERMISSION OF THE AUTHORS" and reports the same six-sample LA-ICP-MS analysis at preliminary stage. The 2016 published version refines methodology (four-region test added, transformation methods specified, statistical reporting tightened) and contains the citable form of the result. Paper 2's manuscript ref list (`docs/paper2_empirical/Paper2_Empirical_Evaluation.md` line 369) cites the 2016 version, which is the correct choice. The INDEX should be updated to reference `hill_etal_2016` as the canonical entry, with `hill_etal_2010` retained only as a historical pointer.

**Paper 2 prose accurately represents this paper's findings.** The relevant passage in Paper 2 (line 39) reads: "Copper was traditionally attributed to Lake Superior (~1,600 km), but recent LA-ICP-MS analysis of six specimens (Hill et al. 2016) indicates that several match Nova Scotia or southern Appalachian sources as well as, or better than, Lake Superior. The framework does not require Great Lakes copper specifically; what matters for the signaling argument is that geochemical sourcing identifies the materials as non-local and unfakeable, so any of these candidate source attributions still places copper in the long-distance index-signal class." This is a fair summary of Hill et al. 2016's actual findings (Claims 1-4 above). The Paper 2 framing correctly handles the Nova Scotia question by noting "as well as, or better than" rather than overstating the Maritime-only attribution.

## Summary Assessment

Hill, Greenlee, and Neff (2016) provide LA-ICP-MS compositional analysis of six copper artifacts from Poverty Point, comparing them against 62 native copper source samples from the Lake Superior, Appalachian, and Maritime regions. Across three discriminant function tests (2-region, 3-region, 4-region), all six PP copper samples were consistently assigned to eastern sources (Maritimes/Appalachians) rather than Lake Superior, with the four-region test placing all six in Nova Scotia specifically. The authors acknowledge two methodological limitations transparently: a small PP sample (~3.2% of the known assemblage) and underrepresentation of southern Appalachian sources in the comparison set, which means the Nova Scotia clustering may reflect sampling gaps rather than actual provenance. The result overturns the longstanding archaeological assumption that PP copper came from the Lake Superior region, while the broader pattern (PP copper is from a geologically constrained, geographically distant source ~800-1600 km away) is unchanged. For the costly-signaling framework's purposes, the paper supports the claim that PP copper is an unfakeable index of long-distance acquisition; the exact source region is a separate empirical question that does not affect the signaling argument.
