## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "Proxy data for eastern North American hydroclimate indicate strong and persistent multi-millennial droughts during the Holocene, but climate model simulations often fail to reproduce the proxy-inferred droughts."
- **Page**: p. 1 (abstract)
- **Type**: empirical / methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (synthesis of prior proxy data and model comparisons)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the core problem the paper addresses. The existence of multi-millennial droughts across the Holocene is well-documented by proxies but poorly captured in models. This data-model mismatch has hindered understanding of the drivers of moisture variability.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "The secular summer insolation trend, combined with the Laurentide Ice Sheet deglaciation and its effect on atmospheric circulation, together explain the extent and duration of drier-than-present climates."
- **Page**: p. 1 (abstract)
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (supported by proxy-model synthesis across paper)
- **Assessment**: Key mechanistic claim. Two drivers account for multi-millennial drought: (1) summer insolation trends and (2) LIS deglaciation effects on atmospheric circulation. Both are orbital/glacial-scale forcings, not stochastic, implying that the broad drought pattern during the Poverty Point period was structurally driven, not random.

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "In our simulations, temperature-driven increases in evapotranspiration exceed regional precipitation gains, drying much of the region during the mid Holocene. This suggests that the mid-Holocene multi-millennial drought was driven by similar processes compared to the drying trajectory projected for mid-latitude North America over this century, which is also primarily driven by warming."
- **Page**: p. 1 (abstract)
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (supported by simulation results, Fig. 4)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to Poverty Point environmental context. During the mid-Holocene (which includes the Poverty Point period), drought was driven not by reduced precipitation but by temperature-driven evapotranspiration exceeding precipitation gains. This means the mechanism of environmental stress was elevated warmth and water loss, not simply lack of rain.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "Proxy data for past moisture variations suggest that the 20th- and 21st-century hydroclimate regime in North America is unusually wet relative to the Holocene, with most of the Holocene characterized by drier-than-present conditions for most of the United States (except southwest) and southern Canada."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (multi-proxy consensus from prior literature)
- **Assessment**: Establishes that drier-than-present conditions were the norm for most of the Holocene, including the Poverty Point period. Modern conditions are the anomaly, not the mid-Holocene drought. This is relevant to parameterizing environmental uncertainty (sigma) in the model: Poverty Point populations lived under drier baseline conditions than the present.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "Key features of prior paleoclimatic reconstructions include long-term increases in moisture availability that produced wetter conditions today across much of mid-latitude North America compared to the early- and mid-Holocene, as well as abrupt millennial and centennial departures from these trends."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (well-established in prior literature, multiple citations)
- **Assessment**: Confirms both the long-term drying trend during the Poverty Point period and the superimposed centennial-scale variability. The centennial departures are critical for the costly signaling model: they represent the shorter-term environmental uncertainty that would have directly affected decision-making by Poverty Point populations.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "In the well-studied MW region, the temporal evolution of the multi-millennial Holocene drought matches that identified in previous studies based on shifts in pollen percentages of forbs and moisture-sensitive tree species, corresponding pollen-based precipitation reconstructions, and lake-level reconstructions, with drying commencing at ca. 10 ka and with peak drought conditions reached by ca. 7 ka."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (multi-proxy convergence with prior literature)
- **Assessment**: In the Midwest, peak drought was reached by ca. 7 ka (5000 BCE), well before the Poverty Point period. By the Poverty Point period (ca. 3.7-3.1 ka), conditions were recovering from peak drought but still substantially drier than present. This places Poverty Point populations in the wetting phase of the Holocene drought cycle, though still under drier-than-modern conditions.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "NE shows an early-Holocene drought followed by an increasing water balance and multi-century variability since 11 ka."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (fewer prior data exist for the Northeast compared to the Midwest)
- **Assessment**: The Northeast shows a different temporal pattern from the Midwest, with multi-century variability superimposed on a wetting trend. This regional differentiation is relevant because it demonstrates spatial heterogeneity in hydroclimate across eastern North America during the Holocene.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: Water balance reconstructions show that "in the MW, peak aridity is reached later in mid Holocene (ca. 7 ka), with a reduced amplitude of ca. -110 mm."
- **Page**: p. 3
- **Type**: empirical (quantitative)
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on BRT-calibrated pollen reconstructions, Fig. 2a)
- **Assessment**: Provides a quantitative magnitude for Midwest Holocene drought: approximately 110 mm reduction in water balance relative to preindustrial levels. By the Poverty Point period (ca. 3.7-3.1 ka), the Midwest water balance had recovered somewhat from this minimum but was still negative relative to preindustrial.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "For the mid-Holocene -8 to 5 ka, the most severe drought conditions were driven by high PET during the substantially warmer than preindustrial summers."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (supported by model decomposition in Fig. 4)
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to Poverty Point conditions. The period 8-5 ka (6000-3000 BCE) encompasses the onset and early portion of the Poverty Point period. Peak drought was driven by high potential evapotranspiration (PET) from warm summers, not by low precipitation. This means the environmental stress during the Poverty Point period was a combination of warm temperatures and high evaporative demand.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "The onset of neoglacial cooling and increased precipitation around 4 ka alleviated the drought conditions and allowed water balance to increase by 2 ka."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (supported by both proxy and model results)
- **Assessment**: Neoglacial cooling beginning around 4 ka (2000 BCE) initiated a shift toward wetter conditions. This timing overlaps with the early Poverty Point period (beginning ca. 1700 BCE / 3.7 ka). The Poverty Point occupation thus occurred during a transitional climate phase: conditions were ameliorating from peak mid-Holocene drought but had not yet reached modern levels.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "It is noteworthy that the shift in PET from negative values before 8 ka to positive, but declining, afterwards predominantly shaped the evolution of the annual water balance curves in our simulations, as the transient dynamics in PET overprinted the roughly linear increase of annual precipitation from 12 to 4 ka."
- **Page**: pp. 4-5
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (supported by simulation decomposition, Fig. 4)
- **Assessment**: PET dynamics dominated water balance evolution throughout the Holocene, including the Poverty Point period. Even though precipitation was increasing, the evapotranspiration signal dominated. This is significant for understanding the resource environment: even with adequate rainfall, high evaporative demand could reduce effective moisture for ecosystems and the resources they supported.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "Sub-millennial climate events and periodicities: Drawing on the subset of well-dated and high-resolution fossil pollen records available for the MW and NE regions, our reconstructions confirm the presence of submillennial climate events and periodicities in temperature and moisture levels superimposed on the multi-millennial climate trends."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on high-resolution subset analysis, Figs. 5-6)
- **Assessment**: Critical for the Poverty Point model. Centennial-to-sub-millennial variability was superimposed on the multi-millennial drought trend. This variability, rather than the slow millennial trend, would have been the environmental uncertainty experienced by human populations on decision-relevant timescales.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "At centennial smoothing bandwidths, water balance and T_jul in all regions show significant variations (lower panels in Fig. 2, a, b) which are, however, generally not coherent among regions (Fig. 5a). Also, the amplitude of the centennial shifts (up to ca. 50 mm; Fig. 5b) is smaller than that of the multi-millennial aridity trend."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical (quantitative)
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (directly measured from reconstructions)
- **Assessment**: Provides a quantitative estimate of centennial-scale moisture variability: up to approximately 50 mm shifts in water balance. These centennial variations were not spatially coherent across regions, meaning they represent regional-to-local variability that would have differentially affected different parts of the Poverty Point exchange network. This spatial incoherence is relevant to the costly signaling model because it creates conditions where inter-regional cooperation and information exchange become valuable.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: "The outstanding, spatially coherent centennial event occurs at ca. 5.5-5.0 ka, initiating with a significant centennial-scale rise in water balance in all regions at ca. 5.5 ka (Fig. 2a) and leading to a transient multi-century moisture maximum (Fig. 5a)."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (identified in reconstructions and corroborated by independent proxy data)
- **Assessment**: The 5.5-5.0 ka wet event (ca. 3500-3000 BCE) slightly precedes the Poverty Point period. This centennial-scale wet episode interrupted the mid-Holocene aridity, suggesting that the centuries leading into the Poverty Point period experienced a brief amelioration of drought. Shuman (ref. 29) "also identified a period of increased moisture at 5.6-4.5 ka spanning the eastern North American mid-latitudes as the outstanding centennial-millennial scale deviation from the Holocene multi-millennial trends in this region" (p. 6).

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "Numerous significant periodicities in water balance and T_jul were found in wavelet analyzes, which we ran on both the transient simulations and a subset of proxy reconstructions based on high-resolution pollen sequences available for the MW and NE clusters... A notable clustering of periodicities around 0.2 ka is observed for both reconstructions and simulations in MW and NE."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (wavelet analysis of both proxy and model data, Fig. 6)
- **Assessment**: The ~200-year periodicity in moisture variability is significant for the Poverty Point model. Over the ~600-year Poverty Point occupation, populations would have experienced approximately 3 cycles of this variability. This quasi-periodic moisture oscillation represents a predictable-enough environmental rhythm that populations could have adapted to through aggregation and signaling strategies.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "The -200-year periodicities are also found in earlier simulation experiments with and without solar forcing, and may either reflect the role of solar forcing on North American hydroclimates, since cycles around 210 years has been linked to Suess/de Vries cycles, or represent internal variability."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (multiple possible explanations, not definitively resolved)
- **Assessment**: The ~200-year moisture cycle may be driven by solar forcing (Suess/de Vries cycles) or internal climate variability. For the Poverty Point model, the cause matters less than the existence of quasi-periodic centennial variability. If solar-forced, this variability would have been a persistent feature of the climate throughout the Poverty Point occupation.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: "In NE reconstructions, the significant periodicities cluster around 400 and 500 years, especially for T_jul (Fig. 6b). Similar periodicities were observed in the marine sediment cores retrieved from the North Atlantic and Arctic oceans and also in earlier analyses of lake-level reconstructions as well as the pollen data in the NE region."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (detected in wavelet analysis, corroborated by independent records)
- **Assessment**: Multi-centennial periodicities of 400-500 years in the Northeast complement the 200-year periodicity in the Midwest. Different regions experienced moisture variability on different timescales, reinforcing the spatial incoherence of sub-millennial climate variations. For the Poverty Point exchange network, which connected populations across multiple regions, these different periodicities would have created variable risk environments across the network.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: "The (relative) drought conditions through much of the Holocene, as delineated by both our reconstructions and the MPI-ESM and EC-Earth simulations, suggest that the recent and modern climate is unusually wet while drier conditions seem to be the norm during most of the Holocene. This period of enhanced aridity was largely due to elevated summer insolation, temperature, and evaporative demand."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (supported by convergent proxy-model evidence throughout the paper)
- **Assessment**: Reiterates that Holocene aridity, including during the Poverty Point period, was the baseline condition, not the exception. The mechanism (elevated summer temperatures driving evapotranspiration) implies that resource stress during the Poverty Point period was thermally driven. This is relevant for parameterizing environmental uncertainty: sigma values should reflect drier-than-modern baseline conditions with superimposed centennial variability.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "Future simulations project that, as greenhouse gas concentrations rise and global temperatures increase, midcontinental North America will experience an increase in precipitation yet a decrease in plant-available soil moisture, because of enhanced evaporative demand."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: interpretive (projection)
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on established climate projections, IPCC)
- **Assessment**: The paper draws a direct parallel between mid-Holocene drought mechanisms and projected future drying: both are driven by temperature-induced evapotranspiration exceeding precipitation gains. This analogy suggests that the mid-Holocene provides a reasonable analog for understanding future drying trajectories, and that the environmental stress experienced by Poverty Point populations may recur.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "It is hence plausible that the reversal of the natural neoglacial cooling observed over recent millennia through anthropogenic global warming might cause a return of midcontinental aridity in eastern North America, for which the early to mid Holocene serves as a reasonable natural analog."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (plausibility argument, not a demonstrated projection)
- **Assessment**: Reinforces the analogy between mid-Holocene and future climate conditions. The early-to-mid Holocene drought pattern provides a baseline for understanding what drier conditions in eastern North America look like, and the Poverty Point period falls within this analog window.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "Warming and drying rapidly increased to maximum drought conditions at 8 ka where the strong warming caused higher PET than today, while annual precipitation, despite a gradual rising trend, remained almost as low as during the late deglaciation (Fig. 4). This implies that drought is driven by both the higher PET and clearly lower annual P than under preindustrial conditions."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical (quantitative interpretation of simulations)
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on model results, Fig. 4)
- **Assessment**: While this describes conditions at 8 ka (well before Poverty Point), it establishes the trajectory from which conditions were recovering during the Poverty Point period. By 3.7-3.1 ka, PET was declining (as summer insolation decreased) and precipitation was increasing, but the system had not yet returned to preindustrial equilibrium.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: The PC1 of water balance reconstructions explains 37.0% of total variance, with positive loadings at nearly all sites (50 out of 53), capturing "the significant shared water balance trend of all sites with an early-to-mid-Holocene drought transitioning into a late-Holocene wetting pattern."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical (quantitative)
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (PCA of 53 sites, Fig. 3)
- **Assessment**: The dominant mode of Holocene moisture variability (37% of variance) is the multi-millennial drought-to-wet transition. This first principal component confirms that the broad-scale drying trend was spatially coherent across eastern North America. During the Poverty Point period (ca. 3.7-3.1 ka), the PC1 scores (Fig. 3c) show the system was transitioning from negative (dry) to positive (wet), consistent with ameliorating but still dry conditions.

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: PC2 (22.5% of variance) "represents the temporal variation in longitudinal water balance contrast" with "clusters of positive loadings in MW and negative loadings in NE." PC2 sample scores reach a maximum in the Early Holocene (ca. > 8.5 ka), "representing the period of maximum contrast in water balance anomalies due to peak multi-millennial drought in NE before the early wet conditions ended in MW."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical (quantitative)
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (PCA results, Fig. 3d-e)
- **Assessment**: The second mode of variability captures an east-west moisture gradient. During the Poverty Point period, this gradient was still present but weakening. This spatial differentiation in moisture availability across eastern North America would have created heterogeneous resource environments across the Poverty Point exchange network, potentially increasing the value of inter-regional cooperation.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: "As the early Holocene climate warmed, however, the persistence of low precipitation favored drought conditions from 11 ka onward. For the mid-Holocene -8 to 5 ka, the most severe drought conditions were driven by high PET during the substantially warmer than preindustrial summers. Conversely, the onset of neoglacial cooling and increased precipitation around 4 ka alleviated the drought conditions and allowed water balance to increase by 2 ka."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical / interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (convergent proxy-model evidence, Fig. 4)
- **Assessment**: Provides the complete chronological framework for mid-Holocene drought and its alleviation. The period 8-5 ka saw the most severe drought (driven by high PET from warm summers). Neoglacial cooling from ~4 ka (2000 BCE) began alleviating drought. The Poverty Point period (ca. 3.7-3.1 ka / 1700-1100 BCE) thus falls during the transition from peak drought to amelioration, a period of declining but still elevated aridity relative to modern conditions.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Based on observed or measured data (proxy reconstructions, simulation outputs, direct measurements)
- **Interpretive**: Inference or explanation drawn from data
- **Methodological**: Describes analytical approaches, data sources, or study design
- **Quantitative**: Provides specific numerical values or magnitudes

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Supported by convergent proxy-model evidence, multiple independent datasets, or well-established methods
- **Moderate**: Supported by analysis but with caveats (e.g., spatial or temporal limitations, multiple alternative explanations)
- **Assertion without evidence**: Claim made without supporting data in this paper

## Summary Assessment

### Relevance to Poverty Point Project

This paper provides the most comprehensive synthesis of Holocene moisture variability in eastern North America available, combining machine-learning-based proxy reconstructions with an ensemble of four climate model simulations. Its relevance to the Poverty Point project is substantial but requires careful application given the latitudinal offset between the study regions (42-51N) and Poverty Point (32.63N).

### Key Findings for the Costly Signaling Model

1. **Environmental baseline**: The Poverty Point period (ca. 3.7-3.1 ka) occurred during a transition from peak mid-Holocene drought toward wetter conditions. Conditions were drier than present but ameliorating, driven by declining summer insolation and the onset of neoglacial cooling.

2. **Drought mechanism**: Mid-Holocene drought was driven primarily by temperature-induced high evapotranspiration exceeding precipitation, not by low rainfall alone. This means environmental stress operated through elevated warmth and moisture loss rather than simple aridity.

3. **Multi-scale variability**: Two scales of variability are documented: (a) the multi-millennial drought-to-wet transition (PC1, 37% of variance), and (b) centennial-scale variations with ~200-year periodicity in the Midwest and ~400-500-year periodicity in the Northeast. The centennial variability (amplitude up to ~50 mm in water balance) was spatially incoherent across regions, meaning different areas experienced drought at different times.

4. **Implications for sigma (environmental uncertainty)**: The centennial-scale variability is most relevant for parameterizing sigma in the model. The spatial incoherence of this variability means that inter-regional cooperation (a key prediction of the costly signaling model) would have been adaptive: groups in different regions would experience different centennial drought events at different times, making information exchange and resource sharing valuable.

5. **Implications for aggregation**: The 5.5-5.0 ka (3500-3000 BCE) wet event slightly precedes the Poverty Point period, suggesting that conditions may have been temporarily favorable for aggregation just before Poverty Point's fluorescence. The subsequent return toward drier conditions during the Poverty Point period would have increased the value of cooperative strategies.

### Caveats

- The study regions are centered at 42-51N; Poverty Point at 32.63N falls in the southeastern US, south of these clusters. The Midwest results are most applicable but represent conditions several hundred kilometers north of Poverty Point.
- The paper focuses on water balance (P minus PET), which is a useful integrative measure but does not directly capture resource-relevant variables like river discharge, flood frequency, or ecosystem productivity.
- The centennial variability estimates come from a subset of high-resolution records; the full spatial coverage may differ.
