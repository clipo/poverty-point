## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "The subsistence economy which supported Poverty Point culture in northeast Louisiana is at present incompletely understood."
- **Page**: p. 73
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence
- **Assessment**: Sets the stage for why new subsistence data from peripheral sites matters. Directly relevant to the question of what economic base supported monumental construction and aggregation.

---

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: Analysis of faunal and floral material from the J.W. Copes site "suggests that the site may have been occupied year-round and that the subsistence system may have been characterized by specialization in the procurement of deer and fish."
- **Page**: p. 73
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Central claim of the paper. Year-round occupation at a peripheral Poverty Point site challenges a purely seasonal-aggregation model. Specialization in deer and fish procurement is directly relevant to resource base supporting the signaling system.

---

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: "Our understanding of the Poverty Point cultural system has been impeded by a lack of evidence for the subsistence strategies which supported the cultural developments." This lack of information is "in part due to the concentration of investigations at the type site, where preservation of organic remains is generally poor."
- **Page**: p. 74
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Explains why subsistence data from peripheral sites like J.W. Copes are essential. The Poverty Point type site itself preserves organic remains poorly, creating a gap in understanding the economic basis for monumental construction.

---

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: "Past inferences about dietary strategies which have ranged from suggestions of large scale maize agriculture to intensive hunting-gathering, have been based on the apparent organizational superstructure of the Poverty Point cultural system and on ecological argument rather than on archaeobiological data."
- **Page**: p. 74
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Important methodological critique. Previous subsistence models were inferred from social complexity rather than direct evidence, a circular reasoning problem. Relevant to how we parameterize subsistence in the signaling model.

---

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: Thomas and Campbell (1978) reported test excavations at localities on the periphery of the Poverty Point site that provided some evidence of subsistence practices. Floral remains included "the nuts of hickory and pecan, acorns, persimmons, hackberry, chenopodium, polygonum, and wild bean, as well as domesticated squash (Shea 1978)."
- **Page**: pp. 74-75
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (cites prior published analysis)
- **Assessment**: Documents the range of plant foods at Poverty Point periphery sites, including both wild gathered foods and possibly domesticated squash. Relevant to understanding the breadth of the subsistence base.

---

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "While it is possible that the squash associated with Poverty Point materials recovered by Thomas and Campbell could have been derived from a later ceramic occupation, it is not unreasonable to suggest that small-scale gardening comprised a minor component of a hunting-gathering-gardening complex (Thomas and Campbell 1978:271), since squash cultivation has been documented as early as 2400 B.C. in the Midwest."
- **Page**: p. 75
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Raises the possibility that Poverty Point subsistence included small-scale horticulture alongside hunting and gathering. If confirmed, this would indicate a mixed subsistence strategy rather than pure foraging.

---

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: "It has become clear that for reasons of soil pH and the abrasive action of active alluviation of floodplain sites, archaeobiological remains are generally no longer extant."
- **Page**: pp. 75-76
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Explains the taphonomic bias affecting Poverty Point subsistence studies. Floodplain sites, which would be key locations for understanding resource exploitation, generally lack preserved organic remains.

---

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: The Copes site "is located on a natural levee adjacent to a low-lying arcuate brake, approximately 1.6km west of Joes Bayou and about 13km south-southeast of the Poverty Point site."
- **Page**: p. 77
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the spatial relationship between J.W. Copes and the main Poverty Point site. At 13km distance, Copes is within a reasonable daily foraging radius but represents a distinct residential locus, relevant to understanding settlement system structure.

---

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: "The brake was formerly a segment of a former Arkansas River course which flowed through the area between 6000 and 5000 B.P. (Lenzer 1978). At the time of Poverty Point occupation, the brake was apparently an oxbow lake or swamp."
- **Page**: p. 77
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (cites geomorphological study)
- **Assessment**: The site's proximity to an oxbow lake/swamp environment is significant for understanding aquatic resource access. Relevant to the ecotone diversity hypothesis.

---

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: "Prehistorically, the region was forested in bottomland hardwoods, including several species of oak, red gum, pecan, and in low areas, tupelo gum and cypress."
- **Page**: p. 77
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents the forest composition surrounding the site. Nut-bearing hardwoods (oak, pecan) and bottomland species relevant to understanding gathering opportunities and the ecological context of site location.

---

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: "The site covers an area of approximately 0.5ha. At the center of the site is a 200m2 roughly oval deposit of undisturbed Poverty Point midden. The midden is lenticular in cross section with a maximum thickness of 1.0m."
- **Page**: p. 77
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes site size and midden dimensions. A 0.5ha site with 1.0m of midden represents a substantial accumulation for a small peripheral site, suggesting repeated or sustained occupation.

---

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: "Prior to recent land leveling activities, the Poverty Point component was buried beneath a substantial Tchefuncte-Marksville habitation midden. This accumulated midden apparently served to protect the organic remains associated with the Poverty Point component."
- **Page**: p. 77
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Explains the exceptional organic preservation at J.W. Copes. The later Tchefuncte-Marksville midden sealed and protected the Poverty Point deposits, making this site unusual for the region.

---

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: A total of 980 artifacts were recovered from the test unit. The composition "confirms that the midden is an unmixed Poverty Point component."
- **Page**: p. 79
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes that the assemblage is a clean Poverty Point deposit, not mixed with later occupations, increasing confidence in the faunal associations.

---

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: There are "apparent changes in the relative frequencies of types of Poverty Point objects that parallel those documented by Ford at the Poverty Point site (Webb et al. n.d.:48). Specifically, there is a relative decrease in cylindrical objects in the upper levels and an associated increase in melon-shaped and cross-grooved types, suggesting that the Poverty Point component may span several hundred years of occupation."
- **Page**: pp. 79-80
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The stratigraphic change in PPO types parallels the sequence at the type site, suggesting that Copes was occupied across much of the Poverty Point period (potentially several hundred years). This is relevant to duration and continuity of the settlement system.

---

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "The lithic assemblage recovered is quite small. Raw materials are similar to those considered to derive from nonlocal sources. Included among recovered lithics is one cube of galena. Galena such as that collected at the Copes site apparently was mined from deposits in southeastern Missouri (Walthall et al. 1982)."
- **Page**: p. 80
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Even this small peripheral site participated in the long-distance exchange network (galena from Missouri, ~800km away). This is directly relevant to the signaling model: exotic goods circulated not just at the main site but throughout the settlement system.

---

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: "There were 142 bones recovered from Hillman's excavation and the 1980 test unit that could be identified at least to class (Table 2). In addition, several hundred identifiable bones, mainly small fish, were recovered from flotation samples (Table 3)."
- **Page**: p. 80
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides the quantitative basis for faunal analysis. The large number of fish bones from flotation indicates that fish were a major resource at the site.

---

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: "Species included deer, raccoon, grey squirrel, swamp rabbit, mallard, crow, turtle, and 15 species of fish. Deer and fish dominated the assemblage."
- **Page**: p. 80
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Deer and fish dominance establishes a dual-resource procurement strategy. The diversity of species (mammals, birds, reptiles, fish) indicates broad-spectrum foraging. Relevant to modeling subsistence resource structure.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: From Table 2, the faunal inventory shows: deer (41 elements), deer/elk (2), large mammal (18), catfish/Ictalurus spp. (24), blue catfish/I. furcatus (12), flathead catfish/Pylodictus olivaris (14), bowfin/Amia calva (2), freshwater drum/Aplodinotus grunniens (1), buffalo/Ictiobus spp. (2), among other species. Total identifiable elements: 142.
- **Page**: p. 82 (Table 2)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantitative faunal data. Deer (41 elements) and catfish species (at least 40+ elements combining all Ictaluridae) dominate. This confirms the dual deer-fish subsistence pattern. Catfish species (channel, blue, flathead) indicate exploitation of river/bayou habitats.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: "Annuli growth on fish vertebrae indicates that the fish were collected mainly during the early through late summer."
- **Page**: p. 80
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Direct seasonal indicator. Fish were taken primarily in summer. Combined with deer data, this helps establish the seasonal round at the site.

---

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: "The preponderance of deer suggests that the site may have been occupied during the fall or winter, since deer hunting is most productive during that time of year (cf. Smith 1975)."
- **Page**: p. 80
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Seasonal inference from faunal composition. If fish indicate summer and deer indicate fall/winter, together they suggest multi-season or year-round occupation. This is the basis for the year-round occupation argument.

---

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "The only direct evidence for seasonality in the deer assemblage is a nearly complete skull with antlers shed, suggesting an early spring kill."
- **Page**: p. 80
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The shed-antler skull indicates spring deer procurement, adding a third season (spring) to the evidence for site occupation. Combined with summer fish and fall/winter deer abundance, this strengthens the year-round occupation interpretation.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: "The presence of a mallard in the collection may point to a winter season occupation, although mallards are known to inhabit the Lower Mississippi Valley throughout the year (cf. St. Amant 1959)."
- **Page**: pp. 80-84
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Mallard presence is an ambiguous seasonal indicator since they are year-round residents. Author acknowledges the limitation.

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: "The presence of nuts and Chenopodium may reflect late summer or early fall collecting activities."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Botanical remains add another seasonal indicator (late summer/fall). Combined with fish (summer), deer (fall/winter), and shed antler skull (spring), this provides evidence for occupation across multiple seasons.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: Carbonized floral remains from flotation samples included "a Chenopodium sp. seed, hickory or pecan and acorn shells, cane, abundant wood charcoal (not yet identified), and one wild bean pod (cf. Strophostyle sp.)."
- **Page**: p. 80
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents gathered plant foods at the site. Hickory/pecan, acorns, and wild beans represent important storable food resources. Chenopodium could be cultivated or wild-collected.

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: "Although a larger sample will be required to adequately evaluate the seasonal use of the site, the present assemblage does point to the possibility of a year-round occupation."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The author's own summary of the seasonality evidence. Cautious but points toward year-round occupation. If peripheral sites like Copes were occupied year-round, this changes the model from pure seasonal aggregation to a more complex settlement pattern.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: "The results of this analysis bears reporting since greater sedentism has long been a suspected attribute of Poverty Point settlement patterns."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Places the Copes data in the broader context of sedentism debates. If Poverty Point period populations were more sedentary than typical hunter-gatherers, this affects how we model aggregation dynamics and the costs/benefits of signaling.

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: "The appearance of a more sedentary settlement pattern, as part of the evolutionary trend which resulted in the Poverty Point Culture, may have been permitted by or may have been a response to a hunting strategy involving bottomland species that selectively concentrated on the procurement of deer and the harvesting of large quantities of riverine fish species."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Proposes a causal link between a specialized deer-and-fish subsistence strategy and increasing sedentism. The bottomland/riverine focus would have provided a sufficiently stable resource base to reduce mobility, which is directly relevant to modeling conditions that enabled aggregation and monumental construction.

### Claim 28

- **Statement**: "There is also some suggestion that this hunting-gathering economy may have been supplemented by small-scale gardening, including the cultivation of squash and perhaps small seed annuals."
- **Page**: p. 84
- **Type**: speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Acknowledges the possibility of horticultural supplementation but evidence is not strong. Chenopodium could be cultivated but could also be wild. The squash evidence comes from a different site (Thomas and Campbell 1978) with potential contamination from later occupation.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited

## Summary Assessment

Jackson 1981a provides rare direct subsistence evidence from a Poverty Point period site, based on well-preserved faunal and floral remains from the J.W. Copes site, a small (0.5ha) peripheral settlement located 13km south-southeast of the Poverty Point type site. The key findings for the costly signaling project are: (1) subsistence was dominated by deer and fish, suggesting a specialized dual-resource procurement strategy focused on bottomland environments; (2) seasonal indicators from fish vertebrae (summer), deer abundance (fall/winter), shed antler skull (spring), and nut/seed remains (late summer/fall) collectively point to possible year-round occupation rather than purely seasonal use; and (3) even this small peripheral site participated in the long-distance exchange network (galena from southeastern Missouri). These findings bear directly on the aggregation dynamics model: if peripheral sites were occupied year-round or nearly so, then the Poverty Point settlement system was more sedentary than a simple seasonal-aggregation model implies, and the resource base (deer + fish + gathered plant foods) was sufficiently productive to support reduced mobility, which in turn could have enabled the sustained labor investment required for monumental construction.
