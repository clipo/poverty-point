## Claims

### Claim 1: Four hypotheses for why Ju/'hoansi men hunt large game

- **Statement**: From the Abstract: "Here, I will use data from studies that have been carried out over a 34-year period among the Ju/'hoansi (!Kung Bushmen) to evaluate four hypotheses concerning why foragers target large game: reciprocity, costly signaling, nepotism, and long-term political goals aimed at bringing about conditions conducive to cooperative breeding. I will propose on the basis of qualitative and quantitative data that (1) all four hypotheses identify benefits that are gained by Ju/'hoansi through producing and distributing a surplus of meat, (2) different benefits might be important at different stages of a man's life, and (3) the pursuit of broader political goals merits serious consideration in accounting for men's work effort."
- **Page**: p. 407 (Abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive (paper's central thesis)
- **Evidence cited by author**: 34-year longitudinal Ju/'hoansi dataset; multiple field seasons by Wiessner and colleagues
- **Notes**: Frames the paper as a multi-hypothesis evaluation, not a defense of any single mechanism. Paper 1's costly-signaling framework would lean on the costly-signaling hypothesis as one of the four.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (long-term dataset)
- **Assessment**: Establishes that no single mechanism — including costly signaling — explains all the observed patterns; instead, multiple benefits accrue through large-game hunting at different life stages.

### Claim 2: Costly signaling supports first-marriage entry but not enduring polygyny

- **Statement**: "While signaling hidden abilities through large-game hunting appears to be an important factor for at least first marriage, once bride service has been completed, there is little indication that signaling via hunting success played a role in men outcompeting other men for mates, even though men hunt actively into their 50s and 60s."
- **Page**: p. 421 (Section 4.1.2 Quantitative evidence, end of subsection)
- **Type**: Interpretive (drawing from Table 1 quantitative comparisons)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Table 1 demographic comparisons between good and poor hunters in /Kae/kae and hxaro samples
- **Notes**: Important nuance: the costly-signaling-as-mate-acquisition mechanism is partially supported (gets first marriage) but not as a sustained reproductive-success engine.
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Refines but does not undermine the costly-signaling hypothesis. Useful for the Paper 1 framework: signaling matters, but the specific quantitative mechanism Paper 1 attributes to it (10-30% additional resources during shortfalls) is not directly supported by this paper.

### Claim 3: Good hunters have ~2× more hxaro partners than poor hunters

- **Statement**: From Table 2 (p. 422): "Number of hxaro partners: Good hunters and wives n=9, N=386, Mean ± S.E. = 42.9 ± 4.83; Poor hunters and wives n=9, N=213, Mean ± S.E. = 23.7 ± 3.63; p=.006." The text states: "The number of hxaro partners of good hunters and their wives were significantly greater than those of poor hunters and their wives."
- **Page**: p. 422 (Table 2 + surrounding text)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Wiessner's 1974 hxaro field study of 955 hxaro partnerships among 59 adults and 14 children
- **Notes**: This is the closest specific quantitative finding to what Paper 1 attributes to Wiessner 2002. The premium is 80% (42.9 / 23.7 ≈ 1.81×, i.e., 81% more partners), not the "10-30%" Paper 1 cites.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Supports a hxaro-network premium for good hunters' families but does NOT support the specific "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" figure Paper 1 attributes to this paper. The actual partner-count premium is ~80%, much larger than 10-30%.

### Claim 4: Good hunters have substantially more household possessions

- **Statement**: From Table 2 (p. 422): "Number of household possessions: Good hunters and wives N=544, Mean ± S.E. = 60.4 ± 4.79; Poor hunters and wives N=366, Mean ± S.E. = 40.7 ± 5.97; p=.02." Text adds: "Comparison of number of material possessions held by the families of good and poor hunters such as beadwork, blankets, clothing, equipment, etc. (Table 2) reveals that good hunters had significantly more possessions than poor hunters. For both, approximately 69% of possessions were received in hxaro and the rest recently made or purchased and destined for hxaro networks."
- **Page**: p. 423 (Table 2; Section 4.2 prose)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: 1974-1975 field-study survey of household material possessions
- **Notes**: 60.4 / 40.7 = 1.48× ratio (48% more possessions). Most possessions (69%) are hxaro-acquired, so this directly indexes hxaro-network productivity.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantifies the hxaro-network premium for good-hunter families in possessions terms (48% more), again differing from Paper 1's "10-30%" attribution.

### Claim 5: Good hunters have many more distant-kin hxaro partners

- **Statement**: From Table 2 (p. 422): "Number of distant kin who are hxaro partners (r<1/8): Good hunters and wives N=248, Mean ± S.E. = 27.6 ± 4.10; Poor hunters and wives N=103, Mean ± S.E. = 11.4 ± 2.40; p=.004." Text on p. 423: "good hunters and their wives had a mean of 16.2 more partners who were related to them by r=1/16 or less than did poor hunters and their wives. These results suggest that... in hxaro exchange with distant kin where nepotistic benefits are lower, people were more willing to engage in partnerships with good hunters and their wives than with poor hunters and their wives, presumably because they anticipated greater material or social payoffs."
- **Page**: pp. 422-423 (Table 2; surrounding analysis)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Hxaro partnership genealogies with kinship coefficients
- **Notes**: 27.6 / 11.4 = 2.42× ratio for distant-kin partnerships (142% more). The distant-kin difference is interpreted as evidence that the partner-choice premium is operating through hxaro selection (people prefer to partner with good hunters' families when nepotistic incentives don't dominate the choice).
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Most directly relevant to Paper 1's "partner-choice premium for visibly cooperative individuals" claim. Supports the Hawkes & Bliege Bird 2002 framing in qualitative terms; the actual magnitude (142% more distant-kin partners) is well above any "10-30%" range.

### Claim 6: Good hunters' camp-residence stability is roughly 2× longer than poor hunters'

- **Statement**: From Table 3 (p. 425): "Duration of camp residence (in years), 1964-1998: Good hunters n=9, Mean ± S.E. = 32.0 ± 1.41; Poor hunters n=9, Mean ± S.E. = 17.4 ± 2.46; p=.0001."
- **Page**: p. 425 (Table 3)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Wiessner's longitudinal residence-tracking 1964-1998
- **Notes**: 32.0 / 17.4 = 1.84× ratio. Good hunters maintain settled, stable camps; poor hunters move frequently. Highly statistically significant (p=.0001).
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes that good hunters' families have substantially more residential stability, which Wiessner argues facilitates accumulating close-kin co-residence and supports cooperative breeding (the long-term political goals hypothesis).

### Claim 7: Good hunters' wives' children survive at higher rates

- **Statement**: From Table 4 (p. 426): "% of children ever born who survived to age 15: Good hunters' wives 83%; Poor hunters' wives 67%." And: "Children surviving to age 15: Good hunters' wives Mean ± S.E. = 3.3 ± 1.00 (n=9); Poor hunters' wives Mean ± S.E. = 2.0 ± 0.53; p=.25" (note: marginal significance for combined, but the percentage difference is substantial).
- **Page**: p. 426 (Table 4; p. 425 surrounding text)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Howell (1979/2000) demographic data combined with Wiessner's hxaro-sample observations
- **Notes**: 83% vs 67% survival = 16 percentage points absolute, or 24% relative advantage in survival (83/67 = 1.24×). Authors note this difference "may be attributed to a number of factors: genetic inheritance, provisioning, or growing up in a community with dedicated alloparents."
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (Wiessner is cautious about whether this is paternal provisioning vs. broader family-network effects)
- **Assessment**: A specific quantitative finding that COULD ground a partner-choice / network-access premium claim. The 24% relative survival advantage is closer to the "10-30%" range Paper 1 attributes to Wiessner, but it's a child-survival outcome rather than the "additional resources during shortfalls" Paper 1 specifies.

### Claim 8: Good hunters' children co-reside as adults at much higher rates

- **Statement**: From Table 4 (p. 426): "% of adult married children who were co-resident in 1998: Good hunters' wives 84%; Poor hunters' wives 31%; p=.03." Text on p. 425-426: "Having a father who is a good hunter thus appears to attract married children to the camp, allowing both husbands and wives to enjoy much greater opportunities to invest in their grandchildren and to be assisted by their children later in old age."
- **Page**: pp. 425-426 (Table 4; p. 426 prose)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: 1998 follow-up census combined with 1974 marriage data
- **Notes**: 84% / 31% = 2.71× ratio (171% more co-residence). The largest demographic-outcome difference reported in the paper.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (statistically significant; p=.03)
- **Assessment**: Establishes the late-life payoff to good hunters and their wives. Directly relevant to the Wiessner argument that meat is converted to long-term political goals (cooperative breeding conditions) rather than to short-term reciprocity.

### Claim 9: Hxaro provides 69% of household possessions and alternate residences during shortfalls

- **Statement**: "Hxaro supplied families with material possessions from arrows to clothing to beadwork. For example, in 1974, 69% of the possessions of Ju/'hoansi at /Kae/kae were obtained through hxaro (Wiessner, 1986). Moreover, the underlying relationship furnished people with alternate residences in times of food shortage or conflict. A survey of the visits made by 20 !Kung adults during 1968 and 1974 lasting 1 week or longer revealed that the average !Kung made 1.5 extended visits a year with a mean duration of 2.2 months per visit. Eighty of the 86 extended visits recorded (93%) were made to camps in which ego or his/her spouse had hxaro partners (Wiessner, 1981, 1986). Hxaro also structured meat sharing outside the camp."
- **Page**: p. 422 (Section 4.2 mid-section)
- **Type**: Empirical (function of hxaro)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Wiessner 1981, 1982, 1986
- **Notes**: 69% of household possessions from hxaro; 93% of extended visits during shortfalls and conflicts to hxaro-partner camps. These are the closest quantitative findings to "additional resources during shortfalls" — and they are NOT 10-30%; they are 69-93% of the relevant flows being mediated by hxaro.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: This is the most direct empirical claim about hxaro's role in shortfall buffering. The figure of 93% of shortfall-period extended visits being to hxaro partners is the closest analog to "additional resources during shortfalls" — and it shows hxaro is the dominant mechanism, not a marginal 10-30% supplement. Paper 1's "10-30% additional resources" attribution does not match this evidence either; if anything, the actual figure is much larger.

### Claim 10: Large-game hunting provides up to 80% of diet during hunger months

- **Statement**: "Calories provided by large-game hunting made a substantial contribution to the welfare of the hunter and fellow camp members. For example, figures from 17 months of subsistence studies between 1996 and 1998 in the Nyae Nyae area show that during two 'hungry months' when food was scarce, large kills provided more than 80% of the diet (Wiessner & N!aici, 1998). Approximately 20% of this meat was given to relatives outside the camp."
- **Page**: p. 416 (Section 3.3 Surplus production: hunting and healing)
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence cited by author**: Wiessner & N!aici 1998 (subsistence-study data)
- **Notes**: During hungry months, large-game hunting provided more than 80% of camp calories. This is a within-camp calorie measure, not a between-good-vs-poor-hunter comparison.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides another large quantitative figure (80% of diet during shortfalls) that is much larger than the "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" Paper 1 attributes to this paper. The 80% is a community-level dependence on large-game hunting during shortfalls, not an individual-network premium.

### Claim 11: Hxaro is delayed reciprocity, not tit-for-tat

- **Statement**: "Hxaro... involved a roughly balanced, delayed exchange of nonfood gifts — beads, arrows, tools, clothing — that gave information about the status of an underlying relationship. Partners were said to 'hold each other in their hearts' and be willing to offer various kinds of assistance when one had and the other was in need (Wiessner, 1982, 1986, 1994). Hxaro partners of adult men and women were selected from the wide range of consanguineous kin. A detailed study of 955 hxaro partnerships of 59 adults and 14 children (Wiessner, 1977, 1982) found that the average Ju/'hoansi adult had 16 partners though the range was 2-42. Sets of partners were comprised of individuals of both sexes and all ages, with a variety of skills. Partnerships were well distributed in space — approximately 18% of partners resided in ego's camp, 24% in neighboring camps 1-15 km away, 25% in camps 16-50 km away, and 33% in camps between 51 and 200 km away (Wiessner, 1982). Exchange of hxaro gifts was carried out discreetly so as not to attract attention."
- **Page**: p. 421 (Section 4.2)
- **Type**: Empirical (hxaro-network description)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Wiessner 1977, 1982, 1986, 1994
- **Notes**: Critical for the framework's "obligation network" parameterization. Average adult has 16 partners (range 2-42); partners distributed in space (18% same camp, 33% in distant camps 51-200 km away). These are the quantitative network parameters for the Ju/'hoansi case that could ground a calibration in the costly-signaling framework's network module.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The 16-partner mean and the 18-24-25-33% spatial distribution are the most directly modelable quantitative parameters for the framework's obligation-network module. More relevant to Paper 1's framework than the disputed "10-30%" claim.

### Claim 12: Multiple hypotheses, multiple benefits at different life stages

- **Statement**: From Concluding remarks (p. 429): "Until now, some studies of why men hunt large game have sought one explanation to the exclusion of others in order to demonstrate the power of a particular approach. However, long-term data from the Ju/'hoansi suggest that, in addition to provisioning, signaling, reciprocity, nepotism, and long-term political objectives were all outcomes achieved by producing and distributing a surplus of meat. Moreover, the data indicate that different motivations might be important at various stages of a man's life."
- **Page**: p. 429 (Concluding remarks)
- **Type**: Interpretive (synthesis)
- **Evidence cited by author**: Synthesis of all four hypothesis tests
- **Notes**: Wiessner's overall conclusion is non-reductive: signaling, reciprocity, nepotism, and political goals all contribute, and their relative importance shifts over a man's life. This complicates any single-mechanism interpretation Paper 1 might want to draw.
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (Wiessner's own conclusion)
- **Assessment**: Useful for Paper 1's broader framing: the framework's "λ_W" parameter is itself a composite of multiple mechanisms (within-group prestige, partner choice, network access, etc.), consistent with Wiessner's multi-mechanism reading. Provides theoretical-pluralism support for the framework's bracketing rather than its specific quantification.

## Claim Type Definitions

- **Empirical**: Direct observation or measurement reported by the author
- **Interpretive**: Inference from empirical data; not directly observable
- **Speculative**: Acknowledged by the author as tentative, or basis unclear
- **Methodological**: Claim about how to conduct or interpret research

## Evidence Strength Ratings

- **Strong**: Claim is well-supported by the evidence presented
- **Moderate**: Claim is partially supported; some evidence but gaps remain
- **Weak**: Claim is poorly supported by the evidence presented
- **Assertion without evidence**: No evidence cited; claim is stated as fact without support

## Verification Notes

**Critical attribution issue with Paper 1.** Paper 1 line 147 attributes to Wiessner (2002): "high-network individuals maintain access to roughly 10-30% additional resources during shortfalls." This specific "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" claim is **not** present in this paper. The paper does provide several quantitative findings about good-vs-poor hunter premiums, but they differ in both magnitude and metric:

- Number of hxaro partners: Good hunters' families have 80% more partners (42.9 vs 23.7; Table 2)
- Number of household possessions: 48% more (60.4 vs 40.7; Table 2)
- Number of distant-kin hxaro partners: 142% more (27.6 vs 11.4; Table 2)
- Camp-residence duration: 84% longer (32.0 vs 17.4 years; Table 3)
- Children surviving to age 15: 24% relative advantage (83% vs 67%; Table 4)
- Adult married children co-resident: 171% more (84% vs 31%; Table 4)

The paper's strongest shortfall-related quantitative claims are:
- 93% of extended visits during shortfalls and conflicts go to camps with hxaro partners (p. 422)
- 69% of household possessions are obtained through hxaro (p. 422)
- 80% of diet during "hungry months" came from large-game kills (p. 416)

**None of these is "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls."** Paper 1's attribution either:
(a) misremembers the paper's specific figures,
(b) intends a different Wiessner paper (Wiessner 1982, 1986, or 1996 are the candidate alternatives that focus more directly on hxaro mechanics), or
(c) generalizes from the broader literature without being able to attach a specific number to Wiessner 2002.

**Recommended fix for Paper 1 line 147 (already partially revised after the Hawkes 2000 audit)**: Replace the "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" attribution with one of:
- *Qualitative form*: "Wiessner's (2002) hxaro analysis of Ju/'hoansi exchange documents that high-network individuals (including good hunters' families) have substantially more hxaro partners (80% more), more distant-kin partnerships (142% more), and longer camp-residence stability (84% longer) than poor hunters' families, with large-game kills providing more than 80% of the diet during hunger months — quantifying a meaningful but variable network-access premium."
- *Specific quantitative form, with the right number*: "Wiessner's (2002) hxaro analysis of Ju/'hoansi exchange estimates that good hunters' families maintain ~80% more hxaro partners than poor hunters' families (Wiessner 2002:422 Table 2), with hxaro partners providing 93% of the camps visited during food-shortage periods (p. 422)."
- *Cite Wiessner 1982 or 1986 for the original hxaro-as-shortfall-buffering data* if the "10-30%" figure derives from the foundational hxaro papers rather than this 2002 synthesis.

The Paper 1 fix should be in the same style as the Hawkes 2000 fix already applied: replace the unsupported quantitative claim with a documented qualitative or correctly-quantified one. The bracketing argument for $\lambda_W \in [0.05, 0.30]$ should then be re-examined: if neither Hawkes 2000 nor Wiessner 2002 supports the specific "10-20%" or "10-30%" figures originally attributed to them, the bracketing rests entirely on Hawkes & Bliege Bird 2002 alone. That bracketing may need further adjustment after Hawkes & Bliege Bird 2002 is independently audited.

## Summary Assessment

Wiessner (2002) presents a 34-year longitudinal Ju/'hoansi (!Kung) dataset evaluating four hypotheses for why men hunt large game: reciprocity, costly signaling, nepotism, and long-term political goals (cooperative breeding). The paper's main empirical findings document substantial differences between good and poor hunters' families across multiple measures: hxaro partner counts (80% more for good hunters), household possessions (48% more), distant-kin hxaro partners (142% more), camp-residence stability (84% longer), child survival (24% relative advantage), and adult-married-children co-residence (171% more). Wiessner's overall conclusion is non-reductive: all four hypothesized benefits accrue through large-game hunting, with relative importance shifting over a man's life. Costly signaling supports first marriage but not enduring reproductive success in polygyny. The hxaro-network mechanism is dominant in shortfall buffering: 93% of extended visits during shortfalls/conflicts go to hxaro-partner camps, and 69% of household possessions are obtained through hxaro. **Paper 1's specific "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" attribution to this paper is not supported by the paper's actual quantitative findings**, which range from 24% to 171% across different measures. Paper 1 line 147 should be revised to remove the unsupported figure, in the same style as the Hawkes 2000 fix already applied.
