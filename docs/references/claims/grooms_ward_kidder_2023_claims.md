## Claims

### Claim 1
- **Statement**: "Poverty Point is a sprawling complex of geometric earthworks covering approximately 200ha in north-eastern Louisiana. Its builders moved an estimated 1 000 000m^3 of earth to create the monumental landscape (Kidder 2011). Mound A is among the largest mounds in North America, comprising approximately 238 500m^3 of earth, and was built in no more than 90 days (Ortmann & Kidder 2013)."
- **Page**: p. 1455
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Kidder 2011; Ortmann & Kidder 2013)
- **Assessment**: Key site parameters. Updates the total earthmoving estimate to 1,000,000 m^3 (from earlier 750,000 m^3). Mound A volume of 238,500 m^3 and construction in 90 days or less confirms rapid construction.

### Claim 2
- **Statement**: "Recent work demonstrates that at least one segment of the iconic concentric ridges, Ridge West 3, was also built rapidly (Kidder et al. 2021). Geophysical survey has identified 36 timber post circles up to 62m in diameter in a 25ha area around the anthropogenic plaza, further demonstrating the site's complex construction history (Hargrave et al. 2021)."
- **Page**: p. 1455
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Kidder et al. 2021; Hargrave et al. 2021)
- **Assessment**: Confirms rapid ridge construction and documents the 36 timber post circles. Multiple construction events and feature types indicate a complex, episodic construction history rather than gradual accretion.

### Claim 3
- **Statement**: "While 80 chronometric dates are available for the Poverty Point site, the chronology of the site is still debated and remains equivocal (Ortmann 2010). Most chronological models propose similar occupation spans (c. 3600-3000 cal BP) but differ in their proposed pace of earthwork construction."
- **Page**: p. 1457
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the current consensus date range for Poverty Point occupation at c. 3600-3000 cal BP. Notes that the 80 available dates still leave the chronology debated, particularly regarding the tempo of construction.

### Claim 4
- **Statement**: "Kidder et al. (2021) argue that most earthwork construction was rapid and took place late in the occupation (after c. 3400 cal BP) but others contend that construction progressed more gradually throughout the history of the site (DOI 2013: 30-56; Gibson 2019: 33; Hargrave et al. 2021)."
- **Page**: p. 1457
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (represents ongoing scholarly disagreement)
- **Assessment**: Documents the key debate about construction tempo. The "rapid and late" model (Kidder et al. 2021) versus "gradual throughout" model has direct implications for the signaling framework: rapid, late construction is more consistent with costly signaling episodes during aggregation events.

### Claim 5
- **Statement**: "In contrast to the Poverty Point site chronology, our findings show that Jaketown was occupied by people who demonstrated practices traditionally associated with the Poverty Point culture by c. 4500-4000 cal BP and who built mounds by c. 3400 cal BP. These dates run counter to the hypothesis that all Poverty Point culture sites are coeval; in fact, our data indicate that the monumental landscape at the Poverty Point site may be the culmination of deeper and more varied histories, rather than the catalyst for regional cultural change."
- **Page**: p. 1457
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on Bayesian chronological modeling of 32 radiocarbon dates)
- **Assessment**: Central thesis of the paper. Jaketown's occupation predates Poverty Point by 400+ years. This inverts the traditional diffusion model: Poverty Point is an endpoint of convergence, not a point of origin for cultural radiation. Directly relevant to the signaling model's treatment of Poverty Point as an aggregation site drawing in diverse groups.

### Claim 6
- **Statement**: Jaketown Phase 1 (initial occupation): Start 4570-3820 cal BP (2 sigma), probably 4440-4000 cal BP (1 sigma). "The AMS dates are associated with a large biconical PPO, numerous other PPO fragments, and some non-local lithic flakes; based on visual inspection, seven of the nine chipped stone pieces recovered from the pit are novaculite sourced to central Arkansas (Lehmann 1982: 14)."
- **Page**: p. 1459
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (AMS dates from secure stratigraphic context)
- **Assessment**: Documents PPO use and non-local lithic exchange (novaculite from central Arkansas, ~280 km) at Jaketown by c. 4500-4000 cal BP, approximately a millennium before these practices are documented at the Poverty Point type-site. This is the earliest dated context for PPOs and imported lithics in the LMV.

### Claim 7
- **Statement**: Jaketown Phase 2 (intensive occupation): Start 3585-3395 cal BP (2 sigma), probably 3505-3425 cal BP (1 sigma). "During this intensive phase, people at Jaketown were acquiring non-local lithics, including soapstone originating in southern Appalachia (Gibson 1994b) by c. 3500 cal BP."
- **Page**: pp. 1459, 1464-1465
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (AMS dates and stratigraphic evidence)
- **Assessment**: Soapstone acquisition from southern Appalachia (~900 km) by c. 3500 cal BP at Jaketown, during intensive occupation and before earthwork construction. Carbon residue from a soapstone sherd returned a date of 3565-3395 cal BP (B-555137). Long-distance exchange networks were established before monumental construction began.

### Claim 8
- **Statement**: Jaketown Phase 3 (earthwork construction): Start 3425-3365 cal BP (2 sigma), probably 3405-3375 cal BP (1 sigma). "Mounds A and X were built quickly c. 3400 cal BP. In both mounds, radiocarbon measurements from the initial mound deposits overlap with those from the mound surfaces. The abrupt stratigraphic boundaries between mound fill deposits and the absence of pedogenesis in the mound fill layers or at interfaces also indicate rapid construction."
- **Page**: p. 1464
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (stratigraphic and radiocarbon evidence)
- **Assessment**: Mound construction at Jaketown began by c. 3425-3365 cal BP, "a century or so before the construction of Mound A and the ridges at Poverty Point" (p. 1465). Construction was rapid, paralleling the pattern documented at Poverty Point. This demonstrates that rapid earthwork construction was not unique to the type-site.

### Claim 9
- **Statement**: "Stratum 2 contains two intact PPO concentrations, two hearths and the long bones of mammals likely to be deer, orientated in a way that suggests a single deposition. Based on this assemblage and macrobotanical remains recovered from this context, we interpret Stratum 2 as a feast or rapid sequence of feasts held sometime between September and November (Ward et al. 2022: 769)."
- **Page**: p. 1464
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (interpretation of depositional context and seasonality indicators)
- **Assessment**: Documents a feasting episode at Jaketown immediately preceding earthwork construction, with fall seasonality (September-November). The feast/construction sequence is directly relevant to the signaling model's prediction that monumental construction occurs during seasonal aggregation events.

### Claim 10
- **Statement**: "This represents a revival of earthen monumentality in the LMV after a millennium-long hiatus (Saunders et al. 2005; Gibson 2006; Saunders 2010, 2012). Significantly, our chronological model suggests that this revival is not necessarily linked to the creation of the monumental landscape at Poverty Point, and thus that the earthworks at Jaketown are not the result of diffusion from Poverty Point."
- **Page**: p. 1465
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on chronological modeling showing Jaketown mounds predate Poverty Point mounds)
- **Assessment**: Mound building at Jaketown predates Poverty Point mound construction, meaning the "revival" of monumentality after the Middle Archaic hiatus did not originate at the type-site. Multiple independent origins of mound construction are consistent with the signaling model's framing of convergence rather than diffusion.

### Claim 11
- **Statement**: Jaketown Phase 4 (post-flood occupation): Start 3245-2455 cal BP (2 sigma), probably 2860-2530 cal BP (1 sigma). "Between c. 3300 cal BP and 2780 cal BP, the LMV experienced more frequent and intense flood events linked to global climatic changes and attendant fluvial reorganisation of the Mississippi River."
- **Page**: p. 1464
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing Kidder et al. 2018)
- **Assessment**: A catastrophic crevasse splay flood between c. 3300 and 2780 cal BP buried the Late Archaic occupation at Jaketown. Post-flood cultural deposits represent people "using a distinct material culture from that of the population before the flood." This flooding event corresponds to the terminal/post-Poverty Point climate disruption documented by Kidder (2006).

### Claim 12
- **Statement**: "The Poverty Point site therefore appears to be an endpoint, a collective cultural outcome of the convergence of many groups with distinct histories and practices (Sassaman 2005; Kidder & Sassaman 2009; Kidder 2011, 2012; Spivey et al. 2015)."
- **Page**: p. 1454
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (supported by chronological evidence showing Jaketown predates the type-site)
- **Assessment**: Core interpretive claim. Poverty Point is recast from a center of innovation that radiated outward to an endpoint where multiple traditions converged. This convergence model aligns directly with the costly signaling framework's treatment of the site as an aggregation point for diverse groups.

### Claim 13
- **Statement**: "Chronostratigraphic data from Jaketown show that the site's inhabitants were engaging in practices typically associated with the so-called Poverty Point culture by c. 4500-4000 cal BP. These dates are conservatively 400 years earlier than the oldest dates derived from the Poverty Point site."
- **Page**: p. 1454
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (Bayesian chronological model)
- **Assessment**: Quantifies the chronological priority of Jaketown over Poverty Point at a minimum of 400 years. This is the key empirical finding that overturns the traditional culture-historical framework treating Poverty Point as the origin site.

### Claim 14
- **Statement**: "Long-distance lithic exchange was rare before the peak of the Poverty Point site (Gibson 1994a), suggesting that this context is an early manifestation of exchanges that later become socially significant in the region."
- **Page**: p. 1465
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (citing Gibson 1994a)
- **Assessment**: The early exchange at Jaketown (novaculite, soapstone) represents the beginning of what later intensified into the massive exchange networks centered on Poverty Point. For the signaling model, this suggests exchange networks preceded and enabled rather than resulted from the monumental construction.

### Claim 15
- **Statement**: "Despite these similarities, the monumental landscape at Poverty Point is unprecedented; the shape, configuration and enormous scale of the earthworks are novel to Native North America. These unique characteristics reflect the creation of a new group identity at Poverty Point, to which multiple groups contributed (Sassaman 2005)."
- **Page**: p. 1465
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: While mound construction was not invented at Poverty Point (Jaketown built mounds earlier), the scale and form of the earthworks at Poverty Point are unprecedented. The convergence of multiple groups created something qualitatively new. This is consistent with costly signaling: the unprecedented scale of investment serves as a signal precisely because it exceeds what any single group could produce.

### Claim 16
- **Statement**: "The enormity of earthwork construction at the site suggests some form of organisation to direct those efforts, yet there is no obvious evidence of hierarchy. Models of diffusion have historically been employed to assert that external forces are responsible for cultural innovations. When such a model is inverted, as we recommend for Poverty Point, how can we conceptualise innovation and resulting social change? Where does it come from if not a 'centre'?"
- **Page**: p. 1466
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Explicitly identifies the organizational puzzle that the costly signaling model addresses: how to explain coordinated monumental construction without hierarchy and without diffusion from a center. The convergence model creates the theoretical space for signaling-based cooperation as the coordination mechanism.

### Claim 17
- **Statement**: "The trend over the past few decades has been to apply the chronology of the type-site, implicitly or explicitly, to other Poverty Point-affiliated sites. Some have gone as far as to advise that archaeologists should not bother dating such sites because of their assumed contemporaneity with the type-site (Gibson & Melancon 2004: 228)."
- **Page**: p. 1457
- **Type**: Empirical (documenting disciplinary practice)
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Illustrates how the diffusion model created a self-reinforcing assumption of contemporaneity that discouraged independent dating of regional sites. The convergence model requires independent chronological control at each site.

### Claim 18
- **Statement**: "Poor chronological control, coupled with ambiguous criteria used to designate a site as a Poverty Point affiliate, results in an overreliance on the chronology of, and material traits derived from, the type-site to explain Native American history in the American Southeast."
- **Page**: p. 1456
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Methodological critique: the "Poverty Point culture" designation assumes diffusion and contemporaneity, which the Jaketown chronology falsifies. The authors avoid the term "Poverty Point culture" in favor of "Late Archaic" for Jaketown's occupation between c. 4500 and 3200 cal BP.

### Claim 19
- **Statement**: "The history of Poverty Point is the opposite of what we should expect if it were a site that marked the beginning of a phenomenon (Kidder 2012: 467). The earthworks were built exceptionally quickly at the end of the site's occupation."
- **Page**: p. 1456
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on radiocarbon and geoarchaeological evidence)
- **Assessment**: Key observation for the signaling model. If Poverty Point were the origin of the "culture," one would expect earthworks at the beginning. Instead, rapid monumental construction occurs at the end, consistent with the site being a culmination point of convergence rather than an origin.

### Claim 20
- **Statement**: "In total, 32 radiocarbon samples have been collected from the Jaketown site (Table S1 in OSM). We use OxCal 4.4 software (Bronk Ramsey 2009a) to create a chronological model, calibrating measurements using the IntCal20 calibration curve (Reimer et al. 2020). We omit six radiocarbon measurements, including one AMS date, and all five of Ford and Webb's (1956) radiometric dates."
- **Page**: p. 1459
- **Type**: Methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the Bayesian modeling methodology: 32 total samples, 26 used after outlier removal, OxCal 4.4 with IntCal20. The exclusion of Ford and Webb's 1950s dates is justified by established outlier detection protocols.

### Claim 21
- **Statement**: "Large postholes documented in Trench 1 and Mound X may represent ceremonial infrastructure like the timber circles documented in the plaza at Poverty Point, although the temporal relationship among these features remains unclear."
- **Page**: p. 1465
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (morphological similarity only; postholes at Jaketown are ~0.3 m diameter, timber circles at PP are much larger)
- **Assessment**: The presence of postholes at Jaketown that resemble Poverty Point timber circles suggests shared architectural practices across sites, though the comparison is tentative. If confirmed, this would indicate convergent architectural traditions, not diffusion.

### Claim 22
- **Statement**: The four-phase Jaketown sequence spans: Phase 1 (initial occupation, 4570-3820 cal BP at 2 sigma), Phase 2 (intensive occupation, 3585-3395 cal BP at 2 sigma), Phase 3 (earthwork construction, 3425-3365 cal BP at 2 sigma), Phase 4 (post-flood occupation, 3245-2455 cal BP at 2 sigma).
- **Page**: pp. 1459-1464
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (Bayesian chronological model with OxCal 4.4)
- **Assessment**: The complete four-phase chronology shows a long pre-construction occupation at Jaketown (>1000 years) before mound building began. The gap between Phase 1 and Phase 2 may indicate episodic rather than continuous occupation. Phase 3 earthwork construction overlaps with but slightly predates Poverty Point construction.

## Summary Assessment

Grooms, Ward, and Kidder (2023) present a revised Bayesian chronology for Jaketown that overturns the traditional model treating Poverty Point as the origin of a radially diffusing culture. The Jaketown chronology shows occupation with PPOs and imported lithics by c. 4500-4000 cal BP, intensive occupation with Appalachian soapstone by c. 3500 cal BP, and mound construction by c. 3400 cal BP, all predating equivalent evidence at the Poverty Point type-site by 400 years or more. The paper reframes Poverty Point as a convergence point where multiple groups with independent histories came together, rather than a center from which cultural innovations radiated outward. For the costly signaling model, this convergence framework is highly supportive: it aligns with the treatment of Poverty Point as an aggregation site where diverse groups cooperated through costly signaling mechanisms. The finding that exchange networks and material culture practices preceded monumental construction by centuries suggests that signaling through exchange predated and enabled the monumental signaling that followed. The absence of hierarchy despite massive coordinated construction remains the central puzzle that the signaling model addresses.
