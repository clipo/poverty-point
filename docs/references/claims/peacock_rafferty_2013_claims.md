## Claims

### Claim 1
- **Statement**: "Recent work in eastern North America has demonstrated that mound building first occurred in the mid-Holocene, as much as 6000 years ago, when human groups practiced a hunting-gathering lifestyle. Early (Archaic-period) mounds are concentrated in areas where environmental conditions show high-amplitude fluctuations over time. Under such conditions, evolutionary theory predicts that population-leveling activities (bet-hedging) would be selected for."
- **Page**: p. 253 (abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Core thesis of the chapter. Directly relevant to the Poverty Point project: mound building among hunter-gatherers is predicted by bet-hedging theory under conditions of environmental variability. This parallels the costly signaling framework but frames the mechanism in terms of population-leveling waste rather than honest signaling.

### Claim 2
- **Statement**: "Contrary to recent assertions, existing data on paleoclimate and the timing of mound construction in the Lower Mississippi Valley (LMV) do not falsify Hamilton's hypothesis. Bet-hedging also explains why early mounds are more common in the southern LMV, where environmental conditions are particularly variable and where sedentary settlement patterns had evolved by the mid-Holocene."
- **Page**: p. 253 (abstract)
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Responds to Sampson's (2008) critique that mound dates fall in climatically calm intervals. The geographic prediction (more mounds in the more variable southern LMV) is testable and relevant to understanding Poverty Point's location.

### Claim 3
- **Statement**: Mound building "occurred in some parts of what is now the southeastern United States as much as 6000 years ago (Kidder and Sassaman 2009). Not only did this discovery effectively double the known antiquity of monumental earthen architecture in North America north of Mexico, but multiple occurrences show that the practice of mound construction was common to many Middle (6000-3500 BC) and Late (3500-500 BC) Archaic-period societies in Florida and the Lower Mississippi River Valley."
- **Page**: p. 253
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes the temporal and geographic scope. Mound building was practiced by hunter-gatherers for thousands of years before Poverty Point, which falls in the Late Archaic range given here. Poverty Point is not an isolated anomaly but part of a broader LMV tradition.

### Claim 4
- **Statement**: "The uses to which Archaic mounds were put is uncertain and likely was variable. Very few seem to have been burial mounds. Artifacts from some mound layers are similar to those found at associated off-mound habitation areas (Peacock et al. 2010) and at non-mound habitation sites (Kidder and Sassaman 2009). Agriculture was not practiced during the Middle or Late Archaic periods in the area in question, where dependence on maize-based agriculture did not evolve until well into the 2nd millennium AD."
- **Page**: p. 254
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Confirms that Archaic mound builders were not agriculturalists, reinforcing the hunter-gatherer context for Poverty Point. The absence of clear utilitarian function for the mounds supports interpreting them as "wasteful" in the evolutionary sense.

### Claim 5
- **Statement**: "The famous c. 3500 year old Poverty Point site in northeastern Louisiana, which has mounds and earthworks but little pottery, had already strained the progressive paradigm but was accommodated by treating it as intermediate in complexity between the Archaic and the later pottery-bearing and mound-building Woodland period (Willey 1966, 291), even if it was somewhat 'evolutionarily' precocious (e.g., 'America's First Chiefdom' - Gibson 1974)."
- **Page**: p. 254
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents how Poverty Point was awkwardly forced into progressive cultural evolution frameworks. The bet-hedging/waste model offers an alternative that does not require attributing "chiefdom" status to hunter-gatherers.

### Claim 6
- **Statement**: Neiman (1997, 269) critiqued the "signs of power" argument "on the grounds that it 'does not explain why the energy expenditure that denotes power is wasteful', as mound and monument building appears to be." Arguments about monumental architecture representing complexity "tend to be circular (Saunders, J. 2004, 147), as when mounds are assumed to reflect social inequality and social inequality is then invoked to 'explain' mounds."
- **Page**: p. 255
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Identifies the key explanatory gap that both bet-hedging and costly signaling models address: why is the signal wasteful? Traditional complexity-based explanations are circular. The evolutionary framework provides a non-circular mechanism.

### Claim 7
- **Statement**: "Costly signaling involves expending energy on an activity that is sub-optimal in terms of resource return but which can be understood as enhancing fitness in ways that are beneficial to both the sender and receiver of the signal." Costly signaling "usually is held to evolve in stable or favorable environments (Aranyosi 1999)."
- **Page**: p. 256
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Distinguishes costly signaling from bet-hedging on the basis of environmental conditions: costly signaling evolves in stable environments, bet-hedging in variable ones. This is a key theoretical claim, though the distinction may be overstated; the Poverty Point project argues that costly signaling can operate under environmental uncertainty when signals facilitate cooperation that buffers against that uncertainty.

### Claim 8
- **Statement**: "Bet-hedging involves actions that also divert (and thus 'waste') energy from resource-maximizing activities. Because they dampen population growth, such actions are advantageous over the long term in areas where environmental conditions fluctuate considerably in amplitude from mean carrying capacity (Dunnell 1989; 1999; Simons 2011)."
- **Page**: p. 256
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Core definition of bet-hedging as applied to mound building. The mechanism is population dampening: groups that "waste" energy grow more slowly during good times but survive environmental downturns that destroy non-wasteful populations. This is the geometric mean fitness argument from evolutionary biology.

### Claim 9
- **Statement**: "It has been shown by simulation studies that, in such situations, the geometric mean of population growth will be larger in wasteful than in non-wasteful populations, thus demonstrating that wasteful activities can be fixed in a population via selection (Madsen et al. 1999). Non-wasteful populations tend to grow rapidly and then crash during environmental downturns when carrying capacity temporarily decreases, while populations practicing waste have a less variable trajectory."
- **Page**: p. 256
- **Type**: Empirical/Modeling
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: References the Madsen et al. (1999) simulation demonstrating the formal evolutionary logic. This is the same geometric mean fitness principle that underlies bet-hedging in biological evolution. Directly relevant to the Poverty Point ABM: the model should show that signaling populations have higher geometric mean fitness than non-signaling populations under environmental variability.

### Claim 10
- **Statement**: "While waste, such as mound-building, will be selected for in unstable environments, it is expected to cease during periods of greatest instability, as people turn their energy to increased resource acquisition or intensification rather than to wasteful activities." Dunnell pointed out that "the role of waste in coping with environmental perturbation is twofold: 1) the use of energy itself necessarily lowers the birthrate...; and 2) it provides, through its temporary abatement, a reservoir of time that an organism can devote to subsistence and/or reproduction in difficult conditions" (Dunnell 1989, 45).
- **Page**: p. 258
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Critical nuance: waste/mound building is expected to occur during relatively stable periods within a generally variable environment, and to cease during the worst downturns. This addresses Sampson's critique that mound dates fall in calm intervals. Highly relevant to modeling Poverty Point: signaling behavior should be predicted to increase during stable periods and decrease during acute crises.

### Claim 11
- **Statement**: "For bet-hedging to be selected, there must be enough environmental variability to make it advantageous; this does not have to be the most extreme variability that the environment ever has generated or ever can generate." Evolutionary theory "clearly posits that mound building, once established, can be a successful bet-hedging strategy only if it occurs during relatively stable periods. These are the times when non-bet-hedging populations grow and consequently are subject to extinction or dramatic decrease when a drastic enough episode of instability occurs."
- **Page**: p. 258
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Refines the temporal prediction: bet-hedging populations build mounds during the good times (when non-hedging populations overshoot carrying capacity), providing them a survival buffer when bad times come. This resolves the apparent paradox of mound construction during stable periods.

### Claim 12
- **Statement**: "Mound building and other possibly wasteful traits, while passed to others via cultural transmission, do not appear continuously in an area solely because of cultural transmission (i.e., they are not stylistic, sensu Dunnell 1978); rather, they are seen as functional traits (Dunnell 1978), ones that are selected for under appropriate conditions. The current understanding of mound building through time in the LMV well reflects this expected distribution. A paper recently authored by Joe Saunders (2010b) shows three distinct periods of mound building, in the Middle Archaic, Late Archaic, and later prehistoric periods (Fig. 5), an episodic pattern consonant with a functional trait."
- **Page**: p. 259
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The episodic distribution of mound building in the LMV (appearing and disappearing across millennia) is consistent with a functional trait selected under appropriate environmental conditions, rather than a continuously transmitted cultural tradition. This periodicity is a testable prediction of the bet-hedging model.

### Claim 13
- **Statement**: The earliest mounds in the LMV "began far to the south" and "mound building seems subsequently to have spread north." The south-to-north pattern is documented in Table 1 (radiocarbon dates from secure Archaic-period mound contexts), with Monte Sano (6570+/-60 RCYBP) as the earliest and Helena Crossing (2100+/-75 RCYBP) as the latest/northernmost. Poverty Point has an earliest mound date of 3386+/-49 RCYBP (Ortmann 2010).
- **Page**: pp. 263-264, Table 1
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents a south-to-north spread of mound building in the LMV, which Peacock and Rafferty interpret as reflecting time- and space-transgressive environmental changes (sea-level rise, meandering regime development). Poverty Point's date places it in the middle of this sequence. This geographic pattern is a prediction of the bet-hedging model: mounds should appear first where environmental variability is greatest.

### Claim 14
- **Statement**: "The obvious concentration of Archaic mounds in the southern part of the LMV (Fig. 1) also meets predictions of the bet-hedging model, as a number of physical factors (fluvial geomorphology, proximity to Gulf storms, etc.) combine to make this area the least stable part of the drainage and arguably the most unstable landscape in prehistoric eastern North America."
- **Page**: p. 265
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Geographic prediction of the bet-hedging model: mounds concentrate where environmental instability is greatest. Poverty Point lies in this southern LMV zone. This supports the project's argument that Poverty Point's location was not coincidental but reflects environmental conditions favoring costly behavior.

### Claim 15
- **Statement**: Sedentariness is "a density-dependent phenomenon, which will be selected for as a region becomes packed with people who use a given technology and resource exploitation pattern. When this occurs, it is no longer possible for groups to move seasonally, because all areas already are inhabited. In this situation, sedentary settlement can evolve only if diets are broadened or resource use is intensified."
- **Page**: p. 266
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Sedentariness is treated as a co-variant of bet-hedging, not a prerequisite. When populations are sedentary and cannot relocate during environmental downturns, bet-hedging becomes more strongly selected. This is relevant to the Poverty Point aggregation model: if seasonal aggregation created periods of effective sedentariness at the site, it would strengthen selection for wasteful behavior.

### Claim 16
- **Statement**: "Madsen et al. (1999) have shown that bet-hedging is less likely to evolve if populations have an opportunity to move to another area during periods of environmental stress. For situations in which settlement is sedentary, this option usually is removed."
- **Page**: p. 266
- **Type**: Interpretive/Modeling
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Key theoretical point linking sedentariness to bet-hedging. Mobile populations can respond to environmental stress by relocating; sedentary populations cannot and thus benefit more from bet-hedging. For Poverty Point, the question is whether aggregation behavior or population packing limited mobility enough to favor bet-hedging.

### Claim 17
- **Statement**: Poverty Point "saw construction of six large concentric ridges at the type site, Poverty Point, in northeast Louisiana. The site also has six earthen mounds, with the largest, Mound A (Figures 7 and 8), containing 238,500 m3 of earth (Kidder et al. 2009)." Gibson (2006) "argues that the rapid and large-scale earthmoving evident at Poverty Point (Kidder et al. 2009; Ortmann 2010) is evidence that the mounds and earthworks could not have been built without effort being expended over multiple seasons, therefore indicating that people lived year-round at the site."
- **Page**: p. 266
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides the Mound A volume figure (238,500 m3) from Kidder et al. 2009. Gibson's sedentariness argument based on the scale of construction is noted, though Peacock and Rafferty treat this as consistent with, not necessary for, the bet-hedging model.

### Claim 18
- **Statement**: "The amount of waste at Poverty Point is so much larger than at earlier or contemporary sites as to suggest a change in the scale of selection for bet-hedging, from the individual/household to the community, representing an instance of group selection (Mayr 2000, 131-132)."
- **Page**: p. 270
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Proposes that Poverty Point represents a shift from household-level to community-level bet-hedging/group selection. This is a significant theoretical claim: the sheer scale of Poverty Point construction (750,000 m3 of earth moved) exceeds what individual households could accomplish and implies collective action. This aligns with the project's costly signaling argument, where group-level signaling coordinates cooperation.

### Claim 19
- **Statement**: "At Poverty Point, as pointed out by Gibson (2006), there is a many-fold increase in the amount of dirt moved (750000 m3), some of it quite rapidly (Ortmann 2010). As noted above, Poverty Point also shows large quantities of energy invested in obtaining exotic stone (Gibson 2007)." The plaza area "has been investigated by geophysics and small-scale excavation, revealing that this putative open area actually contained many massive circular post structures, far too large for domiciliary architecture, that show evidence of multiple rebuilding episodes (Greenlee et al. 2010)."
- **Page**: p. 270
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the scale of waste at Poverty Point: 750,000 m3 of earth, exotic stone procurement, and non-domestic post structures with multiple rebuilding episodes in the plaza. All of these represent costly activities that are difficult to explain under standard utilitarian frameworks but predicted by bet-hedging/costly signaling models.

### Claim 20
- **Statement**: "We do not wish to imply that wasteful behavior is a universal explanation for the spread of mound construction or other 'monumental' architecture. We do, however, suggest that evolutionary theory, which provides testable hypotheses, is appropriate for scientific analysis of the phenomena in question."
- **Page**: p. 270
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The authors are cautious about universalizing the bet-hedging explanation while maintaining that evolutionary theory provides the proper scientific framework. They acknowledge other evolutionary processes may also be at work.

### Claim 21
- **Statement**: "There are sites in the LMV, such as Poverty Point in Louisiana and the Slate site in Mississippi (Lauro and Lehmann 1982), that contain vast quantities of raw materials imported from hundreds of kilometers away. The area was home to a remarkable Middle Archaic-period zoomorphic bead industry (e.g., Connaway 1982; see also Johnson 2000; Saunders, J. et al. 2005), requiring an as-of-yet poorly understood technology for drilling, shaping, and polishing hard chert pebbles (Carr 2008, 217-218). Poverty Point was the location of a later, equally elaborate, lapidary industry (Webb 1982; see also Johnson 1993). That such apparently wasteful activities also were concentrated in the lower LMV is more than coincidence."
- **Page**: p. 270
- **Type**: Empirical/Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Connects exotic goods acquisition and lapidary industries at Poverty Point to the bet-hedging model. The geographic concentration of these "wasteful" activities in the environmentally variable lower LMV is treated as a prediction of the model, not coincidence.

### Claim 22
- **Statement**: "Evolutionary theory does not invoke human intentionality except as a source of variability upon which selection can act (Dunnell 1988). Archaeologists working in a behavioral reconstruction mode consistently fail to understand the difference in analytical scale provided by evolutionary analysis." Saunders et al. (2005, 664) stated that bet-hedging "does not explain mound-building [at the Middle Archaic-period Watson Brake site], unless folk memory of past calamities or fear of future ones was invoked to get it started. In our view, this is an untestable proposition." Peacock and Rafferty respond: "We agree that their statement is an untestable proposition, just as all appeals to prehistoric agency ultimately are untestable."
- **Page**: p. 271
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Addresses a common misunderstanding of evolutionary explanations: bet-hedging does not require that people consciously anticipated environmental downturns. Selection operates on behavioral variation regardless of the actors' intentions. This is directly relevant to the costly signaling framework, which also does not require conscious strategy.

### Claim 23
- **Statement**: "Acknowledging that we cannot know proximate reason frees us to seek ultimate cause, and allows archaeology to achieve insights about the human condition that are unavailable to other disciplines."
- **Page**: p. 271
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Methodological statement distinguishing proximate (intentional) from ultimate (selective) explanation. The Poverty Point project similarly focuses on ultimate causation: why costly signaling was adaptive, not why individual actors chose to participate.

### Claim 24
- **Statement**: "Mound building could appear at any time at a low level, become more visible when selected for, and diminish or cease in the most extreme circumstances, when energy is turned toward subsistence instead. It may reappear in the same region, or it may not."
- **Page**: p. 259
- **Type**: Interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Describes the expected temporal pattern of mound building under the bet-hedging model: episodic appearance and disappearance driven by environmental conditions, not continuous cultural evolution. The three phases of LMV mound building (Middle Archaic, Late Archaic, later prehistoric) match this pattern.

### Claim 25
- **Statement**: During the period c. 4800-3800 cal BP, there is "considerable evidence for massive flooding episodes and sediment aggradation in the LMV," with "regional climate was wetter and colder, with increased Gulf storm frequency and amplitude." This was followed by "amelioration of climate-driven instability between 3800 and 3000 cal BP" which "saw extensive settlement in the LMV," as "people associated with the Late Archaic Poverty Point culture rapidly colonized topographically elevated, well-drained natural levees" (Kidder et al. 2008, 1264). "A second spate of mound building corresponds with this period (Saunders, J. 2010b)."
- **Page**: p. 262
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Establishes the paleoclimate context for Poverty Point: the site was occupied during a period of relative stability (3800-3000 cal BP) that followed a period of significant environmental instability. This is consistent with the bet-hedging prediction that mound building occurs during stable periods when non-hedging populations would overshoot carrying capacity.

### Claim 26
- **Statement**: "Another period of instability marked by large-scale, climate-driven flooding and a 'hyperactive' period of hurricane activity occurred between about 3000-2500 cal BP, during which Late Archaic mound building (Saunders, J., 2010b) and trade networks were disrupted, coastal shell rings ceased to accumulate, and large areas of the LMV possibly were depopulated (Adelsberger and Kidder 2007; Kidder 2006; 2010)."
- **Page**: p. 262
- **Type**: Empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents the environmental disruption that coincides with the end of the Poverty Point phenomenon (c. 3000-2500 cal BP / 1050-550 BCE). The bet-hedging model predicts that mound building ceases during extreme instability, when energy is redirected to subsistence. This provides a mechanism for Poverty Point's abandonment that is independent of social collapse narratives.

## Summary Assessment

Peacock and Rafferty 2013 provides a sustained argument for applying bet-hedging theory (following Dunnell 1989 and Hamilton 1999) to explain Archaic-period mound building in the Lower Mississippi Valley. The chapter is directly relevant to the Poverty Point costly signaling project in several ways. First, it establishes that mound building among hunter-gatherers is predicted by evolutionary theory under conditions of environmental variability, without requiring complex social hierarchy or agricultural surplus. Second, it refines the temporal prediction of the waste model: mound building occurs during relatively stable periods within a generally variable environment and ceases during extreme downturns, resolving the apparent paradox raised by Sampson (2008). Third, it identifies a south-to-north geographic spread of mound building in the LMV that mirrors the time-transgressive development of environmental instability. Fourth, it explicitly discusses Poverty Point as representing a possible shift from household-level to community-level bet-hedging, given the unprecedented scale of construction (750,000 m3) and exotic goods acquisition. Fifth, it distinguishes bet-hedging from costly signaling on environmental grounds (variable vs. stable environments), a distinction that the current project may need to address or reconcile, since the Poverty Point model integrates signaling with environmental uncertainty. The chapter also provides a useful Table 1 of radiocarbon dates from secure Archaic-period mound contexts in the LMV, with Poverty Point's earliest date at 3386+/-49 RCYBP (Ortmann 2010).
