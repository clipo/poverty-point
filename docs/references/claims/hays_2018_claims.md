## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: "The Poverty Point site (16WC5), located in northeast Louisiana, is unlike any other archaeological site in the world." Archaeologists use terms like "singularity" and "sui generis" to characterize its unusualness in North American prehistory.
- **Page**: p. 1 (article p. 1)
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Supports the project framing that Poverty Point requires a special explanatory framework (costly signaling) rather than standard hunter-gatherer models.

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: "Hunting and gathering groups are traditionally not thought to be capable of creating monumental construction or engage in extensive exchange systems because they, in general, are not typically sedentary nor do they typically have the large food surpluses that agriculture provides."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Frames the Poverty Point paradox central to the project: why would hunter-gatherers build monuments and participate in long-distance exchange?

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: There are three major anomalies associated with Poverty Point: "the massive earthworks, the voluminous and diverse artifacts, and the Poverty Point Objects (PPOs)."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Identifies the three core anomalies the signaling model must explain.

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: Mound A "is over 23 m in height and over 6 ha in extent, appears to have been built in about three months" (citing Ortmann and Kidder 2013).
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (referencing excavation data)
- **Assessment**: Critical data point for the signaling model. Rapid construction of Mound A implies coordinated labor from large aggregated populations, consistent with signaling during seasonal aggregation events.

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: "The area of Poverty Point is rich in plant and animal resources but it is essentially a stoneless land with the closest gravels suitable for use over 30 km away. Therefore all the tens of thousands of stone artifacts (e.g., thousands of kg of chert, hundreds of plummets) found at Poverty Point were transported to the site from distances ranging from 30 to over 500 km" (citing Gibson 2001).
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to exotic materials as costly signals. The transport distances quantify the cost of signal production.

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: "The volume and diversity of stone artifacts at Poverty Point exceeds most archaeological sites in North America, including ones occupied by people over 2,000 years later who produced agricultural surpluses."
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Underscores the anomalous scale of material accumulation at a pre-agricultural site, strengthening the case that special mechanisms (signaling) are needed to explain it.

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: Using Webb's 1982 counts, "Poverty Point had 2,790 whole or partial plummets, most made of magnetite or hematite imported from sources in Arkansas that were hundreds of kilometers away. Other areas with roughly contemporaneous sites have only a few stone plummets per site" (citing Goldstein 2004).
- **Page**: p. 1
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Quantifies the disparity between Poverty Point and contemporaneous sites in exotic artifact quantities. Relevant to modeling signal intensity.

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: After Poverty Point, "hematite and magnetite plummets essentially disappear from the Southeast. Therefore, their abundance and utility in the Southeast is bound up in the Poverty Point culture."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Suggests that exotic artifact circulation was tied specifically to the Poverty Point signaling/aggregation system and collapsed with it.

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: Table 1 provides comparative artifact counts between Poverty Point, Indian Knoll, and Riverton culture sites. Poverty Point numbers include: plummets 2,790; projectile points 11,714; stone beads 1,214; microliths ca. 30,000; copper objects 155; stone gorgets 546; pendants 296; celts/adzes/axes 445; hammerstones 348; banner/boat stones 67; galena objects 702; clay figurines 133; pipes 50+; steatite objects 2,221. Indian Knoll and Riverton have far fewer in all categories.
- **Page**: p. 2 (Table 1)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Key comparative data for the project. The order-of-magnitude differences between Poverty Point and contemporaneous sites support the argument that Poverty Point served a unique aggregation/signaling function.

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: Gibson (1973:129) estimated that Poverty Point holds around 15 million PPOs. They are also quite numerous at other Poverty Point culture sites, particularly Claiborne (22HA501) and Jaketown (22HU505), "which each contain about 12,000 recorded PPOs. Even small sites have several hundred of them."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (the 15 million figure is an estimate)
- **Assessment**: The sheer volume of PPOs at the type site implies intensive, large-scale cooking activities consistent with feasting during aggregation events.

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: PPOs are "hand-molded baked-clay objects that were formed into a series of standardized shapes (e.g., melons, cylinders, and biconicals)." They are the "most diagnostic artifact of the Poverty Point culture."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Establishes PPOs as the signature artifact of the culture. Their standardized forms imply shared cultural conventions across the exchange network.

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: Webb (1968) "delineated the extent of the culture, he used sites containing PPOs as his boundary markers."
- **Page**: p. 2
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: PPOs serve as the defining marker of the Poverty Point interaction sphere, relevant to understanding the spatial extent of the signaling network.

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: "No other culture manufactured them in such enormous amounts and no other culture made them with the care, variety, and standardization of form as the people of the Poverty Point culture."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The uniqueness of PPO production supports the argument that Poverty Point culture had distinctive communal practices tied to aggregation.

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: Previous explanatory models for Poverty Point have included: "a ruling class from the Ohio Valley (Ford and Webb 1956), the center of a great chiefdom (Gibson 1973), the site of a great trading fair (Jackson 1991a), and it has even been characterized as the 'forgotten city' (Ellerbe and Greenlee 2015)."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: methodological
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence (summary of prior work)
- **Assessment**: Documents the range of competing hypotheses. The signaling model offers an alternative to all of these.

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: "The archaeological data do not support the model that a sizable, single ethnic community continuously resided at Poverty Point. The great volume of the artifacts and their diversity and the unusual and relatively rapidly built large earthworks at Poverty Point only make sense if there were large numbers of people from different ethnic groups coming together at the site."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly supports the aggregation model central to the project. Multiple groups coming together seasonally is a prerequisite for the signaling framework.

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: Kidder (2011:112) highlights that "Poverty Point has a remarkable variety of formal tool types, whereas its contemporary sites have typically one or two formal tool types."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (citing specialist literature)
- **Assessment**: Artifact diversity at the aggregation site versus satellite sites supports the interpretation that Poverty Point drew diverse populations.

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: Jackson (1991b) "has argued that the available data on foods consumed by local populations in the Poverty Point area indicate that it is more likely that the site was occupied periodically rather than continuously."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly supports the seasonal aggregation model in the project.

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: Jackson's analysis of flora and fauna at nearby Copes (16MA47), where preservation is good, "indicated no regional stress on subsistence resources. Indeed the Copes residents were highly selective in their subsistence pursuits, which would not occur if there was a large resident population."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Suggests that the region's carrying capacity was not strained, supporting the idea that aggregation was voluntary and motivated by social (signaling) rather than subsistence reasons.

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: Spivey and colleagues (2015) proposed that "Poverty Point was a pilgrimage site." They argue the site "can best be accounted for as the center of a multiethnic gathering." Sassaman (2005:358) "describes a similar conception of Poverty Point as 'the ultimate ethnogenetic event of ancient Native America as various populations converged in northeast Louisiana from points north and south.'"
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The pilgrimage/multiethnic gathering model is compatible with the costly signaling framework. Pilgrimage creates the audience for signals.

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: Hays argues "that a primary focus of periodic journeys to Poverty Point was a series of large-scale ritualized feasts" that "were centered on world renewal and creation stories."
- **Page**: p. 4
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Central thesis of the paper. Feasting as the primary activity during aggregation events provides the social context in which costly signaling (monument construction, exotic display) would operate.

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: "Feasts are sensual experiences that extend beyond the practical necessity and ordinariness of daily meals" and can give participants "a sense of communitas, which is marked by a release from day-to-day structural role-playing and a feeling of cohesion within a group."
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (citing anthropological literature)
- **Assessment**: The concept of communitas at feasts parallels the cooperation-building function of signaling in the project model.

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: "Putting on the 'best' feast and even a 'legendary' one will give the hosts social prestige and probably attract more participants and followers in the future."
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly connects feasting to competitive signaling dynamics. Feast quality functions as a signal of group capacity.

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: Hayden (2014) divides sociopolitical organizations into five categories, each with characteristic types of feasts. For Poverty Point, the relevant category is "transegalitarian hunter-gatherers" because this is "in all likelihood, the sociopolitical level of the Poverty Point culture (e.g., Gibson 2001)."
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Classifies Poverty Point's social organization, relevant to calibrating the signaling model. Transegalitarian societies are the appropriate comparison.

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: "While simple or generalized hunter-gatherers share food, transegalitarian hunter-gatherers commonly hold feasts supported by food surpluses (Hayden 2014; Woodburn 1982). In effect, feasting replaces the sharing ethic of generalized hunter-gatherers."
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Key distinction for the model. The transition from obligatory sharing to competitive feasting creates the conditions for costly signaling.

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: "Feasting establishes ties between groups near and far, and this creates alliances that act as social safety networks (Hayden 2009:600)."
- **Page**: p. 5
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the risk-pooling and cooperation benefits in the signaling model. Feasting as alliance-building aligns with signaling as a mechanism for facilitating inter-group cooperation.

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: In his analysis of festivals in Mesoamerica, Wells (2007:33-35) "notes that work party feasts serve and support multiple purposes including subsistence activities, craft production, architectural constructions, and the integration of dispersed groups."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate (ethnographic analogy)
- **Assessment**: Provides a mechanism linking feasting to monument construction. Work-party feasts could explain how aggregation events produced the earthworks.

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: "For the Poverty Point culture, the construction of the earthworks would have been labor intensive and feasts could have supported and even attracted people to participate in their creation."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly connects feasting to earthwork construction, supporting the project's argument that monument building was a cooperative activity facilitated by aggregation.

### Claim 28

- **Statement**: Feasts in transegalitarian societies have three aspects particularly relevant to Poverty Point: (1) "large and successful feasts help cement and maintain alliances between visiting groups, and they also advertise the desirability of being affiliated with the hosts"; (2) "feasts are often used to attract and support communal labor for projects"; (3) "crafts are often produced by and for the community in preparation for feasts (Spielmann's ritual mode of production)."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: All three aspects map onto the signaling model: alliance formation, labor mobilization for monuments, and craft production of ritual items (including PPOs and exotics).

### Claim 29

- **Statement**: Ortmann and Kidder (2013) "estimate that it [Mound A] was built in about three months with a workforce ranging between 1,000 and 3,000 laborers plus their families."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (based on excavation data)
- **Assessment**: Critical quantitative data for the model. The labor force estimate constrains the aggregation population size parameter. 1,000-3,000 laborers implies a total aggregation population of several thousand.

### Claim 30

- **Statement**: Five of Hayden's feasting signatures pertaining to craft production are present at Poverty Point: "(1) unusual preparation and (2) serving vessels (the steatite vessels and the spiculate pottery [Hays and Weinstein 2004]); (3) prestige items; (4) ritualized paraphernalia for public rituals (the figurines, beads, and gorgets); and (5) ritualized items for smoking or drinking (the tubular pipes)."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Documents multiple archaeological signatures of feasting at Poverty Point, strengthening the case for ritualized aggregation events.

### Claim 31

- **Statement**: "Direct evidence of feasting in the form of food remains is scarce at Poverty Point, even though the area has a great number of economically useful plant species and abundant fish (Jackson 1991b:141-148)."
- **Page**: p. 6
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Important caveat. The paucity of food remains is attributed to "acidic loessal soils that ensure poor preservation of organic remains," not to absence of feasting.

### Claim 32

- **Statement**: "There is, however, abundant direct evidence for large-scale cooking activities: the PPOs. There is considerable archaeological evidence that many PPOs were used in cooking activities, but they probably served other functions as well (Hays et al. 2016:214)."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Links the enormous PPO assemblage to large-scale cooking, consistent with feasting during aggregation.

### Claim 33

- **Statement**: According to Webb (1982:37), "the people of the Poverty Point culture primarily made six major types, which constitute about 87% of the total classified: biconical plain and grooved, cylindrical grooved, cross grooved, and melon shaped, with or without end grooves."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the standardization of PPO forms, relevant to understanding shared cultural conventions across the network.

### Claim 34

- **Statement**: "The standard forms are found at a considerable number of sites within the LMV, with distances of over 480 km between the sites (Hays et al. 2016:217). Moreover, most of the same basic types are found at the major well-excavated sites."
- **Page**: p. 7
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: PPO standardization over 480+ km indicates a shared cultural system spanning the full extent of the Poverty Point interaction sphere.

### Claim 35

- **Statement**: Table 3 provides detailed distribution of PPO types across sites. At Poverty Point (16WC5): biconical 1,248; biconical grooved 1,602; cylindrical grooved 4,579; cross grooved 3,359; melon 1,682; melon w/end grooves 3,116; spheroid 224; biscuit 138; amorphous 866. At Claiborne (22HA501): biconical 1,153; biconical grooved 2,069; cylindrical grooved 1,171; cross grooved 2,006; melon 2,106; melon w/end grooves 1,356; spheroid 377; biscuit 22; amorphous 476.
- **Page**: p. 10 (Table 3)
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Detailed quantitative data on PPO type distributions. The variability in proportions across sites may encode social information about different groups.

### Claim 36

- **Statement**: "The vast majority of PPOs at Poverty Point and Jaketown are buff to reddish in color, suggesting that they were fired repeatedly in oxidizing conditions. Gibson (2001) observed, moreover, that many PPOs are oxidized in the interior."
- **Page**: p. 8
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Physical evidence that PPOs were repeatedly used in cooking, supporting the large-scale food preparation (feasting) interpretation.

### Claim 37

- **Statement**: Pierce (1998:178) concluded that "the wide variety of shapes appear to have no impact on performance." McGee's (1995:114) experiments indicated that "amorphous or round forms work just as well (or perhaps better) at retaining and releasing heat in baking food as the standard PPO forms."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (experimental evidence)
- **Assessment**: Critical finding. If shape does not affect cooking performance, then the standardized PPO forms must serve a non-functional purpose, possibly social signaling or group identity.

### Claim 38

- **Statement**: "Systematic thermal and cooking experiments indicate that heating and cooking with simple clay lumps is a more efficient technology than the relatively complex forms used by Poverty Point people."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (experimental evidence)
- **Assessment**: Strengthens Claim 37. PPO form complexity is not driven by functional efficiency, implying social or ritual motivations for their elaboration.

### Claim 39

- **Statement**: "Replication experiments show that PPOs take more steps and effort to create than simple amorphous objects (Pierce 1998). While the standard PPO forms are not complex, they nonetheless require some skill to make."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (experimental evidence)
- **Assessment**: PPO production involves extra effort beyond what is functionally necessary, a hallmark of costly signaling. The additional investment in form is a cost that serves social purposes.

### Claim 40

- **Statement**: Hunter (1975:65) "found that the cylindrical-grooved objects were particularly difficult to replicate. He notes with apparent chagrin that in his replications 'only a few examples were produced that resembled even the poorest example of this type.'"
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (experimental evidence)
- **Assessment**: Some PPO types required genuine skill, supporting the idea that PPO production could signal competence or group identity.

### Claim 41

- **Statement**: "Worldwide archaeological and ethnographic data on the use of baked-clay objects indicate that round or amorphous forms were preferred for heating and cooking purposes (Hays et al. 2016:230). This suggests that the standardized PPO forms were probably not dictated by varied baking or roasting needs."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Cross-cultural comparison further undermines purely functional explanations for PPO shape variation.

### Claim 42

- **Statement**: "The number of PPOs dwindles to only two or three objects per site in well-excavated Early Woodland period middens on the Gulf Coast, and PPOs are virtually absent from the Tchefuncte/Early Woodland component at Jaketown where they were replaced by the tetrahedron (Heller et al. 2013; Henry et al. 2017)."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: PPO disappearance correlates with the collapse of the Poverty Point system, suggesting they were integral to the cultural practices (feasting/aggregation/signaling) rather than just a cooking technology.

### Claim 43

- **Statement**: The distribution of PPO types between sites is "very uneven." Some sites contain almost exclusively one type (e.g., Lake Enterprise Mound has almost only biconical grooved), some sites lack or have very few of certain types (e.g., Terral Lewis lacks cylindrical grooved PPOs), and some sites contain all types (e.g., Poverty Point and Claiborne).
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong (Table 3)
- **Assessment**: Uneven distribution supports the interpretation that PPO types encoded social identity. Sites with all types (Poverty Point, Claiborne) are aggregation centers where multiple groups came together.

### Claim 44

- **Statement**: "At Poverty Point, the only site with good stratigraphic information on the distribution of PPOs, all six main types are present from the lowest levels to the upper levels (Connolly 2001:105)."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: All PPO types present throughout the occupation sequence rules out temporal change as an explanation for type variation, strengthening the social/group-identity interpretation.

### Claim 45

- **Statement**: "PPOs have been found in areas with abundant river gravels and at sites with abundant fire-cracked rock (Hays and Munson 2016; Hays et al. 2016:230). Furthermore, PPOs are not found in all areas of the Southeast lacking river gravels (Southerlin 1989)."
- **Page**: p. 9
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Undermines the explanation that PPOs were simply substitutes for cooking stones. Their distribution is culturally, not environmentally, determined.

### Claim 46

- **Statement**: "Even in the stoneless land of the Poverty Point site, people were transporting in considerable amounts of sandstone for use in cooking." Ford and Webb (1956:39) note that "a considerable amount of fire-cracked rock was scattered through the midden deposits."
- **Page**: p. 10
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: People at Poverty Point used both PPOs and transported stone for cooking, further demonstrating that PPOs were not merely functional substitutes for rock.

### Claim 47

- **Statement**: "Since technical reasons and the distribution of gravels seem to be insufficient for explaining the variety and standardization of PPO types, or their distribution in the Eastern Woodlands, it follows that social and historic factors are likely to be the key determinates."
- **Page**: p. 10
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Key conclusion: PPO shape variation is socially, not functionally, determined. This supports treating PPOs as potential signals of group identity in the model.

### Claim 48

- **Statement**: Webb's (1982:38) category of rare or unusual PPOs "are frequently elaborately decorated and have highly specialized forms and, unlike typical PPOs, they often are not oxidized through repeated firings (Hays et al. 2016:232)."
- **Page**: p. 10
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Unusual PPOs lack evidence of cooking use, suggesting they were made primarily for display or social purposes rather than food preparation.

### Claim 49

- **Statement**: "Unusual forms are found most frequently at Poverty Point and Claiborne, the two sites with the largest proportions of exotic artifacts, perhaps they were tokens signifying a connection to a locale in the far-flung Poverty Point exchange system."
- **Page**: p. 10
- **Type**: speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Suggests a connection between unusual PPOs and the exchange network. If unusual PPOs functioned as tokens of exchange connections, they would be a form of individual-level costly signal.

### Claim 50

- **Statement**: Miniature PPOs are "noticeably smaller than standard PPO types, are typically well-executed objects, and a number of them, particularly the spheroids, have precise tool incising." They are unlikely to have functioned primarily as cooking elements because of their size.
- **Page**: p. 10
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Miniature PPOs appear designed for display/social functions rather than cooking, supporting non-utilitarian interpretations.

### Claim 51

- **Statement**: "I argue that neither Gibson's initial characterization of Poverty Point as a chiefdom nor his later interpretation of it as a great town built by a single community fit the archaeological data well. Poverty Point's massive earthworks and its extraordinarily large and diverse artifact assemblages make sense if there were large numbers of people, forming multiethnic groups, at the site."
- **Page**: p. 10-11
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Supports the project's multiethnic aggregation model over chiefdom or permanent settlement models.

### Claim 52

- **Statement**: "By the end of the Late Archaic, primary forest efficiency provided sufficient security to permit activities such as construction of large earthworks and accumulation of wealth for the dead" (citing Dye and Watson 2010:164).
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Provides ecological context: Late Archaic resource efficiency created the surplus conditions that enabled monumental construction and aggregation.

### Claim 53

- **Statement**: "The number of PPOs at Poverty Point suggest, however, that the feasts hosted there were much larger and more numerous than at any other Late/Terminal Archaic site (e.g., Heyman et al. 2005)."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The scale of feasting at Poverty Point was exceptional compared to contemporaneous sites, consistent with its role as a regional signaling hub.

### Claim 54

- **Statement**: "At Poverty Point these items [nonutilitarian/ceremonial] are widely distributed across the site ridges, suggesting that no person or group had control over their use and that they were used in activities that spanned the site ridges (Webb 1982:67)."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The non-exclusive distribution of prestige items across ridges suggests communal rather than hierarchical control, consistent with a transegalitarian signaling model rather than a chiefdom model.

### Claim 55

- **Statement**: "This pattern fits well with Spielmann's argument that in small-scale societies craft items are made by the group and for the group for use in feasting."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Communal craft production for feasting fits the cooperative signaling framework better than models based on individual elite control.

### Claim 56

- **Statement**: "Interestingly, it appears that their value drops dramatically after the 'collapse' of the Poverty Point culture around 1000 BC, because many of these items are either very rare or not present in Early Woodland/Tchula period sites in the LMV."
- **Page**: p. 11
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The collapse of the value system for exotic/ritual items after 1000 BC supports the interpretation that their value was maintained by the aggregation/signaling system. Once the system collapsed, the items lost their signaling function.

### Claim 57

- **Statement**: Hays conjectures "that PPOs were used most often as a specialized type of cooking element encoded with social information that was created for and used in ritualized feasting contexts."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Central interpretive conclusion. PPOs as socially encoded cooking implements for feasting integrates their functional and social roles.

### Claim 58

- **Statement**: Hays and colleagues documented four geographic patterns in PPO distributions (Hays et al. 2016): "(1) biconical PPOs appear to be strongly associated with people north of the LMV; (2) cross-grooved and cylindrical-grooved PPOs are strongly associated with the LMV and the Gulf Coast; (3) sandy-paste mulberries likely originated on the Gulf Coast; (4) the white, cane-punctated PPOs at Poverty Point may signify ties to west Tennessee sites where they likely originated."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Geographic associations between PPO types and regions directly support the interpretation that PPO types encoded group/regional identity, relevant to distinguishing groups at aggregation events.

### Claim 59

- **Statement**: "The two Poverty Point culture sites with the largest assemblages, Claiborne and Poverty Point, would have the most diverse assemblage of artifacts since, as Conkey (1980) argued, aggregation sites for hunters and gatherers tend to have a greater diversity of artifact styles than their dispersion sites."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Applies Conkey's aggregation-site model to PPO distributions. The diversity at Poverty Point and Claiborne marks them as primary aggregation centers.

### Claim 60

- **Statement**: "Perhaps distinctions in PPO forms would have been useful to differentiate and distinguish ethnic groups when large numbers of people came together at Poverty Point. One possibility is that the different PPO types may have served as emblems of clans or other social units, just as Sassaman (2010) and Townsend and Sharp (2004) have argued for bannerstone types."
- **Page**: p. 12
- **Type**: speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Speculative but directly relevant to the signaling model. If PPO types served as group emblems, they would function as low-cost identity signals within the aggregation system.

### Claim 61

- **Statement**: The unusual PPOs and miniatures "may have functioned, in part, as tokens of connection to the widely dispersed Poverty Point culture." Hays et al. argued that they "may have served as part of a socio-economic bundle that mediated or translated relationships within the Poverty Point culture (sensu Pauketat 2013)" (Hays et al. 2016:214).
- **Page**: p. 12

- **Evidence strength**: Weak

## Summary Assessment

Hays (2018) argues that the Poverty Point archaeological record is best explained as the product of periodic, large-scale ritualized feasting events associated with multiethnic aggregation. The paper's most significant contribution to the costly signaling argument is threefold. First, it provides quantitative evidence (Table 1, Table 3) documenting Poverty Point's extraordinary artifact assemblage relative to contemporaneous sites, reinforcing the need for a special explanatory framework. Second, it marshals experimental evidence (Pierce 1998; McGee 1995; Hunter 1975) demonstrating that PPO shapes do not improve cooking performance and that simple clay lumps are equally or more efficient, which implies that the investment in standardized PPO forms served social rather than purely functional purposes; this extra investment in non-functional elaboration is structurally analogous to costly signaling. Third, the geographic patterning of PPO types across sites supports the interpretation that PPO forms encoded group identity information, relevant to distinguishing social units during aggregation events. The paper's limitation is that the feasting hypothesis, while consistent with multiple lines of evidence, relies heavily on indirect indicators because organic food remains are poorly preserved at Poverty Point.
