## Claims

### Claim 1

- **Statement**: Mound A measures almost 200 m along its north-south axis and just over 200 m along its east-west axis, with an estimated volume between approximately 178,000 m^3 (Shenkel 1986) and 238,000 m^3 (Kidder et al. 2006:95).
- **Page**: p. 22
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Critical for labor investment calculations. The range of volume estimates reflects different measurement methods. The 238,000 m^3 figure from Kidder et al. 2006 is the more recent estimate. Directly relevant to signaling cost parameters.

---

### Claim 2

- **Statement**: Mound A consists of two sections, the tall, conical "wing" section on the west and the shorter, flat "tail" section or platform to the east. The platform portion of the mound rises to a height between 8-10 m above the surface of Macon Ridge. A ramp-like feature appears to connect the summits of these two sections.
- **Page**: p. 22
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the complex, planned architecture of Mound A, relevant to arguments about coordinated construction effort.

---

### Claim 3

- **Statement**: Mound B is approximately 60-62 m in diameter and rises to a height of nearly 6 m. Ford and Webb (1956:37) argued that Mound B may have stood as high as 10-12 m when it was originally constructed. The volume of this mound has been estimated at approximately 5,000 m^3 (Shenkel 1986).
- **Page**: p. 22
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides volume estimate for Mound B, useful for comparative labor investment calculations across mounds.

---

### Claim 4

- **Statement**: Mound E rises about 3.5-4 m high and has basal dimensions of about 100 m north-south by 110 m east-west. A large portion of the southern end of this mound has been truncated by historic land-leveling activities. Comparison of historic and modern aerial photographs suggests that between 15-25 m has been removed from the southern slope.
- **Page**: p. 25
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Mound E dimensions suggest it was originally larger than its current form. Important for total earthwork volume calculations.

---

### Claim 5

- **Statement**: Mound D is a small, flat-topped, rectangular mound located near the southern ends of the concentric ridges. Mound D rises 1-2 m above the plaza and has basal dimensions of about 40 m north-south by 35 m east-west.
- **Page**: p. 25
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Later shown (Claim 13) to not be a Poverty Point-era construction; it was built much later atop the southern ridge. This is important for correcting total mound counts.

---

### Claim 6

- **Statement**: Lower Jackson Mound is located just over 2 km south of the Poverty Point earthworks and is dated nearly 2,000 years older than Poverty Point (Saunders et al. 2001). Despite this age discrepancy many researchers have suggested Lower Jackson mound may have been incorporated into the design of Poverty Point or utilized as a guide for construction of the other earthworks (Clark 2004; Gibson 2000:91).
- **Page**: p. 28
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Relevant to site planning and the intentional alignment of mounds. Suggests continuity of place across millennia, consistent with the site's special significance.

---

### Claim 7

- **Statement**: Much of the fill used for construction of the Poverty Point earthworks was probably obtained from the large borrow areas located immediately to the west of Mound A.
- **Page**: p. 28
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Identifies likely sediment sources for earthwork construction, relevant to understanding labor logistics and construction scale.

---

### Claim 8

- **Statement**: The ridges were built in discrete stages with intervening midden deposits (Connolly 2003a:18; Ford and Webb 1956:32). Ford and Webb (1956:32) also noted that at least some of the earth used to construct the ridges was taken from the swales or ditches separating each ridge.
- **Page**: p. 34
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The staged construction with intervening midden is consistent with episodic building, potentially corresponding to seasonal aggregation events.

---

### Claim 9

- **Statement**: Ford and Webb (1956:128) concluded Poverty Point was a terminal Archaic cultural manifestation characterized by long distance trade, elaborate ideology, and numerous distinctive artifact types, including baked clay cooking balls, steatite vessels, plummets, adzes, celts, hoes, and a small core and blade lithic industry. Poverty Point was considered to be a chiefdom with a large, sedentary, socially ranked population in the thousands that relied on agriculture for subsistence.
- **Page**: p. 34
- **Type**: interpretive (historical)
- **Evidence strength**: Weak (superseded)
- **Assessment**: Historical context only. The chiefdom/agriculture interpretation has been largely abandoned. Relevant as a contrast to current understanding.

---

### Claim 10

- **Statement**: Gibson (1973) claimed residents at Poverty Point subsisted by hunting and gathering. To support his argument, he pointed out that all known Poverty Point sites are situated at the edges of two or more "ecotones," which would have allowed for exploitation of numerous seasonally available resources.
- **Page**: pp. 36-37
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant to the ecotone positioning hypothesis in the signaling model. Gibson's observation supports the argument that the site location maximized resource diversity.

---

### Claim 11

- **Statement**: Jackson (1986:537) noted high occurrences of nuts, fish, and deer remains and suggested these subsistence remains indicated year-round occupation. He argued, however, that the Poverty Point site was probably occupied seasonally (1986:537), and concluded a sedentary hunter-gatherer population of any substantial size at Poverty Point would have quickly led to environmental degradation.
- **Page**: p. 39
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Directly supports the seasonal aggregation model. Jackson's argument that large sedentary populations would degrade local resources is consistent with the hypothesis that Poverty Point functioned as a seasonal aggregation center rather than a permanent settlement.

---

### Claim 12

- **Statement**: Jackson (1991) also suggested ethnographically documented trade fairs provide a better model for the development and construction of Poverty Point than previous interpretations that stressed chiefly authority and a large sedentary population. According to Jackson (1991), large numbers of people from different social groups may have convened periodically to construct the earthworks at Poverty Point and trade material goods, presumably leaving these items behind when the populations dispersed.
- **Page**: p. 39
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Highly relevant to the signaling framework. Jackson's trade fair model anticipates the aggregation hypothesis. Periodic convening of dispersed groups for construction and exchange aligns with the costly signaling model.

---

### Claim 13

- **Statement**: Only four mounds (A, B, C, and E) at the site are attributable to the Poverty Point occupation; the nearby Motley mound may also be a Poverty Point era construction. Mound D at Poverty Point is a later construction. Whether this architectural feature was built during the Late Woodland Coles Creek period or during the historic occupation of the area is unknown.
- **Page**: p. 297
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Corrects the mound count to four confirmed Poverty Point-era mounds, not five. Important for accurate labor investment estimates.

---

### Claim 14

- **Statement**: The Mound A platform was built rapidly in two distinct stages. Stage I was only 6 cm thick in the profile excavation, but identification of this stage across the platform substantiates the notion that it was a separate building episode from the overlying Stage II. Shortly after construction of Stage I, the Stage II mound was built from massive amounts of basket loaded fill.
- **Page**: p. 247
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Critical for understanding construction tempo. Rapid, staged construction is consistent with collective labor mobilization during aggregation events.

---

### Claim 15

- **Statement**: Construction of the Mound A platform Stage II must have proceeded quickly. No activity surfaces, construction hiatuses, or erosion features were identified macroscopically within the fill of Stage II, and micromorphological analysis of the outer surfaces of individual basket loads revealed no exposure to weathering, bioturbation, or pedogenic processes (Arco 2006; Kidder et al. 2006). These contact zones between individual loading events failed to reveal any evidence that Stage II was exposed to rainfall during its apparently rapid construction (Kidder et al. 2006).
- **Page**: p. 248
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Among the strongest evidence for rapid construction. Micromorphological analysis at the basket-load level showing no weathering is compelling evidence that construction was continuous and fast, possibly completed in weeks to months. Critical for signaling arguments about visible, coordinated collective action.

---

### Claim 16

- **Statement**: The terrace surrounding the Mound A platform consists of over 19,300 m^3 of fill and covers over 5,200 m^2 of ground, nearly doubling the horizontal extent of the Mound A platform (Kidder et al. 2006).
- **Page**: p. 249
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Adds substantial volume to Mound A construction totals. The terrace alone represents a major labor investment.

---

### Claim 17

- **Statement**: Sediments used to construct the Mound A platform consisted almost exclusively of silts and silt loams with few clay-sized particles. These silt and silt loam sediments were obtained from numerous sources throughout the vicinity of the Poverty Point site, and included brown, yellow, red, black, and gray sediments. The sediments comprising the platform must have originated from different source areas, but the admixture and heterogeneous appearance of individual basket loads within the platform suggests they were mixed together before being used as construction fill.
- **Page**: p. 249
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The intentional mixing of sediments from multiple sources before loading indicates planned, organized construction activity, not haphazard filling. Suggests coordinated procurement from multiple locations simultaneously.

---

### Claim 18

- **Statement**: The submound midden beneath the Mound A platform does not contain any debris from the manufacture of chipped stone tools. In addition, PPOs and PPO fragments are entirely absent from the submound midden. These findings are striking given that chipped stone tool manufacture typically produces large quantities of micro-debitage, and chipped stone debris along with PPOs constitute the overwhelming majority of artifacts from all known midden contexts at the Poverty Point site.
- **Page**: p. 246
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The midden beneath the Mound A platform is distinctly different from typical ridge middens, suggesting the area served a different function before mound construction. The absence of domestic debris contrasts with ridge habitation evidence.

---

### Claim 19

- **Statement**: AMS dates from the submound midden beneath the Mound A platform returned ages of 3020 +/- 40 b.p. and 3040 +/- 40 b.p., suggesting construction of the Mound A platform was initiated fairly late in the overall sequence of occupation at Poverty Point. The calibrated 2-sigma ranges place these dates between approximately 1413-1130 cal. B.C.
- **Page**: pp. 246-247
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides chronological constraints for Mound A platform construction, placing it in the latter portion of the Poverty Point occupation sequence.

---

### Claim 20

- **Statement**: Each mound was constructed in a series of three major flat-topped stages (Kidder et al. 2004). Floors were constructed over the top of each of these major construction stages in Mound B. The flat-topped mound stages in Mound B are remarkably uniform, each approximately 1.22 m thick (Ford and Webb 1956; Kidder et al. 2004).
- **Page**: p. 254
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The uniformity of stage thickness in Mound B (each ~1.22 m) suggests standardized construction episodes, consistent with planned, repeated labor events.

---

### Claim 21

- **Statement**: Mounds B and E were clearly not intended to be exact duplicates of one another; however, similarities in their construction techniques, location, and architectural form suggest they may have been constructed synchronously and may have served similar functions.
- **Page**: p. 255
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Synchronous construction of two mounds at opposite ends of the ridge complex would require a large, coordinated labor force, relevant to arguments about collective action capacity.

---

### Claim 22

- **Statement**: The construction of Stage II in the Mound A platform must have occurred quickly and must have necessitated a relatively large labor force. Mounds B, E, as well as the ridges also appear to have been constructed quickly (Kidder et al. 2004; Gibson 2000:96). While not as massive as the Mound A platform, these other mounds and ridges would have undoubtedly required a considerable investment of labor.
- **Page**: p. 259
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Core claim for the signaling model. Rapid construction requiring large labor force is precisely what the costly signaling framework predicts: visible, costly collective investment that cannot be easily faked.

---

### Claim 23

- **Statement**: Mound C consists of the series of 13 extremely thin platforms, or activity surfaces, as well as the relatively thick conical mound cap. These lower platform mound stages are unlike any other construction at Poverty Point. These platforms are much thinner than construction stages in the other mounds and are composed of discrete deposits of variously colored and textured sediments.
- **Page**: p. 259
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Mound C's unique construction (13 thin platforms) suggests a different function and construction pattern from the other mounds. Its gradual, episodic construction contrasts with the rapid construction of Mounds A, B, and E.

---

### Claim 24

- **Statement**: Unlike the other earthworks at Poverty Point, Mound C does not appear to have been constructed rapidly. Many of the initial platform stages of Mound C show evidence of cultural activities before construction of the next overlying platform. Mound C was built in smaller, more numerous stages than the other mounds at Poverty Point; and great care seems to have been taken in selecting differentially colored and textured sediments for construction.
- **Page**: p. 260
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Mound C represents a different construction rhythm, potentially with extended use periods between episodes. This contrasts with the rapid, labor-intensive construction of the larger mounds and suggests different social functions.

---

### Claim 25

- **Statement**: Virtually all of the mounds and ridges at Poverty Point appear to have been constructed primarily as a series of flat-topped platforms (Ford and Webb 1956; Gibson 2000; Kidder et al. 2006; Kidder et al. 2004; Kuttruff 1975; Ortmann 2003). These platforms display considerable variation in thickness between mounds, but indicate that the primary goal of mound (and ridge) construction at Poverty Point was to create architectural features with flat summits.
- **Page**: pp. 261-262
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The flat-topped platform construction convention indicates standardized building practices and potentially functional use of summit surfaces.

---

### Claim 26

- **Statement**: Gibson (2000:96) suggests that most of the radiometric dates from Poverty Point indicate the earthworks were constructed between approximately 1600 to 1300 cal. B.C. (2-Sigma level). Connolly (2002:74), however, has re-evaluated a large suite of these same dates and determined that construction and occupation of the site occurred between approximately 1700 to 1100 cal. B.C. (1-Sigma level).
- **Page**: pp. 264-265
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Provides the chronological range for the site. The 1700-1100 B.C. range from Connolly is the broader, more conservative estimate. Important for temporal parameters in the signaling model.

---

### Claim 27

- **Statement**: Fifty-six radiocarbon and accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS) assays have been obtained from various contexts at Poverty Point. In addition, two optically stimulated luminescence (OSL) dates have been obtained from Mound E, and two thermoluminescence (TL) dates have been obtained from ceramic vessel sherds recovered from the north sector ridges.
- **Page**: p. 264
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the size of the radiometric database available for chronological analysis.

---

### Claim 28

- **Statement**: The 19 radiocarbon/AMS dates from the Poverty Point ridges with less than 100 year errors, while not evenly distributed or comprehensive in their coverage of the site, suggest the period from 1769 to 980 cal. B.C. may provide a general time frame for the initial construction and utilization of the ridges. This range provides a more conservative (i.e., broader) estimate for the Poverty Point period occupation of the site.
- **Page**: pp. 268, 272
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The ~800-year span (1769-980 cal. B.C.) represents the maximum estimated occupation period for the ridges. This is broader than the 1700-1100 B.C. range commonly cited.

---

### Claim 29

- **Statement**: Construction of the majority of stages in Mound B most likely occurred sometime after 1737 cal. B.C. If the actual construction of Mound B was initiated shortly after this date, this feature would appear to be one of the earliest dated constructions at the site.
- **Page**: p. 272
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Provides a terminus post quem for Mound B construction. If Mound B is among the earliest constructions, it suggests mound building began early in the site's history.

---

### Claim 30

- **Statement**: The date from the Mound C Stage VIII platform indicates initial construction of the lowermost platforms in Mound C must have occurred before 1436 cal. B.C. Unlike Mounds B and E, the Mound A platform, and the ridges, construction of Mound C may have been a slower, more gradual process, thereby distinguishing it from other earthen architecture at the site.
- **Page**: p. 274
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Mound C's gradual construction contrasts with rapid construction of other features, suggesting different social processes or functions.

---

### Claim 31

- **Statement**: All of the mounds and ridges at Poverty Point were constructed and utilized over approximately 800 years between 1769 and 980 cal. B.C. Gibson (2000:96) suggests the earthworks were constructed between 1600 and 1300 cal. B.C. and speculates the earthmoving took as little as a single or few generations.
- **Page**: p. 300
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The total occupation span (~800 years) is the conservative estimate; Gibson's narrower range suggests actual construction may have been compressed into a much shorter period. The "single or few generations" speculation by Gibson is relevant to signaling intensity.

---

### Claim 32

- **Statement**: Micromorphological analysis of the Mound A platform indicates it was probably constructed in a few months (Kidder et al. 2006). If the Stage I portion of this architectural feature does underlie the Mound A cone, as suggested by soil core A-88, then the cone must have also been constructed quickly. Similarly, Mounds B and E do not show any evidence of substantial temporal breaks in construction.
- **Page**: p. 300
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The most specific claim about construction speed. "A few months" for the Mound A platform (~238,000 m^3 including the cone, or at minimum the platform portion) implies an extraordinarily large labor force. This is the single most important claim for the signaling model's labor investment parameters.

---

### Claim 33

- **Statement**: Mounds A and E display no evidence of cultural debris on the surfaces of these platforms. In addition, Mound B revealed only limited evidence of activities on the surfaces of various platforms. Unless the surfaces of the construction platforms in Mounds A and E (and possibly B) were meticulously cleaned, any activities that were conducted on these mounds did not result in the accumulation of midden deposits.
- **Page**: p. 262
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The absence of domestic debris on mound surfaces sharply distinguishes mounds from ridges (which are full of domestic refuse). This suggests mounds served non-domestic functions, consistent with their role as symbolic or signal-bearing architecture rather than habitation.

---

### Claim 34

- **Statement**: The summits of the platform mound stages at Poverty Point do not display evidence that they were used for any of these activities [production and display of ritual paraphernalia, feasting, mortuary structures, elite residences, temples, charnel houses]. The Poverty Point mounds were also not utilized in the same manner as Middle Woodland-period mortuary mounds. The only evidence of human remains ever recovered from the Poverty Point site consists of the proximal end of a femur recovered from the Stage I ("ash bed") construction of Mound B.
- **Page**: p. 303
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Poverty Point mounds do not fit any standard functional category for mounds in the Southeast. This functional ambiguity is consistent with the signaling interpretation: the mounds' primary purpose may have been the act of construction itself rather than subsequent use.

---

### Claim 35

- **Statement**: There is no evidence for the presence of social elites from excavation of the mounds or ridges, so if some individuals had differential control over labor resources within the Poverty Point community this power was apparently not expressed in differential access to other resources.
- **Page**: p. 309
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Critical finding. The absence of evidence for social hierarchy or differential access to resources, despite massive construction, is consistent with the costly signaling model as an alternative to hierarchical/chiefdom models. Construction may have been organized through voluntary collective action rather than elite coercion.

---

### Claim 36

- **Statement**: The pace of mound construction coupled with the size and planned arrangement of these architectural features suggests some individual(s) must have directed or controlled this activity. If these offices or positions of social control existed, were they permanent or ephemeral?
- **Page**: p. 308
- **Type**: speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence
- **Assessment**: Ortmann raises the question of leadership but cannot resolve it from the data. The possibility of ephemeral leadership roles is compatible with the signaling model where temporary aggregation leaders coordinate construction.

---

### Claim 37

- **Statement**: It is possible that ephemeral positions of social leadership were established to direct construction of the massive earthworks. Impermanent positions of social authority are documented ethnographically among some groups of hunter-gatherers such as the Arapesh of New Guinea (Tuzin 2001).
- **Page**: pp. 310-311
- **Type**: speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Ortmann raises the possibility of temporary, task-specific leadership, which aligns with aggregation-based social organization. The ethnographic parallel with the Arapesh is suggestive but not strongly applicable.

---

### Claim 38

- **Statement**: It is also unclear whether earthwork construction at Poverty Point was an ongoing activity undertaken by a permanently resident population or an activity engaged in by a seasonally aggregating population. Faunal remains from the contemporaneous Copes site (Jackson 1986, 1989) and the Middle Archaic Watson Brake site (Saunders et al. 2005) provide limited evidence for year-round sedentism. By analogy there is no reason to assume a seasonal occupation at Poverty Point; however, there are no data available to test this hypothesis at present.
- **Page**: p. 311
- **Type**: speculative
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Ortmann explicitly identifies the sedentary vs. seasonal aggregation question as unresolved. The signaling model assumes seasonal aggregation; this passage confirms that neither interpretation is ruled out by current data.

---

### Claim 39

- **Statement**: Mounds A, B, and E are all situated outside of the earthen ridges along a common axis that also intersects the Middle Archaic Lower Jackson mound. The linear arrangement of these mounds suggests they were intentionally aligned. Mound C is the only architectural feature located inside the plaza. Mound C occupies a special place on the landscape, distinct from any other earthwork at the site.
- **Page**: p. 298
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The intentional alignment of mounds along a common axis with a 2,000-year-older mound demonstrates planned site design incorporating long-lived landscape memory.

---

### Claim 40

- **Statement**: The mounds were not homogenous or haphazard piles of sediment. The sediments used for construction were carefully selected from various sources, and some sediments were additionally prepared before use. Mounds B and E consist of alternating layers of reddish-brown and light gray or white sediments. The Stage II construction of the Mound A platform and the conical cap overlying Mound C may also reflect symbolic color associations. These sediments were intentionally mixed from a variety of colors and textures that presumably had some cultural significance.
- **Page**: p. 305
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Careful sediment selection and preparation indicates construction was not merely functional but had symbolic or ritual dimensions. Relevant to understanding the social meaning embedded in the act of construction.

---

### Claim 41

- **Statement**: The construction of such massive earthworks raises numerous questions about the nature of social relationships between the individuals responsible for their construction. The social, political, and economic factors underwriting the construction of the Poverty Point earthworks remains one of the most enigmatic characteristics of this terminal Late Archaic site.
- **Page**: p. 308
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Assertion without evidence
- **Assessment**: Ortmann explicitly identifies the central puzzle that the signaling model aims to address: how hunter-gatherers organized massive collective construction without clear evidence of hierarchy.

---

### Claim 42

- **Statement**: Poverty Point constitutes the largest set of monuments ever constructed by hunter-gatherers in North America. The large quantity and diversity of domestic debris suggests Poverty Point was a large residential center. The mounds at Poverty Point were constructed quickly; but unlike the ridges, these mounds were not used to support residences or conduct domestic activities. The massive scale and planned construction of the earthworks combined with the requisite organization of a large labor force suggests the presence of social leaders at Poverty Point, but it is still not possible to characterize the nature of social relationships at Poverty Point or the level of sociopolitical complexity achieved by this hunter-gatherer community.
- **Page**: p. 314
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Ortmann's concluding statement frames the core paradox: rapid, massive, planned construction by hunter-gatherers with no clear evidence of hierarchical control. This is precisely the puzzle the costly signaling framework addresses.

---

### Claim 43

- **Statement**: Sloping deposits of basket loaded fill in Stage II suggest it was built by creating dome-shaped piles of earth that were subsequently linked by filling in the spaces between piles. Individual basket loads vary in size and the presence of iron oxide staining indicates some of these sediments were wet when loaded into the mound.
- **Page**: p. 248
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Documents the specific construction technique for Mound A platform: coordinated teams creating individual piles that were then linked. This technique could accommodate multiple work groups operating simultaneously, consistent with aggregation-based labor organization.

---

### Claim 44

- **Statement**: A large portion of the relatively flat plaza at Poverty Point is an artificial construction created by redepositing midden into old erosion features and other topographically low areas (Ortmann 2003; Woodiel 1990).
- **Page**: p. 306
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: The plaza itself required significant labor to construct and level, adding to total earthwork volume estimates. Plaza construction represents additional communal labor investment beyond the mounds and ridges.

---

### Claim 45

- **Statement**: Ridges were typically built as a series of adjacent piles of earth that were linked together by filling in the gaps between piles and leveling the upper surfaces to form small flat-topped platforms (Gibson 2000:93; Kuttruff 1975). The greater size of the Mound A platform, compared to Mounds B and E, may have necessitated this different construction technique.
- **Page**: p. 258
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: The "pile and link" construction technique used for both ridges and the Mound A platform suggests groups may have worked simultaneously on separate piles. This is consistent with a labor organization model where multiple bands each contribute discrete units of work during aggregation.

---

### Claim 46

- **Statement**: Mound C is the only mound at Poverty Point with midden accumulations and cultural features on the summits of its construction platforms. Thin deposits of midden and a few cultural features, including an ochre-filled pit, were identified on the summits of the initial construction platforms comprising Mound C. These platform summits, however, do not appear to have been utilized for the same suite of domestic activities as the Poverty Point ridges. No evidence for cooking or serving, and only limited evidence for production of stone tools was identified on the summits of the Mound C construction stages.
- **Page**: p. 302
- **Type**: empirical
- **Evidence strength**: Strong
- **Assessment**: Mound C's unique character, with activity surfaces but no domestic activities, suggests specialized (possibly ritual or ceremonial) use distinct from both the other mounds (no activities) and the ridges (domestic activities).

---

### Claim 47

- **Statement**: Mound C's nearly central position within the plaza supports the notion that this feature served some ritual and presumably communal purpose (Knight 1986:680, 2004:319). The construction of Mound C was also the most carefully planned and intricately designed of any of the Poverty Point earthworks.
- **Page**: p. 307
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Moderate
- **Assessment**: Mound C's central, visible position within the plaza would make it an ideal focal point for communal gatherings, consistent with the aggregation model.

---

### Claim 48

- **Statement**: Kidder and Sassaman (2007:17-18) argue for social inequality as a prerequisite to the construction of large-scale earthworks such as those at Poverty Point. They suggest institutionalized positions of social leadership were necessary to direct the labor efforts required for construction. They do not, however, offer suggestions for how to identify offices or positions of social inequality in the archaeological record of Archaic mound sites.
- **Page**: p. 309
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Ortmann notes that the Kidder and Sassaman argument for required social inequality lacks testable archaeological implications. The signaling model offers an alternative mechanism (voluntary costly signaling) that does not require pre-existing hierarchy.

---

### Claim 49

- **Statement**: Gibson (2004) argues that social inequality was not necessary to direct the labor efforts required to construct the Poverty Point earthworks. Rather than viewing the Poverty Point earthworks as a result of labor obligations between individuals and groups of individuals with different social privileges, Gibson suggests the mounds and ridges were constructed for the greater good of the community. According to this model, individuals within society were motivated by a sense of "beneficent obligation" or "debt of gratitude" to the community brought about by gift giving as well as communalistic sentiments such as patriotism or religious fervor (Gibson 2004:259-260).
- **Page**: p. 310
- **Type**: interpretive
- **Evidence strength**: Weak
- **Assessment**: Gibson's "beneficent obligation" model is an alternative to hierarchy-based explanations but lacks a clear evolutionary mechanism. The costly signaling framework provides a more rigorous theoretical basis for voluntary collective construction without requiring altruism.

### Claim 50

## Summary Assessment

Ortmann's 2007 dissertation provides the most comprehensive stratigraphic and chronological analysis of the Poverty Point mounds available. The key findings for the signaling project are: (1) Mound A platform was constructed rapidly, probably in a few months, based on micromorphological evidence of no weathering between basket loads; (2) all four confirmed Poverty Point-era mounds (A, B, C, E) were built as series of flat-topped platforms, with estimated volumes of 178,000-238,000 m^3 for Mound A and 5,000 m^3 for Mound B; (3) mound summits display little to no evidence of domestic activities, in sharp contrast to the ridges, suggesting mounds served non-utilitarian functions; and (4) despite massive labor investment, there is no archaeological evidence for social hierarchy or differential access to resources. Ortmann explicitly frames the central paradox, rapid monumental construction by hunter-gatherers without evidence of coercive leadership, that the costly signaling model aims to resolve. The evidence for rapid, planned, coordinated construction requiring large labor forces is strong and directly supports the model's prediction that construction functioned as a costly, visible signal of collective commitment.
