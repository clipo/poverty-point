## Relevance to Argument

This paper proposes awe as a prosocial emotion that facilitated community formation and cooperation at Poverty Point. While Sanger does not engage directly with costly signaling theory, his framework identifies a proximate psychological mechanism that complements the signaling model. Costly signaling theory explains why individuals invest in apparently wasteful cooperative behavior (to credibly advertise underlying quality to potential partners), but it does not fully specify the psychological process through which participation in monumental construction generates cooperative returns. Sanger's argument that awe, triggered by monumental architecture, large-scale communal labor, and mass gatherings, diminishes self-importance and increases willingness to cooperate provides exactly such a mechanism. In the signaling model, awe could function as a mediating variable: construction signals generate awe in participants and observers, which in turn lowers the psychological barriers to cooperation, creating a positive feedback loop between signaling investment and cooperative returns. The paper also reinforces several empirical points about Poverty Point: rapid construction, mass gatherings of diverse groups, centralization of regional social networks, and the absence of coercive hierarchies, all of which are consistent with the signaling framework.

## Data Presented

The paper presents no new empirical data. It is a theoretical and interpretive synthesis that applies psychological research on awe to the existing Poverty Point archaeological record. The archaeological evidence cited (site dimensions, construction chronology, timber circles, exotic materials, network structure) is drawn entirely from published sources, primarily Gibson (2000), Kidder (2002, 2011), Kidder et al. (2021), Ortmann & Kidder (2013), Hargrave et al. (2021), Clay (2023), and Spivey et al. (2015). The psychological evidence is drawn from a substantial body of experimental and observational studies conducted over the past two decades.

## Methodological Notes

This is a theoretical paper, not an empirical one. Its contribution is the application of a well-developed body of psychological research on awe to an archaeological case study. The argument proceeds by: (1) reviewing how archaeologists have studied emotions; (2) introducing the psychological literature on awe; (3) presenting Poverty Point as a case where awe was both produced and consequential; and (4) arguing that understanding awe helps explain community formation at the site. The primary methodological limitation is the inferential gap between modern psychological experiments (which measure responses to relatively mundane stimuli in laboratory settings) and the emotional experiences of people 3,000 years ago encountering unprecedented monumental constructions. Sanger acknowledges this limitation explicitly, noting that modern studies "are based on rather mundane awe-inspiring events and likely grossly underestimate the magnitude of impact created by more monumentous encounters" (p. 1471) and that "past peoples had very different emotional worlds that only partially intersect with our own" (p. 1471). The paper does not generate testable predictions or propose methods for distinguishing the awe framework from alternative explanations (e.g., costly signaling, shared cosmology, coercion).

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Kidder and Grooms 2025**: Shares the 1,000,000 m^3 earthmoving estimate and the rapid construction timeline. Kidder and Grooms frame Poverty Point as a "revitalization" site; Sanger's awe framework provides a psychological mechanism for how revitalization gatherings could forge new social bonds.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: The rapid construction of Mound A (months, not years) is central to Sanger's argument that construction events themselves generated awe.
- **Kidder 2011**: Provides the construction chronology and gathering evidence that Sanger uses to identify awe-inducing conditions.
- **Clay 2023**: The recursive timber circle construction/decommissioning sequence provides evidence for repeated awe-generating events over time.
- **Hargrave et al. 2021**: Documents the 36+ timber circles and ridge construction episodes that expand the evidence for monumental construction beyond the mounds.
- **Sassaman 2005, 2010**: Provides the "black hole" network centralization framework that Sanger uses to characterize Poverty Point's social impact.
- **Spivey et al. 2015**: Contributes the network analysis showing Poverty Point's centralizing effect on regional exchange.
- **Gibson 2000, 2004**: Provides baseline site description and the shared cosmology interpretation that Sanger positions as complementary to but insufficient without the awe framework.
- **Hays 2019**: The feasting interpretation provides another context for awe-generating communal activities.
- **Quinn 2019**: Not cited by Sanger, but Quinn's review of costly signaling theory in archaeology identifies the need for proximate mechanisms connecting signals to cooperative outcomes, which Sanger's awe framework addresses.
- **Peacock and Rafferty 2013**: Not cited by Sanger, but their bet-hedging framework for Archaic mound building provides an alternative evolutionary explanation that could be compared with the awe framework.
- **Dunnell and Greenlee 1999**: Not cited by Sanger, but their "waste" hypothesis provides the theoretical context for why costly behavior (including awe-generating monumental construction) could be adaptive under environmental uncertainty.

## Relationship to Costly Signaling Theory

Sanger does not cite or engage with costly signaling theory directly. However, the two frameworks are complementary rather than competing:

1. **Signaling theory** explains the ultimate (evolutionary/adaptive) logic: individuals invest in costly cooperative behavior because credible signals of quality attract cooperative partners and deter competitors.
2. **Awe framework** identifies a proximate (psychological) mechanism: participation in or observation of monumental construction triggers awe, which diminishes self-interest and increases cooperative motivation.
3. **Integration**: In a combined model, monumental construction serves as both a costly signal (advertising group capacity/commitment to potential partners) and an awe-generating experience (psychologically facilitating the cooperation needed to produce the signal). This creates a positive feedback loop: investment in signaling generates awe, which lowers the psychological cost of further cooperative investment.

The key distinction is that costly signaling theory requires costs to be borne to ensure honesty, while the awe framework suggests that the emotional experience of participation may partially offset those costs by making cooperation psychologically rewarding. This is not necessarily a contradiction: signaling theory requires net costs (after accounting for benefits), and awe-generated psychological rewards do not eliminate the real resource costs of construction labor.
