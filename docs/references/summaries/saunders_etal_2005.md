## Relevance to Argument

Watson Brake is the most important comparative case for the Poverty Point costly signaling model. As the largest Middle Archaic mound complex in the Lower Mississippi Valley (ca. 3500-3000 B.C.), it demonstrates that hunter-gatherer monumental construction predates Poverty Point by roughly two millennia, establishing that such investment was possible without long-distance exchange networks, formal social hierarchy, or agricultural surplus. Several findings have direct bearing on the signaling model: (1) Watson Brake's ecotone location mirrors the ecological diversity hypothesis proposed for Poverty Point; (2) the complete absence of non-local exchange goods distinguishes Watson Brake from Poverty Point and suggests exotic goods acquisition was a later innovation, not inherited from the mound-building tradition; (3) the authors' explicit rejection of Hamilton's (1999) bet-hedging model, on the grounds that mounds were built during stable periods between ENSO pulses rather than during crises, presents a challenge that must be addressed; and (4) the 1,300-year gap between Watson Brake's abandonment and Poverty Point's emergence, with no evidence of cultural continuity, means Poverty Point's signaling system was likely an independent development rather than a direct inheritance.

## Data Presented

The paper presents results from field investigations conducted between 1993 and 1999, including:

**Chronometric data**: 27 viable radiocarbon dates from seven mounds and one ridge (Table 1); 18 accepted dates after rejection of anomalous samples. Six OSL dates from mound fills (Table 2). Dates establish occupation from ca. 4000 B.C. and construction from ca. 3500 to after 3000 B.C.

**Stratigraphy**: Detailed profiles of seven north mound test units (Figure 4) and south mound cores (Figure 6); Mound A core profile to 5.6 m depth (Figure 7). Fill units numbered sequentially for each mound, documenting 1-6 construction episodes per earthwork.

**Artifacts**: 347 kg of fire-cracked rock; 16.9 kg of flaked lithics (32,640 pieces) from north mounds; 34.2 kg of fired earthen objects. Lithic assemblage includes 159 flake cores, 16 blade cores, 70 bladelets, 93 drill preforms, 154 finished drills, 24 finished bifaces, and chert beads (Tables 5, 6).

**Faunal data**: 12,927 total specimens (NISP) from Mound B shell midden (Table 7); 56+ vertebrate taxa; 18 mussel species (Table 8); 114 fish otoliths with seasonal data (Table 9). Fish dominate by NISP (58.82%) and weight.

**Botanical data**: Hickory nutshell dominant; goosefoot, possible marshelder, grape, and hackberry seeds recovered from Mound B and ridge K/A.

**Soils data**: Detailed soil horizon descriptions for Mound A-platform (Table 3); particle size distribution, pH, organic carbon, cation exchange capacity, and base saturation for all horizons (Table 4).

## Methodological Notes

The investigation relied primarily on small test units (1 x 1 to 1.5 x 1 m), auger probes, and continuous cores rather than large-area excavation. This strategy minimized damage to the earthworks but limits spatial understanding of features, activity areas, and intra-site organization. Faunal analysis is based almost entirely on the Mound B basal midden (B-tr? and B-1), the only context with adequate preservation due to the shell-buffered pH; the extreme acidity (pH 4.0-5.0) of all other fill contexts destroyed organic remains. This means subsistence data may not be representative of all occupation periods. The seasonal indicators (fish otoliths, deer epiphyses, migratory bird remains) represent a small sample (39 seasonally diagnostic otoliths) from a single midden context, and the authors appropriately caution that all-season representation does not necessarily demonstrate permanent year-round residence. The radiocarbon dating program produced 27 dates, of which 18 were accepted; the authors document their rejection criteria in detail and demonstrate stratigraphic ordering within individual mounds to validate the dating.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Poverty Point (Gibson 2000, 2001; Kidder 2002)**: Watson Brake predates Poverty Point by ~2,000 years. Both are regionally dominant sites with ecotone locations, clean central enclosures/plazas, and hunter-gatherer economies. Key differences: Poverty Point has massive long-distance exchange, Watson Brake has none; Poverty Point is an order of magnitude larger; the 1,300-year gap shows no cultural continuity.
- **Hamilton 1999**: Saunders et al. directly engage and reject Hamilton's bet-hedging/wasteful behavior hypothesis, arguing construction occurred during stable conditions between ENSO pulses, not during environmental crises.
- **Clark 2004; Sassaman and Heckenberger 2004**: Two competing models for the geometric design of Middle Archaic mound complexes. Clark argues for shared astronomical/geometric design; Sassaman and Heckenberger argue mounds were not standardized for astronomical purposes due to varied orientations. The paper finds partial support for both.
- **Jackson and Scott 2001**: Faunal analysis of Mound B midden, the primary dataset for subsistence reconstruction; data presented in Table 7.
- **Johnson 2000**: Lithic analysis from Watson Brake providing the artifact data in Tables 5 and 6.
- **Dunnell 1989**: Hamilton's bet-hedging model is derived from Dunnell's application of evolutionary theory to archaeology. Saunders et al.'s rejection of the bet-hedging hypothesis for Watson Brake is relevant to any extension of this framework to Poverty Point.
- **Sampson and Saunders 2005**: Companion paper correlating Middle Archaic mound dates with ENSO chronology from Laguna Pallcacocha.
- **Ford 1956; Webb 1982**: Earlier Poverty Point-focused references; the 1,300-year moundbuilding hiatus between Watson Brake and Poverty Point documented by Saunders et al. challenges any assumption of cultural continuity.
- **Jackson 1986**: Jackson's work on hunter-gatherer sedentism in the Lower Mississippi Valley during the Poverty Point period provides context for the seasonal occupation patterns documented at Watson Brake.

## Verification Notes

All claims were extracted directly from the PDF. Page numbers correspond to the *American Antiquity* 70(4) pagination (pp. 631-668). The abstract (p. 631) claims "all-season occupation," but the detailed discussion (p. 661) qualifies this: "it would be premature to suppose that it reflects year-round (permanent) residence here." The claims file reflects this more cautious assessment. The authors' rejection of Hamilton's bet-hedging model (p. 664) is based on correlation between Watson Brake dates and the Laguna Pallcacocha ENSO record; this correlation has not been independently verified for the Lower Mississippi Valley and depends on the regional applicability of an Ecuadorian proxy record. The count of Middle Archaic mound sites (13 in Louisiana, 1 in Mississippi) was current as of 2005 and may have changed since publication. The PDF is confirmed present in the repository at `docs/references/pdfs/Saunders_etal_2005.pdf`.
