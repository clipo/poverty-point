## Relevance to Argument

Hays (2018) argues that Poverty Point functioned as a multiethnic aggregation center where periodic, large-scale ritualized feasting was a primary activity. This interpretation directly supports the costly signaling framework in three ways. First, the paper provides quantitative comparative data (Table 1) demonstrating that Poverty Point's artifact assemblage exceeds contemporaneous sites by orders of magnitude, reinforcing the argument that a special explanatory mechanism is required. Second, experimental evidence compiled from multiple researchers (Pierce 1998; McGee 1995; Hunter 1975) demonstrates that standardized PPO forms confer no cooking performance advantage over simple clay lumps, meaning the extra labor invested in elaborate PPO shapes served social rather than utilitarian purposes; this non-functional elaboration is structurally analogous to costly signaling. Third, the geographic patterning of PPO types across sites (biconicals associated with northern regions, cross-grooved and cylindrical-grooved with the Lower Mississippi Valley) supports the interpretation that PPO forms encoded group identity, which would have been useful for distinguishing social units during aggregation events. The paper's emphasis on feasting as the social mechanism that drew populations together and supported communal labor projects (earthwork construction) provides the behavioral context within which monument-based and exotic-goods-based signaling would operate.

## Data Presented

Table 1 (p. 2) provides comparative artifact counts between Poverty Point, Indian Knoll, and Riverton culture sites across 14 artifact categories, demonstrating Poverty Point's order-of-magnitude advantage in all categories. Table 3 (p. 10) presents detailed PPO type distributions across multiple Poverty Point culture sites (Poverty Point, Claiborne, Jaketown, Lake Enterprise Mound, Terral Lewis, and others), with counts for nine PPO categories (biconical, biconical grooved, cylindrical grooved, cross grooved, melon, melon with end grooves, spheroid, biscuit, amorphous). These tables provide primary quantitative data for modeling signal intensity and calibrating aggregation site characteristics. The paper does not present new excavation data or original analyses; it synthesizes published counts from Webb (1982), Gibson (1973, 2001), Ford and Webb (1956), and Connolly (2001).

## Methodological Notes

The paper is primarily a synthetic interpretive argument rather than a report of new fieldwork or analysis. Hays draws on Hayden's (2014) feasting typology to classify Poverty Point's social organization as transegalitarian and applies Conkey's (1980) aggregation-site model to explain PPO type diversity patterns. The feasting argument rests on indirect indicators (PPOs as proxies for large-scale cooking, presence of serving vessels and ritual paraphernalia, earthwork labor mobilization) because direct food remains are poorly preserved in the acidic loessal soils at Poverty Point. The experimental evidence cited for PPO non-functionality comes from Pierce (1998), McGee (1995), and Hunter (1975), not from experiments conducted by the author. The paper is published in *Southeastern Archaeology*, a peer-reviewed journal of the Southeastern Archaeological Conference.
