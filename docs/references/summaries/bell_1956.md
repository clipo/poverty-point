## Relevance to Argument

Documents the first reported copper artifact from Poverty Point, a native copper plummet perforated for suspension, confirming that materials from the Great Lakes region (approximately 1,600 km distant) reached the site. The artifact's form, designed to be worn or hung, is consistent with a display or signaling function rather than a utilitarian one, supporting the interpretation that exotic materials at Poverty Point served as costly signals of access to long-distance exchange networks.

## Data Presented

- Single artifact: one native copper plummet
- Measurements: 48 mm length, 20 mm diameter
- Manufacturing technique: cold-hammered from a copper nugget (inferred)
- Photograph provided (Fig. 1), captioned "Length 1 7/8 inches"
- No chemical or metallurgical analysis
- No excavation data; surface find from a private collection

## Methodological Notes

- The artifact is a surface find with no stratigraphic context, so its temporal placement within the Poverty Point occupation sequence cannot be confirmed independently.
- Spatial provenience is approximate, based on the collector's recollection 12 years after the find (1944 to 1956 publication).
- The artifact was in a private collection (James Durham, Oklahoma City), not an institutional repository. Its current whereabouts are not documented in this note.
- The identification as "native copper" and the manufacturing inference ("probably hammered") rest on visual inspection rather than compositional analysis.
- As a single specimen, it cannot support claims about the frequency or volume of copper at Poverty Point; it establishes only presence.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Haag and Webb 1953** (in collection): Directly cited by Bell as the source establishing that copper was previously unreported at Poverty Point. Haag and Webb's artifact inventory for Poverty Point (p. 245) lists hematite plummets, galena, quartz crystals, and steatite but no copper, confirming Bell's claim of novelty.
- **Lipo et al. 2012** (in collection): Reinterprets groundstone plummets (hematite/magnetite) as loom weights rather than fishing weights. Bell's copper plummet is a different artifact class (metal, smaller, perforated for suspension) but belongs to the broader category of "plummets" at Poverty Point. The copper plummet's perforation and small size (48 mm) make it less likely to be a loom weight and more consistent with a pendant or ornament.
- **Blackburn 2003** (in collection): Discusses magnetite and hematite use at Poverty Point. Copper represents an additional exotic mineral material in the assemblage, distinct from but complementary to the iron oxide minerals Blackburn documents.
- **Webb 1968, Webb 1982** (in collection): Later comprehensive treatments of Poverty Point culture and site that would incorporate Bell's copper find into the broader material inventory.
