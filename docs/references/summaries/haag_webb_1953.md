## Relevance to Argument

This foundational short paper contributes to the costly signaling argument in three ways. First, it provides the earliest comprehensive inventory of the Poverty Point artifact assemblage, including multiple exotic materials (steatite, hematite, galena, quartz crystal, red jasper) that document the extensive long-distance exchange networks central to the signaling model. Second, it presents early stratigraphic evidence that the massive mounds at Poverty Point (70 feet / ~21 m) and the Motley site (51 feet / ~15.5 m) were constructed during prepottery times, establishing hunter-gatherer monumental construction in the lower Mississippi Valley. Third, it documents the regional extent of the Poverty Point cultural complex across sites in Louisiana, Mississippi, and Arkansas, supporting the interpretation of a regional interaction network centered on Poverty Point as an aggregation hub. The paper also records intra-site spatial patterning at Poverty Point Plantation, with microblade production along earthwork ridges and cooking activities in separate areas, suggesting organized activity zones within the site.

## Data Presented

No systematic quantitative datasets. The paper presents morphometric measurements for two microblade tool types (perforators and flake knives) with ranges and means but no standard deviations or sample sizes. Observations about artifact frequencies and spatial distributions are qualitative ("by the thousands," "in profusion," "comparatively scarce"). Stratigraphic observations at the Motley mound and Mound A are described in narrative form without scaled profiles. Mound heights are given (70 feet for Mound A, 51 feet for Motley mound). Raw material identification for microblade cores (tan chert stream pebbles) is noted without petrographic analysis.

## Methodological Notes

This is a four-page note in *American Antiquity* combining results from fieldwork at multiple sites (Jaketown, Poverty Point Plantation, Insley, Calion) conducted between 1948 and 1952. The observations represent a mix of systematic excavation (trenches at Jaketown), profile examination (Motley mound, Mound A), and surface collection (Poverty Point Plantation). No formal sampling strategy is described for artifact recovery or spatial analysis. The microblade morphometrics provide useful baseline measurements, though the lack of sample sizes limits their statistical utility. The stratigraphic observations are descriptive rather than measured. Despite these limitations, both authors (Haag as an academic archaeologist and Webb as a physician-archaeologist with extensive field experience) were careful observers, and their empirical claims have generally been confirmed by subsequent research.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Depends on Phillips, Ford, and Griffin 1951 for the initial Jaketown excavations establishing the Poverty Point cultural horizon.
- Ford's 1952 aerial photographs of Poverty Point, mentioned here, became the basis for subsequent site mapping and the recognition of the concentric ridge plan.
- Mound construction evidence anticipates and is confirmed by Ortmann and Kidder 2013 on Mound A construction, including basket-loading techniques.
- The exotic material inventory is consistent with and elaborated by Gibson 2000 and later compilations of the Poverty Point exchange network.
- The distinction between local raw materials for utilitarian microblades and exotic materials for display objects connects to the costly signaling framework: utilitarian production relies on local resources while signaling objects require costly long-distance procurement.
- Jaketown is discussed further in Ford, Phillips, and Haag 1955; site comparisons are relevant to understanding the hierarchy of Poverty Point period sites.
- The regional site distribution data (Insley, Calion) contributes to understanding the network of secondary sites that may have participated in aggregation events at Poverty Point.

## Verification Notes

Claims were extracted from a scanned PDF (JSTOR) with journal pagination pp. 245-248. Page numbers are reliable. The authors' identification of Poverty Point as a prepottery horizon has been confirmed by subsequent radiocarbon dating and stratigraphic analysis. Their mound height estimates (70 feet for Mound A) correspond closely to modern measurements (~22 m). The "revolutionary idea" of prepottery mound building, initially met with skepticism, is now the established interpretation. The assumption of "prepottery agriculture" (p. 248) is incorrect by modern understanding; Poverty Point people were hunter-gatherers, which strengthens rather than weakens the costly signaling argument since it means monumental construction occurred without an agricultural subsistence base.
