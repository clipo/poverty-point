## Relevance to Argument

This paper fundamentally complicates the prevailing assumption that Poverty Point's concentric ridges were built rapidly according to a single, detailed plan. Through the most extensive geophysical survey at the site (25.47 ha of magnetic gradient coverage), the authors identify 36 timber circles and 64 ridge construction components that reveal an iterative, modular construction history with episodes of building, use, repair, rebuilding, and possible deconstruction spanning the full occupation sequence (ca. 1650-1100 BC). For the costly signaling model, these findings are significant in several ways. First, the evidence for sustained, episodic construction rather than a single massive labor event suggests that monument building was a recurring costly behavior rather than a one-time investment, consistent with a signaling system maintained over generations. Second, the hypothesis that innermost ridges were built first and outer ridges added later in response to increasing aggregation scale connects construction sequence to population dynamics. Third, the 36 timber circles in the plaza area, interpreted as communal/ritual facilities with evidence of repeated rebuilding, provide architectural evidence for the kinds of repeated group activities (feasting, ceremony) expected in a signaling system. Fourth, evidence for possible deconstruction and reuse of ridge fill complicates simple volumetric labor estimates.

## Data Presented

The paper presents results from a 25.47-ha magnetic gradient survey conducted over 13 years (2006-2019) using Geoscan Research FM256 gradiometers in 20 x 20 m blocks at 0.5 m transect intervals with 8 data values per linear meter. Complementary magnetic susceptibility data from 2.5- and 5-cm-diameter soil cores and downhole in situ measurements were used to ground-truth the gradient results. Small-area (2 m^2) hand excavations were conducted at four timber circles (10, 12, 14, 26). Twelve radiocarbon determinations calibrated with Calib Rev 8.20 are presented in Table 1 (six from timber circle features, two from the Causeway/Mound E Ridge, two from Mound F, one from Mound E Ramp). Figure 12 provides a comprehensive map of all 64 ridge components with labeled identifiers. Downhole susceptibility transects across ridges SW-1 and SW-2 documented the subsurface anatomy of the negative perimeter anomalies.

## Methodological Notes

The magnetic gradient survey is methodologically robust and represents the first large-area geophysical investigation of the Poverty Point site. The identification of timber circles as circular magnetic anomaly complexes is well supported by targeted excavations that confirmed postholes at the predicted locations. However, several limitations should be noted. First, the survey covered only approximately 15% of the 163-ha site, leaving large areas uninvestigated (particularly the wooded portions of ridges and the northern and eastern sectors). Second, the 64 ridge components are identified through negative perimeter anomalies interpreted as intentional architectural features marking ridge edges prior to construction; while this interpretation is supported by the two tested locations (ridges SW-1 and SW-2), it has not been verified across the full range of components. Third, the radiocarbon dates from timber circle postholes are from fill deposits that "are unlikely to represent a primary deposit" (p. 198), making the dates compatible with but not definitively attributable to circle construction or use. Fourth, the authors acknowledge that the actual number of timber circles and ridge components is "likely significantly greater" than documented because "geophysical surveys often detect only a portion of the cultural features and deposits that are actually present" (p. 207).

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Britt et al. 2002**: Earlier, smaller geophysical survey of the southwest sector that documented three circular anomalies on Ridge 1 (10-15 m diameter), which are likely among the 36 circles now documented in the larger survey.
- **Ford and Webb 1956**: Original site documentation; Hargrave et al. cite their excavation profiles showing "flank midden" on ridge slopes and thin horizontal surfaces of fired earth PPOs suggesting brief occupations during construction.
- **Gibson 2001, 2019**: Provides competing hypothesis that outermost ridges are earliest and innermost are latest; Hargrave et al. argue the reverse based on number of ridge components.
- **Haag 1990**: Earlier plaza excavations (xu2, xu4) that documented 104 postholes; three of Hargrave et al.'s circles (8, 9, 10) intersect Haag's excavation units, providing context for his posthole patterns.
- **Kidder 2002**: Systematic topographic mapping revealing five ridges (not six); Hargrave et al. document that ridges 1 and 2 are actually composite features.
- **Kidder and Grooms 2025**: Both papers challenge the assumption of rapid, planned construction; Kidder and Grooms emphasize rapid but late construction, while Hargrave et al. emphasize iterative and modular construction over time.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: Mound A built in months; Hargrave et al. note it may not be appropriate to assume the ridges and plaza similarly reached their final forms rapidly (p. 206).
- **Sassaman 2005**: Documents multi-scalar spatial organization; the timber circles and ridge components add new layers to the architectural complexity Sassaman describes.
