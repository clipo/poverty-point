## Relevance to Argument

This dissertation provides the most comprehensive stratigraphic, chronological, and constructional analysis of the Poverty Point mounds. Its findings are foundational for the costly signaling model in three respects. First, micromorphological evidence demonstrates that Mound A was constructed rapidly, probably within a few months, requiring coordination of a large labor force. This rapid, collective, visible investment is the core prediction of the signaling framework. Second, the mound summits show no evidence of domestic or utilitarian activities, distinguishing them from the ridges and supporting the interpretation that the act of construction itself, not subsequent use, was the primary social function. Third, despite the massive scale and planned character of the earthworks, the archaeological record contains no evidence of social hierarchy, differential resource access, or coercive leadership. This combination of rapid monumental construction, non-utilitarian function, and absent hierarchy is precisely the paradox that costly signaling theory resolves without invoking chiefdom-level organization.

## Data Presented

- Stratigraphic profiles and construction sequence analyses for Mounds A, B, C, and E
- Volume estimates: Mound A 178,000-238,000 m^3; Mound A terrace 19,300 m^3; Mound B ~5,000 m^3
- Dimensions: Mound A ~200 x 200 m; Mound B ~60-62 m diameter, ~6 m height; Mound E ~100 x 110 m, 3.5-4 m height
- Mound B stage thickness: three stages, each ~1.22 m
- Mound C: 13 thin platforms plus conical cap
- 56 radiocarbon/AMS dates, 2 OSL dates, 2 TL dates compiled from Poverty Point
- 19 radiocarbon/AMS dates from ridges with <100-year errors
- AMS dates from Mound A submound midden: 3020 +/- 40 b.p. and 3040 +/- 40 b.p.
- Micromorphological analysis of basket-load contacts in Mound A Stage II
- Sediment composition data from multiple mounds (color, texture, source identification)
- Artifact presence/absence data from mound summit surfaces vs. ridge middens

## Methodological Notes

- The dissertation synthesizes stratigraphic excavation data with micromorphological analysis (Kidder et al. 2006; Arco 2006) to establish construction tempo. The micromorphological evidence for rapid construction is among the strongest available, showing no weathering at the interface between individual basket loads.
- Radiocarbon dates are compiled from multiple sources and research campaigns spanning decades, introducing potential inter-laboratory variability.
- The conservative chronological range (1769-980 cal. B.C.) is broader than most commonly cited ranges; the narrower estimates depend on which dates and error thresholds are accepted.
- Mound D is demonstrated to be post-Poverty Point, which corrects labor estimates in earlier literature that include five mounds.
- Volume estimates vary significantly by method (Shenkel 1986 vs. Kidder et al. 2006), and the 238,000 m^3 figure for Mound A includes the entire feature. The platform alone is a subset of this total.
- The sedentary vs. seasonal occupation question is flagged as unresolved; the dissertation does not provide new faunal seasonality data to address it.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Kidder et al. 2006** (cited extensively): Provides the micromorphological data that Ortmann draws on for rapid construction arguments; supplies the 238,000 m^3 volume estimate for Mound A.
- **Ford and Webb 1956** (cited extensively): Original Poverty Point excavation report; Ortmann refines and corrects their interpretations, including the Mound B height estimate (originally 10-12 m, now ~6 m) and ridge construction sequence.
- **Gibson 2000** (cited extensively): Gibson's site monograph; Ortmann engages with Gibson's narrower construction chronology (1600-1300 cal. B.C.) and "beneficent obligation" model for labor organization (Gibson 2004).
- **Jackson 1986** (in collection): Provides faunal evidence and the argument for seasonal occupation over sedentism; supports aggregation model.
- **Jackson 1991** (cited): Trade fair model for Poverty Point; anticipates the aggregation-based signaling framework.
- **Connolly 2002/2003** (cited): Provides the broader 1700-1100 cal. B.C. chronological range and documents staged ridge construction with intervening midden.
- **Shenkel 1986** (cited): Lower volume estimate for Mound A (178,000 m^3), contrasting with Kidder et al.'s 238,000 m^3.
- **Kidder and Sassaman 2007** (cited): Argues social inequality was prerequisite for earthwork construction; Ortmann notes they offer no archaeological criteria to identify such inequality.
- **Saunders et al. 2001** (cited): Dates Lower Jackson Mound ~2,000 years before Poverty Point, establishing deep landscape history.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013** (in CLAUDE.md): Later publication by the same author building on this dissertation's findings about Mound A construction.
