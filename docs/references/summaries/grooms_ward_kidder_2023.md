## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides the most current Bayesian chronology for the Jaketown site and, by extension, fundamentally reframes the temporal and causal structure of the Poverty Point phenomenon. The traditional culture-historical model treats Poverty Point as the type-site and origin of a radially diffusing cultural complex. Grooms et al. demonstrate through AMS dating and OxCal 4.4 Bayesian modeling that Jaketown was occupied with PPOs and imported lithics by c. 4500-4000 cal BP, at least 400 years before the earliest dates at the Poverty Point site (c. 3600 cal BP). Mound construction at Jaketown (c. 3400 cal BP) also predates the major construction episodes at Poverty Point. The paper reinterprets Poverty Point as an endpoint of cultural convergence rather than a center of innovation.

For the costly signaling model, this convergence framework is critical. It supports the treatment of Poverty Point as an aggregation site where diverse groups with independent histories converged, rather than a polity that organized peripheral sites. The chronological evidence shows that exchange networks and shared material culture practices (PPOs, imported lithics, soapstone) preceded monumental construction by centuries, suggesting that signaling through exchange enabled and preceded monumental signaling. The absence of hierarchy despite massive coordinated construction at the convergence point is precisely the puzzle the signaling model resolves: costly signaling provides the coordination mechanism that hierarchy-based models cannot identify in the archaeological record.

## Data Presented

The paper presents a four-phase Bayesian chronological model for Jaketown built from 26 AMS radiocarbon dates using OxCal 4.4 and the IntCal20 calibration curve. The model is constrained by stratigraphic relationships from four re-excavated units (J100, J101, J103, and Trench 1) and three cores. Phase boundaries are reported at both 1 sigma and 2 sigma confidence. Key chronological outputs: Phase 1 start at 4570-3820 cal BP (2 sigma), Phase 2 start at 3585-3395 cal BP (2 sigma), Phase 3 start at 3425-3365 cal BP (2 sigma), Phase 4 start at 3245-2455 cal BP (2 sigma). The chronological model is presented in Figure 4 (multi-plot view) with stratigraphic profiles in Figures 5, 7, and 8. Artifact data include PPOs, novaculite flakes sourced to central Arkansas, soapstone from southern Appalachia (dated by carbon residue to 3565-3395 cal BP), and deer bone from feasting contexts.

## Methodological Notes

The paper's methodological strength lies in its Bayesian chronological modeling with well-documented stratigraphic control. The 2018-2019 re-excavation of previously investigated units allowed direct comparison with earlier results while obtaining new AMS dates from secure contexts. The exclusion of six dates (including all five Ford and Webb 1950s dates) is justified by established outlier detection protocols (Bronk Ramsey 2009b). All posterior density estimates are reported at both 1 sigma and 2 sigma, following best practice (Bayliss et al. 2007). A limitation is that the model applies to Jaketown alone; the authors explicitly do not attempt to build a comparable Bayesian model for the Poverty Point type-site, instead relying on published estimates (c. 3600-3000 cal BP). The gap between Phase 1 and Phase 2 (roughly 500-600 years) is not discussed, and it is unclear whether this reflects a genuine occupational hiatus or a sampling gap. The paper also does not assess agreement indices for the overall model, though individual date agreements are shown in Figure 4.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Kidder and Grooms 2025**: Extends this work by situating Poverty Point as a revitalization site within the convergence framework. Shares the same refined chronology and adds the argument that Poverty Point earthworks were built during the terminal phase as a response to environmental stress.
- **Kidder 2006**: Provides the paleoclimate context for Jaketown Phase 4 (post-flood): the catastrophic flooding between c. 3300 and 2780 cal BP that buried Jaketown is the same event documented by Kidder (2006) as causing the Late Archaic to Woodland transition.
- **Kidder et al. 2021**: The rapid construction of Ridge West 3 at Poverty Point parallels the rapid mound construction documented at Jaketown, suggesting this was a regional pattern rather than unique to the type-site.
- **Ward et al. 2022**: Companion paper by the same research group providing the material culture and foodways analysis that supports the cultural distinction between Jaketown and Poverty Point. Provides the feasting interpretation and seasonality data cited in this paper.
- **Sassaman 2005**: The convergence model builds on Sassaman's argument that Poverty Point represents the recapitulation of multiple strands of Native American history. Grooms et al. provide the chronological evidence that Sassaman's framework requires.
- **Saunders et al. 2005**: Watson Brake's millennium-long hiatus before Poverty Point provides the temporal context for Jaketown's "revival" of monumentality after c. 3400 cal BP.
- **Ortmann 2007; Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: Provide the construction chronology and labor estimates for Poverty Point mounds against which the Jaketown construction dates are compared.
- **Hays 2018**: PPOs found in Phase 1 at Jaketown connect to the broader debate about PPO function and social encoding; the early date pushes the origin of this practice back considerably.
- **Jackson 1986**: Jackson's model of Poverty Point as seasonal aggregation with social network extension through exchange is supported by the convergence model's emphasis on diverse groups coming together.
