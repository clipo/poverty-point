**Full citation**: Kidder, T.R., Henry, E.R., & Arco, L.J. 2018. Rapid climate change-induced collapse of hunter-gatherer societies in the lower Mississippi River valley between ca. 3300 and 2780 cal yr BP. *Science China Earth Sciences* 61:178-189. DOI: 10.1007/s11430-017-9128-8

## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides the most precise chronological data for the catastrophic flood event that terminated Poverty Point culture. Using Bayesian modeling of 16 radiocarbon dates from the Jaketown site in the Yazoo Basin, the authors establish that a crevasse splay from the Mississippi River buried the Poverty Point occupation at Jaketown around 3310 cal BP (median), and that the landscape was not reoccupied until approximately 2780 cal BP, a gap of roughly 530 years. The flood resulted from the Mississippi River's lateral migration from its Stage 2 to Stage 1 course, driven by climate-induced changes in atmospheric circulation and increased precipitation. For the costly signaling model, this paper is critical in four respects: (1) it narrows the date of the environmental shock that collapsed the signaling system to approximately 3310 cal BP, more precise than the 3000-2600 cal BP range in Kidder (2006); (2) it demonstrates that the disruption was catastrophic, not gradual, with a crevasse deposit of 60-75 cm burying occupation surfaces; (3) it documents that the social complexity of Poverty Point did not reconstitute after the flood, with Early Woodland reoccupation showing no monumental architecture, minimal trade, and reduced artifact diversity; and (4) it interprets the monumental earthworks as "landesque capital" that anchored mobile populations to specific places, the physical destruction of which severed the social connections that sustained the signaling system.

## Data Presented

The paper presents 16 radiocarbon dates from the Jaketown site (Table 1), calibrated using OxCal 4.2 with IntCal13. These are used to construct a two-phase sequential Bayesian chronological model (Table 2; Figure 4) with high agreement indices (A_model = 97.9; A_overall = 98). Phase 1 encompasses the Poverty Point occupation (pre-flood), and Phase 2 encompasses the Early Woodland reoccupation (post-flood). The model yields posterior probability ranges for four boundary events: start and end of PP occupation, and start and end of post-flood EW occupation. Stratigraphic data from excavation units J101 and J102 (Figure 3) and extensive coring document the crevasse splay deposit separating the two phases. A LiDAR map of the Jaketown site (Figure 2b) shows mound locations and the approximate boundaries of the crevasse splay. A regional map (Figure 1) shows the geographic relationship between Jaketown and Poverty Point within the Yazoo and Tensas basins.

## Methodological Notes

The paper's strength lies in its integration of stratigraphic evidence with Bayesian chronological modeling. The two-phase sequential model is well-suited to the data because the stratigraphic separation between pre-flood and post-flood contexts is unambiguous. The high agreement indices provide confidence in the modeled boundaries. However, several caveats apply: (1) the 3310 cal BP date is a median, and the 95.4% probability range for the end of PP occupation extends from 3400 to 3140 cal BP; (2) the model assumes the flood event is the boundary between phases, which is supported by stratigraphy but is still a modeling assumption; (3) the Jaketown chronology may not directly apply to Poverty Point or other sites, though the authors note that their dates are "similar to ones reported from the Poverty Point site" (p. 187). The paper explicitly acknowledges that "chronological models in archaeology should not be viewed as final interpretations of the past" but rather as "evolving understandings of past sequences of events" (p. 185).

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Kidder 2006**: The earlier paper established the 400-year chronological gap (3150-2700 cal BP) using Bayesian modeling of dates from across the Mississippi basin. The 2018 paper refines this with site-specific data from Jaketown, yielding a 530-year gap (3310-2780 cal BP) and a more precise flood onset date.
- **Kidder and Grooms 2025**: Extends the argument by interpreting Poverty Point as a revitalization site responding to the environmental instability documented here. The 2025 paper's refined chronology (3535-3135 cal BP) for Poverty Point is consistent with the Jaketown flood onset of 3310 cal BP.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: Cited for the 235,000 m^3 Mound A volume and rapid construction timeline. The rapid construction at Poverty Point takes on additional significance if the site was responding to the same environmental instability that eventually destroyed Jaketown.
- **Shuman and Marsicek 2016**: Provides the independent paleoclimate proxy data (drought at 3.0-2.4 ka, temperature-moisture correlation r = -0.90) that corroborates the climate forcing mechanism invoked here.
- **Shuman 2024**: Cool/wet event at 3400-3100 yr BP corresponds to late Poverty Point and is consistent with the increased precipitation that drove the Mississippi River flooding documented here.
- **Kidder et al. 2008**: Earlier paper by some of the same authors that hypothesized the flood was responsible for the demise of Poverty Point culture; this 2018 paper provides improved chronological precision.
- **Webb 1982**: Cited for the 128,000 km^2 spatial extent of Poverty Point culture and core site parameters.
- **Saucier 1994**: Provides the geomorphological framework for the Stage 2 to Stage 1 Mississippi River transition that caused the flooding.

## Verification Notes

Claims were extracted from the PDF (paginated pp. 178-189 in *Science China Earth Sciences* 61(2)). All quantitative values (radiocarbon dates, Bayesian model results, site dimensions, volume estimates) were verified against Tables 1-2 and the text. The 3310 cal BP date for the flood onset is consistently stated as the "calculated median for the end of the Poverty Point occupation at Jaketown" (p. 187) and matches the median value in Table 2. The 2780 cal BP reoccupation date matches Table 2. The 530-year abandonment figure (p. 186) equals the difference between these medians (3310-2780 = 530). The 675-year occupation duration (p. 186) equals the difference between start and end medians (3985-3310 = 675). No discrepancies were identified between the text and the tables.
