## Relevance to Argument

Wiessner 1982 is the foundational statement of the four-strategy risk-reduction typology (prevention, transfer, storage, pooling) that subsequent literature on hunter-gatherer mobility, exchange, and aggregation built on. For the Poverty Point project, the relevant strategy is pooling: the social distribution of risk through reciprocal sharing networks, of which Ju/'hoansi hxaro is the canonical instance. The paper does not contain quantitative empirical data; the !Kung figures reside in Wiessner's 1977 dissertation, which the comment references rather than reproduces. The audit purpose for this entry was to test whether Wiessner 1982 contains the "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" figure earlier drafts of Paper 1 attributed to Wiessner. It does not. This is now the third audit (Hawkes 2000, Wiessner 2002, Wiessner 1982) confirming that the percentage is not in any Wiessner-paper currently in the project repo and most likely originates in the 1977 dissertation or in secondary-literature paraphrase.

## Data Presented

- **No primary quantitative data**. The paper is a theoretical comment.
- **Cross-cultural ethnographic comparison**: !Kung San, !Ko San, Pitjandjara, Birhor, Pygmies, Kwakiutl, Nunamiut, Peel River Kutchin, Alaskan Eskimo, Ainu, Ammassilik Inuit. Used to ground the typology and the predicted material correlates.
- **Reference data**: Wiessner 1977 dissertation cited for !Kung hxaro spatial distribution, with the qualitative finding that exchange ties favor complementary distant partners over similar proximate ones.

## Methodological Notes

- The paper's mode is theoretical-typological. Predictions about archaeological correlates are sketched rather than tested against any single archaeological case.
- Empirical claims about !Kung exchange ties are reported indirectly through citation to the dissertation; the paper does not give quantitative figures.
- The paper sets up archaeological hypotheses (camp openness, faunal-distribution regularity, stylistic differentiation) that subsequent work has attempted to operationalize.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Direct interlocutor**: Binford 1980 ("Willow Smoke and Dogs' Tails," *American Antiquity* 45). The title of Wiessner's comment is a direct response.
- **Cited references relevant to the Poverty Point project**:
  - Sahlins 1972 — generalized reciprocity as pooling
  - Suttles 1968; Piddocke 1969 — Northwest Coast risk transfer (potlatch)
  - Yellen 1977a, 1977b — !Kung camp plans and butchering rules
  - Ingold 1980 — same-environment-different-organization argument
  - Wiessner 1977 — primary source for !Kung hxaro figures
  - Wiessner 1980a, 1980b — companion theoretical and projectile-point papers
- **Cited by**: This paper is among the most-cited hunter-gatherer theoretical pieces; the four-strategy typology is canonical. The Wiessner 2002 paper extends the empirical record on hxaro through a 34-year longitudinal dataset and supersedes the 1982 paper's reference-only treatment of !Kung quantitative detail.
