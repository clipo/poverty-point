## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides rare direct subsistence evidence from a Poverty Point period peripheral site (J.W. Copes, 13km south-southeast of the Poverty Point type site), where exceptional organic preservation allows faunal and floral analysis. The findings bear on several components of the costly signaling model: (1) the resource base that supported aggregation and monumental construction was dominated by deer and fish, consistent with a specialized dual-resource procurement strategy in bottomland environments; (2) seasonal indicators collectively suggest possible year-round occupation rather than purely seasonal use, complicating a simple seasonal-aggregation model; (3) even small peripheral sites participated in the long-distance exchange network (galena from Missouri), indicating that exotic goods circulated throughout the settlement system, not just at the main site; and (4) the ecotone positioning of the site (natural levee adjacent to oxbow lake/swamp, surrounded by bottomland hardwoods) supports the ecotone diversity hypothesis for site location.

## Data Presented

The paper reports primary faunal and floral data from test excavations at J.W. Copes:

- **Table 1** (p. 81): Poverty Point Object inventory from the test unit. 980 total artifacts; 136 identifiable PPO specimens plus 844 unidentified fragments. Melon-shaped forms dominate (50), followed by grooved (21), cross-grooved melon (8), biscuit (8), biconical (6), amorphous (5), and cylindrical (2).
- **Table 2** (p. 82): Faunal inventory from hand-collected excavation. 142 identifiable elements. Deer: 41 elements; catfish species combined: ~50 elements; smaller contributions from raccoon, squirrel, swamp rabbit, mallard, crow, turtle.
- **Table 3** (p. 83): Faunal inventory from flotation samples. At least 10 fish taxa identified including gar, sunfish (Lepomis spp.), freshwater drum, bowfin, sucker (Catostomidae), catfish (Ictalurus sp.), striped bass (cf. Morone saxatilis), minnows (Cyprinidae), plus squirrel and raccoon.
- **Floral remains**: Chenopodium sp., hickory/pecan shell, acorn shell, cane, wild bean pod (cf. Strophostyle sp.), wood charcoal.

No radiocarbon dates are reported for the Poverty Point component at J.W. Copes. Chronological placement rests on PPO typology and comparison with the type site sequence.

## Methodological Notes

The faunal sample is small (142 identifiable elements from hand-collection), and the author acknowledges that a larger sample is needed to evaluate seasonality adequately. The year-round occupation interpretation rests on combining multiple lines of indirect seasonal evidence (fish annuli for summer, deer abundance for fall/winter, shed antler for spring, nut/seed remains for late summer/fall), each individually equivocal but collectively suggestive. The flotation recovery substantially increased fish species diversity relative to hand-collection alone, demonstrating recovery bias in the excavation method. The site's exceptional organic preservation results from sealing by later occupation deposits, making J.W. Copes atypical among Poverty Point period sites in the region.
