## Relevance to Argument

This chapter develops the bet-hedging explanation for Archaic mound building in the Lower Mississippi Valley, building on Dunnell (1989) and Hamilton (1999). It provides the most sustained published argument connecting environmental variability, the waste/bet-hedging model, and Poverty Point-era monumental construction. The paper distinguishes bet-hedging from costly signaling (arguing the former operates under environmental variability, the latter under stable conditions), a distinction the current project needs to address since our framework integrates signaling with environmental uncertainty. The chapter also proposes that Poverty Point represents a shift from household-level to community-level bet-hedging, given the unprecedented scale of construction and exotic goods procurement.

## Data Presented

1. **Table 1 (p. 264)**: Radiocarbon dates from secure Archaic-period mound contexts in the LMV drainage, with strict selection criteria (charcoal dates on mound contexts only, excluding sub-mound humate dates). Thirteen sites listed from Monte Sano (6570+/-60 RCYBP, earliest) to Helena Crossing (2100+/-75 RCYBP, latest/northernmost). Poverty Point listed at 3386+/-49 RCYBP (Ortmann 2010).
2. **Figure 6 (p. 265)**: Plot of radiocarbon dates vs. UTM northing showing the south-to-north spread of mound building.
3. **Figure 4 (p. 259)**: Radiocarbon dates from Archaic mounds juxtaposed with ENSO pulses (from Sampson 2008), used to counter Sampson's claim that mound building correlates with climate stability.
4. **Figure 5 (p. 260)**: LMV radiocarbon dates showing three episodic peaks of mound construction (from Saunders 2010b).
5. **Paleoclimate narrative (pp. 260-262)**: Synthesis of Holocene landscape evolution in the LMV, drawing on Saucier (1974, 1994, 2001, 2002), Rittenour et al. (2007), Kidder et al. (2008), and others.

## Methodological Notes

The chapter applies Dunnell's (1989) evolutionary "waste" concept, treating mound building as a functional trait subject to natural selection rather than a marker of social complexity. The approach is explicitly selectionist: cultural traits are selected for under appropriate environmental conditions and selected against when conditions change. The authors distinguish their approach from both costly signaling theory (which they associate with stable environments) and traditional social complexity arguments (which they consider circular). Their radiocarbon date compilation (Table 1) uses strict criteria: only charcoal dates from mound contexts, excluding sub-mound samples and dates that violate stratigraphic order. This methodological rigor addresses problems in earlier compilations (e.g., Gibson 1994; Sampson 2008) where dates were uncritically accepted.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Dunnell 1989**: The foundational theoretical source for the "waste" concept; Peacock and Rafferty extend this to the Archaic mound building record
- **Dunnell & Greenlee 1999**: Applied the waste model to Hopewell; Peacock and Rafferty apply the same logic to earlier Archaic mounds
- **Hamilton 1999**: First application of bet-hedging specifically to Middle Archaic mound building; Peacock and Rafferty build on and defend this hypothesis
- **Madsen et al. 1999**: Simulation study demonstrating that wasteful populations have higher geometric mean fitness under environmental variability; provides the formal modeling basis
- **Aranyosi 1999**: Distinguished costly signaling (stable environments) from bet-hedging (variable environments); Peacock and Rafferty follow this distinction
- **Sampson 2008**: Critique that mound dates fall in climatically calm intervals; Peacock and Rafferty argue this reflects a misunderstanding of the waste model
- **Saunders et al. 2005**: Watson Brake paper that dismissed bet-hedging as untestable; Peacock and Rafferty counter that all agency-based explanations are equally untestable
- **Kidder et al. 2008**: Key source for LMV paleoclimate and settlement patterns; provides the environmental context for mound building
- **Ortmann 2010**: Source for Poverty Point's earliest mound date (3386+/-49 RCYBP)
- **Gibson 2006**: Argues for sedentariness at Poverty Point based on construction scale; Peacock and Rafferty treat sedentariness as a co-variant that strengthens selection for bet-hedging

## Verification Notes

- Claims extracted directly from the published chapter text; page numbers correspond to the printed pagination in the *Beyond Barrows* volume
- The authors explicitly discuss Poverty Point on pp. 254, 262, 264, 265-270, making this one of the few published sources that applies bet-hedging theory directly to the site
- The distinction between costly signaling and bet-hedging (p. 256) warrants scrutiny: the authors cite Aranyosi (1999) for the claim that costly signaling evolves in stable environments, but the current project argues signaling can be adaptive under uncertainty when it facilitates cooperation. This is a point of theoretical divergence, not necessarily contradiction
- Table 1 dates were compiled with strict criteria, but the authors acknowledge limitations (footnotes 1-2, p. 264): Monte Sano early dates are not universally accepted, and Helena Crossing and Batesville are post-Archaic sites included because they meet the dating criteria
- The Mound A volume of 238,500 m3 is attributed to Kidder et al. 2009; the 750,000 m3 figure for total Poverty Point earthmoving is attributed to Gibson (2006)
