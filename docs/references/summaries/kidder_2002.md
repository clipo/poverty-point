## Relevance to Argument

This paper is directly relevant to the costly signaling model in two ways. First, it provides revised empirical data on Poverty Point's physical layout, including mound dimensions and ridge counts, that feed into labor cost estimates. Second, and more importantly, it demonstrates that the site is less symmetrical and less rigidly planned than the canonical image (derived from Ford's 1954 map) suggested. The finding that the northern aisle does not exist and that ridges are discontinuous in several sectors supports a model of cooperative, decentralized construction by multiple groups rather than top-down direction by a centralized authority. This is consistent with a signaling framework in which aggregating bands voluntarily contribute labor without elite coercion.

## Data Presented

The paper presents results from a systematic topographic survey of the Poverty Point site conducted in winter 1999 and early summer 2000. The survey produced 10,385 data points using a total station laser transit, yielding the first comprehensive topographic map of the site. The resulting contour map reveals ridge geometry, mound dimensions, and the absence of the previously assumed northern aisle. Quantitative data include mound dimensions (Mound A: 21 m high, 195 x 216 m base; Mound B: 55 m diameter, 6 m high; Mound E: 100 m on a side, 2.5 m tall) and evidence for five rather than six ridges. The paper also includes comparison with aerial photographs and earlier maps (especially Ford 1954) to demonstrate how previous representations distorted the site plan.

## Methodological Notes

The topographic survey represents a major improvement over prior mapping but has limitations. The 10,385 data points, while extensive, still produce interpolated contours whose resolution depends on point spacing, which is not specified in the paper. The survey documents current topography, which reflects both original construction and subsequent modification by agriculture, erosion, and other post-depositional processes. The loss of Ridge 2 to modern land use illustrates this problem: the current surface cannot be assumed to represent the original constructed form. Kidder is appropriately cautious about what the map can and cannot tell us, explicitly stating that the data cannot resolve whether the site was built to a master plan.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Ford 1954**: The primary target of revision. Ford's map established the canonical image of Poverty Point as a symmetrical, planned community with six concentric ridges and multiple aisles. Kidder demonstrates that this image was partly an artifact of Ford's expectations.
- **Britt et al. 2002**: Their geophysical survey of the Southwest Sector independently confirmed ridge-and-swale architecture and provided precise ridge width measurements (20 m for Ridges 2-4, 35 m for Ridge 1), complementing Kidder's topographic data.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: Their later work on Mound A construction chronology builds on the dimensional data reported here (21 m height, 195 x 216 m base) and addresses the question of rapid versus incremental construction.
- **Gibson 2000**: Kidder's mapping revises interpretations presented in Gibson's site monograph, which relied on pre-2000 map data.
- **Webb 1982**: Previous artifact distribution studies based on the six-sector/six-ridge model are identified as needing reevaluation in light of the revised site plan.

## Verification Notes

All claims were extracted directly from the PDF. Page numbers correspond to the *American Antiquity* 67(1) pagination. The claim that Ridge 2 has been "almost completely obliterated in recent times" (p. 96) means the original ridge count could have been six, with destruction rather than absence explaining the discrepancy; Kidder acknowledges this ambiguity. The mound dimensions reported here (Mound A at 21 m high) differ slightly from some other sources that cite 22 m; this may reflect measurement differences or rounding.
