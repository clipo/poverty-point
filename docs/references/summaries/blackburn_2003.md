## Relevance to Argument

This article provides descriptive observations on the material properties of magnetite and hematite used in Poverty Point plummets, documenting the labor cost inherent in working these hard minerals and confirming their non-local origins. The data on material hardness, manufacturing techniques, and geographic distribution of plummet forms are useful for the costly signaling argument regarding exotic goods; the article's speculative compass hypothesis is unsupported and should not be used.

## Data Presented

No primary datasets or quantitative measurements are presented. The article offers qualitative observations drawn from the author's personal examination of plummet specimens from Mississippi Poverty Point sites. Standard mineralogical properties (Mohs hardness, chemical composition) are cited from reference sources. No sample sizes, provenience data, or systematic measurements are provided.

## Methodological Notes

This article was published in the *Central States Archaeological Journal*, an avocational/collector-oriented publication, not a peer-reviewed academic journal. The author, writing from French Camp, Mississippi, appears to be an amateur archaeologist or collector. Claims based on personal observation lack systematic documentation (no sample sizes, no provenience records, no photographs with scales). The compass hypothesis is built entirely on analogy (magnetite is magnetic, therefore it could have been used as a compass) without any supporting evidence. Source attributions for magnetite (Pennsylvania/Adirondacks) lack geochemical backing.

## Source Reliability Considerations

Low reliability for interpretive and speculative claims due to non-peer-reviewed publication venue and amateur author status. Descriptive observations about material properties (hardness, composition) are verifiable against standard mineralogical references and can be used. Observations about plummet forms and manufacturing techniques are plausible but lack the documentation standards expected of academic sources. This source should be used only to supplement more rigorous references, not as a primary authority.
