## Relevance to Argument

This paper reinterprets the function of groundstone plummets, the third most common artifact class in Late Archaic Lower Mississippi Valley assemblages, through geometric morphometric analysis. The finding that plummets were likely warp-weighted loom weights rather than fishing weights or bola stones has direct implications for the costly signaling framework. First, the exotic materials (hematite, magnetite) were selected for their physical density properties rather than for visual display, meaning the acquisition cost was driven by functional requirements for textile production rather than signaling per se. Second, the suggestion that textile production may have been a cooperative activity during seasonal aggregation adds a production-based benefit to the aggregation model alongside communal fishing, information exchange, and mating networks. Third, the possibility that finished textiles were exchanged through long-distance networks would reframe the exchange system from one importing raw display materials to one importing functional components and exporting finished goods. The paper's systematic rejection of traditional functional interpretations (fishing weights, bola, charm stones) on empirical grounds is methodologically rigorous, though the loom weight interpretation remains partially circumstantial because no loom remains have been recovered at Poverty Point.

## Data Presented

The paper presents morphometric data from 78 complete plummets (21 grooved, 56 perforated, 1 knobbed) examined at the Poverty Point State Historic Site museum and curation facility. Measurements include shape characterization relative to the haft centroid, three-dimensional laser scans for volume, surface roughness measures, and XRF analysis for source variability. The paper focuses on shape results; XRF findings are not reported in detail. A bootstrap analysis demonstrates the 78-specimen sample is representative at 99% confidence. The key quantitative finding is the asymmetric constraint on plummet dimensions: width variability relative to the haft is tightly constrained while length is free to vary, a pattern consistent across both grooved and perforated hafting types and across hematite and magnetite specimens.

## Methodological Notes

The analytical framework follows Dunnell's (1978) selectionist distinction between function and style, treating plummet shape as a functional trait under natural selection. The approach is hypothetico-deductive: each proposed function (fishing weight, bola, charm stone, loom weight) generates testable expectations regarding shape constraints, hafting robustness, use-wear patterns, and depositional context. Traditional hypotheses are rejected because they fail to account for the observed width constraint. The loom weight hypothesis uniquely explains all observed properties (marginal hafting, width constraint with length freedom, dense material selection, domestic deposition, temporal restriction, cache occurrence). The principal limitation is that this is an argument from consistency: the data are consistent with loom weights, but no direct evidence of looms (post holes, beam fragments, textile impressions on plummets) has been recovered from Poverty Point.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Webb 1968**: Primary source for plummet counts (2,300+), material proportions, and morphological descriptions at Poverty Point. The present paper's independent sample confirms Webb's material ratios.
- **Blackburn 2003**: Documents hardness and material properties of hematite and magnetite plummets; notes material-hafting correlations (drilled = magnetite, grooved = hematite) that complement the present paper's finding that shape constraints are independent of hafting type.
- **Bell 1956**: Documents a copper plummet from Poverty Point with similar morphology and perforation; copper's density (SG 8.9) would also function well as a loom weight.
- **Dunnell 1978**: Provides the selectionist theoretical framework (function vs. style distinction) that structures the analysis.
- **Ford and Webb 1956**: Documents cache deposits of plummets consistent with stored loom equipment.
- **Kuttruff et al. 1998**: Provides the earliest regional textile evidence from Arnold Research Cave, Missouri.
- **Strickland-Olsen et al. 1999**: Documents Archaic-age textile fragments from Avery Island, Louisiana.
- **Peabody 1901**: First suggested the loom weight interpretation, never previously tested.
- **Gibson 2000; Byrd 1991; Jackson 1991**: Referenced for long-distance exchange networks bringing exotic materials to Poverty Point.
