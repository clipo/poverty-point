## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides the most detailed published account of timber post circles at Poverty Point, a category of monumental construction previously unknown at the site until near-surface geophysical surveys detected them. The identification of up to 36 timber circles, their bimodal size distribution (small: 5-40 m; large: 45-65 m), and the evidence for repeated construction-demolition-rebuilding cycles are directly relevant to the costly signaling model. The timber circles represent labor-intensive construction events (individual posts weighing up to 1,530 kg) that were deliberately torn down and rebuilt, consistent with recurring aggregation episodes where construction itself served as a cooperative signal rather than a permanent investment. The paper also distinguishes two contrasting ritual activity patterns at the site: timber circles as open-air performance spaces with minimal material residues versus the Mound C locale with 16 sequential prepared surfaces rich in imported materials, both active during ca. 1450-1250 BC. Clay's central argument, that Poverty Point was not built to a master plan but is "the physical remnant of a number of abandoned projects" at different locations, supports the aggregation-based model over centralized-authority explanations.

## Data Presented

The paper synthesizes geophysical survey data (magnetic gradiometry and resistance survey) from collaborative work with Michael Hargrave (US Army Corps of Engineers), covering more than 163 hectares of the site. Key datasets include: the spatial distribution and dimensions of 36 timber post circles mapped by magnetometry (Figure 2); excavation data from four tested circle perimeters; detailed measurements from Circle 26 (50.13 m diameter, post hole dimensions); three radiocarbon dates from the Mound C locale (1546 BC, 1343 BC, and anomalous 1683 BC); Ortmann's stratigraphic data documenting 16 sequential prepared surfaces beneath Mound C; and a possible palisade signature from magnetic survey. The paper also references comparative data from Jaketown timber circle segments (Ford et al. 1955) and the bimodal size distribution analysis from Hargrave et al. 2021.

## Methodological Notes

The timber circles are identified primarily through magnetometry, and only four have been tested by excavation at their perimeters. Clay acknowledges this limitation explicitly: "their interiors being known only from magnetic signatures." The interpretation as open-air ritual spaces rests on the absence of interior features, which could also reflect limited excavation. The chronological framework relies on a single radiocarbon date (1150 +/- 40 BC) from miscellaneous charcoal in a post hole of Circle 26, which is not a direct date on a post. The Mound C chronology draws on multiple dates and stratigraphic analysis from Gibson's and Ortmann's excavations, which is more robust. The possible palisade is identified only from an untested magnetic anomaly and is explicitly speculative. Clay's approach is historicizing rather than generalizing: he argues for interpreting individual events and their sequences rather than seeking comprehensive functional explanations for the site as a whole.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Hargrave et al. 2021**: Primary source for the timber circle survey data, size distributions, and spatial clustering. Clay's paper interprets and contextualizes these geophysical findings.
- **Ortmann 2010; Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: Provide the stratigraphic evidence for rapid mound construction and the 16 sequential surfaces at Mound C that Clay contrasts with timber circle use patterns.
- **Kidder et al. 2009, 2021**: Chronological framework for mound and ridge construction; the "history in reverse" interpretation that Clay engages with.
- **Gibson 2001, 2019, 2021**: Excavation data from Mound C locale including sub-mound activity surfaces with imported cherts; the "Dunbar Mound" interpretation.
- **Spivey et al. 2015**: Pilgrimage interpretation of Poverty Point that Clay builds upon, particularly the observation that materials flow into the site with nothing visibly going out.
- **Sassaman 2005, 2011**: Provides the structural/processual interpretation of Poverty Point as multicultural social order; Clay engages with but moves beyond this framework.
- **Kidder and Grooms 2025**: Shares the emphasis on rapid construction, absence of hierarchy, and the interpretation of Poverty Point as a place of gathering rather than permanent settlement.
- **Britt et al. 2002**: Earlier geophysical survey of the site that identified circular anomalies on Ridge 1, which may relate to smaller timber circles.
- **Ford et al. 1955 (Jaketown)**: Comparative timber circle data from a smaller Poverty Point period site in Mississippi.
- **Ward et al. 2022**: Jaketown continued into Woodland period after Poverty Point was abandoned; Poverty Point period sites did not replicate the type site's features.
- **Lipo et al. 2018**: Referenced for geophysical survey suggesting a circle enclosure near Mound B, possibly cancelled by mound construction.

## Verification Notes

All claims were extracted from the published PDF (pp. 187-206 in *American Antiquity* 88(2)). The author is listed as R. Berle Clay (single author), not "Clay et al." as given in the user's initial description. The paper has no co-authors; the citation should be Clay 2023. Dates from the Mound C locale are reported as median ages from Waikato laboratory numbers, cross-referenced with Gibson 2019 and Ortmann 2007. The 36-circle count is described with hedging ("as many as 36"), reflecting uncertainty in the geophysical signatures. The bimodal size distribution is attributed to Hargrave et al. 2021 rather than being original to this paper. The post weight estimate of 1,530 kg (3,390 lb) is Clay's calculation based on the inferred post dimensions, assuming cypress wood.
