## Relevance to Argument

Wiessner 2002 is one of three sources Paper 1 line 147 cites to ground the within-group reward parameter $\lambda_W \in [0.05, 0.30]$ in ethnographic data. The audit finding is that the specific "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" figure Paper 1 attributes to this paper is **not present** in the paper, exactly paralleling the Hawkes 2000 audit finding (where the "10-20% reproductive success advantage" figure was also not in the cited source). The paper does provide solid quantitative differences between good and poor hunters' families across multiple measures, but the specific figure Paper 1 cites is not among them. Paper 1 line 147 should be revised in the same style as the already-applied Hawkes 2000 fix: replace the unsupported number with documented qualitative or correctly-quantified claims.

## Data Presented

- **Sample**: 9 good hunters and 9 poor hunters, plus their wives, in /Kae/kae and hxaro samples (combined samples sum to 14 vs 12 in some analyses)
- **Geographic scope**: /Kae/kae (NE Botswana), Nyae Nyae (Namibia), broader Ju/'hoansi communities
- **Time depth**: 34-year longitudinal record (1964-1998), incorporating Wiessner's own fieldwork (1973-1975, 1977, 1996-1998), Howell's demographic data (1979/2000), Lee's census (1964, 1968-1969), and Wilmsen's hunting/income data (1973-1976)
- **Hxaro network data**: 955 partnerships among 59 adults and 14 children (Wiessner 1977, 1982)
- **Subsistence data**: 17 months of subsistence-study observations 1996-1998 in Nyae Nyae (Wiessner & N!aici 1998)
- **Demographic outcomes**: 1998 follow-up census combined with marriage/birth records back to 1964

## Methodological Notes

- **Sample sizes are small** (n=9 good vs n=9 poor hunters in /Kae/kae sample). Wiessner explicitly acknowledges in the conclusion that "sample sizes in the above analyses are too small to be more than suggestive of worthwhile areas for further research" (p. 429).
- **Non-random sampling**: hunters were classified as good or poor based on community reputation and hunting-effort observations; not random sample of camp populations.
- **Statistical reporting**: t-test (two-tailed) p-values reported for all key comparisons. Several findings reach p<.05 (hxaro partners p=.006, distant kin p=.004, camp duration p=.0001, adult children co-resident p=.03), some only marginal significance (children surviving p=.05 hxaro sample only).
- **Combination across samples**: /Kae/kae and hxaro samples partially overlap; combined sample analysis reported as supplementary; details in Appendix A.
- **Multiple testing**: paper reports many comparisons across Tables 1-5; multiple-comparison corrections are not reported but the strongest findings (p<.01 or p<.001) would survive standard corrections.

## Verification Notes

**The specific "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" figure Paper 1 attributes to Wiessner (2002) is not present in this paper.** The paper's quantitative findings about good-vs-poor hunter premiums range from 24% (child survival) to 171% (adult-children co-residence), with hxaro-network premiums of 80-142%. The strongest shortfall-period numerical claims are:
- 93% of extended visits during shortfalls go to hxaro-partner camps (p. 422)
- 69% of household possessions obtained through hxaro (p. 422)
- 80% of diet during hunger months from large-game kills (p. 416)

None of these matches "10-30%."

**Recommended Paper 1 fix (parallel to the Hawkes 2000 fix)**: Replace the specific "10-30% additional resources during shortfalls" with documented qualitative or correctly-quantified claims from this paper. Crosscheck-log entry to be added documenting this finding and the resolution.
