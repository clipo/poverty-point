## Relevance to Argument

Jackson's dissertation is one of the most directly relevant sources for the Poverty Point costly signaling project. It provides a sustained critique of the chiefdom interpretation of Poverty Point and develops a theoretical framework in which sedentary hunter-gatherers cope with environmental variability through the extension and regularization of social and ritual networks, including exchange of exotic goods as a form of "social storage." This framework closely parallels the signaling model's core mechanism: seasonal aggregation, monumental construction as collective ritual activity, and exotic goods exchange as network maintenance. Jackson also provides key empirical data on subsistence (nut-dominated botanical assemblage, fish-dominated fauna, absence of cultigens), chronology (critique of the short "florescence" model), and the spatial distribution of exotic goods (not restricted to elite contexts or large sites). Collectively, these findings support an interpretation of Poverty Point as a seasonal aggregation center rather than a permanently occupied chiefdom capital.

## Data Presented

Jackson presents several categories of primary data. Archaeobotanical data from systematic flotation of Poverty Point features include quantified plant remains: 3,712 hickory nutshell fragments and 7,778 acorn fragments across 35 samples, with hickory present in 30 samples and acorn in 21. Only single specimens of Polygonum and Chenopodium were recovered. Faunal data include 552 bones from Poverty Point components, of which 141 were taxonomically identifiable, dominated by freshwater drum, catfish, and mud-musk turtle. A compilation of published radiocarbon dates for Poverty Point and related sites is provided in Table 1. Gibson's population estimates are reviewed and critiqued: approximately 4,000 people calculated through clay object use rates (777 families/4,662 persons), ridge crest house spacing (740 houses/4,440 people), and ridge earth volume labor (1,350 workers, implying 4,050 total). Ethnographic data on Australian hunter-gatherer aggregations (500-1,000 individuals) provide scale comparisons.

## Methodological Notes

This is a Ph.D. dissertation (541 pages in two volumes) that combines theoretical analysis, literature review, archaeological data presentation, and fieldwork results. Jackson's approach is notable for its systematic critique of assumptions underlying the chiefdom model for Poverty Point, methodically evaluating the evidentiary basis for claims about social complexity, population size, and subsistence. The theoretical framework draws heavily on optimal foraging theory and behavioral ecology applied to sedentarization processes. Jackson's own excavation data come from peripheral localities at Poverty Point and sites in the Lower Yazoo Basin. The archaeobotanical data are from systematic flotation, providing quantified evidence, but faunal preservation is poor (calcined, fragmentary bone), limiting the reliability of faunal conclusions. The dissertation was written in 1986, so subsequent excavation and dating work (particularly by Kidder, Ortmann, and colleagues) has substantially refined the chronology and construction history of the site.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Ford and Webb 1956**: Jackson critiques their master-blueprint construction model and agricultural assumption as foundational errors in Poverty Point interpretation.
- **Gibson 1973, 1975, 2000**: Jackson systematically evaluates and challenges Gibson's chiefdom model, population estimates, and socio-political interpretations while acknowledging Gibson's ecological argument about Macon Ridge/floodplain juxtaposition.
- **Johnson 1980**: Key ally for Jackson's argument; demonstrated that access to exotic materials in the Yazoo Basin depended on source distance, not proximity to redistributive centers, and that exotics were not restricted to large sites.
- **Goad 1980**: Cited for evidence of episodic ridge construction and wide distribution of exotic materials.
- **O'Shea 1981**: Jackson adopts the "social storage" concept as a mechanism for converting food surplus into durable exchange goods, directly relevant to the signaling model's exotic goods mechanism.
- **Thomas and Campbell 1978**: Provides the archaeobotanical evidence for fall-winter occupation and the seasonal aggregation hypothesis that Jackson builds upon.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013** (published after this dissertation): Later work on Mound A construction chronology substantially revised the timeline and confirmed rapid, episodic construction.
- **Kidder et al. 2008**: Subsequent work reinforced some of Jackson's critiques and provided improved radiocarbon chronology.
- **Sassaman 2005**: Later theoretical treatment of Poverty Point as structure, event, and process extends some of Jackson's themes.

## Verification Notes

Claims were extracted from a scanned PDF of the complete dissertation (two volumes, 541 pages). Page numbers correspond to the printed pagination in the original document. The radiocarbon dates reported in Table 1 are uncorrected and use the old half-life; they should not be used directly without recalibration. Jackson's subsistence data (archaeobotanical and faunal) are primary data from his own excavations and systematic flotation, making them among the more reliable empirical claims. Population estimates attributed to Gibson are accurately reported by Jackson but are themselves methodologically weak, as Jackson demonstrates. The "social storage" framework attributed to O'Shea (1981) is accurately summarized. The ethnographic aggregation data (Flood 1980, Lourandos 1977, 1985) are cited with specific figures and can be checked against the original sources.
