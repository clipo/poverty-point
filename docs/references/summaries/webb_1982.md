## Relevance to Argument

This monograph is the most comprehensive single-source reference on Poverty Point culture available for its era, synthesizing decades of fieldwork and analysis of approximately 120,000 artifacts across more than 100 sites. It provides foundational data on virtually every aspect of the Poverty Point phenomenon relevant to the costly signaling project: site dimensions and construction labor, exotic material sourcing and volumes, intrasite artifact distributions, seasonal aggregation indicators, ecotone positioning, settlement hierarchy, and the simultaneous collapse of construction and trade networks. Webb's explicit identification of "strategic ecotones" as the ecological constant across all Poverty Point sites (p. 5) directly supports the project's ecological diversity hypothesis. The detailed inventories of exotic materials (copper, galena, steatite, lapidary items) with confirmed source locations provide the empirical foundation for modeling signaling costs. Webb interprets the system through a chiefdom model (following Gibson 1974b), which should be evaluated critically against the project's costly signaling framework.

## Data Presented

Webb presents an extensive empirical record based on decades of fieldwork:

**Artifact inventories**: Approximately 120,000 catalogued objects (Tables 8-18), including 29,000+ classified baked clay objects, nearly 30,000 microflint blades and tools, 2,790 plummets, 4,945 steatite fragments, 155 copper objects, 740 galena items, and 1,536 lapidary items. Detailed typological breakdowns are provided for each major artifact class.

**Site measurements**: Precise dimensions for ridges (outer diameter 1,208 m, total length 11.2 km), Mound A (21.3 m high, 207 x 210 m base), Mound B (6.6 m high, 59.4 m diameter), Motley Mound (15.5 m high, 171 x 122 m base), and the Mound A platform (7 m high, 73 x 91 m base).

**Material sourcing**: Confirmed source identifications through mineralogical and trace element analysis for steatite (NE Alabama/NW Georgia), galena (Potosi, Missouri and Upper Mississippi Valley), and generalized source attributions for copper (Great Lakes), novaculite (Ouachita Mountains), and northern gray flint (Illinois/Indiana). Source map presented as Figure 32.

**Intrasite distribution**: Analysis of 18,727 provenience-designated artifacts across site sectors (Table 16), showing spatial patterning of artifact types by sector.

**Raw material inventory**: Table 17 enumerates 689 galena cubes/nodules, 395 quartz crystals, 410 red jasper pebbles, 1,044 varicolored pebbles, 227 hematite nodules, and numerous other imported materials.

**Radiocarbon dates**: Table 1 compiles dates from Poverty Point, Teoc Creek, Claiborne, and Dragline sites, ranging from 2825 +/- 90 B.C. (Dragline) to 865 +/- 255 B.C. (Poverty Point).

**Regional site data**: Maps and descriptions of sites in five major river drainages and the Gulf Coast, with site dimensions and artifact summaries for Jaketown, Claiborne, Teoc Creek, Cowpen Slough, Copes, and Terral Lewis.

## Methodological Notes

This is a comprehensive culture monograph, not a study with a specific hypothesis-testing methodology. Webb synthesizes data from multiple excavation campaigns (1950s-1970s), surface collections, and analyses by numerous researchers. The artifact classifications follow typological systems developed by Ford, Webb, and Gibson over several decades. Population estimates (4,000-5,000) use Ford and Webb's 1956 methods, which predate modern demographic estimation techniques and should be treated with caution. The chronological framework relies on uncalibrated radiocarbon dates, which have been substantially refined by subsequent work. Material sourcing uses mineralogical analysis (steatite) and trace element analysis (galena), which provide relatively strong provenance determinations. The intrasite distributional analysis assumes that surface-collected and excavated samples are representative of original depositional patterns, an assumption that may not hold uniformly across the site. Webb's interpretive framework is explicitly hierarchical (chiefdom model following Gibson 1974b), which colors his characterization of social organization throughout.

## Connections to Other Sources

- Ford and Webb 1956 is the foundational prior work, providing initial site descriptions, population estimates, and the bola weight interpretation of plummets.
- Gibson 1974b provides the chiefdom organizational model that Webb adopts throughout.
- Thomas and Campbell (no date given) provide the seasonal aggregation data and population estimates cited in Claims 22 and 68.
- Mineralogical analysis by Carpenter (University of Missouri) confirmed steatite sourcing to the southern Appalachians (Claim 42).
- Trace element analysis identified galena sources as Potosi, Missouri (Claim 45).
- Haag and Webb 1953 is a predecessor study on the microflint blade technology.
- Jackson 1986 provides complementary data on hunter-gatherer sedentism in the Lower Mississippi Valley.
- The ecotone positioning documented here (Claim 10) aligns with the project framework's ecological diversity hypothesis.
- The decline pattern (Claims 61-62) parallels collapse dynamics studied in the Rapa Nui and Chaco Canyon signaling projects.
- Blackburn 2003 and Lipo 2012 provide additional data on plummet materials and functions.
- Kidder and Ortmann's subsequent work has substantially refined construction chronology and labor estimates.

## Verification Notes

Claims were extracted from a scanned PDF of the published monograph (100 pages, 32 figures, 18 tables). Page numbers match the original pagination. All quantitative claims (artifact counts, site dimensions, material percentages) were verified against the tables and text of the PDF during extraction. The chronological framework (dates, cultural stages) has been superseded by subsequent radiocarbon work, particularly by Kidder, Ortmann, and colleagues. The chiefdom model adopted from Gibson 1974b has been challenged by more recent interpretations. Population estimates should not be relied upon without cross-referencing against modern analyses. Source attributions for copper and some lithic materials are generalized (not geochemically confirmed) and should be verified against more recent sourcing studies where available.
