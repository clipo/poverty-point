## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides the most detailed empirical evidence for the construction history of Mound A at Poverty Point, the largest earthwork at the site and one of the largest pre-Columbian constructions in eastern North America. The geoarchaeological and micromorphological evidence demonstrating rapid construction (less than 90 days) by a large labor force (1,000-3,000 laborers) without hierarchical social organization is foundational to the costly signaling argument. The paper explicitly rejects both the gradual-accrual model (small groups over centuries) and the ranked-society model (centralized authority directing labor), instead proposing situational leadership and ritual practice as the mechanisms for labor mobilization. This directly supports interpreting Mound A construction as a costly signaling event during seasonal aggregation: a massive, visible, coordinated investment of labor that required no permanent hierarchy but instead functioned through ritual commitment to a corporate group. The chronological position of Mound A as the terminal major construction event (ca. 3261 cal. yr B.P., near the end of Poverty Point occupation) suggests an intensification of cooperative investment late in the site's history.

## Data Presented

The paper presents data from excavation of a 3 m wide x 10 m deep profile on the south side of the Mound A platform, plus 89 solid soil cores (5.08 cm diameter) from across the mound. Nineteen micromorphology thin sections were collected from the profile excavation and soil cores. Four AMS radiocarbon dates from submound deposits are reported in Table I. Table II provides a comprehensive matrix of labor estimates across four excavation rates and three construction durations, with population projections at three laborer-to-total ratios. The paper includes topographic maps (Figures 1-3), a schematic cross-section of the construction sequence (Figure 4), profile photographs (Figures 5-6), photomicrographs of contact zones (Figure 7), OxCal models (Figures 8-9), and a probability density function for the construction/use interval (Figure 10).

## Methodological Notes

The geoarchaeological approach combining macro-scale profile analysis with micromorphological thin-section examination is methodologically rigorous. The weathering argument is well-constrained by 120 years of instrumental climate data, though the authors acknowledge they cannot establish a minimum construction time, only a maximum of approximately 90 days. The labor estimates use Erasmus' (1965) experimental rate of 2.6 m^3/person-day as the primary figure, but this rate excludes transport costs and represents digging with a wooden stick only. Other experimental rates (Gomez-Pompa et al. 1982: 1.1 m^3/person-day; Muller 1997: 1.2 m^3/person-day) yield substantially higher labor requirements. The radiocarbon dates come from submound contexts only (no dates from mound fill were considered reliable), providing a terminus post quem but no direct constraint on when within the post-3261 cal. yr B.P. period construction occurred. The maximum use interval of 107 years (mean 44, median 38) is derived from the difference between submound dates and the modeled terminal date for the entire Poverty Point site.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Ortmann 2007**: Dissertation providing the full dataset; this paper is the peer-reviewed distillation. The dissertation reports the volume range as 178,000-238,000 m^3; this paper uses 238,500 m^3.
- **Kidder et al. 2009**: Unpublished final field report from the 2005-2006 seasons; provides the 238,500 m^3 volume estimate and construction details cited extensively here.
- **Kidder and Grooms 2025**: Updates the chronology and expands the interpretive framework; estimates total site earthmoving at 1,000,000 m^3; characterizes the site as a revitalization center.
- **Kidder 2006**: Provides the terminal chronology; the abandonment of Poverty Point shortly after ca. 3200 cal. yr B.P. documented here connects to Kidder's documentation of the 400-year hiatus and climate-driven collapse.
- **Gibson 2000, 2004**: Ortmann and Kidder specifically engage with Gibson's "labor freely given" model, agreeing that construction was communal but arguing it required stronger direction than Gibson implies.
- **Jackson 1986**: The seasonal aggregation model proposed by Jackson is implicitly supported by Ortmann and Kidder's labor estimates, which require large populations that could only be assembled through aggregation.
- **Sassaman 2005**: Cited for the structural and ritual framework; the structured deposition evidence supports Sassaman's view of Poverty Point as meaningful symbolic practice.
- **Peacock and Rafferty 2013**: The bet-hedging framework for Archaic mound building is complementary; the rapid construction and absence of hierarchy are consistent with communal costly behavior under environmental uncertainty.
- **Saunders et al. 2005 (Watson Brake)**: Provides the precedent for rapid mound construction at earlier Middle Archaic sites, though Watson Brake's construction was episodic with 200+ year hiatuses, unlike Mound A's single-event construction.

## Verification Notes

Claims were extracted from the published journal article (paginated pp. 66-86 in *Geoarchaeology* 28(1)). The volume estimate of 238,500 m^3 (p. 67) differs slightly from the range reported in Ortmann (2007) of 178,000-238,000 m^3 and the round figure of 238,000 m^3 sometimes cited; the 238,500 m^3 figure is attributed to Kidder et al. (2009). The "<90 days" construction estimate should be cited carefully: it is a maximum duration derived from climate constraints, not a direct measurement of construction speed. The paper was received July 2012, accepted October 2012, and published online in 2012, though the journal issue is dated 2013 (volume 28, issue 1). Citations in the literature use both 2012 and 2013 dates; the correct publication year for the journal issue is 2013.
