## Relevance to Argument

This article documents differential participation in the Poverty Point exchange network across 28 Yazoo Basin sites, organized into four tiers based on the diversity of foreign material source areas represented. The data provide direct evidence for the costly signaling model in several ways. First, the tiered distribution of exotic materials shows that access to distant sources was uneven across the network, consistent with differential signaling capacity. Second, the temporal sequence of source exploitation (from Gibson 1973) demonstrates that the signaling system was dynamic, not static, with shifts in preferred material sources over the site's occupation. Third, the observation that Poverty Point populations continued using costly steatite vessels despite having knowledge of ceramic technology represents a deliberate choice to bear higher costs for exotic materials, precisely the kind of behavior predicted by costly signaling theory. Fourth, the hypothesis that the Mississippi River's shift to its present meander stage (ca. 650 BCE) severed exchange routes provides a physical mechanism for network disruption and potential system collapse. The article's distinction between the Yazoo Basin's exchange trajectory and Poverty Point's trajectory is relevant to modeling how signaling networks operated differently at peripheral versus central nodes.

## Data Presented

- Presence/absence matrix (Table 2) of foreign lithics, steatite, and ceramics across 28 named Yazoo Basin sites from six source regions: Tennessee River, Upper Mississippi/Ohio Rivers, SE Missouri, Steatite, Fiber-tempered pottery, and Alexander pottery
- Four-tier grouping of sites based on material diversity
- No quantitative artifact counts; data are presence/absence only
- Source attributions based on visual lithic identification rather than geochemical sourcing
- Temporal sequence of Poverty Point source exploitation drawn from Gibson (1973)

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Gibson 1973**: Lehmann draws on Gibson's dissertation analysis of temporal shifts in raw material source exploitation at Poverty Point. This temporal sequence (Ouachita/Tennessee River to midwestern dominance) is foundational to the article's argument about divergent exchange trajectories.
- **Gibson 1984**: Cited for the observation that Poverty Point populations preferred steatite vessels despite ceramic knowledge.
- **Saucier 1981**: Provides the geomorphological dating (ca. 650 BCE) for the Mississippi River's shift to its present meander stage, the proposed mechanism for exchange network disruption.
- **Webb 1982** (in collection): Cited for the absence of Wheeler and Alexander sherds around the Poverty Point type site.
- **Lauro and Lehmann 1982**: Co-authored earlier Yazoo Basin study, suggesting continuity of research program.
- **Byrd 1991** (summary in collection): Byrd's work on Poverty Point culture regional context may provide complementary data on site distributions discussed here.
