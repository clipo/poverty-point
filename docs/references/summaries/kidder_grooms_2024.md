## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides the most current and rigorous radiocarbon chronological framework for Poverty Point and related sites. Its three core findings are directly relevant to the costly signaling model. First, the rejection of a unified "Poverty Point culture" eliminates the assumption that the type site was the center of a hierarchical polity from which cultural traits diffused outward, supporting instead a model of independent regional groups converging at an aggregation hub. Second, the compressed site occupation of approximately 164-397 years (95.4% hpd) eliminates the temporal depth required by gradualist political evolution models, making rapid collective action explanations (including costly signaling) more plausible. Third, the demonstration that earthwork construction occurred in a narrow window late in site occupation (ca. 3300-3225 cal BP), requiring an "extraordinarily large labor force for a short interval," is directly consistent with the seasonal aggregation and collective signaling mechanism at the core of our model. The finding that Jaketown practiced mound building, exotic exchange, and characteristic artifact forms 400+ years before the Poverty Point site supports the interpretation that Poverty Point was a late convergence point for pre-existing regional practices, not the origin of those practices.

## Data Presented

The paper presents a comprehensive Bayesian chronological analysis of 157 radiocarbon dates from 12 sites attributed to the Poverty Point culture in the LMV. Each date is assessed using a four-tier chronological hygiene ranking system (following Hamilton and Krus 2017 and Napolitano et al. 2019). Seven iterative models are run in OxCal 4.4 using the IntCal20 calibration curve, applied to three datasets: (1) CULTURE (all PP-associated dates), (2) SITE (PP type site dates only), and (3) TPQ (terminus post quem for earthwork construction at the type site). Key quantitative outputs include:

- Model 4 CULTURE: 3555-3070 cal BP (95.4% hpd), n=71
- Model 4 SITE: 3535-3135 cal BP (95.4% hpd), n=34, A_model=117.3
- OxCal SPAN for PP site occupation: 164-397 years (95.4% hpd)
- Model 5 Earthwork TPQ: construction begins 3400-3200 cal BP, ends 3320-3045 cal BP (95.4% hpd), n=11
- Jaketown chronology: initial occupation ca. 4500-4000 cal BP; intensive occupation ca. 3600-3400 cal BP; mound building burst ca. 3400 cal BP

KDE_Plot summaries (Figures 3, 4, 7, 8) visualize the probability distributions across models and sites. The 11 TPQ dates used in Model 5 are individually listed in Table 3 with lab numbers, radiocarbon ages, contexts, and references.

## Methodological Notes

The paper's iterative modeling approach is its primary methodological contribution. Rather than simply discarding "bad" dates, the authors apply progressively stricter hygiene criteria across Models 1-4, allowing readers to see precisely how each filtering step affects the chronological estimate. The use of charcoal outlier models (Bronk Ramsey 2009b) accounts for unknown inbuilt age in wood charcoal samples. The Model 5 TPQ analysis uses a conceptually distinct approach: dating only organically enriched/culturally modified contexts that are stratigraphically below construction deposits, providing terminus post quem dates for earthwork building rather than attempting to date construction directly. The authors note that no terminus ante quem dates exist for any constructed deposit at Poverty Point, meaning the end of construction cannot be directly dated; Model 4's end boundary serves as the best available estimate for site abandonment.

A potential limitation is that the SITE and CULTURE models use a single-phase contiguous model, which assumes all dated events belong to one continuous phase. If there were multiple discrete occupation phases separated by hiatuses, this model would smooth over them. However, the excellent agreement indices for Model 4 SITE (A > 100) suggest the single-phase assumption is statistically justified for the hygienically filtered dataset.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Kidder and Grooms 2025** (kidder_grooms_2025): Extends the chronological arguments of this paper into a broader interpretive framework, arguing Poverty Point was a revitalization site. Uses the same chronological data to estimate total earthmoving at 1,000,000 m3 and construction within weeks to years.
- **Kidder 2006** (kidder_2006): Earlier Bayesian analysis of the Archaic-Woodland transition. The 2024 paper provides substantially more refined chronology for the Poverty Point period specifically, and the construction timeline is now narrower than the 2006 estimates.
- **Ortmann 2007** (ortmann_2007): Provided the original micromorphological evidence for rapid Mound A construction. This paper's Model 5 extends the rapid construction finding to ridges, causeway, plaza fill, and multiple mounds.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: Cited for sub-Mound A TPQ dates that anchor Model 5. The 2013 finding that Mound A was built in months is now contextualized within the broader pattern of late, rapid construction.
- **Grooms 2022; Grooms et al. 2023**: The Jaketown chronology that demonstrates practices attributed to "Poverty Point culture" originated at Jaketown 400+ years before the type site.
- **Sassaman 2005** (sassaman_2005): Provided the structuralist interpretation of Poverty Point as a multicultural social order. The 2024 chronology challenges Sassaman's assumption of temporal depth but supports his emphasis on Poverty Point as a convergence point.
- **Gibson 2019**: The primary target of critique; represented the "gradual, continuous" interpretation with earthwork construction spanning five centuries.
- **Peacock and Rafferty 2013** (peacock_rafferty_2013): Applied bet-hedging theory to Archaic mound building. This paper's rapid construction chronology creates a tension with Peacock and Rafferty's model of mounds as gradual "waste," but supports the general principle that mound building was a costly, episodic behavior.
