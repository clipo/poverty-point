## Relevance to Argument

This paper supersedes the unpublished `hill_etal_2010` SAA symposium precursor as the citable source for the LA-ICP-MS analysis of Poverty Point copper provenance. It reports that all six analyzed PP copper artifacts assign to eastern (Appalachian / Maritime) sources rather than Lake Superior, refining the long-distance exchange picture central to Paper 2's §1 introduction and the costly-signaling framework's index-signal claim. The paper's qualitative finding — PP copper is from a geologically constrained, distant source — is what Paper 2 line 39 cites; the specific Nova Scotia attribution is not load-bearing for the framework.

## Data Presented

- **6 PP copper artifacts** from existing Poverty Point Curatorial Facility collections, representing ~3.2% of the known PP copper assemblage of ~188 objects. Forms: 1 bead (GC-1, 0.84 g), 2 flat sheet fragments (GC-2 and GC-3), 1 nugget (GC-4), 2 rolled sheet/tube fragments (GC-5 and GC-6).
- **62 native copper source samples** from the Rapp et al. 2000 collection (Michigan Technological University): 7 Maritime (Cap d'Or NS, Trout River NL), 9 Appalachian (Cornwall PA, Greenstone PA, Gold Hill NC), 46 Lake Superior (Michipicoten ON, Quincy MI, Minesota MI, Centennial MI, Champion MI, Minong/Isle Royale MI, Snake River MN).
- **LA-ICP-MS methodology**: New Wave Research UP-213 laser ablation system + GBC Optimass time-of-flight + Agilent 4500 quadrupole; SRM 1107 Naval Brass, SRM 500 Unalloyed Copper, SRM 1110 Red Brass standards; 4 spots per artifact, average used as elemental signature; power transformations applied (Hill 2012a:110-113).
- **13 elements measured (PPM)**: Al, Fe, Ni, Cu, Zn, As, Ag, Cd, In, Sn, Sb, Au, Pb (Table 3, p. 9).
- **Three discriminant function analyses**: 2-region (Wilks' Λ = 0.618, p = 0.011, 85.5% reclassification); 3-region (Wilks' Λ = 0.392, p = 0.001, 82.3%); 4-region (Wilks' Λ = 0.302, p ≤ 0.003, 82.3%).
- **Visualization**: Figure 2 (functions 1 and 2 for the four-source test, showing PP samples cluster with Nova Scotia centroid); Figure 3 (functions 2 and 3, same pattern); Figure 4 (panel a-c source probabilities for each PP sample across the three tests).

## Methodological Notes

- **Small artifact sample.** 6 / ~188 = 3.2% of the known PP copper assemblage. Authors flag this as "preliminary" and a "test of concept." Generalization to the full assemblage requires additional sampling.
- **Asymmetric source sampling.** 74% of source samples are from Lake Superior. Appalachian sources are underrepresented (9 samples across 3 site groupings); southern Appalachian and Piedmont sources are largely missing. This means a "Nova Scotia" assignment in the four-region test may be a Maritime/Appalachian artifact rather than actual Maritime provenance, as the authors explicitly acknowledge (p. 11, p. 15).
- **No diachronic resolution.** Authors acknowledge that the artifacts cannot be securely dated, so changes in source utilization over the PP occupation cannot be detected from this dataset. Diachronic analysis is identified as priority follow-up (p. 16).
- **Spot-sampling of artifacts.** Four spots per artifact were averaged. Internal heterogeneity in copper composition is real but the four-spot averaging is the standard mitigation.
- **Discriminant function analysis is appropriate** for the multi-source classification problem but is sensitive to source-region definition. The authors explicitly tested three different source-region partitions to address this; results are robust on the headline (no Lake Superior) and consistent on the Maritime-vs-Appalachian split.
- **Power transformations** (Hill 2012a:110-113) are used to enhance normality in the elemental concentration data; this is a standard practice in archaeometric work. The earlier 2010 SAA paper used un-averaged, un-transformed data with "substantially the same result" (p. 8), strengthening the robustness of the headline finding.
