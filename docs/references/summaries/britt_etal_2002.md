## Relevance to Argument

This report provides precise measurements of ridge architecture at Poverty Point that support the claim of pre-planned, coordinated monumental construction. The geophysical evidence for deliberate engineering (uniform ridge spacing, intentional use of different soil types, possible structural remains on Ridge 1) strengthens the case that construction required organized labor mobilization consistent with aggregation-based cooperative episodes, a core mechanism in the costly signaling model.

## Data Presented

The report presents results from a multi-instrument geophysical survey conducted 21-22 June 2001 covering:
- 13,600 m^2 by gradiometer (magnetic gradient)
- 2,800 m^2 by resistivity
- 1,600 m^2 by electromagnetic conductivity
- 400 m^2 by ground-penetrating radar

Survey areas included the Western Sector (east of Mound A) and the Southwest Sector (Ridges 1-5). The gradiometer proved most effective, producing clear images of subsurface ridge architecture in the Southwest Sector. Resistivity provided supporting data. GPR and EM conductivity yielded limited results due to soil conditions. Key quantitative data include ridge widths (20 m for Ridges 2-4, 35 m for Ridge 1) and inter-ridge spacing (25 m), as well as dimensions of circular anomalies on Ridge 1 (10-15 m diameter).

## Methodological Notes

This is primarily a methodological study testing geophysical survey applicability at Poverty Point. The gradiometer survey was most successful, clearly resolving ridge-and-swale architecture in the Southwest Sector. The GPR performed poorly due to conductive, clay-rich loess soils. The survey covered only a small fraction of the total site area (~160 hectares), so results from the two survey areas cannot necessarily be generalized across the entire site. None of the geophysical anomalies were ground-truthed through excavation, so interpretations of specific features (circular anomalies, possible storage pits, potential structural remains) remain unconfirmed.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Gibson 2000**: Cited for site dating (1,730-1,350 BCE), Crowley's clay mining, and construction techniques. Gibson's site monograph is a primary source for contextual claims in this report.
- **Webb 1982**: Cited for artifact distribution patterns across the site, particularly microflint blade concentrations in the Southwest Sector and PPO distributions.
- **Kidder 2000**: Cited for framing Poverty Point as an early example of non-agricultural social complexity.
- **Saunders et al. 2001**: Cited for the identification of six semi-concentric rings and five mounds.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013** (not cited in this report but relevant): Their later work on Mound A construction chronology can be compared with the punctuated construction hypothesis suggested here.

## Verification Notes

All claims were extracted directly from the PDF report. Page numbers follow the report's internal pagination (pp. 1-28 main text). Several claims rely on personal communications (Glen Greene's coring data via Dennis LaBatt; Robert Connolly on artifact presence in swales) and should be treated with appropriate caution. The geophysical anomaly interpretations (circular features, possible storage pits, clay retaining walls) are not ground-truthed and remain hypothetical. The ridge dimension measurements (Claims 13-15) are the most robust empirical contributions, as they derive directly from the geophysical data.
