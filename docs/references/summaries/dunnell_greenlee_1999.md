## Relevance to Argument

This paper develops the evolutionary "waste" hypothesis, which provides the theoretical precursor to costly signaling models applied in this project. The argument that costly nonreproductive activities (earthwork construction, exotic goods acquisition) are adaptive under environmental unpredictability and selected against when conditions improve maps directly onto the Poverty Point costly signaling framework, particularly regarding why monumental construction and exotic exchange emerged and why they ceased.

## Data Presented

The paper draws on multiple empirical datasets for the Ohio River Valley and broader Eastern Woodlands:

1. Great Lakes lake-level records as proxy for regional climate (warmer/wetter conditions at end of Middle Woodland)
2. Bald cypress tree-ring data from North Carolina corroborating the climatic shift
3. Stable carbon isotope data from human skeletal remains in the Ohio Valley, showing timing and individual-level variation in maize adoption
4. Archaeological evidence for the timing and differential decline of Hopewell elaboration forms (earthworks, exotics, mortuary complexity)
5. Warfare indicators (or lack thereof) during the Middle to Late Woodland transition

No data are presented for Poverty Point directly; the Hopewell case serves as the empirical test of the waste hypothesis.

## Methodological Notes

The paper applies Dunnell's (1989) evolutionary "waste" concept as a selectionist framework, treating cultural elaboration as a phenotypic trait subject to natural selection rather than as a reflection of social complexity or elite power. The approach differs from costly signaling theory in terminology and intellectual genealogy (selectionist archaeology vs. behavioral ecology) but converges on the same core logic: costly behaviors are maintained because they confer fitness advantages under specific environmental conditions. The key methodological contribution is specifying testable empirical implications of the waste hypothesis (population increase after waste reduction, correlation with climatic amelioration, differential persistence of waste forms) and evaluating them against the archaeological and paleoclimate record.
