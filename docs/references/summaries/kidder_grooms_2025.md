## Relevance to Argument

This paper is directly relevant to the costly signaling project in several respects. First, it provides an updated chronological framework (drawing on Kidder and Grooms 2024 Bayesian modeling) that constrains mound and ridge construction to the final phase of site occupation, measured in weeks, months, or at most a few years rather than decades or centuries. This rapid construction by a voluntary, geographically dispersed labor force aligns with the signaling model's prediction that cooperative monument building functions as an honest signal during brief aggregation episodes. Second, the paper quantifies the exotic artifact assemblage through a comparative table (Table 1), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude differences between Poverty Point and all contemporary sites, providing empirical baselines for modeling exotic goods as costly signals. Third, the paper documents comprehensively the absence of hierarchy, wealth concentration, burials, residential structures, and economic specialization, eliminating political-economy explanations and supporting non-coercive, cooperation-based models. Fourth, the one-directional flow of materials (metric tons of lithic material in, nothing visible out) is inconsistent with redistribution or trade-center models but consistent with ritual consumption of costly goods as irreversible signaling acts. Fifth, the environmental disruption context (ca. 4000-3000 cal BP: increased rainfall, cooler temperatures, high-frequency flooding, meander belt relocation) provides the elevated sigma that the costly signaling model predicts should trigger cooperative investment. Although the authors frame their interpretation through Indigenous moral economy and revitalization movements rather than evolutionary ecology, the behavioral predictions (voluntary aggregation, cooperative labor during environmental crisis, costly material deposition, authority-based leadership) overlap substantially with those of the costly signaling framework.

## Data Presented

The primary quantitative contribution is Table 1 (p. 8), which compiles artifact counts for Poverty Point alongside contemporary sites (Jaketown, Claiborne, and others), drawn from published sources. Categories include ground stone tools, plummets, beads (stone, crinoid, copper), pendants, gorgets, bannerstones, steatite objects, galena objects, figurines, and atlatl weights. These counts demonstrate that Poverty Point exceeds every contemporary site by one to three orders of magnitude in nearly every artifact category. The paper also cites volumetric estimates for earthmoving (750,000-1,000,000 m^3), site area (3 km^2, with ~200 ha of modified landscape and ~17 ha plaza), chronological ranges from Bayesian modeling (site occupation ca. 3535-3135 cal BP), and specific artifact quantities (200-300 soapstone vessels in one cache, 2,800+ plummets, 10,000+ projectile points, 500-1000 kg galena extrapolated). No new primary datasets or excavation results are presented; the paper synthesizes previously published data within a new interpretive framework.

## Methodological Notes

This is a synthetic interpretive paper published in *Southeastern Archaeology*, building on the authors' prior chronological work (Kidder and Grooms 2024). Its strength lies in the comprehensive aggregation of quantitative data from across the Poverty Point literature and the deployment of that data within a coherent interpretive framework (revitalization movements). The Bayesian chronological modeling supporting the rapid construction claim is published separately in Kidder and Grooms 2024 and should be evaluated independently. The revitalization movement framework draws on Wallace 1956 and subsequent applications; the specific application to Poverty Point is novel but relies heavily on ethnographic analogy (bundle ceremonies, Choctaw cosmology, sodality organization) applied to a pre-contact context with no direct historical linkages to documented practices. The "prophetic visionaries" claim (p. 11) lacks direct archaeological evidence. The distinction between "power" and "authority" (p. 5, citing Angelbeck and Grier 2012) is theoretically important but difficult to test archaeologically.
