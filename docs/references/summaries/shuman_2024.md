## Relevance to Argument

This paper addresses whether centennial-to-millennial (cen-mil) climate variability during the Holocene can be distinguished from noise in paleoclimate reconstructions across mid-latitude North America. The analysis compares independent multi-record ensembles of temperature and moisture proxies at four spatial scales (multi-continent, mid-latitude regional, central sub-regional, and NE coastal site-level) over the past 7000 years. The core finding is that cen-mil temperature variability was small (0.25-0.5 degrees C) but real, as it produced correlations between independent datasets exceeding those expected from random surrogates. Two distinct patterns are identified: a broad-scale millennial pattern with NAO-like spatial characteristics and a prominent Mid-Holocene anomaly at 5600-5000 yr BP, and a multi-century (~350-500-year) pattern of hydroclimate fluctuations along the western Atlantic margin.

For the Poverty Point costly signaling model, this paper provides three contributions. First, it quantifies the magnitude of centennial-to-millennial climate variability as 0.25-0.5 degrees C in temperature and demonstrates that this variability, though small, produced detectable ecological and hydrological responses visible in pollen and lake-level records. This provides a constraint on the sigma (environmental uncertainty) parameter: perturbations were modest but ecologically significant. Second, it establishes the temporal framework around the Poverty Point period (3700-3100 yr BP): the occupation falls between two major climate reorganizations at ca. 5000 and 2100 yr BP, and between warm/dry events at 4200-3900 and 2900-2100 yr BP. A cool/wet fluctuation at 3400-3100 yr BP corresponds to the late Poverty Point occupation. Third, the NAO-like spatial structure of millennial variability means that different parts of the Poverty Point exchange network (spanning from the Gulf Coast to the Great Lakes and Appalachians) may have experienced different expressions of the same climate dynamics, making inter-regional cooperation and information exchange adaptive.

The principal limitation is geographic: the study's records are concentrated at 36-52 degrees N, with the densest coverage above 40 degrees N, while Poverty Point sits at 32.6 degrees N. The NAO-like north-south gradient structure means that extrapolation to Poverty Point's latitude is not straightforward, as the sign of temperature anomalies may reverse across the gradient boundary.

## Data Presented

The paper synthesizes eight previously published paleoclimate datasets organized into four spatial scales. At the multi-continent scale: (1) European and North American pollen-based mean annual temperature (Marsicek et al. 2018, 642 fossil pollen records) and (2) water temperature ensemble from marine and lake cores (Marcott et al. 2013). At the mid-latitude regional scale: (3) mean temperature of the warmest month (MTWM) from 16 pollen records and (4) effective moisture from 9 lake level and 2 dust records (both from Shuman and Marsicek 2016). At the central sub-regional scale: (5) MTWM from 6 pollen records and (6) moisture from lake level and dust records in central North America. At the NE coastal site level: (7) alkenone-inferred SST from Scotian Margin core OCE326-GGC30 and (8) lake level reconstruction from New Long Pond, Massachusetts. All time series were binned at 100-year steps, detrended using a 6000-year Gaussian filter, and compared using Pearson's correlations and generalized least squares regression. Spectral analyses used multitaper spectral estimates. Correlations were evaluated against null distributions from 1000 random surrogate time series with matching spectral slopes.

## Methodological Notes

The study's strength lies in its systematic comparison of independent datasets across multiple spatial scales, with formal evaluation against random surrogates. The detrending approach (6000-year Gaussian filter) isolates centennial-to-millennial variability while removing orbital-scale trends. The 100-year time steps provide adequate resolution for multi-century events but cannot resolve sub-centennial variability. The random surrogate approach (power law time series matching the spectral slope of each paleoclimate record) provides a rigorous null expectation for evaluating whether observed correlations exceed noise. However, the geographic coverage is concentrated in mid-latitude North America (primarily 36-52 degrees N), with most records north of 40 degrees N. The central sub-region shows the weakest cen-mil correlations (r = -0.16, not significant), suggesting that the continental interior may have more variable or less coherent cen-mil signals than the Atlantic margin.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Shuman & Marsicek 2016** (shuman_marsicek_2016): This paper builds directly on the mid-latitude temperature and moisture reconstructions from Shuman and Marsicek (2016), using their datasets as two of the eight ensembles analyzed. The earlier paper provides the drought chronology (3.6-3.3 ka and 3.0-2.4 ka episodes) that Shuman (2024) places in the context of broader cen-mil patterns. The two papers are complementary: Shuman and Marsicek (2016) provides the regional structure, while Shuman (2024) evaluates whether the cen-mil features are distinguishable from noise.

- **Salonen et al. 2025** (salonen_etal_2025): Salonen et al. provide pollen-based water balance reconstructions with ~200-year periodicity in the Midwest and ~400-500-year periodicity in the Northeast. Shuman (2024) identifies a similar ~350-500-year spectral peak in Atlantic coastal records. The two papers agree that centennial-scale variability was spatially incoherent across regions, supporting the argument that inter-regional cooperation was adaptive. Salonen et al. provide the mechanistic insight (high evapotranspiration driving drought) that Shuman (2024) does not address directly.

- **Kaufman et al. 2020** (kaufman_etal_2020): Kaufman et al. compile 1,319 Holocene paleotemperature records globally. Shuman (2024) uses the continental-scale subset of this type of compilation (via Marsicek et al. 2018) as one of the eight datasets. The global perspective from Kaufman et al. provides context for the post-6 ka cooling trend within which the Poverty Point period falls, while Shuman (2024) characterizes the cen-mil variability superimposed on that trend.

- **Kidder 2006** (kidder_2006): Kidder identifies a 400-year chronological gap (3150-2700 cal BP) between Poverty Point and Early Woodland, linked to increased flood frequency. The 2900-2100 yr BP warm/dry event identified by Shuman (2024) partially overlaps with this gap, providing paleoclimate context for the post-Poverty Point disruption.
