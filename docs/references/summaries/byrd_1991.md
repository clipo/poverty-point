## Relevance to Argument

This edited volume from the 1986 Poverty Point Conference provides foundational empirical data on subsistence, settlement, and exchange that directly supports the costly signaling/aggregation model. Its most critical contributions are quantified resource uncertainty in nut mast production, geochemical sourcing of exotics to distant quarries, documentation of extreme concentration of exotic materials at the primary site rather than redistribution, and evidence that regional communities were subsistence-independent of a central authority.

## Data Presented

Jackson's Copes site analysis provides the most quantified data in this volume. Key datasets include: (1) faunal NISP counts across 17 mammalian, 28 bird, 18 reptilian, and 26 fish taxa; (2) nut mast variability data from forestry studies showing average yields at 30-45% of maximum and poor years at 2-7%, with synchronized high production only once per decade; (3) seasonality indicators placing occupation from March through November/December; (4) three AMS radiocarbon dates (1490-1090 B.C.). Smith provides distributional counts for steatite (6,352 sherds across 39 sites, 95.7% at Poverty Point) and galena (724 cubes/nodules, 97% at Poverty Point), along with neutron activation analysis identifying specific Appalachian quarry sources. Kidder maps four site clusters in northeastern Louisiana with data on exotic material distributions across them.

## Methodological Notes

The volume compiles papers from the 1986 Poverty Point Conference and includes transcribed discussion sections that capture collegial exchanges among researchers. Smith uses neutron activation analysis (NAA) with rare earth element (REE) curves to source steatite to specific quarries. Jackson's subsistence analysis draws on both archaeological faunal/botanical remains and modern forestry data for nut mast productivity. Bruseth applies Hirth's (1978) gateway community model to interpret site positioning. The PDF filename ("Byrd-2008") is misleading; the actual publication date is 1991.

## Connections to Other Sources

This volume connects to several other sources relevant to the project. Webb's contributions here extend his earlier work (Webb 1977, 1982) on the Poverty Point culture. Kidder's observations on site clusters and autonomous regional development anticipate his later work with Ortmann on Mound A construction (Ortmann and Kidder 2013). Smith's steatite sourcing builds on Walthall et al. (1982) for galena distribution data. Jackson's subsistence analysis at the Copes site provides a detailed case study complementing broader characterizations in Gibson (2000). Bruseth's gateway model draws on Hirth (1978) and Gibson (1980). The Discussion section references patterns later elaborated in studies of directional exchange at Poverty Point.

## Verification Notes

All claims extracted directly from the PDF. Page numbers refer to the volume's printed pagination (pp. 1-203). The PDF contains scan artifacts and some duplicate pages. The PDF filename ("Byrd-2008-The Poverty Point Culture.pdf") does not match the 1991 publication date; this is a digitization artifact. The volume's individual chapter authors (Webb, Bruseth, Kidder, Jackson, Smith, Johnson) are identified in the claims to distinguish their contributions from the editor's (Byrd). The nut mast variability data (Claims 29-30) derives from forestry studies cited by Jackson, not from direct archaeological measurement. The steatite and galena concentration figures (Claims 34, 37) are cumulative counts across all then-known sites and may have been updated by subsequent fieldwork.
