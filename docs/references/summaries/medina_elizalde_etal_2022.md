## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides a high-resolution stalagmite d18O record from War Eagle Cave, Alabama (34.5 degrees N, 86.2 degrees W), spanning 12.2 ka with time resolution ranging from 7 to 44 years. The cave is located approximately 2 degrees north of Poverty Point (32.63 degrees N), and spatial correlation analysis of instrumental precipitation data demonstrates coherent in-phase variability across much of the SE US including the lower Mississippi Valley, supporting the record's relevance to the Poverty Point region. The central finding, that spring-summer precipitation in the SE US increased by approximately 90% during Holocene abrupt cooling events associated with AMOC slowdown, establishes a key mechanism for understanding hydroclimate at Poverty Point's latitude during North Atlantic climate perturbations. However, a critical limitation exists: the stalagmite has a growth hiatus from 4.3 to 2.6 ka BP, meaning the Poverty Point occupation interval (ca. 3700-3100 BP) falls entirely within the gap. The record provides context for conditions before and after Poverty Point, and the replicated AMOC-wetting mechanism can be applied by analogy to the ca. 3.0-2.6 ka BP climate event, but no direct precipitation data exist from this proxy during the occupation itself.

## Data Presented

The study presents 688 d18O and d13C measurements from the PP_nda stalagmite at 500-micron sampling resolution, yielding temporal resolution of 7-44 years depending on growth rate. The chronology is constrained by 22 U/Th dates. Instrumental calibration data include a 5-year precipitation and d18O record (2005-2013) from Tuscaloosa, Alabama, and a century-long spatial correlation analysis of GPCC precipitation data (1901-2013) across the SE US. The d18O time series spans three windows: 12.2-10.8 ka BP, 9.4-4.3 ka BP, and 2.6-0.31 ka BP. Quantitative precipitation estimates are derived through a novel interpretive framework using the observed amount effect in seasonal precipitation. Data are available at the NOAA National Centers for Environmental Information.

## Methodological Notes

The interpretive framework is novel and well-calibrated against instrumental data, but depends on the assumption that the relationship between precipitation amount and d18O observed today held in the past. The authors acknowledge that isotope-enabled climate models for the specific boundary conditions of past events are not yet available, and that potential shifts in moisture source regions could affect the interpretation. The growth hiatus from 4.3 to 2.6 ka BP is a fundamental limitation, as it eliminates direct coverage of the Poverty Point interval. The authors interpret the hiatuses as drought-related (declining drip water infiltration), which itself provides indirect evidence: the 4.3 ka hiatus suggests arid conditions around the time of the globally recognized 4.2 ka event. The quantitative precipitation estimates (90% increase during AMOC events) carry uncertainty from the amount effect slope and from the assumption that seasonal precipitation contributions shifted rather than annual mean precipitation changing uniformly.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Kidder 2006**: Kidder's argument that increased precipitation and flooding caused the Archaic-Woodland transition (3000-2600 cal BP) is consistent with the AMOC-wetting mechanism documented here. If the ca. 3.0 ka climate event involved AMOC-related cooling, this study's replicated pattern predicts enhanced SE US spring-summer precipitation, which would increase flood risk as Kidder proposed.
- **Shuman & Marsicek 2016**: Their drought episodes at 3.6-3.3 ka (during PP) and 3.0-2.4 ka (terminal/post-PP) provide complementary mid-latitude evidence. The Medina-Elizalde record's hiatus prevents direct comparison during these intervals, but the two studies together suggest complex spatial patterns where different latitudes experienced different moisture anomalies.
- **Shuman 2024**: The cool/wet event at 3400-3100 yr BP documented by Shuman at higher latitudes may connect to the AMOC mechanism documented here; both suggest that cooling events enhanced moisture at some latitudes.
- **Liefert & Shuman 2022**: The 4.2 ka event documented at high-elevation Wyoming sites as drought may correspond to the conditions that caused the stalagmite growth hiatus at 4.3 ka BP, though the expression was spatially complex.
- **Bird et al. 2017**: The PNA teleconnection mechanism for midcontinental hydroclimate documented by Bird et al. operates on different timescales and through a different mechanism than the AMOC-driven pattern documented here; both may have been relevant at Poverty Point's latitude.
- **Salonen et al. 2025**: Centennial moisture variability documented in northern Europe was spatially incoherent across regions; the Medina-Elizalde record similarly shows that SE US hydroclimate responded differently from the Caribbean and tropical Atlantic during abrupt events.

## Verification Notes

All claims were extracted directly from the PDF text, with page numbers corresponding to the 18-page article. The growth hiatus from 4.3 to 2.6 ka BP is explicitly stated on p. 5 and is clearly visible in Figure 2. This hiatus is the single most important fact for the Poverty Point project: the record does not cover the occupation interval. The quantitative precipitation estimates (90% increase, range 75-130%) are stated on p. 8 and derived from the amount effect calibration. The cave location (34.5 degrees N) versus Poverty Point (32.63 degrees N) represents approximately 210 km of latitudinal separation; the spatial correlation analysis (Figure 1, p. 3) provides empirical support for coherent precipitation variability across this distance within the SE US interior. No discrepancies between claims and source text were identified.
