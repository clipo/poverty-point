# Summary: Bird et al. 2017

**Full citation**: Bird, B.W., Wilson, J.J., Gilhooly, W.P. III, Steinman, B.A., & Stamps, L. 2017. Midcontinental Native American population dynamics and late Holocene hydroclimate extremes. *Scientific Reports* 7:41628.

**Date summarized**: 2026-03-28

## Relevance to Argument

This paper reconstructs 2100 years of midcontinental US hydroclimate variability from a multi-proxy lake sediment record at Martin Lake, Indiana (41.56 degrees N), and correlates PNA-driven climate changes with Pre-Columbian Mississippian population dynamics. The record has 5-year resolution and spans approximately 100 BCE to 2013 CE.

**The Martin Lake record does not cover the Poverty Point period (ca. 1700-1100 BCE / 3700-3100 cal BP).** The record begins roughly 1000 years after the end of the Poverty Point occupation. This is the paper's primary limitation for the Poverty Point project: it provides no direct hydroclimate data for the relevant interval.

Despite this temporal gap, the paper makes three contributions to the project. First, it establishes the PNA teleconnection as the leading mode of midcontinental climate variability on seasonal to centennial timescales, with particularly strong PNA-precipitation correlations (r = -0.71) over the central Mississippi and lower Ohio River valleys (Fig. S4). If the PNA operated with comparable strength during the Poverty Point period, it would have been a primary driver of the environmental uncertainty (sigma) experienced by populations in the lower Mississippi Valley. Second, the paper quantifies how PNA phase affects precipitation seasonality: under negative PNA (wet midcontinent), southerly warm-season rainfall accounts for approximately 68% of annual precipitation; under extreme positive PNA (dry midcontinent), cold-season northerly sources dominate at 79%. This shift between 68% warm-season and 21% warm-season represents a dramatic change in growing-season moisture availability. Third, the structural argument that climate set preconditions for population aggregation and subsequent abandonment provides a comparative framework for the Poverty Point case, even though the Mississippian system involved agricultural rather than hunter-gatherer populations.

The geographic offset between Martin Lake (41.56 degrees N) and Poverty Point (32.63 degrees N) is approximately 9 degrees of latitude. Both locations fall within the PNA-correlated precipitation zone (Fig. 1), but the specific expression of PNA-driven changes may differ at Poverty Point's latitude.

## Data Presented

The paper presents three primary proxy datasets from Martin Lake, Indiana: (1) calcite oxygen isotope ratios (delta-18O_cal), which track precipitation source (southerly vs. northerly moisture) and PNA state; (2) calcite carbon isotope ratios (delta-13C_cal), which track warm-season thermal stratification duration; and (3) percent lithic material (terrestrial inorganic clastic fraction), which reflects warm-season rainstorm intensity. All three proxies are measured at 0.5 cm intervals yielding approximately 5-year resolution over the 2100-year record. The age model is based on eight AMS radiocarbon dates on charcoal and terrestrial plant material, calibrated with CALIB, plus the modern sediment-water interface as a control point.

The paper also presents a newly compiled regional database of human skeletal delta-13C from more than 1300 individuals across the eastern US spanning the last 2300 years (Fig. 6 and Table S3), which tracks the adoption of maize agriculture.

The Martin Lake record is compared against: (a) reconstructed winter PNA index (1725-1840 CE), (b) PDO reconstructions, (c) Northern Hemisphere temperature anomalies, (d) Midwest summer temperature from pollen records, (e) amoeba testate water-table reconstructions from Hole Bog (MN) and Minden Bog (MI), (f) tree-ring-based western US drought percentage (30-year low-pass filtered), and (g) diatom-inferred salinity from Moon Lake, ND.

## Methodological Notes

The multi-proxy approach is a strength: three independent proxies (delta-18O_cal, delta-13C_cal, percent lithics) respond to different aspects of the same climate driver (PNA-related precipitation seasonality), and their strong covariance (r = 0.81 for oxygen and carbon) provides internal validation. The modern calibration is thorough, including event-based precipitation isotope analysis (n = 98), HYSPLIT back trajectory analysis, and surface water isotope measurements from Martin Lake and regional Indiana lakes (n = 449). The mixing model for past moisture source estimation uses clearly defined end-members (warm-season at -5.5 per mil; cold-season at -16.4 per mil) with stated sensitivity (+/- 2 degrees C equates to +/- 0.5 per mil).

Key limitations: (1) The 2100-year temporal coverage does not reach the Poverty Point period. (2) The correlation between Martin Lake delta-18O_cal and the winter PNA index weakens after 1840 CE due to land clearance effects on watershed hydrology, though the pre-1840 relationship is strong. (3) Extrapolation of the PNA teleconnection framework to the Poverty Point period (3500+ years earlier) assumes stationarity of this atmospheric circulation pattern. (4) Martin Lake at 41.56 degrees N is roughly 9 degrees of latitude north of Poverty Point.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Shuman & Marsicek 2016** (shuman_marsicek_2016): Provides the mid-latitude temperature and moisture reconstructions for the same general region but covering the entire Holocene, including the Poverty Point period. Shuman and Marsicek document drought episodes at 3.6-3.3 ka and 3.0-2.4 ka that directly overlap with the Poverty Point occupation and post-occupation gap. Bird et al. complement Shuman and Marsicek by demonstrating the PNA mechanism driving hydroclimate variability in the midcontinental US, even though Bird et al.'s record does not extend to the PP period.

- **Shuman 2024** (shuman_2024): Evaluates centennial-to-millennial climate variability across mid-latitude North America and identifies multi-century cool/wet and warm/dry fluctuations, including events at 4200-3900 and 3400-3100 yr BP that bracket and overlap with the Poverty Point period. Bird et al. provide the mechanistic framework (PNA teleconnection) that may explain these fluctuations, while Shuman (2024) provides the longer temporal context.

- **Kidder 2006** (kidder_2006): Documents the 400-year chronological gap (3150-2700 cal BP) between Poverty Point and Early Woodland, linked to increased flood frequency. Bird et al.'s finding that PNA phase shifts can produce rapid, sustained changes in warm-season precipitation (e.g., the MCA-to-LIA transition over ~60 years) provides a mechanism for how such abrupt environmental disruptions could have occurred during the Poverty Point-to-Woodland transition, though no direct data exist.

- **Salonen et al. 2025** (salonen_etal_2025): Provides Holocene moisture variability from pollen-based proxies in the Midwest and Northeast, with centennial periodicity (~200 years in Midwest, ~400-500 years in Northeast). Bird et al.'s PNA framework provides a potential mechanism for the spatial incoherence between Midwest and Northeast hydroclimate documented by Salonen et al., since PNA-driven precipitation has different expressions at different latitudes.

- **Kaufman et al. 2020** (kaufman_etal_2020): Global compilation of Holocene paleotemperature records providing the broader context within which PNA-driven variability operates. The post-6 ka cooling trend documented by Kaufman et al. would have shifted the baseline atmospheric circulation patterns that modulate PNA expression.
