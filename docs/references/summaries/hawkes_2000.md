## Relevance to Argument

This chapter provides the empirical and theoretical anchor for treating male big-game hunting as competitive display rather than paternal provisioning, which underwrites the costly-signaling framework's interpretation of why mobile hunter-gatherers invest in cooperative-display activities (including monumental construction). The chapter is cited in Paper 1 §4.2 to ground the within-group reward parameter $\lambda_W$ in ethnographic data, with the caveat that the specific 10-20% reproductive-success premium attributed to it in Paper 1's prose is not stated in this chapter (see Verification Notes).

## Data Presented

- **Field period**: ~1 year (1985-1986) of direct observation among Hadza foragers in northern Tanzania (Lake Eyasi region)
- **Sample size**: 71 carcasses taken across 256 days of observation, 2,072 hunter-days (Hawkes et al. 1991, 1998)
- **Mean carcass weight**: 143 kg (live weight; adjusted when scavenged carcasses were not intact)
- **Hunter daily prey rate**: .034 kg/hunter-day; .12 kg/hr take-home rate
- **Hunter time investment**: 4.13 hrs/day on average
- **Hunter household share**: ≤10% of meat from own kills (significantly larger only for >100 kg carcasses)
- **Skill-ranking interview data**: confirmed inter-year consistency of Hadza men's ranking by hunting prowess (Blurton Jones et al. 1997)
- **Geographic scope**: Eastern Rift Valley of Tanzania; Hadza population ~750 people speaking a common language (Blurton Jones et al. 1992)
- **Comparative chimpanzee data**: cited from Wrangham and Bergmann-Riss 1990 (.037 prey/hunter-day at Gombe)

## Methodological Notes

- The chapter is a synthesis of long-term Hadza fieldwork by Hawkes and colleagues; methods of data collection are not detailed in the chapter itself but are referenced through citations to Hawkes et al. 1991, 1997, 1998.
- The variance argument relies on the geometric-mean computation, which Hawkes correctly notes goes to zero whenever a zero (failure) is encountered. This is the right argument for evaluating *individual* family provisioning under high-variance returns, but the framing assumes households are the operative unit; the chapter's broader argument is that they are not.
- The skill-ranking interview methodology (Blurton Jones et al. 1997) is described in passing but not in detail; the within-cohort consistency claim is robust but the methodology should be checked at the source.
- The cross-species (Hadza vs. chimpanzee) comparison uses different observation protocols and time intervals; the close match in success rates (.034 vs. .037) is striking but the comparison should be read with awareness of the methodological differences.
- Speculative paleoanthropological extensions (Sahul colonization, modern-human cognitive distinctiveness) are clearly flagged by Hawkes as speculative.

## Verification Notes

**Issue 1 (Critical for Paper 1):** Paper 1 line 143 attributes a "10-20% reproductive-success advantage" claim to Hawkes (2000). This figure is NOT present in this chapter. The chapter establishes (a) hunter ranking is consistent across years, (b) better hunters are valuable partners, and (c) the qualitative case for partner-choice premium, but does not provide a quantitative reproductive-success premium. The 10-20% figure likely derives from Hawkes 1991 ("Showing Off: Tests of an Hypothesis About Men's Foraging Goals," *Ethology and Sociobiology*) or a related Hawkes paper.
