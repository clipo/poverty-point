## Relevance to Argument

This chapter is the most comprehensive published review of CST in archaeology and is essential for situating the Poverty Point project within the theoretical literature. Quinn addresses the central critique that archaeological CST has functioned as a "just-so-story" lacking predictive and testable models (Codding and Jones 2007; Quinn 2015), which directly motivates the formal modeling approach taken in the Poverty Point manuscript. Several specific contributions bear on the project. First, Quinn legitimizes group-level signaling, noting that for phenomena like monumental construction, "the signaler is not always a single individual" (p. 281), and that Roscoe (2009) argues signaling co-occurs at individual and group levels. This is essential for Poverty Point, where the signaler is the aggregated community. Second, Quinn establishes that environmental uncertainty drives communal signaling, citing DiNapoli et al. (2017) on Rapa Nui: "In the uncertain and risky environment of Rapa Nui, signaling took the form of communal monument construction that emphasized cooperation" (p. 285). Third, Quinn provides a concrete analytical framework (Fig. 14.2, Table 14.1) with first-order variables (audience, context, attributes, production) and second-order variables (raw material distance, skill level, population density, visibility) that can be applied to Poverty Point materials. Fourth, the signal inflation mechanism (signals lose power as they become common and transferable) provides a testable prediction for temporal dynamics at the site. The chapter does not discuss hunter-gatherer contexts specifically, but the theoretical framework is scale-independent and applicable to aggregation-based societies.

## Data Presented

No primary data are presented. This is a theoretical review chapter. Quinn provides two figures: Fig. 14.1, a continuum of signaling power from non-costly to costly signals (after Quinn 2015); and Fig. 14.2, a general framework diagram for studying costly signaling behavior with material culture showing the interrelationship of signal information, social setting, material culture, audience, context, attributes, production, and abundance. Table 14.1 lists first-order and second-order variables for identifying and explaining material culture-based costly signaling, organized by artifact attributes, production, audience, and context categories.

## Methodological Notes

As a review chapter, Quinn synthesizes rather than generates new data. The analytical framework (Fig. 14.2, Table 14.1) was previously developed for lithics (Quinn 2015, pp. 207-212) and is here generalized to all material culture categories. Quinn explicitly distinguishes three theoretical lineages: (1) biological costly signaling (handicap principle), (2) human behavioral ecology (show-off hypothesis), and (3) non-evolutionary approaches to signaling through material culture (style, conspicuous consumption, gift-giving). The chapter argues these can be synthesized. Quinn acknowledges that problems of empirical testing remain the central challenge, and that "additional modeling is necessary to harness the explanatory power of costly signaling in the archaeological record" (p. 287).

## Connections to Other Sources

- **DiNapoli et al. 2017**: Quinn cites this Rapa Nui/Rapa Iti case study extensively as an example of linking environmental uncertainty to communal monumental signaling. This is from the same theoretical lineage as the Poverty Point project.
- **Neiman 1997**: First archaeological application of CST, focused on Maya monumental architecture and the role of ecological disaster in signaling dynamics.
- **Bliege Bird and Smith 2005**: Key reference for the 2005 synthesis in *Current Anthropology* that brought CST to broad attention in anthropological archaeology.
- **Codding and Jones 2007**: Source of the "just-so-story" critique that Quinn treats as the central challenge for CST.
- **Dunnell 1989; Madsen et al. 1999**: Earlier evolutionary frameworks for waste and cultural elaboration that precede CST's adoption; Madsen et al.'s fitness-as-statistical-summary concept provides a bridge to archaeological applications.
- **Roscoe 2009**: Argues signaling co-occurs at individual and group levels, cited by Quinn as unresolved but essential for community-scale signaling.
- **Kantner and Vaughn 2012**: Religious adherence as costly signal, with Chaco Canyon as case study; pilgrimage as costly signal is analogous to Poverty Point aggregation travel.
- **Galle 2010**: Provides a methodological template (abundance index) for testing costly signaling predictions with archaeological distributions.
- **Glatz and Plourde 2011**: Polities contesting territory through costly monumental architecture in Late Bronze Age Anatolia.
- **Hunt and Lipo 2011**: Rapa Nui moai as costly signals of community cooperative capacity.
- **Wandsnider 2013, 2015**: Multi-level social signaling through monumental civic architecture in Hellenistic/Greco-Roman cities.
- **Graves and Ladefoged 1995**: Early treatment of Polynesian ceremonial architecture as signaling.
