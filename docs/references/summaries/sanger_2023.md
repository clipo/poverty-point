## Relevance to Argument

This paper presents the most fully developed alternative framework for explaining the Poverty Point paradox (monumental construction without permanent hierarchy) that is not a costly signaling model. Sanger draws on anarchist political theory (Clastres 1989; Graeber and Wengrow 2021) and the concept of "institutional flexibility" to argue that Poverty Point communities deliberately oscillated between hierarchical organization during seasonal aggregation events and autonomous social relations during dispersal. The key insight is that monumental construction and ceremonial gatherings served not as platforms for elite advancement but as containment mechanisms: contexts in which aspiring leaders could exercise temporary authority while the community ensured that authority dissolved when groups dispersed.

For the costly signaling project, this paper is important in three ways. First, it provides a complementary rather than competing framework: institutional flexibility describes the social mechanism by which signaling is bounded within aggregation contexts, while costly signaling theory explains why individuals invest in costly displays within those contexts. Second, Sanger's synthesis of evidence for the absence of hierarchy (no elite burials, no restricted access to exotics, no permanent leadership roles) strengthens the empirical foundation for treating Poverty Point as a signaling arena rather than a political economy. Third, the ethnographic analogs for institutional flexibility (Mandan-Hidatsa buffalo hunting police forces, southeastern Green Corn Ceremony, Kolomoki ceremonial/domestic sphere division) provide concrete parallels for the aggregation-dispersal cycle that the signaling model requires.

The paper does not directly engage with costly signaling theory, but the two approaches converge: both reject the claim that monumental construction requires permanent hierarchy, and both treat aggregation events as functionally distinct from daily life. The key difference is explanatory emphasis: Sanger focuses on political dynamics (aggrandizer vs. deaggrandizer competition), while the signaling model focuses on adaptive fitness consequences (cooperation facilitated by honest costly signals).

## Data Presented

This paper does not present new primary data. It synthesizes existing archaeological, ethnographic, and theoretical sources. Key quantitative data cited from other sources include: site area (750 hectares), earth-moving volume (~750,000 m^3 for above-ground constructions), Mound A volume (238,500 m^3), lithic material mass (63,500 kg), construction timeline (Mound A in under 1 year, possibly 3 months), and labor estimate (up to 10,000 people). Ethnographic data include the Mandan-Hidatsa/Crow seasonal authority structure (Lowie 1948) and the southeastern Green Corn Ceremony (Jackson 2003). Archaeological comparison data come from Kolomoki (Pluckhahn 2010).

## Methodological Notes

The paper is primarily a theoretical synthesis rather than an empirical study. Its strength lies in applying anarchist political theory and the institutional flexibility concept to Poverty Point in a systematic way, providing an explicit mechanism for the absence of lasting hierarchy despite massive collective action. The weakness is that the institutional flexibility framework, like the aggrandizer model it critiques, is difficult to test directly against the archaeological record. Sanger acknowledges this limitation (p. 560-561), noting that archaeological examples of institutional flexibility "will often appear as paradoxes" because the fine-grained contextual details needed to identify the mechanism are archaeologically invisible. The ethnographic analogs are well-chosen but involve societies with different subsistence bases and social structures than Poverty Point's Late Archaic hunter-gatherer-fishers. The claim that high-value exotics remained at Poverty Point while PPOs were taken home (p. 565) is presented as interpretive and would benefit from systematic spatial analysis of exotic goods distribution across the regional settlement system.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Graeber and Wengrow 2021** (*The Dawn of Everything*): Primary theoretical source for institutional flexibility concept (pp. 98-111).
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: Source for Mound A construction data (238,500 m^3, single episode, 3 months, 10,000 workers). Sanger builds on their finding that rapid construction requires large aggregated labor forces.
- **Kidder et al. 2021**: Source for rapid ridge construction and updated understanding of construction complexity.
- **Hargrave et al. 2021**: Source for timber circles and evidence of previously unknown terraforming activities.
- **Ward et al. 2022**: Source for the autonomous, multiethnic character of communities around Poverty Point; challenges unified "Poverty Point culture" concept.
- **Gibson 2000**: Primary source for site description and the absence of hierarchy evidence.
- **Lowie 1948**: Key ethnographic analog (Mandan-Hidatsa/Crow seasonal authority).
- **Pluckhahn 2010**: Archaeological analog (Kolomoki ceremonial/domestic sphere division).
- **Kidder and Grooms 2025**: Shares the emphasis on ritual/religious leadership without lasting authority; their "revitalization" interpretation is complementary.
- **Jackson 1986**: Earlier argument for seasonal aggregation and exchange as social storage; Sanger extends this with the institutional flexibility mechanism.
- **Sassaman 2005**: Structuralist interpretation of Poverty Point that Sanger builds on for the multi-scalar social organization.
- **Dunnell and Greenlee 1999**: The "waste" hypothesis provides a parallel evolutionary explanation; Sanger's framework operates at the level of political dynamics rather than fitness consequences.
- **Peacock and Rafferty 2013**: Their bet-hedging interpretation of Archaic mound building is a competing adaptive framework; Sanger does not engage with it directly.
- **Quinn 2019**: CST review that identifies the challenge of distinguishing costly from non-costly signals; Sanger's institutional flexibility framework sidesteps this by focusing on the political function of ceremonial contexts rather than signal cost per se.
