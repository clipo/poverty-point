## Relevance to Argument

This is a foundational synthesis of Poverty Point culture that provides essential quantitative data for the costly signaling framework. Webb expands the known site inventory from 5 to 34 definite sites and triples the analyzed artifact assemblage to approximately 70,000 objects from the type-site alone. Three contributions are central to the project. First, Webb quantifies exotic material acquisition at an industrial scale: 2,300+ plummets (predominantly hematite and magnetite), 1,344 lapidary ornaments, 23,000+ microflints, copper from the Great Lakes, and at least 15 distinct exotic stone types sourced across the central United States. Second, he documents a regional site network spanning from Indiana to the Gulf Coast with clear hierarchical differentiation, where the type-site is uniquely characterized by massive mound construction, geometric village layout, and solar orientation. Third, his earth-moving estimate of nearly one million cubic yards for the type-site earthworks provides the baseline labor investment figure for the signaling model. Webb's Mesoamerican diffusion thesis is largely rejected today, but his empirical documentation of the anomalous scale of exotic material acquisition, lapidary production, and monumental construction by a non-agricultural society remains the core puzzle that the costly signaling framework addresses.

## Data Presented

Webb presents quantitative data at a scale unprecedented for Poverty Point studies. The primary quantitative inventories include: 70,000 total artifacts from 18 collections; 12,000 classified clay balls in 10 types; 10,214 projectile points in 10 major types with raw material source attributions; 23,000+ microflints; 2,300+ plummets with material composition (80% hematite, 20% magnetite) and 134 decorated specimens; 1,344 lapidary ornaments (~1,000 beads, 200 pendants); 617 ground stone items (~500 gorgets); 500+ pottery sherds (82 fiber-tempered, 33 sand-tempered, 247 clay-tempered); 34 copper beads plus other copper objects; and a 56-object bead-maker's kit. Site-level data include Table 1, which lists all 34 definite Poverty Point sites with diagnostic trait presence/absence, and Table 2, which compares artifact inventories at the type-site, Jaketown, and Teoc Creek. Earthwork measurements are provided for Mound A (640 x 70 ft.), Mound B (195 ft. diameter, 21 ft. high), Motley Mound (560 x 400 ft., 51 ft. high), and the concentric ridge system (3,960 ft. diameter, six ridges). The total earthwork volume is estimated at nearly one million cubic yards.

## Methodological Notes

Webb's study synthesizes data from surface collections, amateur collections, and limited excavations rather than systematic modern excavation. Of the 18 collections analyzed, most were assembled by local collectors (especially Carl Alexander) and artifact counts therefore reflect collection biases. Surface finds lack stratigraphic context, making it impossible to determine temporal variation within assemblages. Webb's diagnostic criteria for Poverty Point site identification (clay balls required; primary, secondary, and tertiary trait lists) established a formal classification system still influential today, though subsequent work has refined it. The chronological revision from 800-600 B.C. to 1500-1000 B.C. was prescient but lacked sufficient radiocarbon support at the time. The Mesoamerican diffusion thesis, which structures much of the interpretive discussion, reflects the theoretical framework of the era and should be treated as historical context rather than a current explanation. The earth-moving estimate of "nearly one million cubic yards" for the type-site earthworks is presented without detailed calculation methodology, and subsequent studies have produced varying figures.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Ford and Webb 1956** (Webb 1956 in collection): The predecessor publication that Webb explicitly updates, tripling the artifact sample and expanding the site inventory from 5 to 34. All comparative statements about expanded knowledge reference this baseline.
- **Haag and Webb 1953** (in collection): Earlier description of the Poverty Point site and its artifacts. Webb 1968 builds on and supersedes many of the artifact counts and trait lists from this work.
- **Webb 1982** (in collection): Webb's later synthesis in *Geoscience and Man*, which further refines the culture-historical framework established here.
- **Gagliano and Saucier 1963** (claims in collection): Cited for the coastal Poverty Point sites (Bayou Jasmine, Linsley, Garcia) and their radiocarbon dates.
- **Ortmann 2007** (claims in collection): Subsequent work on mound construction at Poverty Point that refines Webb's earth-moving estimates and construction chronology.
- **Bell 1956** (summary in collection): Webb 1968's copper finds (34 beads plus other objects) substantially expand on Bell's single copper plummet, establishing copper as a recurring if rare material class at the site.
- **Blackburn 2003** (summary in collection): Builds on Webb's observation of the 80:20 hematite-to-magnetite ratio among plummets and the geographic patterning of drilled vs. grooved forms.
- **Lipo 2012** (claims in collection): Reanalyzes the groundstone plummets that Webb inventories here, proposing functional reinterpretation.
- **Pierce 1997** (claims in collection): Addresses the variable shapes of Poverty Point clay balls that Webb classifies into types.
- **Jackson 1981, 1986** (claims in collection): Subsequent subsistence research that rejected Webb's speculation about agriculture, confirming the hunter-gatherer economy that makes Poverty Point's monumentality anomalous.
