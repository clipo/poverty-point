**Full citation**: Kidder, T.R., Kai, S., Henry, E.R., Grooms, S.B., & Ervin, K. 2021. Multi-method geoarchaeological analyses demonstrates exceptionally rapid construction of Ridge West 3 at Poverty Point. *Southeastern Archaeology* 40(3):212-227.

## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides the most direct evidence that ridge construction at Poverty Point, like Mound A construction (Ortmann and Kidder 2013), was exceptionally rapid, with no pauses exceeding days to weeks. Through re-excavation and multi-method analysis (micromorphology, magnetic susceptibility, sequential loss-on-ignition, radiocarbon dating) of a 1x1 m unit on Ridge West 3, the authors demonstrate continuous construction in two stages with no evidence of weathering, erosion, or bioturbation at any fill boundary. The paper further establishes that RW3 and Mound A were built within a similar narrow temporal window (after ca. 3370-3210 cal BP), representing a sudden transformation of the site landscape. For the costly signaling model, this is critical because rapid construction of massive earthworks requires mobilizing a large labor force for sustained, concentrated effort. The absence of construction pauses means the labor investment was genuinely costly (not diluted over generations), the construction act itself was visible and dramatic (consistent with honest signaling), and the coordination required implies social mechanisms capable of motivating collective action without hierarchical authority.

## Data Presented

The paper presents multi-method geoarchaeological data from a re-excavated 1x1 m unit (5276N 4790E) on Ridge West 3 at Poverty Point, originally excavated by Gibson in 1991. Analytical methods include: (1) stratigraphic analysis confirming Gibson's original four-event sequence; (2) magnetic susceptibility (low- and high-frequency) measurements documenting anthropogenic enrichment patterns through the profile; (3) sequential loss-on-ignition (LOI) for organic matter and carbonate content; (4) micromorphology from six thin-section slides identifying three fill types and confirming no weathering at boundaries; and (5) twelve radiocarbon dates (Table 1) from six excavation units, including five new AMS dates. Artifact density data (PPO fragments and chipped stone counts) are presented from both Gibson's 1991 and Connolly's 1999 excavations on RW3. A kernel density estimate (KDE) model of all Poverty Point radiocarbon dates provides the site-wide chronological framework (modeled occupation 3910-2790 cal BP).

## Methodological Notes

The primary strength of this study is its multi-method approach: the rapid-construction conclusion is supported independently by micromorphology (no weathering at boundaries), magnetic susceptibility (patterns consistent with anthropogenic enrichment, not in situ soil formation), LOI (heterogeneous organic/carbonate content reflecting fill mixing, not pedogenesis), and radiocarbon dating (overlapping dates in Aapb and ridge fills). The main limitation is the spatial scope: conclusions are drawn from a single 1x1 m excavation unit. The authors acknowledge this (p. 225: "confirmation of these results from other contexts is needed"). Radiocarbon dates from ridge fill contexts are not in their original depositional location (assumption 4, p. 223), meaning they provide termini post quem but cannot directly date construction. The large error on Tx-5324 (130-year error) limits chronological precision. The inference of fall construction seasonality is based on absence of evidence (no erosion) rather than positive evidence, making it plausible but not demonstrated.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: The direct parallel study demonstrating rapid construction of Mound A (less than a year, no pauses). This paper extends the same conclusion to ridge construction using the same analytical approach.
- **Kidder and Grooms 2025**: Builds on and cites these findings; characterizes the entire earthwork construction as a late, rapid transformation of the site landscape, with total earthmoving estimated at 1,000,000 m^3.
- **Gibson 1993**: The original excavation report for unit 5276N 4790E. The 2021 paper re-excavates and reanalyzes this unit with methods unavailable in 1991.
- **Gibson 1987b, 2000**: Gibson's "decades" estimate for ridge construction is tested and refined: construction was even faster than decades, occurring over days to weeks.
- **Kidder 2006**: The climate change paper provides the terminal constraint for the Poverty Point system; the modeled end date (3060-2790 cal BP) in this paper is consistent.
- **Sherwood and Kidder 2011**: Geoarchaeological perspectives on mound building that established the methodological framework applied here.
- **Jackson 1986**: The episodic construction model consistent with seasonal aggregation is supported by the rapid construction evidence: building happened in concentrated episodes, not gradual accumulation.
- **Connolly 1999, 2001, 2002**: Additional excavation data from RW3 used for artifact density comparisons confirming rapid ridgetop accumulation.

## Verification Notes

All claims were extracted directly from the PDF (paginated pp. 212-227 in *Southeastern Archaeology* 40(3)). Radiocarbon dates were verified against Table 1 in the paper. The 7.5 km^2 site area figure (p. 213) differs from the 3 km^2 figure in Kidder (2006, p. 195-196); the larger figure includes the full Motley-to-Lower Jackson extent rather than just the core earthwork area. The modeled site occupation span (ca. 3910-2790 cal BP, from KDE model in Figure 4) should be noted as broader than the 3535-3135 cal BP range in Kidder and Grooms (2025), which may reflect different date filtering criteria or modeling approaches. The "days, weeks, or perhaps a month or two" construction duration (p. 224) is stated for both RW3 and Mound A; this is an interpretation based on the absence of weathering evidence rather than a directly measured duration.
