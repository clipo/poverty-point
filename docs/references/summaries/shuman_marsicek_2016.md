## Relevance to Argument

This study reconstructs the structure of Holocene climate change across mid-latitude North America using a network of 40 well-resolved paleoclimate datasets spanning temperature, effective moisture, and sea surface temperature proxies. It provides the most directly relevant large-scale paleoclimate framework for understanding environmental conditions during the Poverty Point period (3.7-3.1 ka / 1700-1100 BCE). Three findings bear directly on the costly signaling model. First, the Poverty Point occupation developed within a long-term cooling and moistening trend initiated at 5.5 ka, following the end of the Holocene thermal maximum; baseline conditions were generally wetter than mid-Holocene averages. Second, multi-century drought episodes at 3.6-3.3 ka overlap with peak Poverty Point activity, while a later drought at 3.0-2.4 ka coincides with the terminal phase and post-abandonment period. These episodic perturbations, superimposed on an otherwise favorable moistening trend, create precisely the intermediate environmental uncertainty (sigma) that the model predicts should favor cooperative signaling systems. Third, the strong inverse correlation between temperature and moisture (r = -0.90) means that climate shifts would have affected multiple resource domains simultaneously, amplifying uncertainty for populations exploiting diverse ecological zones. The principal limitation is geographic: the study's records are concentrated above 39 degrees N, with no sites in the lower Mississippi Valley or Gulf Coast, so relevance to Poverty Point (32.6 degrees N) requires cautious extrapolation.

## Data Presented

The study synthesizes 40 paleoclimate records from mid-latitude North America (primarily 39-50 degrees N), spanning the Rocky Mountains to the Atlantic coast. Records include lake-level reconstructions, pollen-based temperature estimates, alkenone-based SST reconstructions, and other proxy types. All records were standardized to z-scores and analyzed at 200-year resolution. The dataset is divided into four sub-regions: western/central mid-continent, eastern Great Lakes/Northeast, northern Atlantic coast, and southeastern sites. Regional mean temperature and effective moisture time series were computed with bootstrap confidence intervals. Rate-of-change analysis identifies intervals of abrupt climate shift. Key quantitative results include the r = -0.90 temperature-moisture correlation, the 51.7% of variance explained by insolation and ice sheet area, and the 25-50% magnitude of abrupt events relative to long-term trends.

## Methodological Notes

The study's strength lies in its multi-proxy, multi-site approach with replication across sub-regions, which provides robust identification of widespread climate events versus local noise. The 200-year temporal resolution is appropriate for detecting multi-century events but cannot resolve sub-centennial variability. Age models for individual records were not re-evaluated; the authors relied on published chronologies. The standardization to z-scores enables comparison across proxies but obscures absolute magnitudes of change at individual sites. The geographic coverage is concentrated north of the Poverty Point region, so extrapolation southward should be treated as providing broad climatic context rather than site-specific conditions. The eastern sub-region results, drawn from nine sites using three data types, are the most geographically proximate to the lower Mississippi Valley and should be weighted accordingly.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Kidder et al. 2008; Ortmann & Kidder 2013**: Poverty Point chronology and construction episodes that can be compared against the climate intervals identified here.
- **Gibson 2000**: Provides the cultural context and occupation dates for Poverty Point that align with the 3.7-3.1 ka climate window.
- **Kelly 2013**: Hunter-gatherer lifeways and resource strategies that would have been affected by the temperature-moisture covariance documented here.
- **Bliege Bird & Smith 2005; Hawkes & Bliege Bird 2002**: Signaling theory framework that predicts intermediate environmental uncertainty should favor cooperative signaling, consistent with the episodic drought pattern documented in this study.
- **Other Gulf Coast/SE paleoclimate studies**: The geographic gap south of 39 degrees N means this study should be complemented by any available paleoclimate records closer to the Poverty Point region.
