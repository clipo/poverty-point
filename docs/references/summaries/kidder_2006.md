## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides the primary empirical evidence for the environmental conditions that terminated the Poverty Point signaling system. Through Bayesian radiocarbon analysis, Kidder demonstrates a basin-wide chronological gap of approximately 400 years (ca. 3150-2700 cal B.P.) separating the end of the Late Archaic/Poverty Point from the beginning of Early Woodland. The paper links this gap to a global climate event (ca. 3000-2600 cal B.P.) that increased flooding frequency and magnitude in the Mississippi River watershed, disrupting exchange networks and destabilizing the resource base. For the costly signaling model, this paper grounds the environmental shock parameter: it identifies the mechanism by which environmental uncertainty exceeded the threshold at which aggregation-based cooperation and signaling remained viable. The documented contrast between Late Archaic complexity and Early Woodland simplification also provides a test implication for the model, as it shows the signaling system did not simply reconstitute after disruption.

## Data Presented

The paper presents Bayesian chronological modeling (OxCal) of radiocarbon dates from multiple regions of the Mississippi River basin, including the lower Mississippi Valley, southeast Missouri, and other sub-regions. These analyses quantify the chronological gap between Late Archaic and Early Woodland occupations. The paper also synthesizes paleoclimate proxy data (delta-14C records, speleothem data, pollen records) to document the global climate event at ca. 3000-2600 cal B.P. Specific quantitative outputs include: the 400-year hiatus in the lower Mississippi Valley (3150-2700 cal B.P.), the 600-year hiatus in southeast Missouri (3100-2500 cal B.P.), and OxCal agreement indices demonstrating statistical robustness of the chronological models.

## Methodological Notes

The paper's strength lies in its systematic application of Bayesian radiocarbon modeling across multiple regions, providing statistically rigorous evidence for the chronological gap. The climate-to-culture causal argument is correlational rather than mechanistic: increased flooding is inferred from climate proxy data and geomorphological evidence, but the specific effects on human populations are reconstructed rather than directly observed. The paper acknowledges this limitation by framing the climate event as "one cause" rather than the sole driver of the Archaic-Woodland transition. The synthesis of global climate data with regional archaeological chronologies is methodologically sound, though the spatial resolution of paleoclimate proxies does not perfectly match the archaeological record in the lower Mississippi Valley.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Kidder and Grooms 2025**: Extends and updates the flooding/landscape disruption argument with more detailed geomorphological evidence for meander belt relocation in the lower Mississippi Valley.
- **Ortmann 2007**: Cites this paper for the approximately 500-year post-Poverty Point hiatus and uses it to frame the uniqueness of Poverty Point as a construction phenomenon.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013**: The rapid construction timeline for Mound A (months rather than years) links to the aggregation-based labor model; the climate event documented here provides the terminal constraint.
- **Saunders and Allen 2003**: Cited for the 1,813 km^2 settlement area and terminal dates.
- **Gibson 2000**: Provides the broader Poverty Point site description and cultural context that this paper situates within a climate framework.
- **Sassaman 2005**: Offers an alternative (structural/processual) framework for Poverty Point that can be contrasted with the climate-driven collapse model.
