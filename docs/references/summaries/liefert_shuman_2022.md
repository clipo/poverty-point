**PDF filename**: Liefert_Shuman_2022.pdf

## Relevance to Argument

This paper examines the expression of the 4.2 ka event (ca. 4200-4000 cal BP / 2250-2050 BCE) in the southern Rocky Mountains using a new carbonate oxygen isotope (d18Ocarb) record from Highway 130 Lake in southeastern Wyoming, compared against existing lake-level, dune-field, pollen, and isotope records from the region. The 4.2 ka event immediately precedes the Poverty Point period (ca. 3700-3100 BP), making it a potential antecedent environmental stressor relevant to the costly signaling model.

The paper's main contribution to the Poverty Point project is twofold. First, it documents a regionally significant drought at 4.2-4.0 ka in the North American interior, with evidence from dune reactivation, pollen-inferred precipitation decline (~150-year drought centered on 4.2 ka), isotopic excursion (2.9 SD peak at Highway 130 Lake), and lake-level declines in Colorado, extending geographically to the Atlantic margin. This places a major environmental disruption 300-500 years before the onset of Poverty Point, consistent with the model's prediction that environmental uncertainty creates conditions favoring cooperative signaling systems. Second, the paper demonstrates that the event's expression was spatially complex: different proxy types, elevations, and hydrologic settings produced different or even opposite signals within the same region. A low-elevation Colorado lake (Hidden Lake) experienced wetting from 4.4-3.7 ka while surrounding high-elevation lakes dried. This spatial complexity is directly relevant to the Poverty Point model's assumptions about environmental uncertainty. Populations occupying diverse ecological zones (as at Poverty Point's ecotone location) would have experienced heterogeneous climate impacts, reinforcing the adaptive value of inter-group cooperation and information exchange.

The principal limitation is geographic distance: the study focuses on the southern Rocky Mountains (41 degrees N, 3199 m elevation) while Poverty Point sits in the lower Mississippi Valley (32.6 degrees N, ~21 m elevation). The paper does not directly document conditions in the Gulf Coastal Plain or lower Mississippi Valley. However, the broad geographic extent of the drought (documented from the Rockies to the Atlantic margin) and the connection to Mississippi River headwaters make the findings indirectly relevant.

## Data Presented

The paper presents new data from Highway 130 Lake (HL), a small (0.02 km2) high-elevation (3199 m) subalpine lake in the Medicine Bow Mountains, southeastern Wyoming. A 333 cm sediment core spanning the early Holocene to present was analyzed for organic and carbonate content (loss on ignition at 550 and 1000 degrees C), d18Ocarb and d13Ccarb (measured via Thermo Gasbench and Delta Plus XL mass spectrometer), and eight AMS radiocarbon dates on charcoal (Table 2, calibrated using IntCal13). The age-depth model was constructed using Bchron and geoChronR. Modern lake-water d18O and dD were measured biweekly from June to October (2015-2017), along with snowfall, snowpack, rain, and groundwater samples. Water level and temperature were logged at 30-minute intervals (October 2016 onward), and specific conductance was measured at 30-minute intervals (January 2017 onward). The paper compares the HL record against published d18Ocarb records from Bison Lake and Yellow Lake (Colorado), lake-level records from Little Molas, Emerald, and Upper Big Creek lakes (Colorado), dune-field chronologies from Ferris/Seminoe and Casper (Wyoming), and pollen records from Long Lake (Wyoming).

## Methodological Notes

The study combines modern limnological monitoring with paleolimnological analysis to interpret the d18Ocarb record. A key methodological insight is that the isotopic response of lakes to climate change depends heavily on site-specific hydrology. Highway 130 Lake is hydrologically open with short residence time, fed primarily by seasonal snowmelt and groundwater. Its d18Ocarb may reflect cold-season (winter/spring) precipitation rather than warm-season evaporative enrichment, unlike the widely applied conceptual models for closed-basin lakes. This means the HL record may track changes in the snow-to-rain ratio or winter precipitation amount rather than summer drought per se. The contrast with Bison and Yellow lakes (which showed no 4.2 ka signal) is attributed to differences in carbonate formation timing: HL may precipitate carbonate in late winter/early spring under ice, while the Colorado lakes precipitate carbonate during summer. This site-specific sensitivity is a strength (it captures a different aspect of climate) but also a limitation (it complicates direct comparison with other isotope records). The radiocarbon chronology includes one potentially reworked age at 119 cm depth, but the overall age-depth model is well constrained for the 4.2-4.0 ka interval by four radiocarbon ages.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Shuman 2024** (shuman_2024): Shuman (2024) places the 4.2 ka event in the broader context of centennial-to-millennial Holocene variability and finds that it does not stand out as the most distinct feature in multi-record ensembles, even though it was regionally significant. Liefert and Shuman (2022) support this conclusion by showing the event was prominent in the southern Rocky Mountains but absent in some nearby proxy records. The warm/dry event at 4200-3900 yr BP identified in Shuman (2024) corresponds to the same event documented here.

- **Shuman & Marsicek 2016** (shuman_marsicek_2016): Shuman and Marsicek document drought at 4.2-3.9 ka as part of a broader pattern of warm/dry events in eastern North America, with moisture reaching its minimum at 4.2-3.9 ka followed by progressive increase. Liefert and Shuman (2022) provide complementary evidence from the western portion of the continent, confirming the drought extended from the Rockies to the Atlantic margin. The Shuman and Marsicek finding that effective moisture progressively increased after 4.0 ka is consistent with the termination of the HL isotope excursion at approximately 4.0 ka.

- **Booth et al. 2005**: Cited extensively for documenting severe centennial-scale drought in mid-continental North America 4000 years ago with apparent global linkages. This is the primary reference for the "megadrought" interpretation of the 4.2 ka event in North America.

- **Carter et al. 2013, 2017, 2018**: Provide the pollen-based evidence from Long Lake for a 150-year drought with increased temperature and decreased springtime precipitation centered at 4.2 ka.

- **Kidder 2006** (kidder_2006): Kidder documents a chronological gap after Poverty Point (3150-2700 cal BP) linked to environmental disruption. The 4.2 ka drought documented by Liefert and Shuman precedes this gap by about a millennium but establishes the pattern of environmental disruption in the region.
