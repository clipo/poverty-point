## Relevance to Argument

This paper directly challenges the premise that "Poverty Point culture" represents a unified cultural system radiating outward from the type site. Drawing on new excavation data from Jaketown (22HU505), the largest contemporary site after Poverty Point itself, Ward et al. demonstrate that diagnostic traits (PPOs, imported lithics, earthwork construction) appeared at Jaketown centuries before they were prominent at Poverty Point. The paper reframes the relationship between the two sites as multipolar rather than hierarchical: communities selectively participated in shared exchange networks and architectural traditions while maintaining localized practices, especially in foodways. For the costly signaling model, this has several implications. First, the signaling system cannot be modeled as diffusion from a single center; multiple communities independently adopted signaling behaviors, consistent with convergence driven by shared environmental and social pressures. Second, the variation in practices across sites (distinct foodways, different architectural details) suggests that what was signaled was not cultural membership per se but rather commitment to specific cooperative relationships, with local identity markers maintained alongside shared participation signals. Third, the temporal staggering of trait adoption supports a model in which communities assess costs and benefits of participation independently rather than responding to a centralized directive.

## Data Presented

The paper presents nine new AMS radiocarbon dates from Jaketown (Table 1), calibrated using OxCal v4.4 and IntCal20. Dates range from 4525-4100 cal BP (earliest pit beneath Mound A) to 3385-3245 cal BP (Mound A surface directly below alluvium). Stratigraphic profiles from Mound A (Figure 5), Mound X (Figure 6), Trench 1 (Figure 3), and excavation units (Figures 4, 7, 8) document construction sequences and post-flood alluvial deposits. Paleoethnobotanical data are presented as percent ubiquity across three sites (Figure 9: Jaketown n=24 samples, Poverty Point n=73, J.W. Copes n=42), showing dramatically different plant-use profiles. Micromorphological, particle-size, magnetic susceptibility, and sediment thin-section analyses were conducted but detailed results are not presented in this paper.

## Methodological Notes

The study reexcavated previously opened trenches from Ford et al. (1955) rather than opening new units, minimizing disturbance to this culturally significant site. Methods included standard stratigraphic description, systematic soil sampling, artifact recovery by screen (6.35 mm mesh) and hand excavation, flotation for paleoethnobotanical recovery (heavy and light fractions to 0.425 mm), and micromorphological analysis of construction fill. Column samples were analyzed for particle size, magnetic susceptibility, and sediment thin sections at Washington University in St. Louis. Radiocarbon dates were obtained on carbonized seeds and nutshell from secure contexts, calibrated using OxCal v4.4 with IntCal20. The paleoethnobotanical comparison (Figure 9) draws on previously published data from Poverty Point (Ward 1998) and J.W. Copes (Jackson 1989), making the cross-site comparison dependent on methodological compatibility across different research programs and recovery methods.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Grooms, Ward, and Kidder 2023** (grooms_ward_kidder_2023): Companion paper providing the full Bayesian chronological model for Jaketown with 26 AMS dates and four occupation phases. Ward et al. 2022 presents the site-level findings that the 2023 paper integrates into a formal chronological framework.
- **Kidder, Grooms, and Ervin 2024** (kidder_grooms_2024): The most rigorous Bayesian chronology across 12 Poverty Point-period sites, which incorporates the Jaketown dates from this paper and reaches the same conclusion about independent site trajectories.
- **Kidder, Henry, and Arco 2018** (kidder_henry_arco_2018): Provides the flood chronology for Jaketown that this paper corroborates: Poverty Point occupation ended ca. 3310 cal BP when crevasse splay buried the site, with a 530-year abandonment gap.
- **Kidder 2006** (kidder_2006): The regional climate change and flooding framework that explains the termination of Jaketown and other LMV sites.
- **Ortmann and Kidder 2013** (ortmann_kidder_2013): Rapid construction of Mound A at Poverty Point parallels the rapid construction documented for Jaketown's Mounds A and X.
- **Kidder et al. 2021** (kidder_etal_2021): Rapid construction of Ridge West 3 at Poverty Point; same construction tempo as Jaketown earthworks.
- **Sassaman 2005** (sassaman_2005): Provides the "corporate and pluralistic" framework for Poverty Point's social geography that Ward et al. follow in situating Jaketown as an associated site of both localized and integrative practice.
- **Hays 2018** (hays_2018): PPOs as cooking tools for ritualized feasting; Ward et al. find feasting deposits with PPOs beneath Mound X, consistent with Hays's interpretation.
- **Jackson 1986** (jackson_1986): Critique of the Poverty Point chiefdom model and argument for seasonal aggregation/exchange as risk-buffering; Ward et al.'s multipolar framework is consistent with Jackson's earlier arguments.
- **Hargrave et al. 2021** (hargrave_etal_2021): Timber circles at Poverty Point; Ward et al. compare the large posts at Jaketown to post circles at Poverty Point, noting similarities but also differences (posts placed in earthwork fill at Jaketown but not at Poverty Point).
- **Lehmann 1990** (lehmann_1990): Foreign lithics in the Yazoo Basin; Ward et al. draw on Lehmann's documentation of exchange patterns to contextualize Jaketown's nonlocal materials.
- **Webb 1982** (webb_1982): Classic Poverty Point culture definition; Ward et al. argue against the culture-historical framework Webb established.

## Verification Notes

All claims were extracted from the PDF (paginated pp. 758-775 in *American Antiquity* 87(4)). Radiocarbon dates in Table 1 were checked against the text and are internally consistent. The earliest date (UGA-41847: 3910 +/- 70 RCYBP, calibrated 4525-4100 cal BP) is from a secure context beneath Mound A and is associated with a biconical PPO. The steatite date (Beta-555137: 3260 +/- 30 RCYBP, calibrated 3565-3395 cal BP) is from Mound X enriched fill. The paleoethnobotanical comparison in Figure 9 uses previously published data; sample sizes are modest (Jaketown n=24, Poverty Point n=73, J.W. Copes n=42) but the differences in persimmon and chenopod representation are substantial. The 80 ha site size for Jaketown is consistent with other published descriptions. The paper's chronological claims are consistent with and further developed in Grooms, Ward, and Kidder (2023) and Kidder, Grooms, and Ervin (2024), both already in the reference pipeline.
