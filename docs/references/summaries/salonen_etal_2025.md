## Relevance to Argument

This paper provides the most comprehensive proxy-model synthesis of Holocene moisture variability in eastern North America, combining machine-learning-calibrated pollen reconstructions from 53 sites with four transient climate model simulations spanning the last 12,000 years. Its findings bear directly on the environmental conditions and uncertainty regime that shaped Poverty Point adaptive strategies.

Three results are particularly relevant to the costly signaling model. First, the Poverty Point period (ca. 3.7-3.1 ka) fell during a transition from peak mid-Holocene drought toward wetter conditions, meaning populations operated under drier-than-modern baselines that were gradually ameliorating. Second, this drought was driven not by reduced precipitation but by temperature-induced evapotranspiration exceeding rainfall gains, a mechanism that affected effective moisture and resource productivity even when rain was adequate. Third, centennial-scale variability with approximately 200-year periodicity was spatially incoherent across regions: different areas experienced moisture stress at different times. This spatial incoherence creates precisely the conditions under which inter-regional cooperation and information exchange, the behaviors predicted by the costly signaling model, become adaptive.

A key caveat is that the study regions (42-51N) lie several hundred kilometers north of Poverty Point (32.63N), so results must be applied with caution. The Midwest cluster is the most geographically proximate and the most directly applicable to Poverty Point's environmental context.

## Data Presented

- Boosted regression tree (BRT) calibrated pollen-based reconstructions of water balance (P minus PET) and July temperature from 53 fossil pollen sites across three regions (Midwest, Great Lakes, Northeast)
- Transient climate simulations from four models: two MPI-ESM experiments (with and without orbital acceleration) and two EC-Earth experiments (with and without Laurentide Ice Sheet effects)
- PCA decomposition of water balance reconstructions (PC1: 37.0% variance; PC2: 22.5% variance)
- Wavelet analyses of centennial-to-millennial periodicities in both proxy reconstructions and model simulations
- Quantitative drought magnitudes: peak aridity approximately -110 mm (Midwest), centennial variability amplitude up to approximately 50 mm
- Time series spanning 12 ka to present, with focus on mid-Holocene drought (8-5 ka) and neoglacial amelioration (4-2 ka)

## Methodological Notes

- Proxy reconstructions use boosted regression trees (BRT) calibrated against modern pollen-climate relationships, a machine-learning approach that handles nonlinear pollen-climate relationships better than traditional transfer functions.
- The study's three geographic clusters (MW: 42-51N, 92-100W; GL: 42-49N, 82-91W; NE: 39-47N, 72-81W) all lie north of Poverty Point (32.63N, 91.41W). The Midwest cluster is closest geographically, but a several-hundred-kilometer latitudinal offset remains. Results should be treated as indicative of broad regional patterns rather than site-specific reconstructions for the Poverty Point locality.
- The water balance metric (P minus PET) integrates precipitation and temperature effects into a single moisture index. This is useful as an integrative measure but does not directly capture resource-relevant variables such as river discharge, flood frequency, or ecosystem productivity.
- The EC-Earth experiments that include Laurentide Ice Sheet deglaciation effects substantially improve the data-model match, particularly for the early-to-mid Holocene. The LIS altered atmospheric circulation (jet stream positioning), affecting moisture delivery to mid-latitude North America.
- Centennial variability estimates come from a subset of high-resolution pollen records; full spatial coverage at this temporal resolution is limited.
