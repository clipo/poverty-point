## Relevance to Argument

This paper develops and empirically validates a mathematical framework for quantifying variation in culturally transmitted technologies, applied to Clovis projectile points. Its relevance to the Poverty Point costly signaling project is primarily methodological and analogical. First, it establishes baseline copying error rates (Weber fraction ~5%) and transmission bias parameters (lambda = 0.38) for hunter-gatherer lithic technologies, providing quantitative benchmarks against which Poverty Point object (PPO) variation could be measured. Second, it demonstrates that artifact size distributions in hunter-gatherer assemblages follow lognormal distributions predicted by multiplicative copying error processes, offering an analytical template for PPO morphometric analysis. Third, the authors explicitly suggest that complex flintknapping may function as a costly signal, directly connecting cultural transmission theory to the signaling framework applied in this project. The paper does not address Poverty Point directly but provides tools and precedents applicable to decomposing sources of variation in PPO assemblages and exotic artifact attributes.

## Data Presented

The paper analyzes a dataset of Clovis projectile point measurements drawn from published compilations. Key data include: (1) projectile point size measurements (length, width, thickness) from multiple Clovis sites across North America; (2) geographic coordinates of find locations used to calculate distance from the ice-free corridor; (3) temporal ordering based on geographic expansion trajectory. The analysis yields specific parameter estimates: Weber fraction sigma_e = 0.063, drift parameter alpha = -0.002, bias parameter lambda = 0.38, and the proportion of total variance attributable to copying error (~23%). No raw data tables are provided in the paper; results are presented graphically and through model fitting.

## Methodological Notes

The analytical approach combines mathematical modeling of cultural transmission with empirical testing using archaeological data. The ACE model treats artifact dimensions as continuous traits subject to multiplicative error during each transmission event, producing lognormal distributions and deterministic drift. The BACE extension incorporates conformist or prestige bias, which bounds variance. The authors use maximum likelihood estimation to fit model parameters to the Clovis dataset. A key assumption is that geographic distance from the ice-free corridor serves as a proxy for generational time, which is reasonable for the initial Clovis expansion but would not apply in the same way to a sedentary or seasonally aggregating population like Poverty Point. The decomposition of variance into copying error versus other sources relies on comparing observed variance against model predictions. The framework is mathematically rigorous and the empirical validation is convincing for the Clovis case.

## Connections to Other Sources

- The ACE/BACE framework builds on Eerkens and Lipo (2005) for the Weber fraction concept and copying error in cultural transmission, and on Boyd and Richerson (1985) for biased transmission models.
- The costly signaling interpretation of flintknapping connects to Bliege Bird and Smith (2005) and Hawkes and Bliege Bird (2002), both cited in the project's theoretical framework.
- The Clovis baseline parameters could be compared against variation in PPO assemblages documented by Pierce (1997) and other Poverty Point lithic analyses.
- The lognormal distribution prediction is testable against PPO dimension data from Poverty Point excavation reports.
- The demographic inference method (founder effects in artifact variation) could be applied to questions about Poverty Point aggregation stability, connecting to Kelly (2013) on hunter-gatherer mobility and aggregation.
