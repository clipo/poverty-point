## Relevance to Argument

This monograph is the foundational publication on the Poverty Point site and culture. It provides the first comprehensive description of the earthwork complex, artifact assemblage, exchange network, and labor investment, all of which are central to the costly signaling model. Three contributions are particularly important for the project. First, the quantitative labor estimates (500,000+ cubic yards of earth, 20+ million basket loads for ridges alone) supply baseline data for modeling construction costs as costly signals. Second, the systematic documentation of imported materials from sources spanning 100 to 1,100+ airline miles (copper, galena, steatite, magnetite, hematite, novaculite, quartz crystal, exotic cherts) defines the exchange network that the model treats as individual-level signaling. Third, the evidence for intensive curation and reuse of imported materials demonstrates that these goods were genuinely costly to obtain, a necessary condition for them to function as honest signals. The authors' interpretive framework (class-structured society, Hopewell invasion, agricultural subsistence) has been superseded, but their empirical descriptions of earthworks, artifacts, and material sources remain primary data for the field.

## Data Presented

- Site plan from aerial photography showing concentric ridge system, mounds, and plaza
- Excavation profiles and trench data from multiple cuts across ridges and mounds
- Volume estimates: 500,000+ cubic yards of earth in ridges; comparable amount in mounds
- Clay ball typology: 9 classified types with stratigraphic frequency distributions
- Clay ball density: 142 g/cu ft in Trench 4, extrapolated to ~24 million site-wide
- Projectile point typology: 2,365 specimens classified into named types (Table 3)
- Microflint inventory: 8,083 specimens across multiple functional categories (Table 5)
- Plummet inventory: 406 specimens with material identifications (Table 6)
- Ornamental items: 76 gorgets, 99+ beads of various types, 13 figurines
- Steatite vessel cache: 2,724 fragments with geological source analysis
- Exotic material inventory: copper, galena, quartz crystal, red ocher, imported cherts
- Exchange network map (Figure 45) showing source locations and distances
- 9 radiocarbon dates from 5 laboratories (Table 9)
- Basketry impressions from mound construction fill
- Cooking pit description with in situ clay ball arrangement

## Methodological Notes

- Excavation was limited in extent relative to the site's size; extrapolations from small trenches to the full 160-hectare site carry substantial uncertainty.
- The clay ball production estimate (24 million) is extrapolated from density in a single trench and should be treated as an order-of-magnitude figure.
- Material source identifications were made by expert opinion (e.g., Raymond Baby for Ohio cherts, H.V. Andersen for steatite geology) rather than formal geochemical sourcing. These identifications are generally plausible but not independently verified by modern methods for all materials.
- Radiocarbon dates were among the earliest obtained from any Lower Mississippi Valley site. The scatter between laboratories and large error ranges reflect the state of radiocarbon dating in the 1950s. Modern AMS dating has shifted the chronology substantially earlier.
- The interpretive framework (Hopewell invasion, agricultural economy, class-structured society) reflects mid-20th century archaeological assumptions that large-scale construction required hierarchical organization and agricultural surplus. These interpretive claims have been superseded.
- The authors candidly describe their social organization model as "a pyramid of speculation" (p. 129), making it clear which conclusions they viewed as provisional.

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Haag and Webb 1953** (in collection): Earlier preliminary report on microblades at Poverty Point; Ford and Webb 1956 expands significantly on the microlith data and reinterprets "Jaketown perforators" as exhausted scrapers.
- **Bell 1956** (in collection): Reports a copper plummet from Poverty Point that Ford and Webb also reference (p. 115). Bell's article provides independent documentation of the copper specimen.
- **Blackburn 2003** (in collection): Provides modern geochemical analysis of magnetite and hematite use at Poverty Point, updating the source identifications that Ford and Webb made based on expert visual inspection.
- **Ortmann 2007 / Ortmann and Kidder 2013** (Ortmann 2007 in collection): Subsequent excavations that refined construction chronology, particularly for Mound A. Their evidence for rapid construction episodes partially supports Ford and Webb's inference of simultaneous occupation but with important modifications.
- **Kidder 2002, 2006** (in collection): Later synthetic treatments incorporating updated chronology, subsistence data, and social organization models that supersede Ford and Webb's interpretive framework while building on their empirical foundation.
- **Jackson 1981, 1986** (in collection): Addresses the subsistence question that Ford and Webb answered incorrectly (agriculture); Jackson establishes hunter-gatherer-fisher subsistence.
- **Lipo 2012** (in collection): Reinterprets groundstone plummets using formal analysis, offering an alternative to Ford and Webb's bola weight interpretation.
- **Ford 1954** (in collection): Ford's earlier summary that precedes the full monograph; some data and interpretations were developed between the two publications.
- **Sassaman 2005** (cited in CLAUDE.md): Reinterprets Poverty Point as structure, event, and process, offering theoretical alternatives to Ford and Webb's culture-historical framework.
