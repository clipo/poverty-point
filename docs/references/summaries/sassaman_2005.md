## Relevance to Argument

Sassaman (2005) provides a multiscalar analysis of Poverty Point that directly supports the costly signaling framework in several ways. First, the paper documents that Poverty Point was a multicultural aggregation site where diverse populations converged from at least three distinct regions (northwest, northeast, south), with biological reproduction subordinated to the social production of "worldly" individuals through travel, exchange, and ritual participation (pp. 338, 357-358). This is precisely the kind of cross-group aggregation context where costly signaling would be adaptive. Second, the paper establishes that the nested concentric ridges were an architectural innovation at Poverty Point with no Middle Archaic precedent (p. 343), representing a novel form of cooperative investment. Third, Sassaman documents the nested spatial structure of the Poverty Point interaction sphere (core at 4 km, periphery at 33 km, distant communities at 200+ km, remote affiliates at 600+ km), which constrains the catchment and travel-cost parameters of the aggregation model (p. 346). Fourth, his observation that exotic materials were not restricted to elites but were unevenly distributed across the site and peripheral communities (p. 356) is consistent with a signaling system based on differential access rather than centralized control. Finally, the demonstration that Claiborne on the Gulf coast functioned as a one-sixth-scale replica of Poverty Point's layout (p. 354) supports the argument that monumental form itself carried signal value that was reproduced at satellite sites.

## Data Presented

- Spatial analysis of four Middle Archaic mound complexes (Watson Brake, Caney, Frenchman's Bend, Insley) demonstrating shared geometric and proportional regularities (pp. 340-341)
- Detailed architectural measurements of Poverty Point: Mound A stands 21 m high with base measuring 195 x 216 m; Mound B is dome-shaped, 55 m diameter, 6 m high; Mound E (Ballcourt Mound) approximately 100 m on a side, 2.5 m tall; enclosure maximum diameter ca. 1.1 km with inner plaza 600 m wide (p. 342)
- Seven maps (Figs. 1-7) showing: site plan with Middle Archaic predecessors; Caney Principal Triangle superimposed at 1x and 2x scales; core encampments at 6x scale; peripheral communities at 6x scale; distant communities at 6x scale; eastern North American raw material sources with regional model; Claiborne superimposed on Poverty Point at 6x scale
- Gibson's (1998, 2000) community organization data: 16 core encampments within 3.56 km radius, peripheral sites beginning at approximately 5 km, 10 distant communities within 128.26 km radius (pp. 346-347)
- Clark's (2004) Standard Macro-Unit (SMU) of 1.666 m (a fathom), with the Caney interval of 86.63 m equaling 52 SMUs (p. 341)
- Lower Jackson Mound dated to ca. 3900 cal B.C. (p. 342, citing Saunders et al. 2001)
- Claiborne cache: 12 whole soapstone vessels in sterile sand, with copper sheet bracelets, copper pendant, and galena (p. 354, citing Bruseth 1991)

## Methodological Notes

- The paper employs a multiscalar analytical approach combining agency and practice theory with spatial analysis at increasing geographic scales. The method is primarily interpretive and comparative rather than quantitative.
- The geometric model (nested circles extrapolated from enclosure proportions at multiples of 6) is heuristic. Sassaman acknowledges that the fit between the model and the actual distribution of sites is approximate, not exact (pp. 347, 350).
- The paper relies heavily on Gibson's (1998, 2000) site classifications for core, peripheral, and distant communities. Sassaman notes (footnote 6, p. 346) that criteria for inclusion in "Poverty Point culture" are debated, and more sourcing studies are needed to establish firm empirical connections.
- The Claiborne-Poverty Point scalar comparison depends on Clark's (2004) geometric analysis. The reciprocal fit is suggestive but the chronological relationship between the two sites is not precisely established (p. 355).
- No original excavation data or artifact analyses are presented; the paper synthesizes previously published data in a new interpretive framework.
- The structuralist interpretations (ranked embankments, cosmological symbolism, dual organization) are speculative and acknowledged as hypotheses requiring testing with independent data (p. 360).

## Connections to Other Sources

- **Gibson 2000** (cited extensively): Provides the foundational data on site layout, construction volume (750,000 m3), community organization (core/periphery/distant), and material culture. Sassaman's multiscalar model is built directly on Gibson's spatial classifications.
- **Clark 2004** (cited extensively): Supplies the geometric analysis, Archaic measurement system (Standard Macro-Unit), and proportional relationships among Middle Archaic complexes that Sassaman extrapolates to regional scale.
- **Kidder 2002** (in collection): Cited for the revised topographic map showing that the enclosure is not symmetrical and aisles are not evenly divided (p. 343). See Kidder 2002 claims file.
- **Saunders et al. 1997, 2000, 2001** (cited): Provide dating evidence for Watson Brake, Caney, and Lower Jackson Mound, establishing the Middle Archaic precedents for Poverty Point construction.
- **Bruseth 1991** (cited): Primary source for the Claiborne site data, including the soapstone vessel cache and associated copper and galena artifacts.
- **Webb 1982** (in collection): Cited for economic specialization interpretations at Poverty Point. See Webb 1982 claims file.
- **Jackson 1991** (cited): "Trade fair" model for Poverty Point, which Sassaman reframes in terms of ethnogenetic convergence rather than economic exchange.
- **Haag and Webb 1953** (in collection): Not directly cited by Sassaman, but relevant as an early inventory of Poverty Point material culture.
- **Ortmann 2007** (in collection): Subsequent to this paper; provides detailed analysis of Mound A construction that bears on Sassaman's claims about rapid construction and labor mobilization.
- **Lipo 2012** (in collection): Reinterprets groundstone plummets; relevant to Sassaman's treatment of exotic materials as signals of persona rather than utilitarian items.
- **Hill et al. 2010** (in collection): Relevant to the exchange network structure that Sassaman describes at the supraregional scale.

## Verification Notes

- All quoted text was verified against the PDF. Page numbers in the claims extraction match the published pages.
- The 750,000 cubic meters figure (p. 338) is attributed to Gibson (2000), consistent with the CLAUDE.md project documentation which lists the same figure.
- The construction date range of 1600-1300 cal B.C. (p. 338) is narrower than the 1700-1100 BCE range used in the project CLAUDE.md. This discrepancy reflects different sources: Sassaman cites the construction window specifically, while the project documentation uses the broader Poverty Point period range.
- Mound A dimensions (21 m high, 195 x 216 m base) on p. 342 differ slightly from the project CLAUDE.md (22 m high). The 21 m figure is Sassaman's reported value; the 22 m figure likely comes from other sources (e.g., Ortmann and Kidder 2013).
- The spatial zones (4 km core, 33 km periphery, 200+ km distant, 600+ km remote) on p. 346 are attributed to Gibson (1998, 2000), not independently measured by Sassaman.
- Footnote 3 (p. 337) notes ongoing debate about Poverty Point's sociopolitical complexity, acknowledging that calling it a chiefdom (cf. Gibson 1973, 1974) is contested.
- No discrepancies found between the claims extraction and the PDF, except that the claims extraction omits several secondary claims and data points now included in this summary.
